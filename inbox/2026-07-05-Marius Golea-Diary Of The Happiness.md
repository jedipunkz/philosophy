Editor’s Note to the Romanian Version

The first version of the Diary of the Happiness was finished around the
beginning of the seventies. But almost immediately after being finished the
manuscript was confiscated in unfortunate circumstances. Only a few friends
got to know about the existence of that manuscript.

Being of a courageous and tenacious nature, N. Steinhardt proceeded to
write the second version, one even ampler and more detailed than the first
version. At the end of this difficult toil a new and - this time - a pleasant
surprise was waiting for him. After reading the first version the state’s
representatives got convinced that that manuscript did not pose any threat to
the “state’s security”, so that finally, as result of the influence exercised by D.
R. Popescu - then the president of the Writers Union - the state’s
representatives gave back to its author the blamed diary.

Feeling the pressure of a possible mind change of the authorities, N.
Steinhardt revised, completed, and corrected both version and then he
immediately hid them in safe places. The present edition has had at its basis
the manuscript of the last version. Our printing enjoyed the author’s approval,
fact attested by his words told by him to us when entrusting us with the
publishing of his manuscript, as also by the fact that he wrote on the sealed
package the label: Literaty Testament.

Among the multitude of significances of the mentioned subtitle we are
only mentioning here that one hinting at the conviction of N. Steinhardt related
to the posthumous character of his work. This is attested also by the fact that
some of the ideas comprised in the present work were reiterated in other works
published during his life.

In order us not to diminish at all the epoque perfume of the original text
we have left unchanged some lexical innovations or particularities. On the
same purpose, we have proceeded to decipher the names of the characters
mentioned by author only using their initials. Thus, the reader can find at the
end of the book (after our own notes-apparatus) a list of the onomastic
abbreviations. The few undeciphered cases represent either persons whom we
haven’t been able to find information about, or persons whose identification
hasn’t been important. The reference of other name-abbreviations or
appellatives can be deduced out of context.

The political testament of N. Steinhardt was entrusted to a few close
friends. We think that it would be too risky to say that author’s gesture aimed
at preparing a collective protest action, though in our opinion all arguments
would lead to such a conclusion.

Virgil Candea

THREE SOLUTIONS

Political Testament

In order to escape a concentrationary universe — and it is not absolutely
necessary that to be a concentration camp or a prison or any other form of
imprisonment; this theory can be applied to any type or to any product of the
totalitarianism — there is the (mystical) solution of the faith. This is what is
going to be all about in the following pages, for the escape is the consequence
of the grace that is essentially selective.

The three solutions we are going to refer to are strictly worldly, they have
a practical character, and they appear to be accessible by anyone.

The First Solution: of Solzhenitsyn

In his book The First Circle Alekzandr Isayevich briefly mention that what
we call the first solution, and then he developed it in his book called Gulag
Archipelago. This first solution consists of one telling to himself, assertively,
when crossing over the threshold of the state-security or of any investigation
agency: I am really dying right now. Such a person is allowed to talk to himself
for solace: so bad for my youth age or woe to my old age, or woe to my wife, or
woe to my children, or woe to my talent or to my goods or to my power, or woe
to my lover, or woe to my wines which I haven’t drunk yet, too bad for the
books I will no longer read, too bad for the strolling I haven’t done, too bad for
the music I will no longer listen to etc. etc. etc. But something is certain and
irreparable: from now on I am dead.

If the individual unhesitatingly thinks so, he will be saved. No one will be
able to do anything to him. There will be nothing him to be menaced with, or
blackmailed, or deluded, or fooled. Once he reckons about himself that he is
dead, there will be nothing left which to scare him or to fool him or to attract
him or to entice him. One will no longer be able to trigger such an individual.
This is because such an individual no longer has — because he no longer hopes
for anything, because he has exited the world - what to aspire to, what to keep
or what to regain, or what him to sell his soul for, or his peace, or his honor.
There is no longer any currency which him to be paid with for the price of his
treason.

It is required, though, a firm and definitive decision. You declare yourself
as dead; you accept to surrender yourself to your death, so that you abolish
any of your hopes. You can feel sorrow for yourself, as madam d’Houdetot did,
you can regret, but this moral and anticipated homicide won’t fail. So that the

The Second Solution: of Alexandr Zinoviev

This is the solution found by one of the characters of the book called
Hollow Heights. That character was a young man called by his nickname The
Trouble-maker. The solution consists of the total inadaptation to the political
system. The Trouble-Maker was homeless; he had no id-card; he didn’t work;
He was a bum, he was a parasite, he was a destitute, and he was a hobo. He
lived from today to tomorrow by taking alms or by finding who know what. He
was dressed up in rags. He occasionally worked when and if he came across
the opportunity. For almost the whole his life he was an inmate or he slept in
the streets. He was wandering around. There was nothing in the world to
convince him to enter the system, not even for at least the most insignificant or
for the dirtiest or for the most unengaging job. Not even a pig-herder he didn’t
want to be, so that he refused even to follow the example of the character from
a novella written by Arthur Schnitzler: that character, because of being
obsessed with his fear of responsibility, he ended to be a pig-herder. NO, The
Trouble-Maker designed himself (in an existentialist manner) once-for-all as a
stray-dog, as a mangy-goat, as a beggar-Buddhist-monk, as a lunatic, like an
insane-for (into) freedom one.

Such a man living at the skirts of the society, he is immune too: neither
upon him can one exert pressure or to deprive him of something or to offer him
something. They can lock him up, they can harass him, they can despise him,
they can mock him: but he evades them. He has consented once for all to live
his life according to the example or to the model of a perpetual nigh-refuge. He
has made a creed out of poverty, out of mistrust, and out of flippancy; he
resembles to a wild-beast, to a mangy-beast, to a highway-robber. He is
Ferrante Palia of Stendhal. He is Zechariah Lichter of Matei Calinescu. He is a
lay-wandering-monk, and unbored traveler (and Wotan descending on this
earth what name does he bear? Der Wanderer), a wandering Jew. And he is
loud-talking, he is much-speaking, he voices the most-dangerous stories, he
doesn’t know what respect means, he talks his mind openly, and he utters with
a loud voice truth which other people fear to whisper. He is the child from the
story of the naked king of Andersen. He is the buffoon of King Lear. He is the
wolf form the fable of La Fontaine: he has no idea what a dog-collar means. He
is free, free, free.

The Third Solution: of Winston Churchill and of Vladimir Bukovski

In short it is this: in the presence of the tyranny, of the oppression, or
the misfortunes, or the disasters, of the dangers, you won’t only refuse to give

living and for fighting them.

In May 1939, Churchill told Martha Bibescu: “There will be war. The
British Empire will collapse. Death will stalk all of us. And I feel that I get
twenty years younger’.

The worse the things are for you, the harder the things get for you, the
more you are hit, the more you are surrounded sand submitted to attacks, the
less you can hope probabilistically and rationally, the more the grey and the
darkness and the viscous intensify and rot up and meander inextricably, the
more directly you face the danger, the more will you be eager for fighting and
the more will you experience a (growing) sentiment of inexplicable and
overwhelming euphoria.

You are besieged from all quarters by forces which are infinitely stronger
than yours: you fight. They defeat you: you defy them. You are lost: you attack.
(So spoke Churchill in the year 1940). You laugh, you sharpen your teeth and
your knife, you are getting younger. You are thrilled with happiness, by that
happiness of delivering blows at your turn, even though you can do that
infinitely less. You not only refuse getting despaired, you not only refuse
admitting yourself to be defeated and destroyed, but you even fully taste the joy
of the resistance, of the opposition, and you experience a rushing-in and
demented sensation of gladness.

This solution, of course, it supposes an exceptionally strong character, a
military conception of the life, a formidable moral hardening of the body, a
steel-will, and a diamond-like spiritual health. It probably supposes also a
sports spirit: you must love the battle as such - the skirmish —- more than you
love the success.

This solution too is salutary and absolute because of being based on a
paradox: as the hit you and as they harm you so badly and as the impose to
you increasingly unjust physical sufferings and as they corner you in
increasingly-without-exit places, you get gladder, you get strengthened, and
you get younger!

Churchill’s solution matches Vladimir Bukovski’s solution too. Bukovsky
said that when he received his first summoning to KGB’s headquarters, he
couldn’t sleep for the whole night. That’s was a natural thing, will say the
reader of Bukovski’s memoires, an as natural as one can get thing; the
incertitude, the fear, the emotion. But then Bukovski continued: I couldn’t
sleep because of excitement. I couldn’t wait for dawn to be before them and to
tell them what I thought about them and to collide them like I was a tank. I
couldn’t imagine a greater happiness than that.

Behold why he couldn’t sleep: not because of fear, not because of worry,
not because of emotion, but for the impatience for shouting out the truth to

I think that such extraordinary words haven’t been uttered or written yet

like I am presenting it, no, by any means, but I am only asking, I cannot but
asking myself — if not this universe, despite containing millions of galaxies each

Conclusion: All three solutions are certain and guaranteed to be effective.
I don’t know if there are other solution for exiting such an extreme situation, of
for escaping a concentrationary universe, or for escaping the trap of a Kafkian
process, or for escaping a domino-like collapse, or from escaping this labyrinth
or this investigation chamber, or from escaping the fear and the panic felt like
you were caught in a mouse-trap, of for escaping such a phenomenal
nightmare.

Take heed at the fact that: Solzhenitsyn, Zinoviev, Churchill, and
Bukovski. A consented, assumed, anticipated, and caused death; the
valiantness accompanied by a rabid gladness. You are free to choose. But you
ought to take heed at the fact that —- worldly speaking — another way to face the
iron-circle — a circle having a good portion made of chalk too (see Siege State
wrote Camus: the ground of a dictatorship is a phantasm: the fear) — I doubt
very much that you can find.

You might protest that the mentioned solutions suppose a life form that
equals death, or a life even worse than death, a life implying the risk of
physical death on every moment. And this is true. Are you amazed? If you were
amazed that would mean that you didn’t read Igor Safarevici, because
otherwise you learnt that the totalitarianism is not so much the coagulation of
some economical, biological, or social theories, as it actually is the
manifestation of an attraction to death. And the secret of them who cannot fit
themselves in the totalitarian abyss is simple: they love the life and not the
death. But who has defeated the death by himself? Only Him Who trampled on
death with death.

I believe, Lord! Help my faithlessness! (Mk. 9: 24)
