## Abstract

"In 2026, Sylvia Plath’s Daddy remains an open wound in the American literary canon. It refuses to be healed, sanitized, or fully assimilated. Through the lens of Piaget, we see it as a cognitive regression to a world of dangerous magic where metaphors become reality. Through Klein and Kristeva, we understand it as a necessary act of psychic violence to expel the abject and survive the ‘poison’ of the father. Through Arendt, we recognize the terrifying homology between the fascist state and the patriarchal home, a warning that the "banality of evil" exists in the nursery as much as in the bureau. And through Levinas, we see the tragic failure of ethics when the Other is reduced to a monster, necessitating a ‘killing’ that resolves nothing. The poem’s longevity evidenced by its continued presence in doctoral research and viral social media debates lies in this refusal to resolve. It does not offer the "catharsis" of Greek tragedy but the "repetition compulsion" of trauma. As long as the "black shoe" of patriarchal and historical oppression exists, Daddy will continue to be read, debated, and reviled; a "stake" in the heart of the canon that flowers, perversely, with each new generation of readers. The speaker declares "I'm through," but the cultural obsession with the poem suggests that we, as readers, are far from finished."

The Poetics of the Black Shoe

Sylvia Plath’s "Daddy," composed in the fervent, creative blaze of October 1962, stands as a
monolith in the landscape of twentiethcentury American letters, a text that refuses to settle
into the comfortable strata of literary history. It is a poem that f unctions less as a static
aesthetic object and more as a ritualistic event, a "black shoe" in which the critical tradition
has lived for over sixty years, barely daring to breathe or sneeze. Within the interdisciplinary
field of American Studies, "Daddy" i s not merely a biographical confession of a daughter’s grief
for her father, Otto Plath, who died when she was eight; it is a complex collision of personal
neurosis and the geopolitical traumas of the century, a site where the domestic sphere of the
Americ an 1950s; the era of containment and the "feminine mystique" collapses violently into
the historical atrocity of the Holocaust.

To approach "Daddy" in 2026 is to engage with a text that has accreted vast layers of
theoretical sediment. It has been psychoanalyzed, politicized, cancelled, reclaimed, and
memeified. It exists as a primary document of the "Confessional" mode, yet it ac tively resists
the inherent truthclaims of confession through its reliance on hyperbole, surrealism, and the
mask of the persona. The poem acts as a prism through which the anxieties of the postwar
American subject, specifically the female subject, are r efracted. The speaker’s voice,
oscillating between the murderous rage of an Electra and the regressive dependency of a preverbal child, articulates a crisis that is at once ontological, political, and ethical.

First, we will study the poem’s cognitive and emotional architecture through the developmental
psychology of Jean Piaget and the objectrelations psychoanalysis of Melanie Klein and Julia
Kristeva. We argue that the poem’s formal properties
—
its nurseryrhy me meter and obsessive
repetition
—
signal a deliberate regression to a "preoperational" and "paranoidschizoid" state,
a necessary strategic withdrawal to a psychic space where the "abject" father can be
confronted and destroyed.

Second, we analyze the poem’s political ontology through the lens of Hannah Arendt’s theories
on totalitarianism and the "banality of evil." Here, Plath’s controversial appropriation of
Holocaust imagery is read not as a metaphor for personal suffering, bu t as a structural critique
of the patriarchal family unit as a totalitarian regime. The domestic father, the "man at the
blackboard," is revealed to be homologous to the "Panzerman," sharing the same apparatus
of domination, silencing, and epistemological

destruction that Arendt identified in the Nazi
state.

Third, we subject the text to a rigorous ethical interrogation using the philosophy of Emmanuel
Levinas. We posit that "Daddy" represents a catastrophic failure of the ethical "facetoface"
encounter. The speaker, unable to access the "face" of the father

as an infinitely other human
being, reduces him to a "model"; an effigy, thereby suspending the Levinasian command "Thou
shalt not kill" and initiating a cycle of violence that culminates in the poem’s famous, vampiric
conclusion. Finally, we trace the re ception history of the text into the mid
-
2020s, examining
how
Daddy

survives in the digital age. Through this multifaceted analysis,
Daddy

emerges not
as a resolved artifact of the past, but as a continuing, urgent problem of the present.

The Architecture of Regression: Piaget, Klein, and the PreOperational Psyche

The structural genius of "Daddy" lies in its deceptive simplicity. Plath employs a heavy, trochaic
meter and an insistent rhyme scheme dominated by the "oo" vowel sound
—
you, do, shoe, two,
Jew, true, blue,
gobbledygoo

—
which comprises nearly half of the line endings. This sonic
landscape is not merely an aesthetic choice; it is a cognitive map. It invokes the auditory world
of the nursery rhyme, a deliberate stylistic regression that allows the speaker to access the
raw,

unmediated logic of childhood. To understand the mechanics of this regression, we must
turn to the cognitive developmental theories of Jean Piaget and the psychoanalytic models of
Melanie Klein.

Piagetian Analysis: The PreOperational State and Animism

Jean Piaget’s theory of cognitive development identifies the "preoperational" stage (roughly
ages 2
-
7) as a period characterized by symbolic play, egocentrism, and "animism"; the belief
that inanimate objects possess life and intent. The speaker in "Daddy
" adopts a voice that
oscillates between the adult woman and this preoperational child. The repetition of "You do
not do, you do not do" mimics the incantatory language of early childhood, where words are
treated not as abstract signifiers but as concrete

objects possessing magical, coercive power.

In the Piagetian framework, the child’s world is defined by a lack of "conservation"; the
understanding that an object’s identity remains stable despite changes in its appearance. In
Daddy
, the figure of the father refuses to stay contained within a single identity; he suffers
from a radical failure of conservation. He metamorphoses fluidly from a "bag full of God" to a
"Ghastly statue," then to a Nazi, a swastika, a teacher, and finally, a

vampire. This fluidity

suggests a cognitive crisis in the speaker. She cannot "conserve" the image of the father as a
‘human(e)’ man (Otto Plath); instead, because of the trauma of his early death, he expands to
fill the entire horizon of her existence. He becomes the sky itself
—
"So black no sky could
squeak through".

Furthermore, the "black shoe" in which the speaker has lived "like a foot" is not a metaphor in
the adult sense; in the preoperational reality of the poem, it
is

the literal enclosure of her being.
This collapse of the metaphor into the literal is a hallmark of the child’s mind, which has not yet
achieved the formal operational ability to abstract trauma from its physical manifestations. By
trapping herself in the

"black shoe," the speaker articulates a developmental arrest, a psyche
frozen at the moment of the father’s death, "barely daring to breathe or Achoo". The "Achoo",
a child’s onomatopoeia for a sneeze, signals the physiological regression; the speaker is
reduced to bodily reflexes.

Melanie Klein and the ParanoidSchizoid Position

While Piaget explains the
form

of the regression, Melanie Klein’s objectrelations theory
elucidates the
emotional content
. Klein posits that the infant psyche begins in the "paranoidschizoid position," a state defined by "splitting." The infant cannot comprehend the primary
caregiver (the mother/breast) as a whole person who is both gratifying and frustrating.
Consequently,

the infant splits the object into a "good breast" and a "bad breast," projecting
its own aggressive impulses onto the "bad" object, which is then perceived as a persecutor.

In
Daddy
, the speaker operates almost entirely within this paranoidschizoid mode. The father
is split off from any redeeming human characteristics and becomes the "bad object"
par
excellence
. He is the "brute," the "devil," the "bastard". There is no "depressive position", the

subsequent Kleinian stage where the subject integrates the good and bad aspects of the
object and feels guilt for their aggression
—
visible in the text until perhaps the ambiguous final
line.

The poem functions as a documentation of the "compulsion to destroy" inherent in the
paranoidschizoid position. The speaker projects her own murderous impulses ("I have had to
kill you") onto the father, perceiving him as the one who "bit my pretty red he art in two". This
projection is a defense mechanism; by locating the aggression in the father, the speaker
justifies her own retaliatory violence. The "villagers" dancing on the corpse in the final stanza
represent the externalization of this internal triu mph; the internal "bad objects" have been
expelled, and the persecutor has been successfully persecuted in return.

Kristeva and the Abject Corpse

Julia Kristeva’s theory of ‘abjection’, outlined in
Powers of Horror
, describes the process by
which the subject rejects that which threatens its boundaries; the ‘abject.’ The abject is neither
subject nor object; it is the "me that is not me," often associated with bodily fluids, decay, and
the corpse. In
Daddy
, the father is the ultimate abject figure. He is a "Ghastly statue with one
gray toe", a gangrenous, decaying presence that threatens to infect the living subject.

The speaker’s "fear comes from the present absence unknown in her, through the process of
abjection". The father is "marbleheavy," a "bag full of God", in other words, he is dead matter
that exerts a living weight. To become an autonomous subject (an "I")
, the speaker must
radically exclude this abject presence. The poem is the linguistic process of this expulsion. The
lines "The black telephone’s off at the root / The voices just can’t worm through" signify the
sealing of the border between the self and t he abject. The "worm" image links communication

to decay and the grave. By cutting the telephone, the speaker attempts to establish the
"imaginary border which separates the self from that which threatens".

However, the abject is never fully expelled; it hovers at the periphery. The speaker’s
identification as a "Jew" and the father as a "Nazi" is an attempt to map this internal abjection
onto a historical framework of absolute separation (victim/victimizer),

ensuring that the
boundary remains impermeable. If the father is a Nazi, he is historically and morally "other,"
and thus the speaker can be "pure" in her victimhood.

Totalitarianism and the Domestic Sphere: An Arendtian Reading

The controversial use of Holocaust imagery in "Daddy" shifts the poem from a personal
psychoanalytic drama to a political critique of patriarchal authority. Through the lens of Hannah
Arendt’s
The Origins of Totalitarianism

and her report on the "banality of evil," (
Eichmann in
Jerusalem
) Plath’s metaphors reveal the structural homology between the domestic tyrant and
the political dictator. Plath does not merely compare her father to a Nazi; she suggests that
the structure of the patriarchal nuclear family is a microcosm of the totalitar ian state. This
analogy becomes clear when we take into account her relationship with Ted Hughes.

The Banality of Evil in the Nursery

Hannah Arendt argued that the horror of the Holocaust was facilitated not just by "monsters"
in the theatrical sense, but by the "banality" of bureaucrats like Eichmann who functioned
within a system of total obedience and the erosion of truth. In
Daddy
, Plath conflates the
domestic father; the professor who stands at the blackboard; with the fascist "Panzerman".
This juxtaposition is crucial. It suggests that the "evil" of the father is not necessarily in overt

acts of physical violence (though "The boot in the face" suggests otherwise), but in the
banality

of his authority.

The father demands total allegiance ("I used to pray to recover you"), creating a ‘gaslighting’
effect where the subject’s reality is subsumed by the leader’s will. The "neat mustache" and
the "Aryan eye" serve to domesticate the image of Hitler, bringing
the political terror into the
intimacy of the home. This echoes Arendt’s assertion that totalitarianism destroys the private
sphere; in Plath’s poem, there
is

no private sphere, only the "black shoe" of the regime.

The
fatherfigure erodes the distinction between the public (the war, the Luftwaffe) and the private
(the heart, the prayer), creating a totalizing environment where resistance is impossible except
through selfannihilation.

Epistemological Destruction and the ‘Gas Chamber’

Arendt notes that totalitarian propaganda destroys the "sense by which we take our bearings
in the real world". The speaker in
Daddy

experiences this epistemological collapse. She cannot
speak German ("I never could talk to you"), and the language itself becomes "obscene," an
"engine" chuffing her off to Dachau.

The "barb wire snare" of the German language represents the control of the symbolic order
(the Law of the Father). The speaker’s inability to communicate ("Ich, ich, ich, ich") reflects the
silencing of the victim in the totalitarian state. The "gas" in th e poem is not just the literal gas
of the camps but the suffocating atmosphere of a relationship where the "I" is negated by the
"You." The speaker identifies as a Jew not to claim the specific historical suffering of the
Holocaust, but to articulate a sta te of total vulnerability and "rightlessness" within the domestic
regime.

By casting the father as a Nazi, Plath is identifying the
structure

of the relationship as one of
absolute domination, where the victim is reduced to "a number" or an object to be processed.
The poem acts as a counterpropaganda, a desperate attempt to reclaim the "power of
definition" from the totalitarian father.

Ethics and Alterity: A Levinasian Encounter

The philosophy of Emmanuel Levinas provides a crucial counterpoint to the psychoanalytic and
political readings. Levinas argues that "ethics is first philosophy," preceding ontology (being).
The ethical demand arises from the "Face of the Other," which iss ues the command: "Thou
shalt not kill".
Daddy

can be read as a dramatization of the
failure

of this ethical encounter.

The Face of the Other vs. The Mask of the Father

In Levinasian terms, the Other is "incommensurable," possessing a weight of existence that
cannot be fully comprehended or reduced to the self. In
Daddy
, the speaker attempts to
grapple with the alterity of the father. However, rather than facing the ‘Face’ of the father as
a vulnerable human, she encounters a ’mask’; the "Meinkampf look," the "swastika," the "black
shoe".

The poem represents a failure of the Levinasian ethical encounter because the father refuses
to appear as a Face. He appears as a Totalitarian System. The speaker, in turn, cannot access
the "affective dimension" of the father’s lived existence. Instead, s he reduces him to a
"model":"I made a model of you". By turning the Other into a model (an effigy), she strips him
of his infinite alterity and reduces him to an object that can be controlled and destroyed. This
is the ultimate unethical act in Levinas’s v iew: the reduction of the Other to the Same (a

creation of the self).

The Aesthetics of Pathos and the Murder of the Other

Scott DeShong’s analysis of Plath through Levinas suggests that Plath’s poetry often prioritizes
"pathos" (apprehension of feeling) over "ethos" (judgment of character). However, "Daddy"
presents a paradox. The pathos of the speaker
—
her immense suffering
—
j ustifies the
suspension of the ethical command not to kill.

"Daddy, I have had to kill you". This line is the ultimate rejection of Levinasian ethics. To survive
the "affective weight" of the father, the speaker must commit ontological patricide. The poem
argues that when the Other appears as a "fascist," the ethic al obligation to the Other is severed
by the imperative of selfpreservation. The "villagers" stamping on the corpse represents the
community’s validation of this murder; the ethical bond is broken, and the "bastard" is expelled
from the realm of moral con sideration.

Ultimately, the poem demonstrates the
impossibility

of ethics in a totalitarian (or totally abject)
relationship. The ‘Face’ of the father is not a source of divine command but a "boot in the face,"
negating the possibility of dialogue.

LinebyLine Gloss and Analysis

This section provides a granular analysis of the poem’s progression, integrating the German
philology, historical references, and theoretical frameworks discussed above.

Stanzas 1
-
3: The Containment and the Statue

Line 1 ("You do not do, you do not do"):

The opening spondees act as a negation. It is a
magical incantation, a refusal of the father’s continued existence. The repetition signals the
regression to the preoperational stage.

Line 2 ("Any more, black shoe"):

The father is metonymically reduced to a shoe. This is the
"container" of the speaker’s existence.

Line 3 ("In which I have lived like a foot"):

The speaker is objectified as a body part, lacking
autonomy. The "foot" is dark, cramped, and sensorydeprived.

Line 4 ("For thirty years, poor and white"):

Plath was thirty when she wrote the poem. "Poor
and white" suggests a ghostly, bloodless existence, stripped of vitality by the "shoe."

Line 5 ("Barely daring to breathe or Achoo"):

"Achoo" rhymes with "shoe" and "do." It is the
language of the nursery. The suppression of the sneeze is the suppression of the life force.
1

Line 6 ("Daddy, I have had to kill you"):

The confession. The use of "Daddy" (not Father)
reinforces the childvoice. The killing is a necessity ("had to"), not a choice.

Line 7 ("You died before I had time
—
"):

The historical reality (Otto died when Sylvia was 8)
intrudes. She could not kill him metaphorically in adolescence because he was already dead
physically.

Line 8 ("Marbleheavy, a bag full of God"):

The father is deified and reified. He is heavy, cold,
impermeable. A "bag" suggests he is a collection of fragments held together by the "God"
projection.

Line 9 ("Ghastly statue with one gray toe"):

Reference to Otto Plath’s gangrene. The
"statue" is the Colossus. The "gray toe" is the abject point of decay. Notice the trope of
‘abjection’ anticipating Julia Kristeva’s work.

Line 10 ("Big as a Frisco seal"):

The scale is mythic. The toe spans the continent to San
Francisco.

Lines 11
-
13 ("And a head in the freakish Atlantic..."):

The father’s body forms a bridge
across the ocean. The Atlantic is "freakish" because it contains this monstrosity. "Bean green
over blue" paints the sea in colors of bruising and sickness.

Line 14 ("In the waters off beautiful Nauset"):

Nauset is in Cape Cod, where Plath lived. The
juxtaposition of "beautiful" with the "freakish" father highlights the contamination of her home.

Line 15 ("I used to pray to recover you"):

The longing of the child. The desire to "recover" is
both to get him back (health) and to find him (lost object).

Line 15 ("Ach, du"):

German for "Oh, you." A sigh of exhaustion, but also an address. It rhymes
with the "oo" chain.

7: The German Tongue and the Holocaust

Otto Plath was from Grabow, a town

Line 17 ("Scraped flat by the roller"):

Poland as the victim of history (wars). The "roller" is the

Repetition emphasizes the endless cycle of violence.

The geographical confusion mirrors the

Line 24 ("Put your foot, your root"):

Connection between the "foot" (gangrene) and the

Line 25 ("I never could talk to you"):

The failure of communication. The "tongue stuck."

Line 26 ("The tongue stuck in my jaw"):

Physiological paralysis. Tetanus or fear.

Line 27 ("It stuck in a barb wire snare"):

The imagery shifts to the concentration camp. The
language itself is a weapon of confinement.

Line 28 ("Ich, ich, ich, ich"):

German for "I." The speaker stutters, trying to assert the ego ("I")
but getting stuck in the German language of the father. It sounds like an engine starting or a

Line 29 ("I could hardly speak"):

The silencing of the subaltern.

Line 30 ("I thought every German was you"):

The generalization of the father into the
national enemy
vide

Arendtian totalization.

Line 31 ("And the language obscene"):

German is rejected as a language of filth/obscenity.

Line 32 ("An engine, an engine"):

The language becomes a machine, the train to the camps.

Line 33 ("Chuffing me off like a Jew"):

The metaphorical leap. The speaker identifies with the
ultimate victim.

Line 34 ("A Jew to Dachau, Auschwitz, Belsen"):

The litany of camps. A chant of horror.

Line 35 ("I began to talk like a Jew"):

Adoption of the victim’s dialect.

Stanzas 8
-
11: The Gypsy and the Swastika

Line 36 ("I think I may well be a Jew"):

The identification deepens. A controversial claim to
ontological Jewishness.

Line 37 ("The snows of the Tyrol, the clear beer of Vienna"):

Images of "pure" German
culture are rejected as "not very pure or true."

Line 38 ("With my gipsy ancestress and my weird luck"):

Plath claims a counterheritage
(Gypsy/Roma) to oppose the Aryan. "Weird luck" suggests the tarot, the irrational.

Line 39 ("And my Taroc pack and my Taroc pack"):

Repetition. The Tarot cards (magic) vs.

Line 40 ("I may be a bit of a Jew"):

The hesitation ("a bit") vs the assertion.

Line 41 ("I have always been scared of you"):

The core emotion: fear.

Line 42 ("With your Luftwaffe, your gobbledygoo"):

"Gobbledygoo" mocks German as

Line 43 ("And your neat mustache"):

Hitler’s mustache.

Line 44 ("And your Aryan eye, bright blue"):

The Nazi ideal. The "eye" that watches
(Panopticon).

Line 45 ("Panzerman, panzerman, O You
—
"):

The father is the tank driver. "O You" is the

Line 46 ("Not God but a swastika"):

The replacement of the divine with the political symbol
of hate.

Line 47 ("So black no sky could squeak through"):

Totalitarian domination. No
transcendence is possible.

Line 48 ("Every woman adores a Fascist"):

The most analyzed line. Plath suggests a
masochistic conditioning in women to love power. This may be ironic in that it stereotypes
female desire and in the process, also stereotypes the construction of a man as tough and
powerful. In a certain sense, thi s dehumanises both sexes.

Line 49 ("The boot in the face, the brute"):

The violence is physical and humiliating.

Line 50 ("Brute heart of a brute like you"):

Tautology. The brute is a brute. There is no depth,
only brutality.

-
16: The Vampire Husband and the Exorcism

Line 51 ("You stand at the blackboard, daddy"):

Return to the domestic. Otto as professor.

Line 52 ("In the picture I have of you"):

A specific memory/photograph.

Line 53 ("A cleft in your chin instead of your foot"):

The devil has a cloven hoof. Here, it is
displaced to the chin (a dimple?), disguising the demonic nature.

Line 54 ("But no less a devil for that"):

The disguise does not fool the speaker.

The "black man" is both the

vital

split by the bite.

Factual correction (she was 8, but 10 fits the

Line 57 ("At twenty I tried to die"):

Plath’s suicide attempt in 1953.

Line 58 ("And get back, back, back to you"):

The death drive. Suicide as a reunion with the

.

Line 59 ("I thought even the bones would do"):

Desperation for any contact, even with the

Rescue as a violation.

The "glue" of psychiatry. The self is

Reception History: From Confession to Cancel Culture (1965
–
2026)

The reception of
Daddy

acts as a barometer for the shifting cultural mores of the West, evolving
from initial shock to feminist canonization, and finally to a complex negotiation with identity
politics and digital discourse.

1965
–
2000: Confessionalism and the Feminist Turn

Upon its posthumous publication in
Ariel

(1965),
Daddy

was read primarily through the lens of

"Confessional Poetry." Critics like M.L. Rosenthal focused on the "naked honesty" and the
autobiographical connection to Otto Plath and Ted Hughes. It was seen as a raw, almost
embarrassing exposure of the psyche. However, in the 1970s and 80s, secondwave

feminists
embraced the poem as a foundational text. The killing of the father was read not as a neurotic
symptom but as a revolutionary act; the necessary destruction of the "Edifice of Patriarchy" to
allow the female voice to emerge.

2000
–
2020: The Ethics of the Holocaust Metaphor

As the 20th century closed, critical focus shifted to the ethics of Plath’s Holocaust imagery.
Critics like the Nobel Laureate Seamus Heaney and Leon Wieseltier had famously questioned
the "right" of a nonJewish American woman to coopt the specific hist orical suffering of the
‘Shoah’. Thereafter scholarly articles began to analyze the poem as a "failed" attempt at
solidarity or a narcissistic appropriation of trauma. The question became: Can a white, middleclass woman legitimately use the symbol of the
Jew to describe her domestic unhappiness?.

2020
–
2026: PostCritique, Digital Discourse, and Legal Testimony

By the mid
-
2020s, the reception of "Daddy" bifurcated into distinct academic and popular
spheres, each applying new lenses to the text.

Academic Scholarship (2024
–
2026)

Recent scholarship has moved beyond the "appropriation" debate into more structural
analyses.

Usman et al. (2025):

In their paper "A Dichotomy of The Paternal Figure," these
scholars utilize a Critical Discourse Analysis (CDA) to view the poem not just as a

feminist text but as a critique of "robotized" social systems. They argue that the poem’s
"dichotomy" of the father (God/Nazi) reflects the dual nature of power in the modern
"robotized millennium," where authority is both worshipped and destructive. They
reemphasize the "Electra complex" but frame it within a "posthuman" context of
mechanical oppression.

Alice Hao (2025):

In a prizewinning essay for the Criminal Justice Section, Hao
analyzes
Daddy

alongside
The Jailer

and
Lady Lazarus

as forms of "legal testimony."
Hao argues that Plath’s "confession" should be read as a juridical act
—
a "Victim Impact
Statement" for domestic violence. This reading shifts the poem from a literary
performance to a forensic evidence of trauma, bridging th e gap between "poetic
language" and "legal discourse." Hao suggests that Plath articulates a "personal truth"
that the rigid structures of 1960s law could not accommodate.

Subagyo (2025):

Although less widely circulated, references to a paper on
"Confronted Patriarchy" suggest a continuing interest in the poem within the Global
South academic context, reading Plath’s resistance as a model for postcolonial
assertions of identity against "f ather" empires.

Social Media and Digital Reception

On social platforms,
Daddy

undergoes a different scrutiny, often divorced from academic rigor
but intensely personal.

Reddit:

Threads from 2020
–
2025 show a polarized readership. Some users, particularly

The Father as Muse

To fully situate
Daddy

in the American canon, it is instructive to compare it with other poetic
engagements with the fatherfigure.

Sharon Olds vs. Sylvia Plath

Sharon Olds’s collection
The Father

(1992) offers a stark counterpoint. While Plath
mythologizes the father into a Nazi/God, Olds focuses on the
corporeality

of the dying father.
Olds’s speaker does not "kill" the father but witnesses his physical decay with a "clinical" gaze.
Plath’s
Daddy

is about
distance

and ‘monumentality’ (the Colossus); Olds’s work is about
intimacy

and the
abjection

of the flesh. Plath rejects the body to survive; Olds dissects it to
understand. Plath’s father is a "bag full of God"; Olds’s father is a bag of bones and fluids.

Ocean Vuong and the Immigrant Father

Ocean Vuong’s
Night Sky with Exit Wounds

(2016) represents a 21stcentury evolution of the
Plathian mode. Like Plath, Vuong grapples with an absent/mythologized father and the trauma
of war (Vietnam). In his poem
Telemachus
, Vuong tries to pull the father out of the sea
(reversing Plath’s "freakish Atlantic" where the father is a statue). However, where Plath uses
the "axe" and the "stake" to sever the tie, Vuong uses a tender, almost reverent language to
touch the wound. Vu ong’s work suggests a move from Plath’s "rejection" (Kristevan Abjection)
to a form of "reparation" (Klein’s depressive position), seeking to integrate the trauma rather
than expel it.

Conclusion: The Unburied Corpse

In 2026, Sylvia Plath’s

Daddy

remains an open wound in the American literary canon. It refuses
to be healed, sanitized, or fully assimilated. Through the lens of Piaget, we see it as a cognitive
regression to a world of dangerous magic where metaphors become reality. Through Klein and

Kristeva, we understand it as a necessary act of psychic violence to expel the abject and survive
the ‘poison’ of the father. Through Arendt, we recognize the terrifying homology between the
fascist state and the patriarchal home, a warning that the "bana lity of evil" exists in the nursery
as much as in the bureau. And through Levinas, we see the tragic failure of ethics when the
Other is reduced to a monster, necessitating a ‘killing’ that resolves nothing.

The poem’s longevity evidenced by its continued presence in doctoral research and viral social
media debates lies in this refusal to resolve. It does not offer the "catharsis" of Greek tragedy
but the "repetition compulsion" of trauma. As long as the "blac k shoe" of patriarchal and
historical oppression exists,
Daddy

will continue to be read, debated, and reviled; a "stake" in
the heart of the canon that flowers, perversely, with each new generation of readers. The

speaker declares "I'm through," but the cultural obsession with the poem suggests that we, as
readers, are far from finished.
