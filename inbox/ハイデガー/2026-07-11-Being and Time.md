## Abstract

<p>This chapter introduces Martin Heidegger's <italic>Being and Time</italic>, emphasizing the importance of selecting a good English translation when studying the text closely. It compares three key translations: Macquarrie and Robinson's 1962 version, Stambaugh's 1996 edition, and its 2010 revision by Schmidt, pointing out differences in accuracy, readability, and annotations. The chapter explains that Heidegger begins <italic>Being and Time</italic> by reviving the ancient question of Being and challenging readers to recognize how mysterious and overlooked this concept truly is. Heidegger argues that understanding Being requires uncovering its meaning through the proper horizon, which he proposes is time. It also asserts that even the most trivial things have meaning because they matter to people, setting the ambitious goal of interpreting time as the horizon for understanding Being.</p>
