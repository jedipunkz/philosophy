preamble LIT

Canterbury Cathedral is rather large. So is this book. What began as a
leisurely stroll has grown into a fairly long walk. So, simply for convenience,
this volume is now one of three. Pd prefer that you read all three,
consecutively, but as with visiting the cathedral you can always skip, or
wander, or browse what happens to interest you. Nevertheless, I hope the
whole project might be read as a single rather meandering essay. And you
can take your own time.

Works about cathedrals fall into familiar categories: tourist guide,
architectural history, lavishly illustrated coffee-table book. There’s also a
library of scholarly studies, and numerous books on the religious function
of cathedrals, their place in contemporary society, and so on. I didn’t want
to write any of those.

For many years I had the task, when family and friends visited Canterbury,
of being the one to ‘show them round’ the cathedral. It’s a pleasant way to
spend a few hours, and I’ve learned a great deal, not just about the
cathedral. Pve also recognised that people have very different ranges of
prior knowledge, so getting the tone right between stating the obvious and
offering overwhelming detail is always tricky. This essay is intended to be
like a conversational ramble with a patient friend, but as an old academic I
can’t wholly avoid a seminar or lecture mode. But what else would you
expect from considering a cathedral?

In particular, I know that my own reaction, when visiting other cathedrals,
is that just being told the dates of long-dead bishops, or details about the
masonry, or the style of arches, doesn’t match what a building such as
Canterbury Cathedral evokes, or clarify what the place seems to say to a
visitor who is awed or impressed or puzzled by it. A cathedral invites us to
think about rather large issues which are difficult to get into focus.

Meandering through the cathedral I remember T. S. Eliot’s phrase about
allowing a ‘half-formed thought to pass’. As we drift and gaze and
comment, we are half-conscious of a complex set of responses, but we’re
tempted simply to shrug and wander on. Trying to articulate how we are
responding to this unique place takes more time than people have in a short
visit.

So this is an attempt to follow through some of my own ‘half-formed’
thoughts prompted by the cathedral. Pve ended up considering a variety of
issues in a loose relation to different parts of the building, as the plan
opposite indicates.

In the first part I tried to think about history and historians, the past, death,
and time The second part was concerned with some issues around art and
iconoclasm, with aspects of the history of science, and problems about
money or economics. These last two segments are concerned with the odd
business of thinking, and finally with music.

But how do you put a cathedral into a book?

Partly by using lots of quotations. Pve treated the many quotations as
retrieved fragments of the past, like the surviving stones of the building
itself. The essay is not intended as a scholarly work and I haven’t used
footnotes but hope I’ve given enough information on my sources to make
them easy enough to find on the net. Pve tried to respect the ‘fair use’
conventions of copyright and I use my own translations (and my son’s)
where possible. To have included colour illustrations would have more
than doubled the price of the book, but it’s easy to find appropriate images
of the cathedral.

The sub-headings are simply to help you find your way about, since there
are many points to pause over and consider or query, but at least, unlike a
real visit, you can take as much time as you like. Ideally, as in old-fashioned
books, I would have incorporated a silk thread as a place marker.

This final volume includes some personal acknowlegements at the end.
We are now more than half-way through the Cathedral and can soon relax
with some music. But first, some thinking about thinking, with the help of

Anselm.

So, if you would care to accompany me...

THINKING WITH ANSELM

I have been visiting Canterbury Cathedral for several decades, often in a
throng of tourists and sometimes with just a friend or two. Almost always,
the quietist and emptiest spot is the small chapel on the south side of the
ambulatory around the choir and sanctuary. Not many visitors enter it, and
those who do usually leave after a few moments. There is little to see.
Certainly nothing of splendour. Tucked away high in a corner, and almost
out of sight, there is a fragment of a mediaeval wall painting, mentioned in
some guide-books. In the apse there is an altar, basically a large block of
black marble, with the name ‘Anselm’ simply inscribed on the step leading
up to it. There is one other tomb, of undistinguished stone, with an epitaph
with a name unfamiliar to most of us. So this side-room chapel is among
the least impressive places in the whole cathedral. Yet it remains one of my
favourite spots. Peaceful, quiet, somewhere to sit and think. Which is
appropriate. Since the chapel is dedicated to one of the most interesting of
European thinkers.

Anselm’s life was discussed in some detail in my comments on the nave,
particularly in relation to the historian Eadmet’s writings about him. A very
brief reminder: he was born in the 1030s, was Archbishop of Canterbury
from 1093 to his death in 1109, and was canonised as a Saint in the late
15th century. Anselm was presumably buried at Canterbury but in the
chapel now dedicated to him there is no gravestone for him or any other
marker of an actual burial.

Indeed, in 1752, when the Cathedral was approached by King Charles
Emmanuel for permission to translate Anselm’s relics to Italy, Archbishop
Herring is recorded as saying that “parting with the rotten Remains of a
Rebel to his King, a Slave to the Popedom, and an Enemy to the married
Clergy” would be of no great concern, and he would even “make no
Conscience of palming on the Simpletons any other old Bishop with the
Name of Anselm”. Nothing came of the request and no grave location was
ever confirmed.

One person whom we know for sure is buried within St Anselm’s Chapel
is Thomas Bradwardine, who was Archbishop of Canterbury for scarcely
more than a month in 1349 and in effect visited the Cathedral only to be
buried there. His tomb has a fine inscription, incorporating a phrase from
Chaucer’s Canterbury Tales which almost ranks Bradwardine with Augustine
and Boethius. Known as Doctor Profundus, in his time Bradwardine was a
major theologian but is now remembered if at all as a pioneer of
mathematical inquiries into velocity at an instant, and for using exponential
functions to explain the laws of motion. It seems discourteous simply to
ignore him, but I want to concentrate on thinking about Anselm.

So far, in my meandering consideration of the cathedral, the various
physical areas have been somewhat arbitrarily associated with different
disciplines, different domains of thinking: the nave and history, Pecham’s

1

tomb and science, the Treasury and economics, and so on, each regarded as
a particular form of intellectual inquiry. In considering Anselm’s chapel I
now want to think, as it were, about thinking itself. Which is a somewhat
problematic notion.

Anselm is today most remembered for the phrase ‘fides quaerens intellectum ,
usually translated as ‘faith seeking understanding’, though zntellectum can also
be taken as meaning something more like ‘intelligence’ or ‘mind’, often
regarded as the distinguishing characteristic of being human. But we now
have a somewhat fuzzy notion of ‘artificial intelligence’, as a kind of
‘thinking’ not by humans but by computers. In focusing on Anselm’s work
I want to explore a range of issues, and not only those associated with
belief, faith, or theology, the most obvious topics to explore when
considering a cathedral.

Anselm’s current reputation is not primarily as a saint or theologian but
as a philosopher, the author of the so-called ‘ontological proof for the
existence of God. Whether God exists is a not unimportant issue if one is
visiting a cathedral, but discussing Anselm’s ‘proof does not necessarily
involve assessing its validity, even though making up your own mind about
the ‘ontological argument’ might well entail reconsidering your personal
belief. However, in considering Anselm’s process of thinking in his
Proslogion I am not concerned so much with the specific discipline of
‘theology’ but rather with the issue of ‘thinking’ more generally. For a start,
how do I even think about thinking?

giddiness

There can be a kind of giddiness involved in trying to think about
‘thinking’. We tend to deploy rather loosely such notions as ‘mind’, perhaps
imagined as a peculiar kind of space inside the brain or skull, or vaguely
visualised as a dark interior, endless in its extent, almost as we might
imagine deep space itself. The curious and elusive inhabitant of that space
we might think of as our ‘soul’ or ‘spirit’, or what the philosopher Gilbert
Ryle memorably characterised as ‘the ghost in the machine’. The process of
‘thinking’ may be felt as not even located ‘inside’ the brain at all but as
somehow non-corporeal, immaterial. We might even experience quasi-
verbal thinking as occurring in a strangely audible but otherwise inaccessible
‘interior’, yet not located at some specific place which the ears can pinpoint
in normal physical space. So we might ask not only what thinking is but
where it is. ‘In our mind’?

The way we loosely use the term ‘mind’ can also be variously taken to
refer to ‘consciousness’, ‘self-consciousness’, of consciousness of the ‘self —
which may include the notion that my ‘self or ‘soul’ is somehow what
makes me the individual T am and enables me to ‘think’. If that soul is
somehow an immaterial spirit then, it is often argued or vaguely supposed,
the self can perhaps survive the demise of the physical body. The notions

of ‘spirit? and ‘consciousness’ can then be associated with the idea of an a-
material and perhaps a-temporal God, conceived or quasi-imagined along
similar lines as a ‘spirit’, but — unlike our individual mind — as somehow
both everywhere and in every time, omnipresent and eternal. We may also
use words like ‘spirit’ or ‘soul’ as a kind of shorthand for various activities
or processes which we indicate by such terms as willing, deciding, choosing,
but the activity of ‘thinking’ remains particularly elusive.

Since ‘thinking’ can also sometimes be experienced as an almost instant
insight, this may also reinforce a notion that ‘spirit’ is somehow non-
temporal. Or perhaps we might envisage the transition from ‘thinking’ to a
completed ‘thought’ as the sudden consolidation of various sequential
processes, like the last digit completing a sequence of numbers which taken
together open a combination lock. Similarly, a ‘thought’ can sometimes be
experienced as a sudden and even unexpected result of ‘making
connections’,

On the other hand, we can be very aware of the peculiar relationship
between the process of thinking and the time ‘it takes’. The rough notes
from which I am dictating this section were originally two scrappy pages
scribbled in a few minutes while nearly falling asleep, but it has taken some
time to shape a mote or less acceptable prose version of which those notes
were some kind of rapid and elliptical shorthand, encapsulating in a few
jottings a considerable amount of later ‘thought’. The prose version you
have just read is not a transcription but an attempted reconstruction of the
connections which were indicated, almost as a sketchy diagram, and jotted
down in those few moments of insomniac rumination. A densely complex
‘thought’ can sometimes seem captured in a few rapid lines of scrawl —
which may afterwards prove an almost indecipherable and unfortunately
irrecoverable ‘half-thought’. In my case, arthritic hand-writing combined
with inadequate dictation software may make any retrieved ‘thought’ utterly
unreliable and incoherent anyway.

Taken together, these various cliché notions or motifs perhaps amount to
a fairly familiar model of what we might experience as ‘thinking’, and most
of the time we can get along quite contentedly without probing much
further into what sense we make of that vague model. However, faced with
Anselm’s so-called ‘ontological proof we can find that we are puzzlingly
unsure of what we are doing as we try to ‘think it through’. The odd feature
of Anselm’s argument is that it seems to be not primarily sequential, linear,
or syllogistic, but presents itself as having been a kind of instantaneous
conclusion, a formulation folding upon itself to imply a degree of self-
evidence, or the sudden understanding of an implication. The argument
may somehow ‘click’, as it seems to have done for Anselm himself after
several days of frustrating effort. Or it may not, for some readers. But what
then is involved in understanding that kind of understanding?

You will have noticed that so far I have not even outlined this so-called
ontological argument, and it will be some time before I do so.

processes

Let me first quote two of the most vivid descriptions of the process of
‘thinking’ to be found in any mediaeval document. One is in the preface by
Anselm himself to his text entitled Pros/ogion and the other is in the Life of
Anselm written by Eadmer, which we considered from a different
perspective earlier. Chapter 19 of Eadmer’s Life is headed :

Concerning the books which he wrote, and what first happened to the
one which he called the Pros/ogion.

At this period he wrote three treatises: On Truth, On the Freedom of the
Will, and On the Fall of the Devil... He also composed another small
book, which he called the Monologion. In this he alone spoke and argued
with himself and while remaining silent concerning all authority of Holy
Scripture, he enquired into and discovered by reason alone what God
is, and what the true faith holds God to be, he proved by invincible
reason that it could not be other than it is. Afterwards it came into his
mind to try to prove by one single and short argument what is believed
and proclaimed about God, that he is eternal, unchangeable,
omnipotent, omnipresent, incom-prehensible, just, righteous, merciful,
true, truth, goodness, justice and not otherwise and how all these are
one in him.

Which matters, as he himself acknowledged, caused him great
difficulty. Partly because such thinking took away his desire for food,
drink and sleep, and which concerned him more it disturbed the
attention which he ought to have paid to matins and to the divine
office at other times.

When he considered this, yet could not entirely grasp what he was
searching for, he supposed that this line of thought was a temptation by
the devil and he tried to dispel it far from his mind. But the more he
tried to dissuade himself, the thought more and more preoccupied him.
Then suddenly one night during the night office the grace of God
illuminated his heart, it all became clear to his mind, and a great joy and
delight filled his inner being. Reckoning therefore that others too would
be pleased if they knew what he had found, he immediately and without
stint wrote it down on writing tablets and gave them to one of the
brethren of the monastery for safe-keeping.

A few days later he asked the monk in charge of them for the tablets.
The place where they had been deposited was searched, but they were
not to be found. The brethren were asked whether anyone had taken
them, but with no result. And to this day no-one has been found who
has admitted that he knew anything about them. Anselm prepared
another writing on the same subject on other tablets, and entrusted
them to the same monk for more careful keeping. He placed them once
more by his bed, in a more secret place, and the next day with no

suspicion of any mischief he found them strewn on the floor beside his
bed and the wax which was on them scattered in bits. After the tablets
had been picked up and the wax fragments gathered, they were handed
over to Anselm. He pieced together the wax and barely managed to
retrieve the writing. Fearing it might be altogether lost by some
carelessness, he ordered it, in the name of the Lord, to be copied onto
parchment. From this, therefore, he composed a small volume, but one
full of weighty enquiries and subtle accreditations, which he called the
Proslogion. Because in this work he speaks either to himself or to God.

Anselm himself gives a shorter account in the prologue or proemium to
the Pros/ogion (as translated by Sidney Norton Deane):

After I had published, at the solicitous entreaties of certain brethren, a
brief work [the Mono/ogion| as an example of meditation on the grounds
of faith, in the person of one who investigates, in a course of silent
reasoning with himself, matters of which he is ignorant; considering
that this book was knit together by the linking of many arguments, I
began to ask myself whether there might be found a single argument
which would require no other for its proof than itself alone (unum
argumentum quod nullo alio ad se probandum quam se solo indigeret) and alone
would suffice to demonstrate that God truly exists, and that there is a
supreme good requiring nothing else, which all other things require for
their existence and well-being; and whatever we believe regarding the
divine being.

Although I often and earnestly directed my thought to this end, and
at some times that which I sought seemed to be just within my reach,
while again it wholly evaded my mental vision, at last in despair I was
about to cease, as if from the search for a thing which could not be
found. But when I wished to exclude this thought altogether, lest, by
busying my mind to no purpose, it should keep me from other
thoughts, in which I might be successful; then more and more, though
I was unwilling and shunned it, it began to force itself upon me, with a
kind of importunity. So, one day, when I was exceedingly wearied with
resisting its importunity, in the very conflict of my thoughts, what I had
despaired of offered itself, so that I eagerly embraced the thoughts
which I was strenuously repelling.

Thinking, therefore, that what I rejoiced to have found, would, if put
in writing, be welcome to some readers, of this very matter, and of
some others, I have written the following treatise, in the person of one
who strives to lift his mind to the contemplation of God, seeking to
understand what he believes (quarentis intelligere quod credit ).

In my judgement, neither this work nor the other, which I mentioned
above, deserved to be called a book, or to bear the name of an author;
and yet I thought they ought not to be sent forth without some title by

which they might, in some sort, invite one into whose hands they fell to
their perusal. I accordingly gave each a title, that the first might be
known as An Example of Meditation on the Grounds of Faith, (exemplum
meditandi de ratione fidei) and its sequel as Faith Seeking Understanding (fides
quaerens intellectum ). But after both had been copied by many people
under these titles, several urged me, and especially Hugo, the reverend
Archbishop of Lyons, who discharges the apostolic office in Gaul, who
instructed me to this effect on his apostolic authority, to prefix my
name to these writings. So that this might be done more fittingly, I
named the first, Mono/ogion, that is, a soliloquy (so#loquium ) but the
second, Pros/ogion, that is, an address (alloquium).

Consider some aspects of these two extraordinary passages. Anselm
struggles to find the precise expression or formulation for the ‘single
argument’ that he is looking for, instead of ‘the linking of many arguments’
he had offered in his previous treatise, the Mono/ogion, and it finally dawns
upon him, quite suddenly, as a kind of immediate insight. He says he chose
to write it down for the sake of his fellow monks and possible readers, but
presumably this was not the whole prose formulation of the complete
Proslogion (some 30 printed pages?) that ‘offered’ itself to him in that
illuminating moment during Matins. Eadmer emphasises Anselm’s need to
get ‘what he had found’ written down and his anxiety when the first written
formulation was lost. And then the second version was also partly
destroyed.

Anselm himself does not mention the peculiar business about the missing
and broken tablets, but the implication of Eadmet’s narrative is that the
insight was such that if the written versions were lost so too was the exact
formulation of the crucial insight. If it had been so desperately difficult in
the first place to arrive at a precise understanding or articulation, one might
question whether the second and third attempts to write down the insight
were indeed faithful to the actual first moment of insight which had
occurred ‘a few days’ earlier. Only after Anselm had reconstructed the
fragments of writing from the second set of tablets ‘with difficulty’ was a
version based upon them finally written onto parchment and safely
preserved.

Eadmer’s account reminds me of an anecdote once told me by the critic
F. R. Leavis about an evening he spent with Ludwig Wittgenstein. Leavis
felt that Wittgenstein had somewhat overstayed his visit, and to persuade
him to leave reminded him that he was due to give a talk at the Moral
Sciences Club the following day and that perhaps he should go home to
prepare it. Wittgenstein at first reacted by saying that he had no need to
leave, since he knew perfectly well what he was intending to say. But then
quite suddenly Wittgenstein struck his forehead in a kind of panic and
dashed for the door. Alarmed by this over-reaction, Leavis reassured him:
“But you said you know what you are going to say!” Wittgenstein replied:

“Of course I know what I am going to say. But I haven't written it down yet.
And I might die tonight.”

in intellectu

There are, it seems, several processes involved in Anselm’s discovering or
constructing what became known as the ‘ontological argument’. There is
the attempt to arrive at an elusive thought, which involves a considerable
amount of frustrated ‘work of thinking’. There is then a moment of
illumination. Then the writing down which finally ‘fixes’ the thought and
protects its fragility or vulnerability, since until a process of ‘thinking’ takes
the form of a reiterable assertion or formulation, whether spoken or
written, there is in a sense no ‘thought’ — in much the way that, say, William
Morris might have regarded the labour involved in a ‘work of art’ as only
complete if an actual art-work is achieved.

Anselm himself uses the analogy of a painter, in Pros/ogion, chapter 2, to
make a somewhat different point:

when a painter thinks beforehand (praecogitat ) what he is going to
execute, he has something in his understanding (¿n intellectu), but he
does not yet think that it actually exists because he has not yet executed
it. However, when he has actually painted it, then he both has it in his
understanding and understands that it exists because he has now made
it (babet in intellectu et intelligit esse quod iam fecit).

The work of thinking can, it seems, involve a struggle to arrive at a set of
connections which are repeatedly liable to disconnection or dissolution, a
pattern or sequence which remains vulnerable to forgetting or distortion
and which may be irrecoverable unless shaped into a formulation which can
be repeated and copied, and perhaps in a literal sense re-traced once it takes
the form of a written text or verbal utterance, which is quotable and
repeatable. It may then be important precisely how one writes ‘it’ down, and
like a poem or a joke it may be crucial that the exact wording is followed or
reproduced. For example, I have just described this process in two different
ways, but do the two accounts articulate precisely the ‘same thought’?

That we can sometimes tell a joke ‘in our own words’ may be part of
what ‘understanding’ the joke means, while in the case of a poem our own
words, as a paraphrase, summary, or translation, will not ‘mean the same’ as
the poem, or ‘have the same effect’. In some instances, at least, there is only
one adequate form of words. Yet we may sometimes say the original words,
ot repeat them ‘mechanically’ (as we say), without ‘understanding’ them. As
I might literally repeat the ontological proof as formulated in Anselm’s own
Latin even if I do not understand his Latin. Which is quite different from
retracing the struggle to arrive at or understand a ‘proof but without
eventually agreeing with it, or regarding it as finally coherent.

Chapter 4 in the Proslogion seems to be trying to clarify a related
distinction between reading with and without ‘understanding’. A 19th-
century English translation of that chapter added a preliminary formulation
(here italicised) of its argument which was not part of Anselm’s own text:

How the fool has said in his heart what cannot be conceived.

— A thing may be conceived in two ways: (1) when the word signifying it is conceived;
(2) when the thing itself is understood. As far as the word goes, God can be
conceived not to exist; in reality he cannot.

How has the fool said in his heart what he could not conceive
(cogitare); or how is it that he could not conceive what he said in his
heart? since it is the same to say in the heart and to conceive (dicere
incorde et cogitare). But if really, indeed since really, he both conceived,
because he said in his heart; and did not say in his heart, because he
could not conceive, there is more than one way in which a thing is said
in the heart or conceived. For, in one sense, an object is conceived,
when the word signifying it is conceived; and in another, when the very
entity, which the object is, is understood (żnzelligitur ).

In reading this passage, are you confident that you have ‘understood’ what
Anselm was thinking, or saying, or writing? Or what he meant by
conceiving ‘in his heart’? Does the added preliminary summary ‘mean the
same’ as what follows in the chapter itself? Other English translations use
the term ‘think’ where this version uses ‘conceive’. Anselm used the Latin
terms cogitare and intelligere. So what are the differences between thinking and
understanding? Let us step back a bit.

philosophy

What we call ‘philosophy’ has often been taken to be a matter of ‘thinking’
and Anselm’s Pros/ogion has acted as a continuing spur to thinking in
philosophy. One could almost write a history of Western philosophy by
tracing the responses to Anselm’s argument, by Descartes, Leibniz,
Spinoza, Kant, etc. Note again that I have not yet actually quoted Anselm’s
‘own’ formulation of the so-called ontological argument. Before I do so,
you might consider whether the following excerpts from some of the
commentators on Anselm constitute the ‘same argument’ even as each
other, and in particular how the different ways in which they are written
shape the arguments presented.

Thomas Aquinas’s version or rendition remains perhaps the most
concise. Aquinas considers what he regards as Anselm’s argument alongside
one by John of Damascus. As usual, Aquinas first rehearses the positions of
others, then offers his own, and finally critiques the positions he rejects.
Note that only the single phrase I have italicised is actually quoted by

Aquinas directly from Anselm’s own text. Here is part of Summa theologiae,
prima pars, Quaestio 2, which begins by citing other arguments:

Article 1. Whether the existence of God is self-evident?

1. It seems that the existence of God is self-evident. Now those things
are said to be self-evident to us the knowledge of which is naturally
implanted in us, as we can see in regard to first principles. But as the
Damascene says, ‘the knowledge of God is naturally implanted in all.’
Therefore the existence of God is self-evident.

2. Further, those things are said to be self-evident which are known as
soon as the terms are known, which the Philosopher says is true of the
first principles of demonstration. Thus, when the nature of a whole and
of a part is known, it is at once recognized that every whole is greater
than its part.

But as soon as the signification of the word ‘God’ is understood, it is
at once seen that God exists. For by this word is signified that than which
nothing greater can be conceived. But that which exists actually and mentally
is greater than that which exists only mentally. Therefore, since as soon
as the word ‘God’ is understood it exists mentally, it also follows that it
exists actually. Therefore the proposition “God exists’ is self-evident.
(Sed intellecto quid significet hoc nomen Deus, statim habetur quod Deus est.
Significatur enim hoc nomine id quo maius significari non potest: maius autem est
quod est in re et intellectu, quam quod est in intellectu tantum: unde cum, intellecto
hoc nomine Deus, statim sit in intellectu, sequitur etiam quod sit in re. Ergo Deum
esse est per se notum. )

To this Aquinas counterposes the following (citing Aristotle, and echoing
a biblical text quoted by Anselm):

On the contrary: No one can mentally admit the opposite of what is
self-evident; as the Philosopher states concerning the first principles of
demonstration. But the opposite of the proposition ‘God is’ can be
mentally admitted: “The fool said in his heart, There is no God’ (Psalm
13:2). Therefore, that God exists is not self-evident.

Aquinas then states his own position:

I answer that: A thing can be self-evident in either of two ways: on the
one hand, self-evident in itself, though not to us; on the other, self-
evident in itself, and to us. A proposition is self-evident because the
predicate is included in the essence of the subject, as “Man is an animal’,
for animal is contained in the essence of man. If, therefore, the essence
of the predicate and subject be known to all, the proposition will be
self-evident to all; as is clear with regard to the first principles of

demonstration, the terms of which are common things that no one is
ignorant of, such as being and non-being, whole and part, and such like.

If, however, there are some to whom the essence of the predicate and
subject is unknown, the proposition may be self-evident in itself, but
not to those who do not know the meaning of the predicate and
subject of the proposition. Therefore, it happens, as Boethius says, ‘that
there are some mental concepts self-evident only to the learned, as that
incorporeal substances are not in space’. Therefore I say that this
proposition, ‘God exists’, is of itself self-evident, for the predicate is the
same as the subject, because God is His own existence as will be
hereafter shown. But because we do not know the essence of God, the
proposition is not self-evident to us; but needs to be demonstrated by
things that are more known to us, though less known in their nature —
namely, by effects.

Finally, Aquinas answers or replies to the opening positions he had
considered earlier:

Reply to 1. To know that God exists in a general and confused way is
implanted in us by nature, inasmuch as God is man’s beatitude. For
man naturally desires happiness, and what is naturally desired by man
must be naturally known to him. This, however, is not to know
absolutely that God exists; just as to know that someone is approaching
is not the same as to know that Peter is approaching, even though it is
Peter who is approaching; for there are many who imagine that man’s
perfect good which is happiness, consists in riches, and others in
pleasures, and others in something else.

Reply to 2. Perhaps not everyone who hears this word ‘God’

understands it to signify shat than which nothing greater can be thought, seeing
that some have believed God to be a body. Yet, even granted that
everyone understands that by this word ‘God’ is signified something than
which nothing greater can be thought, nevertheless, it does not therefore
follow that he understands that what the word signifies exists actually,
but only that it exists mentally. Nor can it be argued that it actually
exists, unless it be admitted that there actually exists that than which
nothing greater can be thought, and this is precisely what is not admitted by
those who hold that God does not exist.
(Ad secundum dicendum quod forte ille qui audit hoc nomen Deus, non intelligit
significari aliquid quo maius cogitari non possit, cum quidam crediderint Deum esse
corpus. Dato etiam quod quilibet intelligat hoc nomine Deus significari hoc quod
dicitur, scilicet illud quo maius cogitari non potest; non tamen propter hoc sequitur
quod intelligat id quod significatur per nomen, esse in rerum natura; sed in
apprehensione intellectus tantum. Nec potest argui quod sit in re, nisi daretur quod
sit in re aliquid quo maius cogitari non potest: quod non est datum a ponentibus
Deum non esse. )

As well as the temptation to try to reformulate Anselm’s argument,
modern philosophers have also found it irresistible to offer various parodic
versions, of which my favourite is probably a version of the following:

Anselm’s ontological proof is the proof of the existence of God than
which no more successful proof can be conceived. A proof which
proves the existence of God succeeds more than one that does not.
Therefore Anselm’s ontological proof proves the existence of God.

The best version of the ontological argument is one that successfully
proves the ontological argument. There must be a best version of the
ontological argument. So there must be a successful ontological
argument.

wrote a further response. That exchange, as we shall see, suggests why the
several attempted ‘versions’ I have quoted are arguably misguided and

Gaunilo

In the continuing argument about the meaning or validity of the Pros/ogion, a
question persists as to whether or not, or in what sense, Anselm intended
the ‘ontological argument’? to be the kind of ‘proof which later
philosophers have assumed it to be. There is no doubt that Anselm’s stated
intention in the earlier Monologion was to demonstrate that the essential
components of a theistic belief could be established on rational grounds
alone, without appeal to traditional or scriptural authority. But are the core
passages of the Pros/ogion what we would now call a philosophical argument
at all? It is time surely, to look at Anselm’s own text.

However, any copy of the Pros/ogion was henceforth, on his own
instructions, always to include the exchange with Gaunilo. So should we
consider Anselm’s response to Gaunilo first? Given how available it is
online and otherwise, Pm not going to discuss Gaunilo’s own complete
argument, but offer a summary version of it:

. they say that there is in the ocean somewhere an island which,
because of the difficulty (or rather the impossibility) of finding that
which does not exist, some have called the “Lost Island.’ And the story
goes that it is blessed with all manner of priceless riches and delights in
abundance .. and . . is superior everywhere in abundance to all those
other lands that men inhabit. Now, if anyone tells me that it is like this,
I shall easily understand what is said, since nothing is difficult about it.
But if he should then go on to say, as though it were a logical
consequence of this: You cannot doubt that this island that is more
excellent than all other islands truly exists somewhere in reality than
you can doubt that it is in your mind; and since it is more excellent to
exist not only in the mind alone but also in reality, therefore it must
needs be that it exists. For if it did not exist, any other island existing in
reality would be more excellent than it, and so this island, already
conceived by you to be more excellent than others, will not be more
excellent. If, I say, someone wishes thus to persuade me that this island
really exists beyond all doubt, I should either think that he was joking,
ot I should find it hard to decide which of us I ought to judge the
bigger fool — I, if I agreed with him, or he, if he thought that he had
proved the existence of this island.

14

Anselm responded to Gaunilo in a way that most subsequent
commentators have found unsatisfactory. Anselm’s reply implies that he did
not himself regard the issue as entirely ‘philosophical’, since he responds to
Gaunilo by explicitly appealing to the fact that Gaunilo was a Catholic
monk. The reply begins (my emphases):

Since it is not the fool [in Psalm 13], against whom the argument of my
little book was directed, who takes me to task in this criticism, but the
author of these objections who is by no means a fool, and is a Catholic,
speaking on behalf of the fool, I think it sufficient that I answer the Catholic
(sufficere mihi potest respondere catholico ).

The final section of Anselm’s reply nevertheless reaffirms that (my
empasis):

I think that I have shown by an argument which is not weak, but sufficiently
cogent, that in my book I proved (puto quia monstravi me non infirma sed satis
necessari argumentatione probasse) the real existence of a being than which a
greater cannot be conceived; and I believe that shis argument cannot be
invalidated by the validity of any objection (nec eam alicuius obiectionis infirmari
firmitate). For so great force does the signification of this phrase contain
in itself (buius prolationis in se continent significatio), that this being which is
the subject of discussion, is of necessity, from the very fact that it is
understood or conceived, proved (probetur) also to exist in reality, and to
be whatever we should believe of the divine substance.

Yet how can a ‘proof rest upon (only) working when offered to a Catholic?
Hardly what we might classify as a philosophical proof. And how could an
argument vot be ‘invalidated by the validity of any objection’?

The crucial passages which are normally taken to constitute ‘the ontological
argument’ are in chapters 2 and 3 of the Pros/ogion. However, the function of
chapter 1 is often not much examined, which is where I want to begin. In

After the prologue or proemium, quoted earlier, there is an ‘exhortation’
which is commonly passed over as if it were merely an extension of the
preliminaries, a kind of opening prayer and not part of the argument. It

begins with the writer addressing and exhorting someone — who, it seems,
is himself:

Chapter 1 : Exhortation of the mind to the contemplation of God.

Well now, little man! flee, for a little while, your preoccupations; hide
yourself, for a time, from your disturbing thoughts. Cast aside, now,
your burdensome cares, and put away your toilsome business. Yield
room for some little time to God; and rest for a little time in him. Enter
the inner chamber of your mind; shut out everything except God, and
whatever can aid you in seeking him; close your door and seek him.
Speak now, my whole heart! speak now to God, saying: “I seek your
face; your face, my Lord, will I seek.” (Psalm 26. 8)

In using that phrase from the 26th Psalm the text switches the form of
address, from addressing himself as “you” to addressing God: “your face,
my Lord” (quaero vultum tuum; vultum tuum, domine, requiro.’).

The text, now speaking in the first person as T, continues to use “you” in
the next passage to address “God” in the second person:

And come you (#) now, O Lord my God, teach my heart where and
how it may seek you, where and how it may find you. (Eza nunc ergo tu,
Domine Deus meus, doce cor meum ubi et quomodo quaerat ubi et quomodo te
inveniat).

Latin does not normally deploy a separate term for the first-person singular,
which is incorporated into the form of the verb. Here the second person is
made explicit in the ‘4 (vocative) and ‘ze’ (object or accusative) forms.

But then, almost immediately, the issue arises as to how “you” can be
addressed if that ‘you’ is not ‘here’ to hear the speaker: “Lord, if you are not
here, where shall I seek you, being absent?” The phrase ‘if you are not here’
(si hoc non es) introduces a significant motif of presence and absence. But this
rather odd question tends to slide past the reader as a merely rhetorical
question, which is immediately countered by the notion that this “you” is
‘everywhere’ — which implicitly explains how the ‘you’ can be addressed at
all, wherever the ‘T’ is located.

the whole story. This is in part a script for a kind of communal
performance as well as a personal utterance or confessional prayer.
Remember that Anselm was regarded as highly unusual in being able to
read silently to himself.

Only after having at length exhorted himself into a devotional frame of
mind and feeling, and provided his readers with a script to do the same
themselves, Anselm finally moves to what is normally regarded as ‘the
argument’ in chapter 2. The chapter heading is in the third-person, stating
that ‘truly God is’ but the text is still initially addressed, in the vocative, to
“Lord”, “Domine”:

Chapter 2 : Truly there is a God (Quod vere sit Deus).

And so, Lord, do you, who gives understanding to faith, give me, so far

What is at work here is a very strange logic. The earlier question had

case of God it would be a strange joke indeed since God is supposed to be

and ‘thete is no God’, then who is being addressed, whether present or

The comparison with a painter might at first seem appropriate, if the issue
is a matter of understanding whether something exists in the mind and / or

But would the analogy work if it were a matter of the painter actually
addressing a human model posing for a portrait painting and saying
something like: “Well, I can see you in my mind, but do you actually exist in
front of me?” And how would it work if the painter were to say: “I
understand that you are what’s called a model, but do you exist?” It might
partly make sense if a friend of the painter were to joke: “So you claim to
have a ‘model’. Yes, I know what that means, but does she exist?” And
what if the painting was, after all, a portrait without a model, so that the
portrait was indeed of somebody who did of exist, but was simply
imagined?

In any case, seeing or imagining a model or even a painting of a model is
not the same as hearing the word ‘model’, which is a matter of whether we
understand the word, or mis-understand it, or have never heard the word
before and don’t understand it at all. A curious problem is actually being
evaded by the comparison with the act of painting. We are being distracted
from noticing that the speaker has switched from talking / that ‘than which
nothing greater can be conceived’ to talking about that ‘than which nothing
greater can be conceived’.

It is this passage which most commentators single out as the crucial step.
Since it sets up the following reductio ad absurdum:

Therefore, if that, than which nothing greater can be conceived, exists
in the understanding alone, the very being, than which nothing greater
can be conceived, is one than which a greater can be conceived. But
obviously this is impossible. Hence, there is no doubt that there exists a
being, than which nothing greater can be conceived, and it exists both
in the understanding and in reality.

(Si ergo id quo maius cogitari non potest, est in solo intellectu: id ipsum quo maius

If you start from a premise which leads to a self-contradiction or to
asserting two opposite propositions which cancel each other out, then your
premise must (normally) be invalid and its contrary must be true. Therefore
‘that, than which nothing greater can be conceived’ cannot exist only in the
intellect.

However, this form of the argument clearly lays Anselm open to precisely
the critique offered by Gaunilo and others: that I can (to use Gaunilo’s
example) indeed conceive of the most wonderful island in the world, but so
conceiving it, and even conceiving that it exists (or even painting it), is not
the same as it actually existing, nor evidence that it does so. The painter
may try to imagine a painting ‘greater than which no painting could be
imagined’, but that doesn’t mean that he or anyone else has painted it or
ever will paint it.

Variations of this fairly obvious critique abound in the numerous
dismissive responses to Anselm and, whether it can be judged effective or
otherwise, it is the issue upon which most critics focus. I want to suggest a
rather different emphasis.

contra-position

Let us continue carefully with Anselm’s text. The following chapter offers,
on my reading, an even more questionable move.

Chapter 3 : God cannot be conceived not to exist.

(Quia non possit cogitari non esse.)

And it assuredly exists so truly, that it cannot be conceived not to exist.
For, it is possible to conceive of a being which cannot be conceived not
to exist; and this is greater than one which can be conceived not to
exist. Hence, if that, than which nothing greater can be conceived, can
be conceived not to exist, it is not that, than which nothing greater can
be conceived. There is, then, so truly a something than which nothing

greater can be conceived to exist, that it cannot even be conceived not

(Quod utique sic vere est, ut nec cogitari possit non esse. Nam potest cogitari esse

cogitari potest. Quare si id quo maius nequit cogitari, potest cogitari non esse: id

There is some disagreement among commentators as to whether this

ot a distinct second argument for the existence of God, or a consideration
as to the properties or characteristics of God. In any case, at the very end of
the argument the text again switches back to a second-person address:

And this being you are, O Lord, our God. (Eż hoc es tu, Domine Deus
noster.)

Anselm may be persuasive, or otherwise, in claiming that he has clinched
his case by appealing to this second apparent reductio ad absurdum and the
apparent contradiction he posits. But there would have been an even
greater peculiarity if he had concluded by saying instead: “This being who
cannot be conceived to exist is you, my Lord.” I might, perhaps,
‘understand’ such a remark (I know what the words mean) but it surely
could not be addressed to anyone.

To do so would involve what Bernard Lonergan once called a ‘contra-
position’: a statement which undercuts itself by the very fact that it is
uttered. What Anselm is perhaps trying to offer is a kind of positive
opposite of a contra-position: a statement which proves itself by the very
fact that it is uttered.

Yet even the statement ‘I am here’ need not entail any actual presence or
existence, if only because the statement can exist simply as a written
sentence, un-tead by anyone, or it may be a recorded utterance within a
radio play, a remark by an artificial intelligence, or an announcement by the
dramatised ghostly father of Hamlet. Or it may be something we imagined
as having been said. In which case it would not even qualify as a
performative utterance. Perhaps like Descartes’ cogito.

addressing

I want to draw breath and step back from this close reading of the text and
consider what I have indicated about Anselm’s ‘ontological’ argument. First,
recall the description given by Eadmer of the process which Anselm claims
had led to his insight. It is clear from that description, which presumably
derives from Anselm himself, that during the course of the several days
during which he racked his brains to arrive at the elusive formulation of a
proof which he constantly sensed was just beyond his grasp, he would have
spent much of the time not ‘doing philosophy’ but praying to the very God
whose nature and existence he was trying to understand.

Part of the process would have been addressing God, asking for help in
the process of thinking, pleading for divine illumination, trying out
alternative formulations in the very ‘presence’ of God, and so on. Anselm
specifically indicates that this protracted process of frustrated intellectual
search intermittently occurred during the liturgical prayers themselves, and
he even recounts the fear that the devil might be thereby tempting him, as
the obverse of his hope or expectation that God would assist him. In other
words, Anselm was throughout addressing “God” — and the coining of the
phrase “that than which nothing greater can be conceived” was in effect
almost finding a new ‘name’ by which to address God.

It seems wholly plausible that the original insight which suddenly struck
Anselm actually took the form of the vocative address: “You, Lord, are that
than which nothing greater can be conceived.” As an illumination from
God himself, that very second-person address was itself, in an odd kind of
way, addressed by God to Anselm. Providing him with the very formulation
that he had been seeking was God’s answer to his prayers. It is God’s voice
which in effect tells Anselm that it is appropriate to address God as “you who
are that than which nothing greater can be conceived.”

26

But when trying to reconstruct the apparently self-sustaining implication
of that gnomic utterance Anselm himself seems later to have attempted to
re-formulate the insight in the third-person mode: ‘God is that than which
nothing greater can be conceived’, or even more impersonally: ‘there is
something than which nothing greater can be conceived’. It is from this
third-person version that the logical conundra and complications arise.

The exact syntax of the apparently cumbersome negative or apophatic
form is needed in order to construct the logical reductio ad absurdum
argument, so that ‘that than which nothing greater can be conceived’ is
preferred to the more obvious and simpler formulation: ‘the greatest which
can be conceived’. That version would not justify the reductio ad absurdum of
the overall argument and would in any case immediately raise the awkward
question ‘the greatest what?”

Without a continuing implicit reliance upon the “you” version, the ‘it
version would not have served Anselm’s purpose at all, since the formal
reductio of the third-person ‘it’ version could apply to something which is
not a ‘person’, whereas the implication of addressing “God” as a person is
required for the argument to have the force that Anselm intends: that the
‘something’ is indeed what we believe “God” to be, even though what is
then required is a further elaboration of the properties of this ‘God’.

The crucial function of the opening address to “God” in the extended
exhortatory rhetoric of the proemium and chapter 1 is to construct the
‘God’ who appears in the following argument as the kind of thing which
(that is, a person who) can be addressed, and indeed who can address
Anselm (as the “you” who is T). The God who figures in the ontological
argument may be referred to as an impersonal afguid but the argument is,
by a persistent slippage, actually about an aguis — not a something ‘than
which nothing greater can be conceived’ but a you, a someone “than which
nothing greater can be conceived”.

Consider the following alternative formulation which would leave open
what kind of a thing the subject of the argument is: ‘cosmog is that than
which nothing greater can be conceived’. We don’t know what the meaning
or possible reference of the unknown word ‘cosmog’ is. In its application to
(a or the?) ‘cosmog’ we don’t therefore know in what sense the word
‘greater’ might apply. Nevertheless, if we allow that we ‘understand’ what
the phrase ‘that than which nothing greater can be conceived’ means, we
can still proceed with the formal argument, the same reductio ad absurdum:
‘cosmog’ would be ‘greater’ if cosmog existed in reality as well as in the
mind, and therefore if cosmog does not exist ‘i re it would not be ‘that
than which nothing greater can be conceived’ and therefore cosmog must
exist. Whatever it, or cosmog, is.

Another variation might be: ‘this present is that than which nothing
greater can be conceived and a present which exists is greater than a present
which doesn’t...2. — and so on. Yet in English whether ‘this present’

27

indicates a gift I am about to give or the temporal moment of the ‘now’
leaves the argument formally valid but wholly vacuous.

In other words, without a prior ‘understanding’ of what is intended as the
kind of being that ‘God’ is, Anselm’s argument, while valid in purely logical
terms (for some commentators), remains empty of any persuasion that the
“God” of chapter 1 is the ‘God’ of chapter 2. Or rather, that the aliquid of
chapter 2 meets the expectations aroused by the address in chapter 1.
Unless the persuasive power of that complex opening exhortation variously
addressed to himself, to the reader, and to “God”, has already had its
intended effect upon the reader, including the T of Anselm himself.

Clearly, however, the “you” form of the argument has no evidential force
by itself. That I can address: “That than which nothing greater can be
conceived” cannot itself act as the opening move in the reductio ad absurdum
argument which Anselm needed to construct.

Yet in a compelling way the original vocative form does seem to have
effectively persuaded Anselm that it could indeed act as the irrefutable
‘single argument’ he had been searching for. And not only because that was
the mode in which the phrase had actually occurred to him.

It is the function of chapter 1 to re-create for his reader, including himself
as his own reader, the experience that will lead to that ‘understanding’.
What the text does is to bring together the “you” addressed in prayer and
the God who, it is argued, has indeed the various metaphysical attributes
which the text as a whole then goes on to elaborate. In other words,
Anselm’s own vocative version of the ‘ontological proof combines in a
single phrase the personal Yahweh or Father of the Old and New
Testament narratives and the impersonal deity of the philosophical
tradition. Which means that the argument was not a coherent single
illuminating insight, however it may have presented itself as such. Though
one might bend grammar and syntax by saying: “you is that than which
nothing greater can be conceived.”

vocative mode

To recap and clarify: in trying to arrive at the elusive ‘single argument’ he
was searching for, Anselm would have had to consider how he could
conceive of ‘God’ at all. To recognise or acknowledge that he could not
intelligibly or coherently conceive of God would have rendered the pursuit
of the desired argument impossible: God would indeed have been ‘greater
than can be conceived’, as in the apophatic theological tradition. But the
insight that this formulation could be replaced by “you are” “that than
which nothing greater can be conceived” permitted Anselm to then further
construct a formal reductio ad absurdum argument which would not have been
possible using the traditional apophatic formulation that God could not be
conceived. However, once he had satisfactorily formulated the reductio ad
absurdum as a ‘proof of the very existence of God, it would then be logically

28

possible (in Proslogion chapter 15) to further allow that God was in any case
‘greater than can be conceived’. To arrive at that position, it was necessary
to take the first step, which was the vocative form of the premise: “you are
that than which nothing greater can be conceived.”

I am not suggesting that the vocative mode of Anselm’s argument is
thereby rendered more, or less, valid than in the more familiar quasi-
syllogistic third-person reformulation of it, but rather exploring how or why
Anselm was so convinced that he had, after the long frustrating process of
seeking the illumination which he describes, actually arrived at a compelling
insight, yet one the elusive grounds for which have in turn frustrated many
subsequent readers of his argument. We might ourselves find, perhaps like
Anselm himself, that we oscillate between both grasping the persuasive
force of the formulation and also registering the elusive emptiness of the
argument. In part, I suggest, this is precisely because the Proslogion
combines, conflates, or confuses the second-person and third-person
versions of the argument.

When Anselm thinks: “you are: something than which nothing greater
can be thought” this obviously, if implicitly, takes it for granted that ‘you
are something’ to begin with, since otherwise the act of addressing ‘you’
would be imaginary and void of existential content. Like addressing a non-
existent model and asking her or him to pose for a portrait. Yet the point of
the address in this form is not to prove existence, but to clarify or to
establish that, as the text explicitly says: “you exist as we believe, and that you
ate what we believe you to be’. The “You” in this address is not so much a
concept as an addressee and the purpose of this formulation is to claim that
the belief we have of ‘God’ as existing must apply to the “You” who is
being addressed. This is clearly a form of circular argument, but perhaps
not simply tautological: “you are what we believe you to be” is not quite
the same as “you are what you ate.”

In that respect, Anselm’s ‘belief is ‘seeking understanding’. In order to
address someone you have to believe that they exist. But on the basis of
that belief one might imagine or conceive the addressee to be various kinds
of being. The King whom you are addressing may be most un-kingly, the
Queen you are addressing may be beautiful or otherwise, the Pope you are
addressing may be wise and saintly, or otherwise. But the “God” you are
addressing in order to be the ‘God’ in whom you believe must, for Anselm,
be all-powerful, all-wise, all-knowing, and so on. That divine “You” is
‘present in my mind’ not primarily as a concept but fundamentally as an
addressee ‘in my heart’. Could anybody ‘believe in the heart’ the conceptual
conclusion to a sequence of formulations in symbolic logic?

Moreover, Anselm is decidedly not postulating the kind of ‘clear and
distinct idea’ which Descartes was later to claim to have conceived,
however indebted or otherwise Descartes may have been to Anselm’s own
argument. It is not for Anselm a matter of trying to get clear what the
concept of God is, in the same way that we might try to get clear what the

29

concept of a cat is, but rather constructing a situation in which Anselm has
a justified confidence in addressing the God in whom he believes. Because
otherwise his whole life-time of praying to “God” would be, in an almost
literal sense, un-thinkable. Though, as he says, Anselm has never seen God
face-to-face, as he might never have met Queen Matilda or Abbot Hugh
face-to-face, yet nevertheless he has confidence in addressing Queen or
Abbot in a letter to them as really existing. He seeks a confirmation of a
similar affirmation of his belief that his addressee “God” exists.

It is then understandable why Anselm, in replying to Gaunilo, should
begin from the position that Gaunilo as a monk who also practised praying
on a daily basis must already have ‘in his mind’ the God whom he has
addressed in that prayer. Anselm is not relying upon an assumption that
Gaunilo would endorse a particular doctrinal statement or a definite
philosophical proposition about the nature and existence of the divine, but
rather upon the confidence that any monk like Anselm himself could not
have practised a life-time of prayer without some compelling awareness of
the God to whom such prayers were directed. This is consonant with the
point often made, that the Pros/ogion is composed in part of fervent prayers
as well as apparently philosophical arguments. Yet for a non-believing
reader a prayer addressed to a silent God, however vividly conceived, is
even less persuasive than an invalid syllogism. Unless, of course, the first
step the ‘Fool’ can be persuaded to take is to address “God” in prayer.

letters

If we now situate the “you” version of Anselm’s argument in the context of
his other writings it might be easier to understand why for Anselm the
second-person address was an integral part of the insight he was claiming to
encapsulate in the Pros/ogion. Over half the texts collected in Schmitt’s
standard six-volume edition of Anselm’s works are letters. I use Schmitt’s
numbering of them. Almost necessarily, though not absolutely so, a letter is
addressed to somebody who is absent. In writing his numerous letters
Anselm was in an obvious sense conjuring up the presence of an addressee
who was absent, and this is palpably part of his own sense of what it means
to write a letter.

Consider for example the opening of Letter 51, “To the monks Herluin,
Gundulf, and Maurice’ (Schmitt III, p. 164, my translation):

If you well remember your delight at how I always longed to see you
when you were present, you will know well enough how much I long to
hear from you now that you are absent.

A familiar epistolatory formula. But Anselm’s longing for an absent

recipient can almost be transformed, by the very act of writing a letter, into
a mutual presence, at least ‘in the mind’ or ‘heart’. Here is Letter 4, written

be read as closely echoing Anselm’s formulations in the Pros/ogion (Schmitt

Whenever I intend to write to you, soul most beloved of my soul,
whenever I intend to write to you, I am uncertain as to how best I
should begin to address you. For whatever I feel about you is sweet and
joyful to my heart; whatever I wish for you, that is zbe best my mind can

This sense of being able to conjure up the absent one, as a ‘soul’ present in
the mind and in one’s heart, wherever the T or ‘you’ may be, applies not
only to someone physically distant but also to the dead:

Wherever Osbern is, his soul is my soul. May I therefore while living receive
for him what I can hope for when dead from my friends, so that they
will be released from such obligation to me when I die.

The italicised phrase here might seem today simply a kind of conceit or
even cliché, but Anselm’s framework for understanding and formulating
such relationships partly depends upon the assumed notion of a non-
corporeal spirit, the ‘soul’ as separable from the body, not only in death but
perhaps as able to be ‘present’? now without the physical presence of the
body. This belief also underpins his fundamental argument about the very
nature of God and, in his other treatises, the notion that non-corporeal
intelligent beings such as angels and devils can and do exist.

himself and be good for you. Farewell.

Anselm frequently seems to be not simply expressing but actually creating
or summoning up an intense love for his absent addressee. Much as he does
in relation to God in the hortatory opening chapter of the Pros/ogion. This
love, like the letter itself, is offered as a kind of gift to the absent addressee
and loving letters in reply are expected as return gifts. Yet the gift of giving
is a curious one and the terms in which Anselm describes or conjures up
that loving relationship may strike a reader today as peculiarly exaggerated.
Yet underpinning the vocabulary of gift and in particular the gift of one’s
‘self is the perhaps even odder implicit notion of ‘sharing’ a soul.

How far Anselm himself might have taken literally this notion of soul-
sharing it is impossible to say but it is not too remote from some of the
emphases he places in his theological treatises upon the gift of ‘grace’, of
the divine being or ‘life’ as actually shared by the human recipients of God’s
love, as a ‘participation’ in that life. Anselm thinks of this gift of love not so
much as given but as shared: the love he sends remains with him, since love
is a relationship, and also almost an entity, like ‘grace’. It is this insight
which arguably informs his extended theological discussion in the
Monologion of the Holy Spirit as actually being the love-relationship between
the Father and the Son.

In Anselm’s letters there is often a peculiar conflation between presence
and absence and perhaps a kind of implicit logic. In writing a letter to an
absent friend Anselm has no reason to doubt the existence of that friend.
Though there may be instances when we are writing to a friend whom we
believe to be still alive but who is in fact already dead at the time of writing,
Anselm in any case believed that there was also some form of after-life
existence which was conceivable.

Similarly, in addressing a prayer to God Anselm has no doubt that the
God who exists in his mind at that moment as an effect of that writing
practice also exists independently of the act of writing. The move from this
to the logical structure of the “you” version of the ‘ontological proof is a
small step: a purely imaginary friend, who exists only in our mind or as the

32

imagined addressee of a letter, would be a less satisfactory friend than one
who actually exists, however absent, or even already dead.

It might be too much to suggest that the addressee of a letter by Anselm
is conceived as one person with two natures, that of an imagined persona and
that of an actually existing human being. Yet it is clearly the case that on
occasion one could indeed be addressing only a projected recipient and,
however fervent or heartfelt the text of one’s address might be, such
rhetorical sincerity does not, of course, guarantee that the addressee has any
other existence than in one’s mind, on paper, or a wax tablet. Any more,
incidentally, than my conceiving of a reader for this text guarantees the
existence of one. Though if you are now reading it my supposition has been
confirmed. Though I don’t know that, or who, “you” are. One might add,
anticipating somewhat, that today’s widespread internet scams
characteristically involve ‘catfish’ personae and rely upon people’s
propensity to project real existence and identity upon what may be only an
entirely artificial persona.

This is not to say that Anselm does not deploy other kinds of argument,
including the notion of the perfect being, or the attempt to argue on the
basis of reason alone in the Monologion, or in the first part of Cur Deus Homo.
Since Anselm explicitly attempts to deploy reason alone in these other
instances, his attempt in the Pros/ogion is taken, perhaps in an unclear way
even by Anselm himself and also by subsequent readers, to be similarly an
attempt to construct a purely rational argument. Which it is, but the core
premise is not a third-person proposition (God is X) but rather a second-
person address (you are: that than which nothing greater can be thought).
Which modifies the very basis of the logic. But may not necessarily
invalidate it. And certainly suggests a range of other considerations.

In the opening paragraph of the preface to the Pros/ogion Anselm writes that
the Monologion had been written ‘in the guise of someone who investigates
things he does not know, by means of a process of silent reasoning within
himself.” The Latin is: ‘in persona alicuius..” — ‘in the persona of someone.’
Anselm would have been aware of persona as the term used for the mask of
an actor, signifying a particular kind of role, as well as its technical use in
discussions of the Trinity. The preface ends by saying: ‘I named the former
work Mono/logion, that is, a soliloquy (so4logium), and the latter, Pros/ogion, that
is, an address (a/loquium).’ The writer of a letter is himself to a large extent
constructed by and dramatised or impersonated in the writing process. We
can almost overhear Anselm adopting the persona he wishes to embody in
any particular letter, and we can in our own process of reading allow
ourselves to construct, impersonate, or enact that persona to a greater or
lesser extent, as well as imagining the implied addressee of the letter. That
Anselm normally dictated his letters to a scribe reminds us that the writing

The point also applies when the writing practice includes what one might
call the ventriloquised reply of an intended addressee. The words attributed
to one’s interlocutor, in the postulated response of the recipient of a letter
ot the attributed reply of a debater in a text composed as a dialogue,
constitute in effect the construction of a persona within a persona. The most
familiar example of such ventriloquism for Anselm would have been the
response of a divine interlocutor to whom one attributes words of comfort
ot rebuke, as in many of the Psalms, which Anselm and the whole monastic
community would have recited every day, spoken or chanted in the choir,
and which provide so many of the phrases deployed in the Pros/ogion.
Anselm can freely adopt a persona of the Psalmist while the inspired words
of the Psalms are regarded as those of God himself addressed to those who
address Him in the words which He himself has provided.

Soliloquies.

Just as a letter by Anselm to a distant monk involved the projection of the

an absent addressee: God himself, or a particular Saint, conjuring them up

ventriloquises on their behalf. It is part of the process of praying these
written prayers, and perhaps of ourselves reading Anselm’s prayers

A further aspect of the ‘ontological argument’ can be illustrated from
Anselm’s letters. In writing to his fellow monks, he addressed them as
brothers or friends or as their spiritual adviser. But Anselm also wrote
letters to such recipients as King Henry, Queen Matilda, Pope Pascal, and
various Abbots and Archbishops. In each case, he addresses somebody
who is physically absent but present ‘in his mind’, as the person from whom
he is seeking a reply or response. But his tone and the construction of his
own persona and that of his addressee is inflected by the social and
ecclesiastical status of his intended recipient. Consider the titular address of
the letter and the terms of deference with which he addresses the Princess
Adelaide:

Brother Anselm, to the venerable lady of royal nobility but nobler in her virtuous
life, Adelaide, may your temporal nobility be so adorned with the ornaments of
virtue that it will merit the eternal happiness of being united with the King of Kings:.

As for the ‘florilegium of the Psalms’ that your highness, dear to me in
God, deigned to require of me, faithful humility could not have fulfilled

it for you more speedily or any better, since our obedience followed
your command devotedly, as much as the command itself proceeded

although it is not encrusted with gold and precious stones, it is certainly
wholly made up of charitable fidelity and offered with faithful charity.

There is an implicit logic in this recognition of high social status,
unavoidable in a hierarchical feudal society. When, in his prayers or in the
Proslogion, Anselm is addressing God he is consciously addressing somebody
immeasurably ‘greater’ than Kings or Popes, someone indeed ‘than whom
nobody greater can be thought’. We can almost ventriloquise Anselm’s
thinking: ‘When I address myself to Queen Matilda or King Henry I am
aware of their greatness in comparison with this wretched little man that I
am. But if they did not exist they would not be as great as I conceive them
to be, nor even greater than I am. Whereas God when I address him is
indeed someone greater than which no-one else can be conceived, not only
because I can conceive him as existing but also because I can conceive him
as necessarily existing.’

One implication of any such ‘necessarily’ would be that though it is
possible to make a mistake and address as “Pope” somebody who is not a
Pope but an anti-Pope, or address as “King” somebody who is not Royal,
and it would also matter if I was addressing a real person who happens to
be dead, in the case of “God” it would be quite unthinkably odd to be
addressing the ‘wrong’ God, especially if my idea of God is in accordance
with the arguments in the whole Pros/ogion, and because, according to the
Monologion, God cannot but be present, always, everywhere.

Moreover, as we have seen in discussing Eadmet’s writings a while ago,
Anselm was far from sure as to his own status, or even secure in his
identity. One letter to Lanfranc is affectionately but almost painfully
convoluted in its intertwined playing with forms of address and terms of
logic, indicating the complexity of Anselm’s not knowing quite what persona
to adopt, though he is clear as to that of his addressee (Letter 57 Schmitt

“Lord and father” and “in all things totally his”. I do not say that I
know how to produce a virtual likeness but I have engraved a cypress

may appear expressed in the finished version. Since therefore I so often

inscribe this form of address to you, asking I complain and complaining

We could link this to Anselm’s own ambivalence as to his status, both
personally and in relation to the respective functions of clergy and state,

kings and archbishops, emperors and popes. Which also raises the issue of
temporary ‘successions’, whereby different occupants of a particular role
might have the same formal status but exhibit a quite different persona. Yet
if there is a good and a better there must be a conceivable best, if great and

who is also permanently and necessarily so. A true Lord and eternal King.
As distinct from all those passing kings and popes who briefly and
‘successively’ occupy thrones and fulfil offices, there is surely someone who
is always there, here, and everywhere, every time, now. Necessarily.

We can now return to the formulation of what has so often been called ‘the
ontological proof, perhaps with a clearer sense of what ‘addressing’ an
‘absent’ person meant for Anselm. The first point is that in praying, Anselm
could experience himself as addressing someone who was very palpably
present to him ‘in his mind’, and there is a progression one can trace from
Anselm addressing with varyingly appropriate terms of greeting,
endearment, respect, and humility, a hierarchy of his various addressees: a
fellow monk, a fellow Abbot, a Bishop, the Archbishop of York, Queen
Matilda, King Henry, Pope Pascal — each addressee being ‘in his mind’ in
various senses ‘greater’ than others in that hierarchy of status and assumed
excellence. The culminating point would be the divine itself, immeasurably
and undeniably ‘greater’ than any other addressee, both present and absent,
indeed beyond presence and absence. The divine addressee is thereby
‘greater’ than that which any can conceive.

However, there is a further twist in the implications of the formula ‘that
than which nothing greater can be conceived’ — however we translate the
term here rendered as ‘conceived’. If it were only a matter of my own
capacity or incapacity to conceive of someone greater even than a Pope,
that might be due to my inadequacy, or even my lack of experience in
appropriate feudal circles. At this point a certain impersonality of the
formulation is required: the phrase must mean ‘that than which nothing

38

greater can be conceived — by anyone’. The implicit appeal is to the
impossibility of any concept encapsulating ‘greatness’ even beyond that
which is being conceptualised.

Yet the person who is being addressed is, in Anselm’s mind, alone
capable of conceiving things ‘greater’ than any one else could conceive. It is
therefore only God himself who is able to judge whether ‘that than which
nothing greater can be conceived’ can indeed be conceived. It would require
divine assistance for the ‘wretched little man’, as Anselm describes himself,
to be able to reach even an apophatic conception of such greatness. Which
is perhaps exactly what Anselm thought had happened at that moment of
prayerful illumination: that God himself had given Anselm this formulation,
even though it was one which only God could fully ‘understand’. Indeed,
God is the only person in a position to assess the validity of the proof of his
own existence. But God doesn’t need any such proof.

For Anselm the divine “You” whom T address must exist, otherwise his
whole life has been a hollow practice, above all the constant praying. In
addressing “You” what T therefore most want is not an answer to a
conceptual problem, a proof of a proposition, but a response to my appeal.
If I address to “You” the plea “answer me!” and there is no answer, then
perhaps it is my fault and I must try harder. As Anselm does in the prayers
which rhetorically work up his sense of guilt, horror, and sin. What is
inconceivable is that there is in fact no addressee to offer any response or
answer at all. Even when “You” doesn’t answer and remains silent. But the
“You” had spoken in that moment of revelation or insight. Chapter 1 of the
Proslogion attempts a reconstruction of a state in which that moment can be
partly or almost captured again, so that Anselm can again address “God”
even in a now silent absence.

If I can address other people as “you”, I can also address myself as “you”.
Anselm does so in many of his written prayers. Pros/ogion chapter 1 begins
with such a self-address. And it is inconceivable that the ‘you’ whom I
address in addressing myself does not exist. One might say that my ‘soul’ is
the ‘you’ whom I address as T. And I am always present to myself.

Descartes’ basic ‘cogito argument, ‘I think, therefore I am’, might be
regarded as a reflexive self-address in a similar mode to Anselm’s
‘ontological proof, such that its primary premise is not the statement that ‘I
think’, but the fact that I am telling myself: “you are thinking.” If I ask
myself the question “am I thinking?” the answer has to be “yes I am.” In
Latin anyway the nominative ‘T is already included within the verb ‘cogito. A
child who believes in Santa Claus addresses him, and it is a built-in premise
of asking Santa Claus for presents that Santa Claus actually exists to be
asked. And also that the child exists. Descartes’ cogito is perhaps an even
more direct rendition of the core of Anselm’s ‘ontological argument’ than
the commentary Descartes implicitly offered upon it.

There is a further assonance between the motif of absence and presence
in the letters, the prayers, and Anselm’s fundamental theological concepts

39

concerning both the divine and the human. In trying to understand what
Anselm meant by his having a conception of God ‘in his mind’ we are
drawn to consider such terms as ‘mind’, ‘spirit’, and ‘soul’. For something to
be both in Anselm’s mind and in the ‘mind of God’ seems to imply that this
shared something — like the love shared between Anselm and his fellow
monks — must be itself non-physical or incorporeal, and it is basic to
Anselm’s argument about the very nature of God that being non-corporeal
is undeniably ‘greater’ than being corporeal. Once it is conceded, or
assumed, that non-corporeal spirits can exist, the way is open to adducing
as attributes of a ‘spirit’ the exercise of capacities which would indeed be
limited by corporeality: being everywhere and knowing everything, being
outside time yet always present.

Without the concept of a non-corporeal spiritual being it is difficult to
affirm the traditional attributes of the Christian God, let alone to make
sense of the doctrine of the Trinity. Within the framework available to
Anselm the distinction between the body and the mind was inextricably
wound up with the notion of ‘spirit’. Without some version of this linchpin
concept of ‘spirit? or ‘soul’ we too might have to rethink some of the
categories we find most familiar. And perhaps one of the terms we would
have to re-think is that of thinking itself.

self-evidence

I have so far considered some immediate contexts for Anselm’s ‘ontological
argument’, including how the Pros/ogion can be situated in his way of life as a
monk. Some broader themes can now be explored, in a fairly tentative way,
exploring or at least broaching a range of connected issues.

Consider for example the implications of another letter by Anselm. In
1077 Anselm sent the draft manuscript of the Monologion to Lanfranc, by
then Archbishop of Canterbury, asking for his comments. It was explicit in
the Monologion that Anselm proposed to validate Christian beliefs about God
by the use of reason alone. We don’t have Lanfranc’s response, but clearly
he expressed disquiet at Anselm’s neglect of the established practice of
citing Biblical texts and patristic authorities as the primary supports for a
theological argument. Here is Anselm’s tactful but firm reply (Letter 77,
Schmitt II. p. 199):

Concerning the matters which are dealt with in that little work, and
which you advise with considerate and wise counsel should be very
meticulously balanced in the mind and discussed with experts in the
sacred texts, and when reason fails us should be buttressed by divine
authority, all this I have done as best I could, both before and after
your loving paternal admonition. It was my intention throughout the
entire argumentation, whatever form it took, that in it I should never
assert anything unless I saw that it could be strongly defended either by

40

an appeal to canonical writings or the works of blessed Augustine. And
now, however much I reconsider what I said, I cannot conceive myself
as having stated anything other than such . . and indeed blessed
Augustine in his book De Trinitate strongly argues these same points so
that, discerning the same matters with my own limited reasoning, I
might speak relying upon his authority. I say this not to defend
anything I said to you but to show that I did not presume to state them
on my own but adopted them from someone else. But whatever the
case, that work awaits your judgement, to be either salvaged or on your
authority.

Despite the somewhat disingenuous claims about Augustine, Anselm
effectively declined Lanfranc’s criticism and put into circulation a version of
the Monologion which asserted its reliance solely upon reason. His initially
preferred title for the treatise had been simply meditatio de ratione fidei.
Anselm can be seen as here making two significant moves within the
received traditions of Christian thinking.

One implication was that his argument would rely upon his own
intellectual resources, his individual selection and validation of a starting
point which he could regard and represent as in effect self-evident. Relying
upon some such ‘self-evident’ premise, the familiar procedures of logical
demonstration could then be followed, leading to a conclusion the truth of
which was validated by extrapolation from the truth of the opening
assertion. Such a procedure has since been characteristic of much ‘modern’
philosophical thinking, as in the Cartesian claim to doubt everything except
what was deemed to be undeniable to Descartes himself. Secondly, Anselm
was in effect declining to seek validation from ecclesiastical authority, a
decision with considerable implications to which we can return later.

However, in the Pros/ogion one crucial difference or development from the
Monologion is that the argument in the later text rather peculiarly rests upon a
claim to an authority apparently greater than that of Augustine and perhaps
even that of the Bible itself. Anselm in effect tells his reader that he had
received a direct divine answer to his fervent prayers, a response which took
the form of God himself providing the crucial phrase ‘that than which
nothing greater can be conceived’. The phrase thereby has a curious double
status. It apparently operates as a premise in a stand-alone philosophical
argument and has been treated precisely as such by later commentators. Yet
it is also claimed to be a divine communication, of a particularly significant
kind, not simply a message from God to a prophet but the direct revelation
of a new ‘name’ for God himself, almost comparable to the enigmatic name
revealed to Moses: ‘I am that I am’.

What uniquely characterises Anselm’s ostensibly philosophical starting-
point is that the premise upon which he bases his position was itself
allegedly provided by the very deity whose existence is supposedly
established by the conclusion to be drawn from that premise: (you are) ‘that

41

than which nothing greater can be conceived’. The peculiar circularity of
this argument proceeds from an ‘illumination’ provided by that same deity.
As we have seen in commenting upon the complex tapestry of personal
pronouns and forms of address in the opening passages of the Pros/ogion,
this self-sustaining loop can even be interpreted as quite literally ‘self-
evident’ since the ‘deity’ which apparently provides the starting-point is
arguably Anselm himself, speaking to himself in two personae, since it is he
who also constructs in the text the persona of the God who speaks to him.
The divine “You” who addresses the ‘you’ of Anselm and the “You” whom
Anselm addresses are two voices in a dialogue in which Anselm actually
speaks both voices himself. Obviously, by speaking in the voice of God one
can be readily convinced that one has heard the voice of God. The problem
of reliable ‘self-evidence’ is resolved by one self attributing to one’s other
self the required confirmation.

The opening section of the Pros/ogion is a ‘dialogue’ in which the divine
‘Yow is in fact represented as largely silent while Anselm addresses God in
the second-person as “You” but also addresses himself as if on behalf of
that God. Anselm had, he claims, experienced a personalised address or
response directly from God, but only in that one moment of suddenly
being given the crucial phrase to be subsequently recognised as constituting
the desired single proof. Anselm is left to work out afterwards the
implications for himself of this compressed formula.

As to some extent so is the reader. In reading the address by Anselm to
himself as “homounculus”, ‘wretched little man’, we are rhetorically drawn
into being invited to take up a similar position of self-address. Once I start
responding to Anselm’s exhortation, I am seduced into accepting the
notion that the ‘speaker’ is both Anselm and myself: I am encouraged by the
text to impersonate or mimic Anselm’s own T, to speak in his words and to
enact them, including those implicitly attributed to ‘God’. It is the initial
move in what is not primarily a logical argument but, as I will suggest
shortly, what I would call a ‘literary conversion’.

We know that Anselm was regarded by his contemporaries as unusual in
being able to read silently to himself. In the very act of reading to oneself
what can seem to be happening is that some process, entity, or ‘voice’ is
experienced as operating ‘inside my head’ or ‘in my mind’, as the assumed
voice of the author whose text I am reading but at the same time my own
voice. This speaking ‘voice’ is not outside me in the external world, but in a
curious non-spatial territory which is somehow associated with my body or
perhaps brain but is not visible or tangible.

Moreover, if I write down my own ‘thought’, it too can then become
experienced almost as the text of somebody other than myself. In some
circumstances one’s own silent ‘thinking’ can easily be interpreted as the
voice of conscience, the voice of reason, perhaps as a remembered parental
voice, or even the authoritative voice of God.

42

This ‘inner voice’ aspect of my activity can present itself as mysterious,
and may seem to provide grounds for claiming, as in some familiar versions
of ‘common sense’, that when we speak to other humans it is these inner
articulations of our ‘spirit’ that we are communicating to the ‘spirit’ of
another person, as intangibly immaterial as my own inner spirit or voice.
The notion of a non-localisable but ever-present divine Spirit can then seem
a feasible assumption and conclusion: that it is the divine spirit who speaks
‘in’ my own mind or heart. Anselm while writing and reading his own text
was both addressing “God” and ‘hearing’ God in the act of also addressing
and persuading himself.

If, as many commentators since Gaunilo have claimed, the alleged logic
of Anselm’s overall argument can be so easily refuted, especially when
couched in the schematic propositions of a formal syllogism, how could
Anselm, who was certainly no ‘fool’, be himself persuaded by such a
patently defective argument? Perhaps because he had read his text himself
and had succumbed to his own textual construction. On Eadmet’s account,
Anselm had indeed been forced to reconstruct and make sense of his own
argument by literally re-assembling its broken fragments. He had necessarily
to become his own collaborative editor and first reader.

Regarded as a logical syllogism, the apparent structure of the Pros/ogion’s
overall ‘proof is constituted by a major premise, that “There is a God’ as
defined, with the minor premise that ‘I can understand this notion of God’,
leading to the conclusion ‘I am therefore right to believe in the existence of
God’, and indeed have no choice not to do so (‘non possum non intelligere).
Whereas the actually operative structure in the Pros/ogion is arguably
somewhat different: I believe (in) God’, who has spoken to me, and since
‘I am right to believe (in) this God’ therefore “There is a God’. The
Proslogion is thus a three-stage process, enacting an initial quasi-performative
persona as already provisionally a believer, through a more or less self-
convincing articulation of the grounds for such belief, to a retrospective
endorsement of the opening position. This structure is underpinned by a
personally directed divine address, a form of private revelation, providing
the self-evidence of the given divine formula. Such a pattern, I want to
suggest, is quite commonly at work in the ‘logic’ of religious ‘belief’, and
perhaps also sometimes in the ordinary experience of sudden ‘insights’
which ‘come’ to us.

literary conversion

Standard accounts of ‘the ontological argument’ present it as depending
logically upon an idea, a concept or conceptualisation, even an implicit
definition of the word ‘God’ encapsulated in the crucial phrase (‘that than
which nothing greater can be conceived’). Any such argument is vulnerable
to objections which regard a particular definition, or the unpacking of the
implications of that definition, as arguably unjustified, unsuccessful,

43

unpersuasive, or inescapably incoherent and self-contradictory. On my
reading, however, Anselm’s process of ‘thinking’ in the Pros/ogion is primarily
grounded upon an action or practice, a participation in a way of life, rather
than rationally derived from the logical elaboration of a concept. The core
action is that of prayer, which constituted a complex combinatory of
reciprocal addressing and being addressed. Anselm is addressing God, who
is envisaged as in return addressing Anselm, if only in that single crucial
moment of apparent illumination. Anselm’s text might thus be regarded as
involving a peculiar form of ventriloquism. Highlighting this aspect of
Anselm’s text does not simply emphasise a possibly dispensable rhetorical
device operating in the text but points to the persuasive core of Anselm’s
argument, and perhaps more widely suggests a way of understanding the
continuing viability of religious belief as an ‘ideological’ commitment.

In so far as we are invited to follow and enact a chain of thought, or a
pathway of thinking, the apparent overall construction of the Pros/ogion is
mis-leading. The articulation of the ‘proof comes early in the treatise, in the
opening chapters, where the reader is invited and assumed to ‘understand’
the crucial but undefined term ‘greater’ without its explicit content being
specified at that point. But then the rest of the treatise explores and explains
the traditional attributes of the divine, and those later chapters can, or
inevitably will, be taken into account retrospectively when the reader later re-
assesses that initially opaque term ‘greater’ in the light of eventually reading
the rest of the treatise.

Anselm’s first use of the term ‘greater’ thereby acts as a prior
summarisation of the traditional attributes of the divine which he is to
expound only later in the text. If we take the criterion of ‘greater’ to be
defined by reference to those attributes then the logic of the formulation of
the treatise as a whole operates as a structural and almost reverse variation
upon the usual organisation of a philosophical argument. The conclusion
precedes the argument in what later scholastic terminology would term a
‘consequent implication’ or circular syllogism.

Put differently, the structural device at work in the text is more
characteristic of a literary fiction than of a philosophical composition, and
Anselm’s Pros/ogion is an elaborate example of a form of textual construction
which I have elsewhere termed ‘literary conversion’ and explored in some
detail in relation to texts by Coleridge, Richardson, T. S. Eliot and others
(see my Literary Conversions, 2015). A ‘literary conversion’ involves not only a
‘suspension of disbelief but a form of double-bind.

A literary text can be so constructed that in order to begin reading it we
effectively have to agree, at least provisionally, to give credence to an
element in the tale without which the rest of the narrative could not be
coherently followed or accepted. As the text develops, however, we realise
that out provisional acceptance has been retrospectively transformed into
an increasingly unavoidable ongoing endorsement without which the text
would fail not only to convince us, but actually to cohere at all. Most

44

obviously, at the end of a detective novel we discover who the criminal was
and we can then retrospectively recognise what was the premise of the
entire plot from the beginning. A classic example would be The Murder of
Roger Ackroyd in which the perpetrator is finally revealed as in effect the
narrator. Raymond Chandler and the nouveau roman in different ways
enjoyed exploiting and subverting the compositional conventions of the
relation between narrator, detective, victim, and criminal. In a skilfully
constructed science-fiction movie we can be seduced into allowing
ourselves to believe in a highly implausible opening sequence which is only
later explained and validated by the subsequent development of the
scenario.

A recent instance of both the overall structure of a ‘literary conversion’
and a self-awareness concerning it as a literary device can be illustrated by
an unusually explicit moment in Abdulrazak Gurnah’s novel Desertion. In a
chapter entitled ‘An Interruption’ which concludes Part One of the novel
Gurnah writes:

I don’t know how it would have happened. The unlikeliness of it
defeats me. Yet I know it did happen, that Martin and Rehana became
lovers. Imagination fails me and that fills me with sorrow. I had
thought that even without knowing the details of their affair I would be
able to get to the truth of it, for imagination is a kind of truth. I do not
mean that as a Romantic solipsism: it is only what I am able to imagine
that is true. I mean that even with very incomplete knowledge, we can
imagine what might have been, and how it might have come about and
proceeded, but I find that in the case of Martin and Rehana I cannot
settle on a sequence of events that seems likeliest. . . . Nonetheless,
whether I am able to imagine it or not, I know that Rehana Zekariya
and Martin Pearce became lovers. I have no choice but to try and give
an account of how their affair might have happened.

Since the whole of Part Two of this multi-generational novel rests upon
the intertwined stories of characters revolving partly around the
consequences of the relationship between Martin and Rehana it is essential
to the later action of the fiction that this unimaginable love affair actually
happened. At the same time the colonial setting and the historical context
of Part One makes the love affair and therefore the consequent narrative
explicitly beyond the imagining of both the novelist and the reader who
nevertheless must imagine it as having occurred.

Anselm’s ‘philosophical’ text is a variation upon this persuasive literary
device. In provisionally accepting the starting point of a procedure or
argument and finding that this commits one to either endorsing later
developments or retrospectively repudiating one’s initial acceptance of the
opening gambit, the later arguments of Anselm’s text offer or provide a
backwards-facing confirmation of the opening move. The overall argument

45

is thus retrospectively self-sustaining, rather than simply or obviously
circular.

One might regard the traditional hermeneutical relationship between the
New Testament and Old Testament as constructed along similar lines, with
the notion of ‘types’ and ‘anti-types’ illustrated in the ‘biblia pauperum’
windows of the cathedral. The structural device which underpins this visual
and theological presentation is the selection of a later incident as vindicating
the status of an earlier ‘prophecy’. Walter Benjamin’s notion of plucking a
moment from the past to constellate with the present has an affinity with
this theological perspective. As perhaps does any serious personal or
political commitment which is recognised in and by a retrospective
confirmation of a significant practical decision, a basic life-choice,
experienced as already previously made and now consciously endorsed in
hindsight. The notion of ‘moral luck’ developed by Bernard Williams, and
discussed in my Upon A House, (2015) has affinities with this structure and
also with the computing process known as back propagation to be
discussed somewhat later.

performance

Anselm was writing a text for his brethren to read, a script for them to
enact, even perhaps reading it aloud to their fellow monks. His text is so
rhetorically and emotionally constructed that they cannot read the opening
address to “God” unless they endeavour to make it their own address to
God, to adopt the persona of believing, which means that they are prompted
already to believe him to exist. That life-choice is indeed endemic in the
ostensible commitment they have previously and continuously made to the
monastic life, and which might therefore seem to render the entire
argument of the Proslogion redundant for them. If by the time they have
finished reading the whole of the Pros/ogion and have been convinced by
Anselm’s arguments, they can sincerely address a God who has been
‘proven’ to exist, though even if Anselm’s argument has not convinced
them they can nevertheless be presumed, as monks, to be able and willing at
least to continue to address God, as “Lord”, “my Lord”, and so to re-enter
into the double-bind of the text. From the very beginning of the text the
whole discourse has effectively been addressed to “God” and they, as
engaged readers, would already have been addressing “God” and not
merely defining a concept. Such an address would not make sense if ‘God’
did not exist, or if — like the Fool — they said ‘in their hearts’ (for reasons
which might have nothing to do with being persuaded or otherwise by
Anselm’s argument) that ‘Gog’ did not exist. Which would be unacceptable
as either premise or conclusion for a conscientious and honest monk
continuing to live in the monastery.

46

However, the overall structure of the Pros/ogion addresses and involves any
reader as if they too were already believing, even if the text claims to
address also those who are not believers, by presenting itself as primarily a
rational third-person statement of a logical proof. What then if a reader
realises that they do not ‘understand’ the ‘argument’, or cannot be sure if
they have understood it, or become uncertain whether it ‘works’ or not?
What do they do at that point in reading the text? Do they simply abandon
it, and stop reading? Or do they, having ‘come this far’ (and not just in the
consecutive reading of the text), continue reading with a kind of ongoing
provisional assent, which can take the form of only sort-of addressing
“God” as the text requires, but as a kind of ‘addressing’ in quotation marks,
a pretence or performance? Does that then count as a quasi-performative
utterance? And does ceasing to read effect a kind of negative commitment,
an unproven and perhaps premature assumption that the ontological proof
does not work, and mil not convince. Or should one venture the experiment
again?

In one sense, this ‘literary conversion’ or double-bind structure is what
Anselm had already provided in the opening proem, regarded as a
performative script, even before the argument (it seemed) had actually
begun. In reading the initial exhortation the reader is already invited to
adopt the persona of one who addresses “God” and therefore as one who
accepts, however provisionally, that such an addressee can exist.

The crucial underlying move is that the God so addressed precisely as the
addressee of a prayer is also to be retrospectively identified with the God
who emerges as the conclusion of the overall philosophical argument.
There may of course be a variety of possible responses by Anselm’s actual
readers, of varying degreees of being persuaded or otherwise, of suspending
belief or disbelief. Many later readers have felt quite genuinely unsure
whether the ‘proof actually ‘works’ or not. As evidenced by a long history
of ambivalent judgements on the purely logical validity of the ‘proof as
‘extricated’ from the whole text.

But what of Anselm himself? I might here ask whether Anselm was
persuaded by his own arguments. Was Anselm absolutely sure himself?
Recall again that in replying to Gaunilo Anselm did not, to begin with,
claim that he would be answering ‘the Fool’ but rather responding to the
Catholic who wrote ‘in the persona of the Fool’. The reason Anselm can
make that distinction is precisely because Gaunilo as a Catholic and as a
fellow monk would indeed regularly and habitually ‘address’ God. As would
the monk Anselm. The operative premise of the whole exercise is the belief
that God can be so addressed. Which he could not be if he did not exist.
Except by a kind of play-acting. But then a chasm opens before Anselm
himself. Was his whole existence as a monk who prayed to God every day
and night only a form of play-acting, an elaborate fiction, a script he was
following? Even if he partly wrote it himself. One might here recognise a

47

variant upon his strenuous affirmation that he really did not want to be an
Archbishop.

Any reader today who, to begin with, finds it distinctly odd to be
addressing a God whom he or she does not believe exists might
nevertheless decide to read the Pros/ogion anyway and try to assess Anselm’s
argument as it emerges, judging it as a valid or invalid philosophical
exercise. But following that process can then curiously become one which
would retrospectively justify the initial address to God which was only a
notional or provisional starting point. Anselm’s chapter 4 is addressed
precisely to such a reader. It asks how (not whether) anyone could say ‘in his
heart’ what he could not ‘conceive’:

Chapter 4 : How has the fool said in his heart what cannot be
conceived?

(Quomodo insipiens dixit in corde, quod cogitari non potest?)

But how has the fool said in his heart what he could not conceive; or
how is it that he could not conceive what he said in his heart? since it is
the same to say in the heart, and to conceive. But, if really, indeed, since
really, he both conceived, because he said in his heart, and did not say
in his heart, because he could not conceive, there is more than one way
in which a thing is said in the heart or conceived.

For, in one sense, an object is conceived, when the word signifying it
is conceived; and in another, when the very entity, which the object is,
is understood. In the former sense, then, God can be conceived not to
exist; but in the latter, not at all. For no one who understands what fire
and water are can conceive fire to be water, in accordance with the
nature of the facts themselves, although this is possible according to
the words.

So, then, no one who understands what God is can conceive that
God does not exist; although he says these words in his heart, either
without any or with some extraneous signification. (Nullus quippe
intelligens id quod Deus est, potest cogitare quia Deus non est, licet baec verba dicat
in corde, aut sine ulla aut cum aliqua extranea significatione.) For God is that
than which a greater cannot be conceived. And he who thoroughly
understands this, assuredly understands that this being so truly exists
that not even in concept can it be non-existent. Therefore, he who
understands that God so exists, cannot conceive that he does not exist.

Anselm’s use of ‘in his heart’ operates as a kind of shorthand for the
entire process summoned up in the opening exhortation. Though in
Anselm’s vocabulary the phrase might be the equivalent of ‘in his mind’, a
matter of intellectual grasp, with ‘the heart’ as the assumed /ocws of mental
activity or comprehension, the force of its deployment here is to imply that
a failure or refusal to grasp intellectually the meaning of the word ‘God’ as
‘that than which nothing greater can be conceived’, and therefore

48

necessarily to accept the existence of God, is a matter of profound
intellectual and emotional insincerity, of pretending to oneself that one has
not understood or conceived when one necessarily has. In this instance, for
Anselm ‘it is the same to say in the heart, and to conceive’ even as he
recognises that ‘there is more than one way in which a thing is said in the
heart or conceived’. So, although Anselm proceeds by a kind of logical
argument, the basic premise or starting point of the argument ‘in his own
heart’ is not a proposition or a statement, but the action of addressing
“God” as “You”.

This is also, appropriately, the final move in the Pros/ogion text. Anselm
concludes the argument with himself, as he began it, with an address to
“God”:

I thank you, gracious Lord, I thank you; because what I formerly
believed by your gift, I now so understand by your illumination, that if I
were unwilling to believe that you exist, I should not be able not to
understand this to be true.

(Gratias tibi, bone Domine, gratias tibi, quia quod prius credidi te donante, iam sic
intelligo te illuminante, ut, si te esse nolim credere, non possim non intelligere. )

So, one might ask, is it possible to understand a sentence without
understanding its meaning?

inspitation

It is certainly possible to state or even to formulate a sentence without fully
understanding it. Anselm claims precisely this: that it was God who
provided the crucial phrase or premise without Anselm initially grasping its
full implications. The theological term most germane to this possibility is
‘inspiration’, notably with reference to the Bible as providing another
obvious context in which to locate Anselm’s texts. It is after all not
necessary that the recipient of divine ‘prophetic’ inspiration should actually
understand it, only be committed to transmitting it. So one related issue is
clarifying the difference between an ‘inspired’ text and one simply
composed by its human author. A brief excursus may be useful before we
return to Anselm.

Traditional accounts of prophetic inspiration have offered descriptions or
explanations in terms of a direct divine dictation, for example hearing a
‘voice’, or a kind of spiritual infusion, perhaps an immaterial guidance not
even fully conscious to or recognised by the author. This notion has been
presented, or defended, in Karl Rahner’s attempt at a modernised scholastic
argument, which can be briefly summarised in some quotations from his
Inspiration in the Bible (rev. ed. 1964, pp. 22, 40, 51, 61):

49

The human author perceives something because God effectively wills
him to do so, with a will which is not merely permissive . . but rather
pre-definine—what is called in technical theology, a praedefinitio formalis .
. The determining factor in an efficacious (as distinct from a merely
sufficient) grace can consist in some external circumstance which God
foresees, through his scientia media, will be decisive in a man’s coming to
his choice and is willed by God as such. . . God wills the church and
brings it into being [because] . . his design for the incarnation of the
Logos includes within itself the founding of the church. . . The
concrete, fully realised essence of the church includes the scriptures;
they are a constitutive element of her. . . The bible is not merely an
externally adopted indifferent instrument of the apostolic church in
performing her task; her normative function for the future church was
exercised precisely by reducing to writing her paradosis, her faith, her
self-constitution. . . the life-situation within which and out of which the
scriptures arise is God’s astonishing and mighty work of manifesting
himself in human history through the prophets and through his Son.
The scriptures’ (formally predefined) emergence from this vital context
is what makes them scriptures.

Decoding the Scholastic terminology, this argument displaces the work of
divine inspiration onto the overall establishment of the church rather than
any individual prophet.

Dennis McCarthy offers an even more revisionist account, here writing of
the ‘Yahwist’ strand in the composition of the Old Testament (Theological
Studies, XXXIV, pp. 553-576, my emphasis):

In the reaction of the writer to his own historical experience resulting in
a new interpretation in the light of Yahwist traditions, we have the
same general factors as in the prophets: experience, tradition and
personal reaction. However, the experience, instead of being a mystical
contact with the divine, came simply from living through a momentous era of
history; we can find no mark of extra-ordinary divine communication,
yet this also is inspired writing.

In both these accounts the ‘inspiring’ action of God has been pushed
further back or behind the immediate process of speaking or writing. No
longer is the divine influence to be located directly in the ‘mind and will’ of
the individual writer, but rather in the human author’s complex reaction to
a divinely-willed historical situation. But what is then left of the difference
between this kind of ‘influence’ and the ordinary processes of trying to
understand one’s historical situation? McCarthy is more straightforward (my
emphasis):

50

The divinely guided community produced texts as an integral part of a
complex process in which was the very life of the community. Further,
this process was the term of the special divine guidance different from
providence, conservation and so on, which God must have exercised
on the community. Osherwise Israel was not the chosen people in any
meaningful sense and its history [was not] salvation history, for all
peoples and all history are under ordinary providence.

Rahner makes a similar point by distinguishing between ‘concursus’ (the
‘ordinary providence’ of McCarthy) and the ‘formal predefinition’ he claims
to be operative in the case of the founding of the church and therefore in
the ‘inspiration’ of the church’s canonical writings. Characteristically,
Rahner chooses to begin from a dogmatic definition, which already implies
a notion of God as agency:

Our starting point is the doctrine of inspiration as established in its
basic outlines by the teaching authority of the church and further
explained and expanded by the theologians, not the bible itself and
whatever it may have to say about inspiration.

This obviously involves him in a circular argument, which he recognises (p.
36). But it would be possible to leave out altogether from his account of the
relation between the community and its self-expression any notion of God-
as-agent.

One could simply say that when the Israelite group finds itself involved in
a new ‘situation’ in its history, it seeks to understand the significance of that
new context, and the result is then a changed collective self-consciousness
which finds expression in a normal cultural way through spokespersons and
leaders. Since an integral part of the particular configuration of this tribal
society is already a reference to its god, “Yahweh’ himself is partially re-
defined in the process. In other words, Rahner’s thesis reduces itself to a
parenthesis in a tautology, as finally in the sentence (p. 61): “The scriptures
(formally predefined) emergence from this vital context is what makes them
scriptures.’

The crucial factor emphasised by both these theologians is that the
biblical writer speaks as the representative for or on behalf of a community
and it is the overarching guarantee of the special status of that community
which retrospectively validates the claim of divine providence. It is the
community’s own acceptance or approval which justifies the message as
divinely inspired. Otherwise Israel was not the chosen people. Again, a self-
sustaining circularity is in play.

Another way of highlighting the problem of ‘inspiration’ is to ask upon
the basis of what extraordinary audacity or authorial arrogance would or
could a human writer dare to present himself (usually male, of course) as
composing the very words of God. Unless the claim of inspiration was

51

taken by the writer himself as self-evident, as underwritten by an individual
experience, perhaps by a vision or voice of God. Only his own belief in that
illumination could justify the self-confidence involved in composing God’s
own text. Or at least a belief in the privileged destiny of the particular
community on whose behalf the writer assumes to speak. Such an account
is of course open to a more sceptical interpretation, including seeing in the
whole process a self-defined version of self-interest, individual or
communal.

The notion that the Bible is ‘inspired’, as a claim founded upon assertions
made to that effect within the Bible text itself, is a variant upon the
circularity of self-evidence, as the Bible acknowledges. Exodus includes a
vatiety of caveats as to why ‘the people’ might not be persuaded to believe
in the claims made by Moses, and he is duly equipped with miraculous signs
and even with a persuasive supplementary intermediary, Aaron, who can
relay the words of Moses which themselves relay the words of God. Here is
Exodus 19:3f., in the perhaps familiar ‘Authorised King James Version’:

And Moses went up unto to God, and the Lord called unto him out of
the mountain, saying, Thus shalt thou say to the house of Jacob, and
tell the children of Israel; Ye have seen what I did unto the Egyptians
and how I bare you on eagles’ wings and brought you unto myself.
Now therefore, if ye will obey my voice indeed, and keep my covenant,
then ye shall be a peculiar treasure unto me above all people: for all the
earth is mine: and ye shall be unto me a kingdom of priests, and an holy
nation. These are the words which thou shalt speak unto the children
of Israel . . . So Moses went down unto the people, and spake unto
them. [20:1f] And God spake all these words, saying, I [am] the Lord
thy God, which have brought thee out of the land of Egypt, out of the
house of bondage. Thou shalt have no other gods before me. Thou
shalt not make unto thee any graven image, or any likeness of any thing
that is in heaven above, or that is in the earth beneath, or that is in the
water under the earth: Thou shalt not bow down thyself to them, nor
serve them: for I the Lord thy God am a jealous God, visiting the
iniquity of the fathers upon the children unto the third and fourth
generation of them that hate me....

We can skip the many words of God as apparently precisely remembered or
exactly recorded in the text and go to Exodus 32:15:

And Moses turned, and went down from the mount, and the two tables
of the testimony were in his hand: the tables were written on both their
sides; on the one side and on the other were they written. And the
tables were the work of God, and the writing was the writing of God,
graven upon the tables.

52

They were presumably quite large tablets if they included all these chapters.

The words of Moses’s God ate explicitly an interpellation, a command in
the vocative addressed to the people and communicated by Moses: ‘you are
to obey the words spoken to you’. We are to believe, on the word of Moses,
that Moses speaks the word of God. And that the written word of God is
now as transcribed in the biblical text itself.

After numerous further words of God, Leviticus opens with a designated
new location for these communications, as specified in Exodus chapter 40:
‘And the Lord called unto Moses, and spake unto him out of the tabernacle
of the congregation, saying...” From chapter 4 onwards Leviticus simply
repeats the formula: ‘And the Lord spake unto Moses, saying. . > or
‘Yahweh spoke to Moses. He said...’. (RC-authorised Jerusalem Bible
translation). Numbers provides a regular date: ‘And the Lord spake unto
Moses in the wilderness of Sinai, in the tabernacle of the congregation, on
the first day of the second month, in the second year after they were come
out of the land of Egypt, saying ...’ Not unlike the first stone tablets, the
Law itself can even be lost, but rediscovered and reconstructed, when
necessary, by following further divine instructions in a new historical
context.

Throughout, the people addressed as “you” by Yahweh are thereby both
constructed as sharing a recalled past and by a promise of becoming the
future “you” of an ongoing covenant with the divine, in the repeated
continuity of a privileged history. To use McCarthy’s phrases, the later
‘inspirations’ of the prophets ‘came simply from living through a
momentous era of history’ motivating them to offer divinely authorised
interpretations of that history without which ‘Israel was not the chosen
people’.

One more example: the opening words of the prophet Joel provides an
identification of the writer himself, ‘the word that was addressed to Joel son
of Pethuel’, as a prelude to a stream of vocative injunctions addressed
variously to elders, drunkards, farmers, priests, and so on, with a regular
reminder: ‘But now — and it is Yahweh who speaks - come back to me with
all your heart’. There is even a promise of the future proliferation of a
shared capacity to speak God’s word (Joe/ 2: 28):

And it shall come to pass afterward, that I will pour out my spirit upon
all flesh; and your sons and your daughters shall prophesy, your old
men shall dream dreams, your young men shall see visions: and also
upon the servants and upon the handmade in those days will I pour out
my spirit. And I will show wonders in the heavens and in the earth,
blood and fire and pillars of smoke. The sun shall be turned into
darkness, and the moon into blood, before the great and the terrible
day of the Lord come.

53

The New Testament Gospels take this process a step further, providing
an account of a new prophet — Jesus of Nazareth — focused firmly upon the
life and actions as well as the sayings of the prophet himself, whose birth at
a determinate time and place can be attested, and whose words are woven
into a quasi-biographical narrative, of a miracle-working man who walks
among ordinary people and can be seen and touched by them. And now it
is not merely the son of Pethuel or Joseph who speaks the word of God but
the very Son of the Father God himself who speaks God’s own word
somehow as God’s own ‘Word’. Moreover, the new significant history
being lived through and re-interpreted is primarily the unique life and
history of this prophet himself, not only as prophesying a future but as
fulfilling the accumulated previous prophecies.

Luke 24: 13-35 provides the culmination of this entire process: after Jesus
has ‘explained to them all the passages, starting with Moses and going
through all the prophets, that were about himself they broke bread with
him ‘and their eyes were opened and they recognised him’. What is self-
evident to the Christ is now made self-evident to his disciples. The word of
Yahweh now has a date, a biography, a location, numerous persuasively
mundane miracles, and a new name of, and a new form of direct address to,
God as “Our Father”. Within that Jesus-centred narrative the Father
himself rarely needs to speak directly, as in the baptismal interpellation (“in
you I am well pleased”) or the ‘Transfiguration’ episode (“This is my son,
my chosen; listen to him!”’) and can even remain silent in response to the
final address: ‘why have you forsaken me?”

The issue of the self-awareness of Jesus himself in coming to his
confirmatory recognition of his own role as the Christ or Messiah obviously
constituted a unique theological problem, which the New Testament writers
to some extent resolve by offering themselves as witnesses of the Father’s
confirmation of Jesus’s claims, most notably in the resurrection narratives
as an event transformative of all history: the inauguration of a new Israel, a
chosen people beyond the boundaries of the old.

Early Christianity generated its own version of traditional prophetic
inspiration and visions, including the confirmatory fulfilment of Joel’s
prophecy in the Apostles speaking in tongues at Pentecost, and culminating
in a text which in the received canonical organisation of the New
Testament texts provides a concluding Revelation. The preamble of the
pseudo-Johannine Apocalypse asserts not a direct divine address but a
layered transmission, an accumulated authorisation:

The revelation of Jesus Christ which God gave unto him . . and he sent
it .. by his angel unto his servant John who bare the record of the
word of God and of the testimony of Jesus Christ and of all the things
that he saw. Blessed is he who reads . .

54

This now multi-layered text can then incorporate direct verbal inspiration
and divine address: ‘And I heard a voice from heaven saying unto me:
Write...’ (14: 13), ‘And he saith unto me: Write... — these are the true
sayings of God’ (19: 9). The composition of Revelations thus partly side-steps
the issue of audaciously inventing God’s own words by its saturating of the
text with a dense tissue of embedded quotations taken from previous
inspired texts, including the Pentateuch and the prophets Ezekiel, Isaiah,
Hosea, Daniel, and of course Joel.

Insofar, therefore, as the decisive historical change now to be
reinterpreted was Christ’s own life, any immediate witnessing of that history
was traditionally supposed to have been concluded with the death of the
last apostle, and their direct witness is then replaced and supplemented by
that of the ongoing but yet again re-defined community. To provide a
guiding interpretation was no longer the function of the prophets but of the
institutional church. The later Roman theological position concerning
‘Tradition’ was the basis upon which authorised biblical interpretation was
claimed to show that it is the church’s own interpretation which is justified
as the authentic interpretation.

This move constitutes another circular argument, not unlike that of a
Communist Party interpreting Karl Marx’s texts as justifying the control of
the Communist Party over the interpretation of those texts. And it no
longer matters whether the prophet himself understands the divine message
he transmits since the task of ‘understanding’ it is not his responsibility but
that of the church.

authority

Regarded in this overall context, Anselm’s letter to Lanfranc, quoted earlier,
implicitly marginalises the dogmatic role of the church itself, while the
Proslogion makes clear Anselm’s independence and audacity in providing his
own new ‘name’ for God. His claim of a new illumination, a new starting-
point, was presumably signalled in Eadmer’s echo of Moses’s broken tablets
in the incident where Anselm had to piece together the broken clay tablets
containing his own first attempt at recording the new name of God.

Anselm does not present himself as a ‘prophet’, but his stance would later
be presented as a proto-Protestant repudiation of the church’s dogmatic
authority over and above that of even the inspired Bible itself. In
McCarthy’s terms, the later Reformation undoubtedly constituted a
significant historical moment, requiring not only new interpretations of the
divine promise but new interpreters, who were not lacking. Which suggests
a further context for Anselm and his ‘proof’.

Whereas the patristic and mediaeval periods had seen a proliferation of
mainly short-lived and localised ‘heresies’ which could fairly easily be
repressed by the encompassing power of a dominant ecclesiastical
hierarchy, the post-Reformation era saw an increased number of instances

55

where an individual inspiration or revelation was no longer submitted to be
assessed, rejected, or endorsed by the existing authoritative community,
‘the’? Roman church, but became the foundation for new faith communities
which could outlast their individual initiators and continue to defy the
existing ecclesiastical hierarchy.

Innovative theological mavericks such as Waldes, Hus, or Wycliffe, had
generated some persistent followers, but most previous such charismatic
figures had been safely incorporated within the existing institutional
boundaries, with for example Benedict, Dominic, and Francis
accommodated as merely founding religious ‘orders’ within the existing
apparatus. What distinguished, say, Luther and Calvin from earlier
reforming figures was that their spiritual stance and theological positions
produced not a safely assimilated movement but a fracturing into rival and
lasting institutional organisations. Their persistence was partly dependent
upon competing secular political patronage but also crucially facilitated by
the major reorganisation of media technology which enabled Luther above
all to disseminate not simply his  individually-voiced alternative
interpretations but widely available printed polemical tracts.

The foundation for such reforming sects, denominations, and churches
tended to be encapsulated under the name of a particular personality whose
status as a divinely endorsed authoritative voice rested upon a variation of
Anselm’s own affirmation of confident self-assertion and independence:
here I stand, I can do no other. Though Anselm himself did not initiate a
religious order, a heresy, or even a school of followers, he has been
plausibly claimed as the godfather of a considerable overall development in
theological and philosophical inquiry. Which again makes him a focal figure.

The early Reformation model of individual founders was to be multiplied.
For example, in the later 17th century the Quakers marked their beginning
from George Fox whose singular vision on Pendle Hill in 1652 confirmed
his personal revelation that it was possible to have a direct experience of
Christ without the aid of an ordained clergy and to rely instead upon
‘inward light of Christ’, or the ‘Holy Spirit’. His followers asserted that they
too could encounter Christ directly and personally, ‘to feel him to be my
root, my life, and my foundation’, as Isaac Penington wrote in 1670. In
19th-century America the Seventh-day Adventist Church was derived from
the idiosyncratic prediction by William Miller, based upon his personal
interpretation of Daniel 8:14-16, that Christ would return to Earth in 1843-
44, later revised to exactly October 22, 1844, and then sensibly replaced by
Hiram Edson’s more circumspectly indefinite reinterpretation of Daniel.
Adventist followers could also appeal for confirmation of their beliefs to
the thousands of visions and dreams recorded by the extraordinary Ellen
Harmon White, as based upon her personal reading of Revelation, who
managed to write more than 5,000 periodical articles and 40 books to
propagate her version of Adventist beliefs.

A generation later, the Jehovah’s Witnesses movement developed from
the Bible Student Movement founded in the late 1870s by Charles Taze
Russell, and was initially characterised by a familiar fundamentalist
interpretation of the Bible as the inspired and inerrant word of God. The
movement quickly found it necessary to control any divergent
interpretations by restricting the reading of the Bible to just one approved
translation, the New World Translation of the Holy Scriptures, while individual
Witnesses were discouraged from formulating doctrines and any merely
‘private ideas’ reached through their own Bible interpretations independent
of Watch Tower Society publications and they were instructed not to
‘advocate or insist on personal opinions or harbor private ideas when it
comes to Bible understanding’. In other words, the Jehovah’s Witnesses
effectively returned to the notion of an authoritative church as necessary to
validate any individual revelations and interpretations.

One might discuss the writings of several other founders of religious
sects precisely as further instances of or variations on the underlying logic
and ventriloquising ‘inspiration’ which constituted Anselm’s core procedure
in the Proslogion. Including not only, say, William Smith and the Book of
Mormon but such quasi-religious variations of idiosyncratic self-persuasion
as the Diary of John Dee, discussed earlier in relation to Meric Casaubon,
whose own treatises on ‘credulity and incredulity’ might also be usefully
recalled.

We might also consider the current revival or reactivation of several
religious-political movements, including Islamic jihadists, Israeli Zionists,
and US evangelical White Nationalists. The justification for these various
religious-political positions characteristically includes appeals to the
authority of a sacred text as the utterances of a singular God who speaks
particularly favourably to only one such self-proclaiming group. The
preferred divine text is often regarded as directly an address from God to
each individual believer, even if indirectly mediated through a human
preacher, imam, minister, charismatic individual, and so on. Each individual
adherent is exhorted personally to choose or endorse a particular version of
God, as distinct from other claimants to that status, if only by confirming
retrospectively a belief-community born into, a faith inherited, or a
commitment already made. Meanwhile, each imam or minister may in
practice encroach upon the sacred text by offering their own paraphrases,
interpretations, commentary, and even interpolations, with the voice of the
preacher offering itself as providing the true voice of God. Especially in the
performances of some American tele-evangelists or in the prayer-meetings
of profitable mega-churches, the line between a divine and non-divine text,
between inspiration and ventriloquism, may seem somewhat porous. As
also the line between rational discourse and gibberish.

Insofar as the fundamental gambit of Anselm’s argument was his
speaking on behalf of God to himself, another obvious text to consider
would be the Koran. Traditional accounts represent the Prophet’s original

illuminations as sometimes a matter of inward hearing, occasionally as an
audible divine voice, and most frequently as the dictation of the angel
Gabriel. The much-disputed Sura VII, 157, can be interpreted as indicating
that the oral revelations to the new Prophet precluded any mere recycling
or incorporation of existing ‘sacred’ writings, including for example the
Syriac liturgical texts which some scholars have proposed as sources for the
Koran, since on this account Muhammad was simply unable to read. The
title ‘Koran’ itself arguably refers to that kind of reading or recitation by
which the related Syriac term geryana denoted a liturgical reading or /ectio,
and which the followers are now instructed to read or recite as the very
words of Allah. One tradition emphasises that the incomparable aesthetic
quality of the Koran itself vindicates its divine origin, since no other text
can match it for beauty and truth. Again a confirmatory claim which is
circular, and a variant upon the allegedly self-evident, once the Koran is
taken as itself providing the requisite criteria of beauty and truth.

In all these various instances a non-believer may recognise the same
pattern of self-persuasion and claimed self-evidence as in the fundamental
procedure of Anselm speaking on behalf of God to himself in the Pros/ogion.

As with Anselm’s reader, each individual believer is necessarily involved
in a process of self-persuasion, and any proclamation or declaration such as
‘I am saved’ or ‘I have been chosen’ is as much an affirmation to oneself as
to an audience of fellow-believers, or opponents. Any adult transition from
comparatively passive membership or affiliation to fully explicit allegiance
ordinarily involves the underlying three-stage process undertaken by a
reader of the Proslogion: a ‘literary conversion’ sequence, from an initial
persona as already a (possible) believer, through a more or less self-
convincing articulation of the grounds for belief, to a retrospective
endorsement of a more committed version of the opening provisional
stance. At its core, what is essentially involved is ‘addressing’ oneself as the
‘yov’ who is undergoing or confirming the conversion process.

This basic structure which appears common in affirmations of religious
allegiance may take the form of a dramatic born-again moment, perhaps an
experience of individual illumination or inspiration, which might sometimes
be encapsulated in an affirmation not simply of conversion but of a willing
self-sacrifice, if only in the form of a substantial financial contribution
towards one’s preferred tele-church.

Arguably indeed, any adoption or endorsement of a basic belief system
rests finally upon some such claim of the ‘self-evident’, whether reinforced
or not by a supplementary illumination. How far this pattern is also
characteristic of political allegiance and even of arriving at self-convincing
theoretical insight would be worth exploring. As would the differences
between the procedures of traditional ‘mainstream media’ and the self-
persuasive operations of internet influencers and conspiracy theorists.

philosophia

Think back again to the implications of Anselm’s starting point. The
peculiar and possibly unique character of Anselm’s God-given phrase was
that it is presented as divinely inspired and yet seems to require no other
validation than itself. It is both ‘inspired’ and wholly self-sufficient,
independent of ecclesiastical authority on both counts. Yet its alleged divine
origin is strictly irrelevant to its status as the self-unfolding premise of a
logical argument, and divine authorship is not what gives the phrase its
philosophical authority or validity. If the implications of the phrase can be
logically followed through to the point where the very existence of the
divine is sustained, it then becomes immaterial whether the phrase derived
from God in the first place.

Moreover, each reader is being offered the experience of receiving the
exact crucial phrase in much the same way that Anselm was himself
allegedly offered it, except that the illuminating encounter now comes from
reading Anselm’s text rather than direct from God. The moment of
‘inspiration’ is now located in the text, not in hearing the voice of God.
Though if the ‘proof is valid it also establishes the very possibility of such
divine inspiration.

Two different directions might therefore be traced from this oddly hybrid
inspitred/philosophical text. The mediaeval church was deeply suspicious of
private revelations or prophetic inspirations and Anselm does not present
himself as a ‘prophet’, but as a philosopher. Yet his stance would later be
represented as a proto-Protestant repudiation of the Roman church’s
dogmatic authority and as the affirmation of an intrinsically personal and
individual ‘faith’, as in Karl Barth’s complex engagement with Anselm in his
influential Fides Quaerens Intellectum (written in 1931, published in 1958).

On the other hand, Anselm’s strictly philosophical argument figured
persistently in the debates which constituted the post-Reformation ‘rational’
Enlightenment. It was Kant who coined the very phrase ‘ontological proof
to characterise the Pros/ogion’s argument as an instance of precisely what
Kant’s entire philosophical system sought to present as epistemologically
problematic, an analytic statement which entailed a synthetic assertion, a
purely logical deduction of an empirical proposition. As the editors of the
Cambridge edition of the 1997 Critique of Pure Reason point out: ‘Kant’s
critique of the ontological argument is in a sense the oldest part of his
critical philosophy’ (p. 747).

But it is then worth noting that Kant’s letter to Marcus Herz (February
21, 1772), often seen as decisively marking the Kantian ‘Copernican
revolution’, is shortly followed in the collected correspondence by several
letters seriously debating Herder’s argument in The Oldest Document that the
mysterious Hermes diagram was ‘the first instruction of the human race’
which came ‘directly from the hand of God’, and was indeed ‘God’s stroke
of the pen’ which preceded and underpinned even the ‘Books of Moses’.

The peculiar problematic of alleged divine ‘inspiration’ persisted into the
age of Enlightenment.

Claims of inspiration are not, of course, the normal context in which
Anselm’s Proslogion has been considered. It is usually treated as a
contribution to philosophical inquiry, since whatever its source, allegedly
divine or otherwise, the crucial phrase can be treated as a possibly valid
premise for a logical argument. However, if we now situate Anselm’s work
in the context of the history of philosophy it is again difficult to know quite
what sort of claims to make for it. Was it, as is sometimes asserted, an
innovation in philosophical thinking, an advance instance of what became
known as scholasticism or perhaps even a much later secular rationalism, by
providing a version of the search for ‘self-evident’ axioms.

The development after Anselm of theology and institutional philosophy
meant that Anselm’s uncertainties about his own status and identity,
discussed earlier in relation to Eadmer’s account of his life, might perhaps
have been partly resolved had he been able to pursue his commitment to
teaching and thinking as a full-time university professional, not as a
monastery grammaticus. But Anselm was a generation too early for such an
academic position, though in Lanfranc’s monastery at Bec he had been both
pupil and teacher in what might be regarded as a proto-university.

As Lanfranc would have insisted, the traditional way of writing theology
had primarily appealed to and was based upon the authority of biblical texts
as offering divinely revealed truths. In the Old and New Testaments the
various appearances of ‘what we call God’ are partly couched in narratives
involving characters who act and speak, and in the promulgation of
commands, promises, and laws envisaged as having their origins ultimately
in the ‘word’ of God.

A Bible-based theology was open to the latent objection that the text or
texts in question can have no self-evident status as revelation, except in a
circular argument, and indeed that the very notion of ‘revelation’ or textual
‘inspiration’ has no coherent credibility. There can also be particular
rejections of the alleged significance or interpretation of specific elements
of the sacred texts in question, whether inconsistencies of narrative or
irreconcilable facets of the ‘characters’ depicted. For example, the Yahweh
in the Old Testament is presented as both vengefully vindictive and
mercifully generous, as both faithful to his promises and apparently
arbitrary in his choices. Much apologetic argument was concerned with
reconciling such apparently incompatible elements found within supposed
revelatory texts.

The partial assimilation or incorporation of Plato’s philosophical
positions into the early formulations of Christian belief during the first few
centuries had often resulted in a theology explicitly deploying Neoplatonic
terms and arguments, but without systematically validating or defending
those positions. Platonic philosophy was often embedded or entangled in
the very way of thinking theologically.

60

By contrast, there is a kind of division or two-stage order implicit in
Anselm’s Monologion : that one could assemble clear rational arguments to
demonstrate the truth, or at least the plausibility, of basic theistic beliefs,
prior to and separate from a subsequent consideration of the specifically
Christian or theological components of that belief system. Anselm can be
seen as deploying an implied distinction between ‘philosophy’ and
‘theology’ which was eventually to become the basis for the standard
pedagogical programme in which candidates for the priesthood would first
study philosophy and then theology. Anselm’s method is thus part of a
wider shift in the professionalisation of thinking itself.

Anselm has too frequently been called the ‘godfather of scholasticism’,
but the epithet does indicate that in the century or so after his death in 1109
significant changes occurred in the ways in which theological ‘thinking’ was
conducted and organised, with an increasing emphasis upon rigorous and
systematic argument. Anselm did not himself provide an articulated
overview of his own theology, but in composing his several separate
treatises he was concerned to draw his readers attention to the
interconnections between his writings, so that, for example, the argument in
the treatise on the procession of the Holy Spirit was to be recognised as
interdependent with the arguments in the treatise on the Incarnation, while
the treatise on the fall of the devil was the contextual framework for the
dialogue on ‘why God became man’ and the treatises on the Blessed Virgin.

In the preface to his treatise on truth, Anselm had written (Schmitt, I,
p.174):

Although obviously these treatises do not cohere by any continuity of
composition, the matter dealt with and their similarity of argumentative
mode required that they be written in the order that I have presented
them. Therefore, although they were impatiently transcribed by some in
a different order before they were completed, I wish them to be
ordered as I have now arranged them.

Anselm insists upon following an overall order for the reading of his
vatious separate treatises because the arguments within them constitute a
sequence, just as the arguments within the Mono/ogion constituted a ‘chain’
of linked steps. Anselm’s insistence that his writings be read in a certain
sequence became a feature of the ‘standard model’ of scholasticism. Indeed,
the first systematic exposition of overall theological argument composed
explicitly for the use of ordinary clergy may possibly have been prompted
by Anselm himself. The E/ucidarium composed by Honorius of Autun
seems to have been written under the direct aegis of Anselm, and perhaps
also of Eadmer, at Canterbury in the late 1090s or early 1100s. It was
translated not only into Anglo-Saxon but also French and German and
some 300 surviving manuscripts attest to its widespread popularity and
likely influence.

From the first part of the 12th century we can see a shift in the location
of theological thinking from the practices of the preacher and the ambience
of the monastery towards that of the university. As it happens, another
figure associated with Canterbury has left an account of those
developments. The Me/a/ogicon of John of Salisbury is partly a memoir of his
twelve years or so from 1136 attending lectures in Chartres and Paris and
includes an account of the increased emphasis on the teaching of logic. He
was subsequently attached to the households of consecutive archbishops of
Canterbury, first Theobald and then Becket, and wrote a Life of Anselm,
commissioned by Becket to support attempts to have Anselm canonized.
John’s Metalogicon was probably written at Canterbury in about 1159, fifty
years after Anselm’s death, and its title echoes those of Anselm himself.
One can suppose that John too once knelt before a tomb or altar dedicated
to Anselm and perhaps even read Anselm’s works in his own autograph
manuscripts then still in the monastic library at Canterbury.

three texts

From the 1120s there gradually emerged a composite text, which was not a
single volume by a single author, but a gradually expanding and eventually
enormous assemblage of quotations from an impressive range of authorities
and commentators, a compilation known as the Gossa ordinaria or ordinary
(ordered) gloss on the Bible. Modelled partly on the layout of manuscripts
of Roman law or the Hebrew Bible, the G/ossa was designed so that on each
page a few lines from the consecutive biblical text were presented in a
central panel which was densely surrounded by quotations excerpted from
numerous different sources and offering different kinds of ‘gloss’ upon the
selected brief unit of text. The complete text of each book of the Bible
could be arranged continuously in this way and provided exactly the kind of
compilation which Lanfranc would have welcomed as an essential tool for
finding and citing the ‘authorities’ he had requested Anselm to provide in
the Monologion. There is some evidence that the exiled Thomas Becket later
commissioned some of the more elaborate extant manuscripts of the whole
work.

There was no attempt within the G/ossa itself to reconcile potentially
incompatible glosses upon each short biblical passage, but the complex
format stimulated the elaboration of different modes of interpretation of
each excerpt according to the traditional patristic four ‘senses’ now often
conveniently arranged together on the same page. The literal, allegorical,
tropological and anagogical senses can be briefly indicated in a well-known
doggerel:

64

Lettera gesta docet, quid credas allegoria,
Moralia quid agas, quo tendas, anagogia.

The literal teaches what happened,

the allegorical, what you should believe,
the moral, what you should do,

the anagogical, where you are going.

The complete G/ossa is not easy to find or to read in print nowadays,
though it was extensively copied and re-copied in manuscript and would
have been in most monastic libraries either partially or in full, but the
complex format made it difficult to print. After his third edition of the glossa
was printed by Froben in 1508 the six huge volumes were supplemented by
one entirely given over to an index of the complete work. Subsequent
printers were so deeply reluctant to have to re-paginate the complex Index
references that the page layout and numbering was preserved exactly
through later editions up to 1617. The standard 19th-century collection the
Patrologia Latina includes the G/ossa but only in an extremely poorly arranged
late version and misleadingly attributes it to a single author, Anselm of
Laon. Very little of the G/ossa has yet been translated into English and it
would still be a formidable design and printing project while an online
critical digital edition would have its own problems.

Reading multiple juxtaposed glosses could make disparities between them
more clearly problematic as requiring reconciliation. In the late 1120s Peter
Abelard composed his Sv et Non, a title translateable as “Yes & No’ or ‘So
and Not So’. The text consisted of over a hundred and fifty paired groups
of statements on specific topics with each of the ‘chapters’ comprising a
collage of opposing authoritative statements, culled from various approved
Church Fathers, from classical writers, and from the Biblical text itself.
These contrasting and apparently incompatible juxtapositions posed the
question as to which source or authority was correct, this one or that, yes or
no? The title and the overall arrangement indicated that the answer might
sometimes be both yes and no, Sic eż Non.

Abelard’s proem to his compilation recalls Lanfranc’s objections to
Anselm’s Monologion, and in effect poses a direct riposte to Lanfranc’s
position (I am gratefully indebted to the 2007 self-published translation by
the late indefatigable Priscilla Throop):

There ate many seeming contradictions and even obscurities in the
innumerable writings of the church fathers. Our respect for their
authority should not stand in the way of an effort on our part to come
at the truth. The obscurity and contradictions in ancient writings may
be explained upon many grounds, and may be discussed without
impugning the good faith and insight of the fathers . . even the best

65

authors often introduce the erroneous views of others and leave the
reader to distinguish between the true and the false. Sometimes, as
Augustine confesses in his own case, the fathers ventured to rely upon
the opinions of others. . . All writings belonging to this class are to be
read with full freedom to criticize, and with no obligation to accept
unquestioningly; otherwise the way would be blocked to all discussion,
and posterity be deprived of the excellent intellectual exercise of
debating difficult questions of language and presentation.

Abelard adds:

In view of these considerations, I have ventured to bring together
various dicta of the holy fathers, as they came to mind, and to formulate
certain questions, which were suggested by the seeming contradictions
in the statements. These questions ought to serve to excite tender
readers to a zealous inquiry into truth and so sharpen their wits.

Abelard’s ‘chapter’ headings for these arrangements of conflicting

How the reader or student was to ‘reach the truth’ and decide between
the contesting authorities was left open by Abelard. He nowhere in the Sv et
non indicated how a ‘proof could adjudicate between contradictory
positions or arguments. Most radically, Abelard’s text implies that a
proposition or even a doctrinal position might be irreconcilable with
another, that a proposition might be both true and false, and may even pose
the possibility of two distinct truths, one philosophical and the other
theological, a position which was later explicitly condemned as heretical.

Significantly, the new ‘Master’ of thinking was now deemed to be
Aristotle, whose basic writings on logic had long been taught as part of the
traditional educational curriculum, but whose other texts were soon to
become increasingly available. As with Abelard’s other work the Sz et non
was not wholly reputable (though we know that Christ Church library had a
copy), but it may have prompted the need to investigate more rigorously
what contrasting propositions might mean if understood in the context in
which they were constructed.

A third innovative and influential work signalled a further development in
how theology was to be studied and taught. Augustine and others had
written a variety of theological treatises upon a range of Christian doctrines,
and Augustine had composed a kind of overall summary, the Enchiridion,
but much of the doctrinal teaching of the Church Fathers was to be found
primarily in separate polemical treatises and scattered in sermons or
extensive commentaries on specific biblical texts. Peter Lombard’s Book of
Sentences which dates from the 1150s offered a different model.

Like the G/ossa and the Sve et non it consisted largely of quotations, not
arranged to highlight local opposing positions but organised according to
an overall treatment of the whole range of Christian doctrine. Like
Abelard’s, Lombard’s text takes the form of a series of ‘questions’ but now
more systematically ordered to constitute a chain of linked arguments
orchestrating relations between individual ‘questions’. In addition to
outlining arguments for and against a particular position Lombard’s
structure provided for an explicit adjudication between opposing positions,
with a section called the Responsio in each quaestio giving his own solution to
the various questions posed.

This attempt at overall organisation was in effect combined with the
approach suggested by Abelard’s Sve e¢ non, but so that each division of
Lombard’s Sentences is presented in the form of ‘questions’ arranged for
discussion in a standard format. The various positions relevant to the
‘question’ are first surveyed by assembling both supporting and opposing
quotations and authorities, but then the Responsio offers a concise argument
supporting the preferred conclusion, and this is followed by a brief
refutation of each of those previously considered positions which are now
to be rejected. The resolution of the contradictions or incompatibilities is
provided predominantly by reasoned arguments rather than by simply
selecting a preferred authority. The impression given by the complex
organisation of the Sentences is of systematically following through a
rationally constructed overall ‘chain’ of arguments, as Anselm had regarded
his Monologion as providing, but now encompassing the whole span of
christian doctrines.

So influential was Lombard’s way of organising his connections between
the multiple components of theological inquiry that every aspiring
university teacher of theology was eventually required to provide his own
commentary upon the Sentences by delivering a series of lectures more or less
following the order and arrangement of Lombard’s text. After that
apprenticeship a lecturer might attempt to provide his own overall
presentation of the scope of christian theology, a summa.

The culminating monument in this genre was Aquinas’s great Summa
theologiae, composed between 1266 and 1273 and comprising more than fifty
volumes in the standard English translation. The Summa combined and
orchestrated the crucial elements of the three texts: the deployment of an
extensive range of scriptural and other authorities, juxtaposed in pro and
contra atguments within a series of questions, and together comprising an
overarching systematicity.

For example, in the same Quaestio as the discussion of Anselm’s
‘ontological argument’, partly quoted earlier, Aquinas offered his own
version of proofs for the existence of God, the famous ‘five ways’. After
considering a variety of authorities and arguments for and against the
proposition being debated in the question ‘Whether God exists?’, Aquinas
provided his responsio (ST I, Q 2, article 3):

I answer that: The existence of God can be proved in five ways.

The first and more manifest way is the argument from motion [change].
It is certain, and evident to our senses, that in the world some things
are in motion. Now whatever is in motion is put in motion by another,
for nothing can be in motion except it is in potentiality to that towards
which it is in motion; whereas a thing moves inasmuch as it is in act.
For motion is nothing else than the reduction of something from
potentiality to actuality. But nothing can be reduced from potentiality
to actuality, except by something in a state of actuality. Thus that which
is actually hot, as fire, makes wood, which is potentially hot, to be
actually hot, and thereby moves and changes it. Now it is not possible
that the same thing should be at once in actuality and potentiality in the
same respect, but only in different respects. For what is actually hot
cannot simultaneously be potentially hot; but it is simultaneously
potentially cold. It is therefore impossible that in the same respect and
in the same way a thing should be both mover and moved, i.e. that it
should move itself. Therefore, whatever is in motion must be put in
motion by another. If that by which it is put in motion be itself put in
motion, then this also must needs to be put in motion by another, and
that by another again. But this cannot go on to infinity, because then
there would be no first mover, and, consequently, no other mover;
seeing that subsequent movers move only inasmuch as they are put in
motion by the first mover; as the staff moves only because it is put in
motion by the hand. Therefore it is necessary to arrive at a first mover,
put in motion by no other; and this everyone understands to be God.
(Ergo necesse est devenire ad aliquod primum movens, quod a nullo movetur: et hoc
omnes intelligunt Deum.)

The argument is offered as strictly philosophical or rational, not
dependent upon quoting biblical texts or citing authoritative predecessors,
though utilising distinctions and terms derived from Aristotle, including the
key concept of ‘motion’ as any form of change. Any riposte or refutation
would therefore also have to be at the level of logical counter-argument, as
also a in the following further argument:

The second way is from the nature of the efficient cause. In the world

of sense we find there is an order of efficient causes. There is no case
known (neither is it, indeed, possible) in which a thing is found to be

69

the efficient cause of itself; for so it would be prior to itself, which is
impossible. Now in efficient causes it is not possible to go on to
infinity, because in all efficient causes following in order, the first is the
cause of the intermediate cause, and the intermediate is the cause of the
ultimate cause, whether the intermediate cause be several, or only one.
Now to take away the cause is to take away the effect. Therefore, if
there be no first cause among efficient causes, there will be no ultimate,
nor any intermediate cause. But if in efficient causes it is possible to go
on to infinity, there will be no first efficient cause, neither will there be
an ultimate effect, nor any intermediate efficient causes; all of which is
plainly false. Therefore it is necessary to admit a first efficient cause, to
which everyone gives the name of God. (Ergo est necesse ponere aliquam
causam efficientem primam: quam omnes Deum nominant.)

Aquinas characteristically constructs what are effectively concise
syllogistic arguments, sequences of propositions which taken together can
be offered as supporting an unavoidable conclusion. The argument from
‘motion’ concludes with the assertion that the first mover is ‘what everyone
understands to be God’, while in the argument from efficient causes the
first efficient cause is that ‘to which everyone gives the name of God’ —
claims about understanding and linguistic usage which are not themselves
strictly part of the logical conclusion.

Aquinas’s mode of argument in these passages is obviously not that of a
commentary on the Bible or a patristic sermon, though he also composed
works in both genres, and throughout the Summa the ‘questions’ he
discussed were often at least indirectly derived from the Bible and the
Fathers. Here his argument is clearly influenced in its focus and terminology
by Aristotle (motion is nothing else than the reduction of something from
potentiality to actuality’, ‘efficient cause’, etc.) and we might want simply to
add Aristotle’s works as a fourth ‘text’ in the 12th-century transformation
of university teaching. The relation to Aristotle was however considerably
more complex, as we will see.

The Philosopher

Taken together, the three texts outlined could be regarded as constituting a
three-stage process, a sequence of knowledge acquisition and intellectual
decision-making: the G/ossa as initial data-gathering or accumulative input,
the Sz et non as preliminary organisation and weighing of evidences, the
quaestio and its dialogical structure as actively generating decisions and
judgements. The primary material forming the basis for this procedure for
thinking remained the Bible itself and it is sometimes overlooked how even
the most abstract Scholastic arguments generally took a biblical text as their
starting point. But the increasing availability of Aristotle’s work provided an
impressive example of precisely such a co-ordinated procedure in action,

70

and one in which the primary material discussed was not biblical texts but
largely generated from within the scope of Aristotle’s oeuvre itself,
especially as its full range became more accessible.

If one reads through the collected works of Aristotle in, say, the Oxford
twelve-volume translation there can be a sense of moving within an
interrelated system of inquiries which taken together almost constitute a
circular loop, from the preliminary text-books on logic to the partly
fragmented architecture of the ‘metaphysics’. Within the overall corpus
there is a range of treatises which primarily comprise ‘data gathering’,
bodies of empirical investigations organised into constructed classifications,
ranging from the diversity of animal species to the differences between
political constitutions, from the multiple devices of rhetoric to the
characteristics of successful drama, from the phenomena of meteorology to
the multifarious behaviour of human beings. And each inquiry is situated
with reference to related or ancillary investigations which underpin or
derive from each treatise in turn, including for example the complex
trajectory which can take us from the analysis of spatio-temporal terms in
the Physics to the conceptual apparatus of the Metaphysics which returns us
again to the foundational logic of the Organon.

Aquinas and many of his generation of professional university
philosophers and theologians approached Aristotle’s work precisely as such
a system or network of linked treatises, and they collectively devoted very
detailed commentaries to each of the Aristotelian texts while constantly
cross-referring in their own work to the complex spectrum of the overall
Aristotle corpus and to the many other commentators upon it.

The established practice of biblical reconciliation and cross-linking was
increasingly applied to this secular body of work, but when the dialectical
approach of the Sz et non was applied to the Bible it primarily involved
reconciling particular texts, whereas when applied to Aristotle it
necessitated trying to reconcile overall Christian doctrine with the whole of
Aristotle, which also involved negotiating a challenge to the Platonic
philosophical elements already incorporated into some traditional Christian
thinking and theology.

All this in a historical context partly shaped by the direct and indirect
impact of a non-Christian religious belief system, the Islamic culture which
was itself the source of transmission for the Aristotelian texts. Which
thereby involved not only changes in pedagogical procedures for thinking
but raised issues concerning the very nature of thinking itself. The first
quaestio in Aquinas’s Summa theologiae is whether theology is a ‘science’. In
Aristotle’s terms the fundamental data or input for a rigorous ‘science’
cannot be simply what he termed endoxa, general ‘common sense’ or ‘public
opinion’, but requires reliable and rigorous examination, including
appropriate empirical investigation. It would not be adequate simply to
assert that the first mover is ‘what everyone understands to be God’ and
Aquinas’s argument for the first mover or cause partly depended upon a

conceptual engagement with Aristotle’s treatise on ‘physics’ as well as his
‘meta-physics’.

Reliance on a purely logical premise such as Anselm’s argument would
certainly not suffice. Though later somewhat discredited as mere
scholasticism, the procedures for ‘thinking’ established in the 12th century
created a basic model which has arguably remained paradigmatic until the
potential shift indicated by recent developments in what is now called
‘artificial intelligence’.

At this point I want to consider in some considerable detail one Aristotle
text in particular, normally referred to as the De anima, which arguably still
profoundly influences any attempt at thinking about thinking, including
‘artificial’ thinking. If the three paradigmatic texts constituted a procedure
ot method for thinking they did not clarify what was doing the thinking.

anima

De anima is the Latin translation of Peri psyche, usually further translated into
English as On The Soul, a title which suggests a primarily philosophical or
even metaphysical treatise. We earlier registered Anselm’s own use of the
term ‘soul’ in his letters and we might too readily think that we understand
what Anselm and the conventional usage of the term ‘soul’ means. In
English it is generally taken to refer to some kind of ‘spiritual’ entity
responsible for individual human identity, and many would today take for
granted this notion of ‘soul’ even if they are unable to give a coherent
account of what the term means. If there is one.

However, for some mediaeval Aristotelians the appearance of Aristotle’s
Peri psyche in a number of Latin translations, with the first version appearing
shortly after Anselm’s death, made the traditional Christian notions of ‘soul’
more problematic by providing an alternative approach from Plato’s and
one less easily amenable to the everyday language of received Christian
beliefs.

Moreover, the De anima is arguably not itself a fully consistent or coherent
text. It is worth noting that Aristotle’s Greek text had been variously
translated into Syriac, Persian, and Arabic, before it was made available in
Latin. There were three main Latin translations, of which one was from the
Arabic rather than the Greek, and there were several major Islamic
commentaries on the text also available in different Latin versions.

The Greek term psyche was normally translated into Latin as ‘anima to
indicate what ‘animates’, what gives life, what makes something alive. But to
translate ‘anima into modern English as ‘soul’ implies that when Aristotle
says that a living plant or an insect has psyche he is supposedly claiming that
a plant has some kind of ‘spirit’ responsible for its individual identity.

Aristotle’s text is however arguably more appropriately regarded as
contributing to a treatise in natural history and the title ‘concerning psyche’
could almost be translated simply as ‘on life’ since the text was probably

72

one of a series of treatises or lectures on what we would now call biology
or zoology, including works with titles such as ‘parts of animals’, ‘history of
animals’, ‘generation of animals’, etc. Anyone who regards Aristotle
primarily as an abstract philosopher should browse through the several
substantial volumes on ‘animals’ where he examines in considerable detail
some five hundred species of living beings, with much of the treatment
based upon his own practical fieldwork, particularly in what we would now
call marine biology. I particularly recommend the dissection of a cuttlefish
treated in some detail. Armand Marie Leroi’s study The Lagoon: How
Aristotle Invented Science (2014) is an excellent introduction.

The De anima treatise is concerned with living beings as distinct from
non-living objects, which were to be considered elsewhere, in the Physics
and related texts. Surveying and summarising the accumulated data from
empirical examinations of animal species, Aristotle outlined a classificatory
schema whereby different kinds of living things are categorised according to
their capacities, from the most basic species of ‘living’ creature which is able
simply to feed and to reproduce itself, through more complex ‘animals’
which are able in addition to move of their own accord, to the human
species which adds to these several capacities an ability also to ‘think’ or
‘reason’, though such English terms cover a range of meanings.

In Aristotle’s classification what differentiates the human psyche from
other animal life is in part the human ‘intellect’ as a function or activity of
the specifically human form of animal life. But Aristotle also readily
characterised some non-human animals as behaving more or less
‘intelligently’ in their own way. He also considered whether the human
intellect may be similar to the workings of those non-corporeal entities
which included the ‘gods’ and the apparently ‘intelligent’ agencies which
directed the systematic processes of the heavens and governed the regularly
ordered motions of the planets. Mediaeval thinkers tended to include the
notion of ‘angels’ in this category of intelligent but non-human agents.
Richard Feynman once remarked that the hypothesis of intelligent ‘angels’
controlling the movements of the planets was not necessarily misleading,
given that we still don’t fundamentally know how ‘gravity’ operates.

However, Aristotle would not have classified any physical machine as a
living animal, in so far as it cannot feed itself or reproduce itself in the way
that even the most primitive living beings can, and therefore in Aristotle’s
schema a computer or ‘artificial intelligence’ would necessarily be assigned
to a different category from humans. If are we translate psyche into English
as ‘soul’ it seems obviously inappropriate to regard a machine, a computer,
an AI, as having a ‘soul’. Yet as we shall see, the discussion in the De anima
has cast a long shadow over such contemporary developments and issues.

In particular, one effect of Descartes’ later categorising of ‘thinking’ as
the exclusive activity of an immaterial ‘soul’ was to re-draw the distinction
between humans and other living beings and to re-classify non-human
‘animals’ as in effect mechanisms. Aristotle’s distinction between in-animate

modified by Descartes’s different classification of animals as wholly material
and distinct from both humans and angels. Which was to cause problems

simplifications

Allow me some drastic simplifications. In the overall Aristotelian schema an
entity, whether animate or inanimate, is regarded as constituted by what is
termed ‘form’ and ‘matter’ (as we earlier considered in relation to Aristotle’s
thinking about ‘economics’). The term ‘material’ is itself problematic. Put
briefly, ‘matter’ (bàn, Aude) is what can potentially be comprised or
constituted in any specific entity, and should not be equated simply with
physical matter, though in many contexts the term can be almost taken to
mean the physical material of which an entity is made. This constitutive
‘matter’ was sometimes designated in mediaeval Latin as materia scripta and
distinguished from ‘primary matter’, which was arguably what we would
now call ‘energy’, that most elementary ‘matter’ which figures in Einstein’s
E = mc?. Aristotle would undoubtedly have been intrigued by such recent
notions as ‘dark matter’ or ‘dark energy’, as possible topics to consider in
his ‘physics’ lectures.

The complementary term ‘form’ is notoriously more difficult to gloss.
The Greek words which are now normally translated as ‘form’ include ‘eidos
(e500) which can be taken to mean an idea, image or concept, and ‘morphe
(uoeyy) which is closer to such English words as shape, structure,
organisation, arrangement, or even design. A ‘form’ can be regarded as what
‘gives’ some constitutive matter the shape, structure, or arrangement which
makes the resulting composite of form and matter into what that entity is.
In its simplest sense, for example, a statue might be made of bronze as its
‘matter’ while its ‘form’ or shape is what makes it recognisably a statue of,
say, Venus. Another statue might be made from the same bronze which has
been melted down and recast into a different shape, as a figure of Zeus.
Another statue made of marble might have the same ‘Venus’ form but its
matter would make it a different statue from the bronze Venus. In a series
of mass-produced identical copies of bronze ‘Venus’ statues each has the
same ‘form’ but is individualised by the different physical bronze or other
physical matter in each instance, its own materia scripta. However, while a
boat made of wood, fibreglass, or metal might have a shape which makes it
look like a boat, a lump of granite shaped as a boat would not work as a
boat (a sea-sick Empress of China commissioned a ‘boat’ made of stone
which was permanently ‘moored’ on the Imperial Lake). A cathedral can
incorporate or embody an overall architectural ‘plan’ though its precise
overall form may be the result of several overlapping and co-ordinated
designs, with the whole building constructed of multiple different materials.

74

And so on. The basic terminology is highly flexible. I can even appropriate
some elements of the ‘form’ of a cathedral for the design of this book.

These obvious examples, however, concern objects made by humans, and
their shape or arrangement, their ‘form’ or ‘formal cause’, can be attributed
to their maker or designer, whose intention in making the object also
provides a ‘final cause’, the end or aim for which the object is made. This is
to think in terms of the implementation of an ‘idea’ which is ‘realised’ in the
material object, as an ‘artificial’ or ‘synthetic’ (guvOntixoc) entity. In some
respects, the ‘idea’ of a human-made object can be envisaged in terms of a
plan, sketch, or outline, though the ‘form’ is not itself ‘matter’, even when
depicted as a diagram drawn on material paper or displayed on a digital
screen.

However, what of ‘natural’ zv-animate material objects such as rocks or
stones, mountains and rivers, which might have been given their present
shape or ‘form’ not by any human or intentional intervention (though
human input may partly contribute) but by a range of natural forces acting
upon the material, a result partly produced by the physical impact of wind,
rain, sea, etc., which act without any intentional ‘final cause’, though
resulting in a specific combination of matter and form, as this rock, this
lagoon, this island.

Aristotle might plausibly construe inanimate ‘form’ today in terms of
chemical processes or inter-acting molecular structures analysable by
appropriate scientific formulae and equations, such as ‘H2O” designating the
‘form’ or arrangement which constitutes water. One concern of Aristotle’s
Physics was to analyse the most basic parameters of natural objects and
processes, including such problematic core concepts as ‘change’, ‘place’,
‘time’, ‘motion’ which pose issues in what we might now call theoretical
physics. However, in most mundane contexts Aristotle’s analytical terms
have become sufficiently familiarised that ‘matter’ and ‘form’ can often be
regarded as providing an almost common-sense terminology and we rarely
stop to ask whether we actually understand their everyday use, and in
particular what precisely we mean by the ‘form’ of an object.

But then how does a distinction between matter and form relate to
animate entities, ‘living’ objects, where again there might seem to be no
architect or designer, no prior plan. In the De anima the ‘form’ of a living
entity is that which is constituted in and by the reproduction process itself,
and the ‘formal cause’ is therefore also a ‘final cause’, in the sense that what
the animal will ‘naturally’ develop into is what is implicit in its ‘form’, as
this specific kind of animal, an instance of this particular species.

The ‘form’ of a walrus is what each walrus will come to be as a walrus: its
‘form’ as a living walrus is precisely to become a walrus, to develop into
what it is to live as a walrus, and it is that specific way of being a walrus
which is reproduced by walruses and only by walruses, each generated by its
walrus progenitors.

75

In several treatises, including De partis animalibus and De generatione
animalium, Aristotle considered in impressive detail how different species
reproduce themselves, and his classificatory system once earned high praise
from Linnaeus as pioneer anatomy and zoology. He made some major
errors, for example mistakenly thinking that the evidence indicated that in
the sexual reproduction of some animals the ‘material’ was primarily
provided by the female and the ‘form’ was largely contributed by the male,
though in other animals this was not the case. But his investigations of
variously different modes of reproduction did not itself answer the question
of what makes an animal a ‘living’ entity, different from both artificially
made objects and inanimate natural things. What indeed is the psyche which
‘animates’? And are we to think of the ‘form’ of an animal as its psyche, as
what makes it alive?

The De anima argues that the ‘material’ of an animal cannot itself explain
‘life’, since the physical ‘matter’ of a recently dead animal is, initially at least,
the very same matter as that of the living animal, and therefore the
corporeal matter could not by itself make it a living animal. Moreover, to
use the Scholastic term, the materia scripta is continually replaced by different
matter, as energy or food is consumed, assimilated, and excreted, while the
‘same’ animal persists. What makes an animal living must therefore be its
‘form’, and it is the way in which the physical material is ‘formed’, or shaped
and organised, which activates or animates that particular ‘matter’, gives it
life, makes it alive as this animal. Nevertheless, without appropriate physical
matter there would be nothing to organise, to animate, and the physical
material must be such that it is able to be so animated, to be or become not
just any animal but this particular kind or species of animal. A living animal
cannot be made out of bronze or rock, and nor can an elephant be made
out of a tadpole, or an eagle give birth to a pigeon, though it is possible to
cross-breed some animals and some plants, as Aristotle and his colleague
Theophrastus registered in the experiments of farmers and gardeners.

Hence the De anima makes it the first requirement of being a living entity
that it is both able to feed on more ‘matter’ and to reproduce itself, its own
form of matter, to become able to make a new entire plant or animal of its
own species. In the act of reproduction it is the living parent(s) which
transmit or generate a potentially living animal, both the ‘matter’ which will
increase by its feeding on other matter and the ‘form’ which continues to
structure that living process. When an animal dies the inanimate matter
remains which, without its animating form, putrifies. The process of
‘putrefaction’ is discussed in the treatise Merereologica (Bk IV, 1) concerned
with various basic physical processes. Aristotle mistakenly believed that
putrefying dead matter could sometimes spontaneously generate very
primitive forms of life, such as maggots, an hypothesis not experimentally
disproven until the 17th century.

One interpretation of the De anima postulated that different living
capacities were acquired cumulatively or progressively, in that an entity after

76

conception might successively grow into a sequence of additional ‘forms’,
with an embryo changing through different stages of transformation from a
vegetable form of life to an animal form. It was debated in some mediaeval
treatises whether this meant that the human embryo began its existence in
the womb as not even a ‘vegetable’ form of life, but only as ‘potentially
alive’, since it was able to nourish itself before birth only in complete
dependence upon its parent, while the growing embryo gradually acquired
more capacities such as sensation and movement. This account envisaged a
sequence of ‘life-forms’ supplementing or replacing each other, as the
butterfly form might be regarded as replacing the caterpillar form,
apparently trans-forming itself into a quite different corporeal organisation.
For some thinkers an entity retained, and was finally composed of, a
‘plurality of forms’. But this still did not clarify quite what a ‘form’ of ‘life’
is, or in what sense psyche is a cause of life.

In a kind of analogue, Aristotle perhaps surprisingly argues (412b 19) that
if the eye were itself a complete living animal its psyche, regarded as its
formal or final cause, would be ‘sight’. Sight is a ‘potential’ of the eye, and
the possibility of seeing is provided or enabled by its shape, its arrangement,
its form. What has to be precisely arranged in a living eye is (in modern
terms) the organisation of the lens, the retina, the optic nerve, as contained
within the overall construction of the eyeball, and it is crucial to the
operation of an eye that these components are in a precise relationship to
each other.

It used to be the practice in English secondary schools that every pupil
was obliged to dissect and analytically describe the eye of a dead cow.
However, since a deceased ‘cow’ is now only ‘meat’, a dead eye is not an
‘eye’ of a cow though we can still refer to it equivocally as such. A dead eye
no longer sees, even if light can still be shone onto the retina. Nor does a
closed eye see, though dreams or memories can produce ‘phantastmata’.
The potential or capacity for sight has to be activated (‘animated’) by the
actual action of seeing, and such seeing is not an activity of the eye alone
but of the body as a whole, since the eye is not in fact an independent
animal but a part of a whole animal. For seeing to occur, the physical eye
has to be connected to the rest of the body through the optic nerves,
various muscles, and so on, while there also has to be something to be seen
and an appropriate medium in which to see. Physical damage or loss of eyes
can make the whole animal blind, just as what ‘happens’ at death involves a
loss of the entire living ‘form’ (psyche) while the remaining ‘matter’, the non-
living corpse, is now shaped by a different form or forms, as physical
processes of decay and dissolution trans-form the material remains into
other compounds. When a living individual animal dies the material body
no longer has the ‘form’ of a living being, any more than a dead eye is able
to see.

though somewhat abstract way of thinking about the ‘animate’ as distinct

search and we still have no scientific consensus as to the origin of life even

‘substantial form’

Aristotle’s own approach to a tentative answer to ‘what is life’ arguably
leads to an impasse, but one which still has ramifications. One attempted
solution to these issues was the notion of the psyche or anima as itself a
‘substantial form’, that the anima is both a form and also itself a subsistent
entity, a ‘substance’, even though in Aristotle’s normal use of these terms

and a form has to be the form of some matter, does the anima therefore
somehow have its own immaterial ‘matter’ distinct from the matter of the
compound object of which it is the form.

knowing & understanding

Which takes us back to the primary concern guiding this overall exploration
prompted by thinking about Anselm. The several complexities concerning
anima and form were directly entangled with the issue of ‘thinking’.
Aristotle’s notion of ‘intellect’, he says, again relates analogically to that of
sight, since in his view the act of seeing involves the transmission to the eye
not the ‘matter’ of the seen object, but its ‘form’, an appearance, a shape, an
image. In Aquinas’s Latin terminology, what reaches the eye is a ‘visible

81

species’, here using the term ‘species’ as in Pecham’s optics, not in the
biological sense.

Similarly, the process of ‘knowing’ involves not the assimilation of the
‘matter’ of what is to be understood but the form of it, with the notion of
an ‘intelligible species’ as analytically comparable to a ‘visible species’. Put
simply, ‘understanding’ initially involves grasping or assimilating not the
‘matter’ but the form, structure, or organisation, of an object of knowledge,
an ‘idea’ (eidos — a term which can refer to both sight and understanding). In
this approach, a capacity for ‘thinking’ comprises a structure of different
stages or procedures, from acquiring knowledge, primarily or ultimately
from the senses, then through the systematisation and organisation of that
knowledge, by the selection, ordering, and arrangement of the abstracted
‘intelligible species’, upon the basis of which suppositions, hypotheses, and
judgements can then be generated, and a ‘thought’ or act of understanding
can be produced, or an assertion can be made.

Not surprisingly, a mediaeval model of these stages or functions of the
‘intellect? closely matched the structured procedures of the Scholastic
quaestio and directly echoes the relation of the three texts in the academic
curriculum as outlined earlier: the G/ossa as initial data-gathering or input,
the Sic et non as preliminary organisation and weighing of evidences, the
dialogical structure of the quaestio as the active intellect, generating decisions
and conclusions.

Moreover, the ‘content’ or, in a different sense, ‘matter’ of a text is
arguably itself ‘non-material’ since there can be different material
instantiations of its ‘form’, for example as reproduced or copied in a spoken
lecture, a dictated script, a manuscript, or memorised ‘in the mind’. As
those prolific writers Aristotle and Aquinas knew very well, an ‘idea’, its
structure, its form, however complex, could be realised and repeated in
different material embodiments.

Since a ‘thought’ on this account is itself non-material, ‘the intellect’ for
mediaeval Aristotelians is not physically located in the same way as a
physical organ is. An ‘intelligible species’ is not necessarily contained within
a specific corporeal location since a ‘content’ can reside not only ‘in’ a text
or in memory but ‘in’ a library, or in today’s terms ‘in’ a database, the cloud,
an external hard drive, and so on, since ‘it? can now be trans-formed even
into a series of digital ones and zeros, as ‘in-form-ation’. Notions of
thinking can change with new technologies or forms of transmission,
storage, and reproduction. But a question for Aristotle or Aquinas about
such technologies would have been whether an in-animate entity (a
computer) could extract the ‘intelligible form’ of an object of inquiry yet
nevertheless would not itself be characterised as ‘knowing’ or understanding
that object.

To put the same point in a slightly different way, for Aristotle and
Aquinas the process of ‘understanding’ itself constitutes a sequential
formation of ‘forms’, not the physical assimilation of a material object.

82

Even if the senses provide the initial input, through registering the impact
of material processes upon them, what intervenes between that physical
input and the activity of the intellect is the ‘intelligible species’, usually also
including ‘phantastmata’ or imaginings generated in the process of
abstraction or de-materialisation. As seeing does not involve incorporating
into the eye the matter of the object seen, but rather the ‘form’ of what is
seen, so the process of knowing is concerned with the ‘form’ of what is to
be understood, abstracting from the compound entity that form or
‘formula’ which, like a design or diagram, is not itself intrinsically material
but an ‘idea’ analytically separable or abstractable from the ‘matter’ of the
entity which is to be known. (This of course allows intermediate processes,
including technological aids which transmit sensual or sensible information
or enhance our own senses, or store and circulate intelligible species. The
model is not solely of an individual’s processes.)

Yet that is only an opening step in a more complex process of
‘understanding’. One indicative problem, for example, which we earlier
considered in relation to iconoclasm, would be how far the intellect can
‘understand’ a concept of ‘God’ without recourse to an ‘image’ of God
(perhaps think here of Anselm’s comment on painting), or in relation to a
more recent dilemma, whether comprehending quantum theory can
dispense entirely with any form of visualisation, as in variants of the
Copenhagen interpretation.

The implication for Aristotle was that ‘the intellect’ is not similar to the
physical sensory organs and is not itself physically material, neither made of
matter nor itself a repository of matter, and since it is the ‘forms’ of objects
which the intellect ‘grasps’ or ‘contains’, not the matter of which they are
composed, the human anima is therefore itself characterised by a certain
mode of ‘non-materiality’.

The specifically human capacity for ‘thinking’ is therefore not ‘located’
corporeally in the way that sensory capacities are related to specific physical
organs, such as sight in relation to the eye or hearing to the ear. This does
not preclude there being a particular and necessary role to be played by the
brain or the ‘heart’ or the ‘mind’ but indicates that thinking, like actually
seeing, is an activity of the animal as a whole, since the specifically human
anima animates the whole human including the bodily sense organs. Or, in
the compact phrase used earlier, ‘the body is clearly not the soul’s actuality,
but rather the soul is the body’s actuality’. Even though, to quote a well
known colophon on a manuscript: “Though it is only the thumb and finger
which writes, it is the whole body which aches.’

Various problem then arise as to whether the anima is itself a compound of

‘form’ and ‘matter’, which could entail an infinite regress, and whether the
capacity of the immaterial anima to ‘know’ remains dependent nevertheless

upon the corporeal matter which provided its sensory input, or whether it is
possible to envisage the persistence of some form of non-corporeal matter,
even possibly a ‘spiritual matter’, which is independent of and can survive
the death of the body’s own corporeal matter. The Scholastic disputes
multiplied. As indeed do today’s neurological disputes.

Crucially for some mediaeval Christian thinkers, one interpretation of the
De anima was that if the psyche is itself a ‘substantial form’, this implies the
argument that the anima or ‘soul’ could therefore continue to exist without
any ongoing corporeal instantiation. Aristotle had suggested that the
particular kind of bodily senses, the eyes and ears we are familiar with, were
not essential to all forms of intelligence, as in the possible case of the gods
or perhaps the entities controlling the heavens. Nevertheless, the notion of
a human form without a living human body was, according to the usual
senses of ‘matter’ and ‘form’, almost unconceptualisable, a logical paradox.
Various commentators therefore disputed whether Aristotle himself
allowed the possibility of the post-mortem existence of an immaterial and
perhaps therefore immortal human psyche. For some present-day Christian
believers those mediaeval arguments continue.

Today, however, Aristotle himself might be most intrigued by the notion
that it is the complex arrangement or ‘form’ of the DNA double helix and
the patterns it varyingly (re)produces which enables the continuing
transmission of ‘life’, of living material. It may no longer be terminologically
appropriate to ask whether DNA is a ‘substantial form’ and we would not
describe someone’s DNA or an individual genome as a ‘soul’ in the popular
fuzzily ‘spiritual’ sense of that term, but Aristotle today might well ask
whether ‘DNA’ is itself a substance, a composite of matter and form, and
how exactly a combination of amino acids is potentially ‘alive’.

It might even be suggested that the discovery of DNA indicates very
much the Aid of answer which the De anima was trying to provide to the
question ‘what is life? Our DNA is what makes each of us a human animal
and a specific inheritance of DNA makes this body, this individual,
identifiably different from any other human. And what is crucial to the
transmission of DNA is the complex double strand, the ‘form’ into which
the components are ‘arranged’.

Aristotle might also be especially interested that it was an ‘artificial
intelligence’ (Alphafold) which managed to analyse the fiendishly complex
convoluted structures of the 20,000 or more proteins which provide the
‘matter’ of a living human body.

The argument of the De anima was primarily conceptual, and the core
relation between ‘matter’ and ‘form’ can be regarded as an analytical
differentiation or ontological distinction between material and structure, or
energy and organisation, such that no energy can exist without being
organised and no organisation can exist without energy. Which includes any
living organism. The De anima poses or formulates questions and suggests a
model without being able to provide related empirical answers. A few

apparently simple questions as ‘what is lifer’ and ‘how does life originate?’
However, even if DNA may now suggest a direction for an appropriate
solution to Aristotelian questions about ‘life’ or ‘psyche’, the issue of
‘intelligence’ remains stubbornly enigmatic. Like his many successors,
Aristotle encountered major conceptual difficulties when thinking about

material body or corporeal means of reproduction entail that each angelic
‘form’ was created as wholly unique, each individual angel its own species,
in some further sense of that term?

In particular, contrary to some interpretations of Aristotle, Aquinas

associated with the specific ‘matter’ of that unique individual of which it is
the ‘form’, so that even if the ‘anima’ survives the physical demise of the

the after-life of a disembodied ‘spirit? but the ‘resurrection’ of each
individual body.

It was therefore especially important that each human person could
somehow be individualised both with and ‘without’ their specific bodily
matter, so that ‘between’ death and the resurrection of the body each
separate individual could be conceived of as still uniquely existing. This
belief was fundamental to the notion of ‘souls’ existing as individuals in
purgatory, a doctrine which significantly underpinned the economy of, for
example, Christ Church Cathedral. And it is this core concept we find taken
for granted in the extensive use of ‘soul’ in Anselm’s letters. Anselm had
not, of course, read the De anima which was not translated into Latin until
after his death, and his conception of a ‘soul’ was in effect still traditionally
Platonist. Yet his way of ‘thinking’ indicated issues we have still not
resolved.

Descattes

The difficulties of this entire set of issues were inherited and partly
modified by the way in which Descartes reformulated the relation of
‘matter’ and ‘mind’. His considerable simplifications marked the dissolution
of an intellectual framework once partly shared by Aristotle and Aquinas,
though Descartes might himself in some respects be regarded as the last
Scholastic. Rather than trying to understand the ‘form’ of the ‘matter’ which
constitutes the human body as the structure or organisation of the
corporeal body itself, Descartes in effect inherited the highly problematic
notion of a ‘substantial form’ and conceptualised the ‘soul’ as itself a wholly
immaterial entity, a ‘substance’ essentially distinct from the body, not unlike
the notion of an angel intrinsically able to exist without a body. Descartes’
notion of the subject, the T of the cogito, was then conflated with a concept
of the ‘mind’ as immaterial and immortal. This was arguably an even less
coherent residue of the attempted mediaeval reconciliation of incompatible
Aristotelian and Christian positions. From that perspective, Descartes
signals not so much an innovation as a reversion to aspects of an older
Platonist tradition.

Following this simplified ‘Cartesian’ notion, for example, many Christians
and other religious believers today, including recent ‘pro-life’ activists or
anti-abortion legislators, would apparently insist that scientific biology is not
the appropriate framework for the explanation or even exploration of the
physical nature of human life, and particularly not for the issues they see as
involved in the status of a human embryo.

One strand in much self-declared ‘Christian’ thinking is that ‘God’
somehow ‘inserts’ an immaterial ‘soul’ into the embryo, though at what
precise stage he intervenes in its origination or development is disputed.
Such positions in effect recapitulate conclusions or suppositions traceable
in and to mediaeval arguments, but often without a corresponding attempt
at any philosophical or conceptual analysis to support the notion that, for
example, even before conception an individual ‘soul’ is somehow in effect a
separate ‘substance’ in its own right. Indeed, the material human body can
be regarded by some, in an even more quasi-Platonist mode, as that which
constrains, restricts, or even imprisons the ‘soul’. What, incidentally, was the
status of the foetus or DNA implanted at the Annunciation, if any such
Christians still believe in the incarnation.

A shift from understanding psyche as the specific organisation of a
particular biological life-form to notions of the ‘soul’ as an immaterial entity
wholly separable from the body has also considerably influenced the
formulation of some philosophical issues concerning ‘thinking’ and
‘intelligence’, generating what can seem intractable conceptual problems,
not least in relation to the so-called mind-body relation. Though thankfully
so far debates about ‘artificial intelligence’ have not yet been subjected to
neo-Christian objections.

Nevertheless, prevailing quasi-Cartesian presuppositions have arguably
fostered a framework in which some neuroscience investigations have
regarded ‘thinking’ as an inward, individual, and ‘mental’ activity, even if
terms such as ‘soul’ and ‘spirit’ are professionally avoided. For reasons
which will become clearer, I would characterise such a notion as the
‘Yorick’ model of intelligence.

Aristotle’s approach had been shaped by a ‘natural history’ framework,
partly based upon considerable empirical investigation of animal species.
Descartes’s philosophy re-drew the dividing line between the human,
regarded as essentially a thinking subject or spirit, and other ‘animals’ which
were effectively relocated into the same category as wholly material entities,
both living and inanimate. The implications of this for responses to a
development of natural history in evolutionary terms were considerable, not
least amongst Christian believers.

so that there would be no less contradiction in conceiving of a God —
that is, of a being supremely perfect, to whom existence was wanting,
that is to say, to whom there was wanting any perfection — than in
conceiving of a mountain which had no valley.

Descartes here presents himself as trying to arrive at a clear and distinct
idea of God akin to the clear and distinct idea he has of a mathematical
number or a geometrical figure. But he then hesitates:

Although, in reality, I might not be able to conceive of a God without
existence, no more than of a mountain without a valley, nevertheless, as
from the simple fact that I conceive a mountain with a valley, it does
not follow that there exists any mountain in the world, so likewise,
although I conceive God as existent, it does not follow, it seems, from
that, that God exists, for my thought does not impose any necessity on
things; and as there is nothing to prevent my imagining a winged horse,
although there is none which has wings, so I might, perhaps, be able to
attribute existence to God, although there might not be any God which
existed.

After thus offering what is effectively a version of Gaunilo’s critique of
Anselm, Descartes then presents himself as pausing, to consider whether
his thinking has somehow gone astray:

So far from this being so, it is just here under the appearance of this
objection that a sophism lies hid. For from the fact that I cannot
conceive a mountain without a valley, it does not follow that there
exists in the world any mountain or any valley, but solely that the
mountain and the valley, whether they exist or not, are inseparable
from one another. Whereas from the fact alone that I cannot conceive God
except as existent, tt follows that existence is inseparable from him, and,
consequently, that he exists in reality; not that my thought can make it
to be so, or that it can impose any necessity upon things; but on the
contrary the necessity which is in the thing itself, that is to say, “he
necessity of the existence of God, determines me to have this thought. For it is not
at my will to conceive of a God without existence, that is to say, a being
supremely perfect without a supreme perfection, as it is at my will to
conceive a horse with wings or without wings.

According to this account, is it Descartes’ own thought, his active work
of ‘thinking’, that is leading him on in this argument, or is ‘this thought’
somehow inescapable, a quasi-logical necessity, requiring only that he
somehow grasp it? And does the same therefore apply to the reader of
Descartes’ text? Must we ‘necessarily’ follow this route, this particular
sequential process of thinking, or could we, for example, have short-

89

circuited the argument by some other process, or even by a sudden
comprehension or instant insight? Descartes continues:

.. It must not be said here that it is necessarily true that I should affirm
that God exists, after I have supposed him to possess all kinds of
perfection, since existence is one of these, but that my first supposition
is not necessary, no more than it is necessary to affirm that all figures of
four sides may be inscribed in the circle, but that, supposing I had this
thought, I should be constrained to admit that the rhombus can be
inscribed there, since it is a figure of four sides, and thus I should be
constrained to admit something false. One ought not, I say, to allege
this; for although it may not be necessary that I should ever fall to thinking about
God, nevertheless, when it happens that I think upon a being first and
supreme, and draw, so to speak, the idea of him from the store-house
of mind, it is necessary that I attribute to him every sort of perfection,
although I may not go on to enumerate them all, and give attention to
each one in particular. And this necessity is sufficient to bring it about
(as soon as I recognise that I should next conclude that existence is a
perfection) that this first and supreme being exists: while, just as it is
not necessary that I ever imagine a triangle, but whenever I choose to
consider a rectilinear figure, composed solely of three angles, it is
absolutely necessary that I attribute to it all the things which serve for
the conclusion that their three angles are not greater than two right
angles, although, perhaps, I did not then consider this in particular.

Despite his emphasis upon clear and distinct ideas, the process of
thinking which Descartes here recounts is itself far from clear. The
trajectory of his thought is presented as an inward process upon which he
has to reflect but which he cannot wholly describe. If one were to ask
Descartes to clarify further what happened in those moments when, as he
says, ‘I easily persuade myself and ‘I think more attentively’, what would he
be able to say? What more detailed account could he provide? Could he
offer additional autobiographical steps in the process he is describing, or
would he arrive at a point at which he could simply claim to have suddenly
acquired a clear and distinct idea, somewhat like grasping the definition of a
triangle — and perhaps in that respect not dissimilar to Anselm’s own
assertion of a sudden illumination.

Note that Descartes claims that it was not ‘necessary’ that he should ever
‘fall to thinking about God’ at all. If however he then pursued the further
question of why he did so ‘fall to thinking’ about God, what kind of answer
would he provide? Would it involve extending his first-person account
further back to include, for example, his educational upbringing, his being
taught basic mathematics and the French and Latin languages in which he
uses the very terms ‘Dieu’ and ‘deus’? Should he indeed have also included
a consideration of his extensive reading, his many conversations, his

90

previous writings, as all variously bearing upon and contributing to his
present account and explaining why he did so ‘fall to thinking’ about God?
Thinking about the term ‘God’ may not have been ‘necessary’ — but it was
surely unavoidable for an educated philosopher in 17th-century Catholic
France. His ‘thinking’ was enmeshed within an entire culture and period.
He claims to ‘draw, so to speak, the idea of him from the store-house of
mind’ but the terms in which he proposes this position are obviously drawn
at least from the very languages in which he writes of them. So is the Latin
or French language ‘the store-house of mind’ and if so where is this
storehouse?

As is clear from these passages, however, Descartes regarded ‘thinking’
as an individual and inward process, a matter of having clear and distinct
‘ideas’, which he might formulate to some extent in language, but only to
recount his memory of his process of ‘thinking’ to his readers. This notion
of ‘clear and distinct ideas’ might be regarded as only a version of the claim
that some words can be clearly defined, so that, as with the term ‘triangle’,
he is primarily unpacking the common definition or use of a word,
including that word which figures in Aquinas’s phrase ‘what we call God’,
rather as ‘I exist’ is an unpacking of what is already included in the Latin
verb cogito, ‘I think’. Indeed, Descartes’ (in)famous ‘cogito ergo sum (I think
therefore I am) might well be regarded as itself a compressed version of an
‘ontological proof even less persuasive as an argument than Anselm’s own,
from which Descartes attempted to ‘prove’ his own existence.

Yet if ‘thinking’ is basically the activity of an individual probing their own
‘inward’ processes, then the next step in Descartes’ autobiographical
account could have been to probe further ‘inwards’, to see what was
happening ‘in’ his physical brain and how his thinking was occurring in
relation to processes ‘inside’ his individual head. This was after all a
characteristic 17th-century procedure of ‘zooming closer’ as a way of
arriving at a more complete truth about a phenomenon, as with those near-
contemporary inventions, a micro-scope enlarging a louse, as in the
Micrographia, or a telescope focusing on a distant planet. Should Descartes,
as it were, lift off the lid of his own skull and peer inside to see if he can
further describe what is happening ‘inside’ even as he ‘thinks’? However,
would any such continuation of his autobiographical account then have to
be couched in a quite different mode of writing, no longer a first-person
subjective reflection from the ‘inside’ but a third-person description from
the ‘outside’, observing and recounting the workings of his brain as an
objective process not as a subjective experience?

However, Descartes himself elsewhere draws an absolute distinction
between the body as extended, as material, and the mind as non-extended
and immaterial. If Descartes were required to fill in the descriptive gaps in
his claim that he at some point thought ‘more deeply’ or ‘more attentively’
(as in the passages above) would he eventually have to end up describing
the relationship between what for him are the irreconcilably different

91

domains of ‘the mind’ and ‘the body’? Descartes endorsed a radical
dichotomy between a non-spatial, mental and immaterial mind, and the
body as extended physical matter. The problem with this dichotomisation
of domains conceived of as separate entities is how then to connect them,
and in particular how far a ‘material’ body can cause ‘mental’ events, or vice
versa. For some of Descartes’ followers this might be posed in terms of
whether corporeal matter itself could in some sense ‘think’? Was ‘thinking
matter’ possible? (See for example Thinking Matter: Materialism in Eighteenth-
Century Britain, by John W Yolton, 1984.) But then, if the related notion of
an immaterial ‘soul’ was itself questioned or rejected, the problem for some
Christian believers was again how an individual ‘mind’ could survive when
separated from the body.

A Cartesian notion of the individual soul or mind as what constitutes our
unique identity as a ‘person’ not only still implicitly underpins widespread
‘common sense’ notions about ‘thinking’ but for much of the 19th and 20th
centuries the basic notion of ‘thinking’ operative in standard scientific
experiments often seemed to have a clear affinity with that of Descartes,
and was defined as a process essentially both inward and individual. (See for
example Nineteenth Century Origins of Neuroscientific Concepts by Edwin Clark
and L. S. Jacyna, 1987, and On the Origins of Cognitive Science by Jean-Pierre
Dupuy, 2009.) A basic Cartesian position still arguably shapes the
framework of some modern neuroscientific research, even though
neuroscientists tend to avoid such terms as ‘spirit? or ‘soul’, or even
‘immaterial’. And now the Cartesian problem of the relationship between
the mind and body has also become the problem of the relation between
‘artificial intelligence’ and computer engineering.

automata

I can here quote almost without comment a lengthy passage from
Descartes’s The Discourse on the Method of Rightly Conducting the Reason and
Seeking Truth in the Sciences, which he claims partly consists of a summary of
the comprehensive treatise which he had decided not to publish in the wake
of the condemnation of Galileo. This long account, which may be
unfamiliar enough to be interesting, at one point uses the phrase “common
sense’ (sensus communis) in a quasi-scholastic technical sense but the
argument as a whole anticipates a familiar “common sense’ position or
attitude which still obtains concerning what Descartes called automata but
today could include ‘artificial intelligence’.

In part V of the Discourse he claims: ‘I had explained all these matters in
some detail in the treatise which I formerly intended to publish’ and he
continues (as translated by John Veitch,. published in 1912; see also
Haldane and Ross, Works I, pp 116f):

92

I had expounded all these matters with sufficient minuteness in the
treatise which I formerly thought of publishing. And after these, I had
shown what must be the fabric of the nerves and muscles of the human
body to give the animal spirits contained in it the power to move the
members, as when we see heads shortly after they have been struck off
still move and bite the earth, although no longer animated; what
changes must take place in the brain to produce waking, sleep, and
dreams; how light, sounds, odours, tastes, heat, and all the other
qualities of external objects impress it with different ideas by means of
the senses; how hunger, thirst, and the other internal affections can
likewise impress upon it divers ideas; what must be understood by the
common sense (sensus communis) in which these ideas are received, by
the memory which retains them, by the fantasy which can change them
in various ways, and out of them compose new ideas, and which, by the
same means, distributing the animal spirits through the muscles, can
cause the members of such a body to move in as many different ways,
and in a manner as suited, whether to the objects that are presented to
its senses or to its internal affections, as can take place in our own case
apart from the guidance of the will.

Nor will this appear at all strange to those who are acquainted with
the variety of movements performed by the different automata, or
moving machines fabricated by human industry, and that with the help
of but few pieces compared with the great multitude of bones, muscles,
nerves, arteries, veins, and other parts that are found in the body of
each animal. Such persons will look upon this body as a machine made
by the hands of God, which is incomparably better arranged, and
adequate to movements more admirable than is any machine of human
invention.

And here I specially stayed to show that, were there such machines
exactly resembling in organs and outward form an ape or any other
irrational animal, we could have no means of knowing that they were in
any respect of a different nature from these animals; but if there were
machines bearing the image of our bodies, and capable of imitating our
actions as far as it is morally possible, there would still remain two most
certain tests whereby to know that they were not therefore really men.

Of these the first is that they could never use words or other signs
arranged in such a manner as is competent to us in order to declare our
thoughts to others: for we may easily conceive a machine to be so
constructed that it emits vocables, and even that it emits some
correspondent to the action upon it of external objects which cause a
change in its organs; for example, if touched in a particular place it may
demand what we wish to say to it; if in another it may cry out that it is
hurt, and such like; but not that it should arrange them variously so as
appositely to reply to what is said in its presence, as men of the lowest
grade of intellect can do.

93

The second test is, that although such machines might execute many
things with equal or perhaps greater perfection than any of us, they
would, without doubt, fail in certain others from which it could be
discovered that they did not act from knowledge, but solely from the
disposition of their organs: for while reason is an universal instrument
that is alike available on every occasion, these organs, on the contrary,
need a particular arrangement for each particular action; whence it must
be morally impossible that there should exist in any machine a diversity
of organs sufficient to enable it to act in all the occurrences of life, in
the way in which our reason enables us to act.

Again, by means of these two tests we may likewise know the
difference between men and brutes. For it is highly deserving of
remark, that there are no men so dull and stupid, not even idiots, as to
be incapable of joining together different words, and thereby
constructing a declaration by which to make their thoughts understood;
and that on the other hand, there is no other animal, however perfect
ot happily circumstanced, which can do the like.

Nor does this inability arise from want of organs: for we observe that
magpies and parrots can utter words like ourselves, and are yet unable
to speak as we do, that is, so as to show that they understand what they
say; in place of which men born deaf and dumb, and thus not less, but
rather more than the brutes, destitute of the organs which others use in
speaking, are in the habit of spontaneously inventing certain signs by
which they discover their thoughts to those who, being usually in their
company, have leisure to learn their language.

And this proves not only that the brutes have less reason than man,
but that they have none at all: for we see that very little is required to
enable a person to speak; and since a certain inequality of capacity is
observable among animals of the same species, as well as among men,
and since some are more capable of being instructed than others, it is
incredible that the most perfect ape or parrot of its species, should not
in this be equal to the most stupid infant of its kind, or at least to one
that was crack-brained, unless the soul of brutes were of a nature
wholly different from ours, and we ought not to confound speech with
the natural movements which indicate the passions, and can be imitated
by machines as well as manifested by animals; nor must it be thought
with certain of the ancients, that the brutes speak, although we do not
understand their language. For if such were the case, since they are
endowed with many organs analogous to ours, they could as easily
communicate their thoughts to us as to their fellows.

It is also very worthy of remark, that, though there are many animals
which manifest more industry than we in certain of their actions, the
same animals are yet observed to show none at all in many others: so
that the circumstance that they do better than we does not prove that
they are endowed with mind, for it would thence follow that they

94

possessed greater reason than any of us, and could surpass us in all
things; on the contrary, it rather proves that they are destitute of
reason, and that it is nature which acts in them according to the
disposition of their organs: thus it is seen, that a clock composed only
of wheels and weights can number the hours and measure time more
exactly than we with all our skill.

I had after this described the reasonable soul, and shown that it could
by no means be educed from the power of matter, as the other things
of which I had spoken, but that it must be expressly created; and that it
is not sufficient that it be lodged in the human body exactly like a pilot
in a ship, unless perhaps to move its members, but that it is necessary
for it to be joined and united more closely to the body, in order to have
sensations and appetites similar to ours, and thus constitute a real man.

I here entered, in conclusion, upon the subject of the soul at
considerable length, because it is of the greatest moment: for after the
error of those who deny the existence of God, an error which I think I
have already sufficiently refuted, there is none that is more powerful in
leading feeble minds astray from the straight path of virtue than the
supposition that the soul of the brutes is of the same nature with our
own; and consequently that after this life we have nothing to hope for
ot fear, more than flies and ants; in place of which, when we know how
far they differ, we much better comprehend the reasons which establish
that the soul is of a nature wholly independent of the body, and that
consequently it is not liable to die with the latter; and, finally, because
no other causes are observed capable of destroying it, we are naturally
led thence to judge that it is immortal.

Of course, in constantly referring his reader to a detailed exposition
elsewhere in an inaccessible treatise which was never published and
probably not even fully composed, Descartes is deploying a device
noticeably similar to that in the Meditations passage, of claiming to have
somehow thought ‘more intensively’ or more reflexively in order to arrive at
the steps in his argument. The device of referring to an unpublished treatise
is itself a variation upon constructing a ‘literary conversion’ to impress and
persuade the sympathetically willing and credulous reader.

brain work

In my pursuit of ‘thinking’, this seems as appropriate a moment as any to
dip, however inadequately, into some modern research into the workings of
a brain, in part to illustrate a contemporary neuro-scientific mode but also
arguably still a basically Cartesian way of approaching the notion of
‘thinking’. There are several shelves and indeed libraries full of more or less
accessible accounts to draw upon, from, say, A.R. Luria’s classic The
Working Brain (1973) through Consciousness and the Brain by Stanislas Dehaene

95

(2014) to more recent exposition such as The Idea of The Brain by Matthew
Cobb (2020), Being You: A New Science of Consciousness by Anil Seth (2021), or
A Thousand Brains: a new theory of intelligence by Jeff Hawkins (2021).

Since the territory is crowded and will undoubtedly be more far familiar
to some readers than to myself, I will simply indicate work I happen to have
found useful and factually informative.

I want to begin by considering the neuron as one focus in the current
understanding of the workings of the brain, and will initially cite and
summarise part of the account in The Mind and the Brain: Neuroplasticity and
The Power of Mental Force by Jeffrey M Schwartz and Sharon Begley (2002).
Other and more recent accounts are easily available.

‘A neuron in the brain consists, typically, of the cell body called the soma
— Greek for ‘body’ — which measures 10 to 100 um across (100 um equals
0.1 mm)’ (page 103f). Inside the soma occur the metabolising processes and
the synthesising of proteins which constitute the essential activity of all
living cells. From each soma sprout numerous tentacles, called dendrites,
which divide into dozens or hundreds of branch points, and which receive
incoming electro-chemical impulses from other neurons.

From each cell soma a single long fibre, called an axon, also emerges
which extends away from the cell body and transmits electrical currents to
the dendrites of other neurons. An axon can be as long as a metre, or only a
few tenths of a millimetre, enabling connections between local or relatively
distant areas of the brain. ‘Information transmitted by one neuron and
received by another takes the form of electrical signals generated by charged
atoms, or ions — in particular, positively charged sodium and potassium ions
or negatively charged chloride ions. The flux of ions across the cell
membrane of a neuron is meticulously regulated by membrane pumps to
give the inside of the cell a net negative charge relative to its surroundings.
Rapid changes in the flux of ions can generate a moving pulse of electric
charge called an action potential. Like a bulge zipping down a jump rope,
the action potential speeds down the axon at up to 200 miles an hour in
vertebrates (though only 30 to 40 miles an hour in invertebrates). It is the
physical embodiment of the information sent from one neuron to another’
(p.104f).

The connection point between a dendrite from one neuron and an axon
from a different neuron is termed a ‘synapse’, which is a gap one millionth
of a centimetre between them, across which there can occur an activation
of responses between the two fibres which we can construe as the
transmission of a ‘signal’. Further: When the pulse of charge arrives at the
synapse, it stimulates the entry of calcium ions, which triggers the process
by which . . tiny vesicles in the presynaptic neuron release
neurotransmitters’ (p.105). These neurotransmitters can take the form of,
for example, molecules of amino acid derivatives or nitric oxide gas. On
average, a single brain neuron forms about 1,000 synaptic connections and
receives about 10,000 inputs, with some cells in the cerebellum receiving up

96

to 100,000. Taking a conservative mean for the number of connections
(1,000), the adult brain has an estimated 100 trillion synapsese. Other
estimates of the number of synapsese in the adult brain suggest 1000 trillion
(p.111).

We can now focus on what particularly concerns me. The process of
making neural connection begins early. At birth a human newborn’s brain
contains some 100 billion nerve cells, and from about three weeks after
conception the embryo has produced neurons, with some 250,000 per
minute averaged over the entire nine months in utero. But:

The fetal brain is profligate in its overproduction of both neurons and
synapses. Not all the axons trying to reach out and touch another will
manage it; the failures die. About half the neurons that form in the
foetal brain died before the baby is born: 200 billion neurons, give or
take, are reduced to the hundred billion of a newborn as neurons that
fail to form functional synapses vanish. . . Synapses are pruned even
more ruthlessly . . By one count, each cortical neuron forms, at the
height of synapsogenesis, 15,000 connections: that works out to 1.8
million synapsese per second from the second month in utero to the
child’s second birthday. Which synapsese remain, and which wither
away, depends on whether they carry any traffic. If they do not, then
like bus routes that attract no customers, they go out of business. By
one estimate, approximately 20,000,000,000 synapsese are pruned every
day between childhood and early adolescence. It is survival of the
busiest. (p. 117).]

All this seems a long way from both Aristotle’s ‘substantial form’ and
17th-century ‘thinking matter’, but also from claims about ‘inserting’ a pre-
existing ‘soul’. However, consider this passage :

Starting in the second trimester of pregnancy . . pfoto-neurons
immediately begin to migrate outward in a journey so challenging that it
has been likened to a baby’s crawling from New York to Seattle and
winding up in the precise neighbourhood, on the right street, at the
correct house that he was destined for from the moment he left
Manhattan. . . How neurons realise that they have reached the final
destination remains a mystery. .. But we do know that it is only when
the immature neurons are in place that they become fully fledged
neurons and put down roots, blossoming with dendrites and spouting
an axon by which they will communicate with, and form a circuit with,
other neurons near and far.

One way to indicate the implications of such an account would be to ask

whether Descartes should have pursued any full autobiographical ‘inward’
description of his own processes of thinking all the way back into the

97

womb itself. Perhaps those gaps in his narrative of insights were ultimately
thanks to some dead or missing neurons or perhaps pruned synapses.

What particularly interests me is that the image of the neuron crawling
‘from New York to Seattle’ derives from one long-established and
influential way of modelling the way in which the human brain operates,
which was to try to ‘map’ the parts of the brain which seemed to be
associated with different functions, actions, or skills.

In one sense, this could be seen as trying to explain the working of the
brain in terms of its structure or arrangement, with the overall ‘form’ and
the finally settled relations between ‘locations’ as the decisive factor in the
differentiation of particular neuronal functions. The developed brain was
regarded and presented in terms of a wonderfully intricate geography, a
complex terrain comprising various more or less distinct ‘areas’ somehow
responsible for hearing or vision, language or ‘reasoning’. Between these
areas could be traced major highways and numerous subsidiary routes
which, like an urban traffic or city Metro diagram, indicated possible
journeys’, as of electro-chemical impulses carrying ‘signals’. By taking EEG
readings and MRI scans, experiments were increasingly successful in
mapping apparent functional areas within a brain’s overall structure, yet
without explaining how that overall structure in some way ‘causes’ or relates
to those functions.

Two vast ‘Brain Projects’ in Europe and America amounted to trying to
reconstruct an entire model brain, as a map of itself (see The Future Brain
edited by Gary Marcus and Jeremy Freeman, 2015). A rather Borgesean
idea, the map being as detailed as the terrain. Both projects were eventually
regarded as unsuccessful but left behind an array of tools and data which at
least helped to postpone the problem indefinitely, since even a complete
‘mapping’ of the brain will not necessarily help until we understand better
whether and how the very model of ‘mapping’ applies to the brain.

In an indirect lineage from Aristotle’s lagoon, numerous investigations
have learned a lot about the brains of frogs, flies, rats, maggots, and
monkeys, and numerous practical medical uses have been developed from
observing and modifying those brain processes which are observable and
experimentally repeatable. Yet sending an electrical impulse to a prosthetic
leg or stimulating specific brain cells to activate a computer’s artificial
speech system still seem to require a mode of explanation of a different
order: how a ‘voluntary’ movement or a new ‘thought’ is generated in the
first place, if indeed there is such a ‘place’. But as far as specifically human
‘thinking’ is concerned no consensus hypothesis or even agreed formulation
of the ‘problem’ has decisively emerged. Where is ‘thinking’? And what is
‘it’?

In the crucial business of securing funding for research programmes the
relative technological availability of the procedures for localised ‘mapping’
of specific areas of the brain meant that as microscopic resolution vastly
improved, some research focussed upon ever more localised areas and

98

eventually on the workings of individual neurons. Using a variety of
techniques, one study demonstrated that a single cell in a specific individual
male subject’s brain fired up only and consistently on his seeing an image of
a particular female television star. A Max Planck Institute study of a minute
block of mouse brain less than one 10th of a millimetre on each side
indicated a staggering total of 153,171 synapses involving just the 89
neurons in that space, out of the perhaps 70 million neurons in the whole
of a mouse brain.

Such microscopic focus could partly displace or compete with research
interest in what kinds of activity and connection can be tracked between
parts of the brain. Were those ‘locations’ simply traffic roundabouts and
switch-points or did the vastly complex reconfigurations of innumerable
neurons themselves somehow process the ‘information’ (what is
‘information’?) delivered to and through them. Part of the illuminating
emphasis of the Schwartz and Begley study was upon precisely the plasticity
of the human brain, its capacity to adapt different ‘areas’ to undertake new
or modified functions, for example in the case of injury or a stroke.

The overall ‘mapping’ approach was supplemented by an increasing
attention on how the ‘journeys’ themselves might construct or constitute
changing configurations of multiply-connected neurons, suggesting that the
networks of connections themselves might be regarded as defining the
relevant ‘areas’, with brain functions related to the complex circuits
connecting neurons rather than the ‘areas’ so connected. Such ‘network’
configurations could be variously observed as both a temporary ‘flickering’
(Doug Hofstadter’s term) and as more lasting flexible re-apportionings of
functions, a kind of constant ‘neuro-plasticity’.

Within this model or approach ‘neural networks’ consisted not so much
of journeys between fixed brain areas, but rather as combinations of neural
connections which were not necessarily permanently traceable but could
themselves be seen as the result of more or less momentary or temporary
combined activations of a ‘population’ of more or less distant neurons, with
connections being flexibly made and unmade, somewhat like Homet’s
Penelope endlessly weaving and unweaving her textiles. Or perhaps like the
changing configurations of lighting arrangements in a theatre during the
course of a performance. Or even the constantly re-organised patterns of
the playing positions of a football team during an actual game.

This neural net ‘connectionist’ model suggested quite other metaphors or
models than that of a fixed or stable cartography, including in particular
images or models implicitly drawn from the momentary bricolage of global
inter-net connections constantly constituted by myriads of users clicking on
sequences of hyperlinks and thereby connecting them in constantly
changing ways to a world-wide-web. It was perhaps such more or less
momentary reconfigurations which constituted the substance of the brain’s
activity. But one might then be tempted to ask whether the ‘world wide
web’ itself might ‘think’ — a vast ‘thinking machine’ though barely

99

approaching the scale of connections found in a normal human brain.
Except that what the web connects are presumably already thinking entities,
including the humans who have so far created it.

Leibniz

At this point there is a temptation to enter the rabbit hole of inconclusive
debates about ‘consciousness’. Instead I want briefly to consider another
17th-century figure who offered a version of Anselm’s ‘ontological proof.
In a passage where he refers to Anselm’s argument, Leibniz in fact
discusses Descartes’ version (New Essays Concerning Human Understanding,
translated by A.G. Langley, 1896, p. 502 ff.):

Although I am for innate ideas, and in particular for that of God, I do

Leibniz then offered his own misleadingly succinct version:

This is very nearly the force of his argument: God is the greatest or (as
Descartes says) the most perfect of beings, or rather a being of supreme
grandeur and perfection, including all degrees thereof. That is the
notion of God. See now how existence follows from this notion. To
exist is something more than not to exist, or rather, existence adds a
degree to grandeur and perfection, and as Descartes states it, existence
is itself a perfection. Therefore this degree of grandeur and perfection,
or rather this perfection which consists in existence, is in this supreme
all-ereat, all-perfect being: for otherwise some degree would be wanting
to it, contrary to its definition. Consequently this supreme being exists.

Leibniz judges the argument cogent but defective, with missing steps
(‘there is still a gap therein’), and ‘imperfect’ but he doesn’t attempt in this
passage either to refute it or to resolve its difficulties, instead proposing that
‘clever men’ should pursue his own suggestions and ‘achieve the
demonstration with the strictness of a mathematical proof:

The Scholastics, not excepting even their Doctor Angelicus, have

misunderstood this argument, and have taken it as a paralogism; in

These comments indicate a much larger project which Leibniz envisaged
and in part initiated. The underlying ambition was in effect to regard all
philosophical arguments and, indeed, all human thinking as susceptible to
assessment by the protocols and criteria of mathematical procedures.
Correct thinking was ultimately logical and logic was ultimately
mathematical, and vice versa. A persistent ambition to systematise the
relation between mathematics and logic could be traced from Leibniz
through Frege, Bertrand Russell (who wrote a substantial book on Leibniz),
and Wittgenstein’s seminars on the philosophy of mathematics in the 1930s
(in which Alan Turing participated).

Boole

George Boole’s 1854 publication An Investigation of the Laws of Thought on
which are Founded the Mathematical Theories of Logic and Probabilities can provide
a succinct and accessible formulation of this tendency and pursuit. Boole
wrote (I have italicised some phrases I later want to cite):

The conditions of valid reasoning, by the aid of symbols, are:

1st, That a fixed interpretation be assigned to the symbols employed
in the expression of the data; and that the laws of the combination of
those symbols be correctly determined from that interpretation.

2nd, That the formal processes of solution or demonstration be
conducted throughout in obedience to all the laws determined as
above, without regard to the question of the interpretability of the
particular results obtained.

3rd, That the final result be interpretable in form, and that it be
actually interpreted in accordance with that system of interpretation
which has been employed in the expression of the data.
. . The necessity of a fixed interpretation of the symbols has already been
sufficiently dwelt upon. ..

A single example of reasoning, in which symbols are employed in
obedience to laws founded upon their interpretation, but without any

sustained reference to that interpretation, the chain of demonstration
conducting us through intermediate steps which are not interpretable, to a final result
which is interpretable, seems not only to establish the validity of the
particular application, but to make known to us the general law
manifested therein.

The employment of the uninterpretable symbol \-1, in the intermediate processes
of trigonometry, furnishes an illustration of what has been said.

The following is the mode in which the principle above stated will be
applied in the present work. It has been seen, that any system of
propositions may be expressed by equations involving symbols x, y, z,
which, whenever interpretation is possible, are subject to laws sdentical in
form with the laws of a system of quantitative symbols, susceptible only of the values
0 and1.. But as the formal processes of reasoning depend only upon
the laws of the symbols, and not upon the nature of their
interpretation, we are permitted to treat the above symbols, x, y, z, as if
they were quantitative symbols of the kind above described. We may in
fact lay aside the logical interpretation of the symbols in the given
equation; convert them into quantitative symbols, susceptible only of
the values 0 and 1; perform upon them as such all the requisite
processes of solution; and finally restore to them their logical
interpretation.

The truth that the ultimate laws of thought are mathematical in
their form, viewed in connexion with the fact of the possibility of error,
establishes a ground for some remarkable conclusions.

The affinity with early computer programming is obvious and the passage
could almost have been written in 1944 not 1854.

For several decades after the breakthroughs of Alan Turing and von
Neumann in the theory, design, and implementation of computers as
‘machines for thinking’ the dominant model was that of logic-based
programming and the primary applications mainly concerned versions of
mathematical calculation. The implication, sometimes made explicit, of
much of this work was that all forms of ‘thinking’ might eventually be
assimilated to such modes of rigorous calculation and quasi-deterministic
operation. Or at least could be transcribed into some form of computer
code implementing logical procedures. A clearly Leibnizian project and
program. In the wake of these developments the human brain itself was to
be regarded and investigated as a form of ‘computing machine’, with the
physical brain as the hardware and ‘thinking’ as perhaps a form of ‘software’
program or ‘operating system’.

It was explicit in Leibniz’s project that all languages could somehow be
assimilated to a single universal vocabulary or lexicon and that all human
languages might be subsumed under an ars combinatoria, arranged according
to a quasi-mathematical template, and that position is already implicit in
Boole’s phrase ‘the necessity of a fixed interpretation of the symbols’.

102

But what of the ordinary everyday kind of ‘thinking’? Certainly, not all
human ‘thinking’ would appear to be logical in the sense that a
mathematical calculation might be so regarded. After all, both Anselm and
Descartes had used natural language, not mathematics or symbolic logic, to
formulate their ostensible proofs. How did ordinary everyday human
language fit into this model of ‘computing’, or indeed figure in models of
the brain’s activity itself, not simply as a matter of labelling an area in which
language ‘occurred’ but understanding how language ‘works’ at all?
