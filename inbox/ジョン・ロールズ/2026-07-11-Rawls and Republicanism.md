## Abstract

Abstract
                  Contemporary republicans stand in an ambiguous relationship with Rawls. Republicanism has often been seen as an alternative to liberalism, and yet Rawls famously asserted that there is “no fundamental opposition” between the two traditions. Only a few years later, however, significant contributions by Philip Pettit and Quentin Skinner transformed republicanism from an obscure political doctrine of mainly historiographical interest into one of the leading approaches to contemporary normative political theory. It is thus worth reassessing how republicans should regard the greatest political philosopher of the twentieth century—especially the character of their debt to Rawls and the extent of their opposition to his particular brand of political liberalism. Republican authors themselves do not always agree on these matters. This chapter will explore what they have said about Rawls, while also advancing a particular view about what republicans should think about his ideas.
