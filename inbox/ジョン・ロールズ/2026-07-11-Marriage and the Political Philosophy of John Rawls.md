## Abstract

Abstract
                  Rawls says little about marriage. What he does say is often connected to his evolving (although for many, never entirely satisfying) treatment of family. Nevertheless, theorists have drawn on—and contested—Rawlsian concepts and frameworks in debates over marriage. This chapter offers a comprehensive overview of debates between Rawls and his interlocutors on the topic of marriage. This debate has, at various times, focused on marriage as a dominant but contested form of constituting family in liberal democratic societies, a fundamental right, an expression of love, the bedrock of society, a linchpin of gender (in)justice, part of the basic structure, and a site and source of primary social goods. The chapter ventures into cutting-edge debates that challenge and reshape the contours of discussions of marriage through and against a Rawlsian lens.
