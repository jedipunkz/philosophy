Spinoza’s Compendium: Between Hebrew and
Latin Grammars of the Middle Ages and the
Renaissance: Verbs versus Nouns

Jacques J. Rozenberg'

ABSTRACT: Drawing on several Jewish and Latin grammars from the Middle Ages and
the Renaissance, Spinoza sought to uncover the universal cognitive value of Hebrew to
gain access to its logical and grammatical structure. Although the Compendium grammati-
ces linguae Hebraeae was apparently intended to shed light on the central themes of the
Tractatus Theologico-Politicus (TTP), rather than the metaphysics of Ethics, I will examine
the thesis proposed by several researchers who have highlighted the similarity between the
linguistic categories of the Compendium and the conceptual categories of the Ethics. I will
attempt to understand how, by projecting the characteristics of Latin onto Hebrew, Spinoza
made the noun primordial in relation to the verb and ultimately neglected the dynamism
of Hebrew.’ Then, I will attempt to test the comparison proposed by some commentators
between the transformational character of Hebrew as identified in the Compendium, and
Noam Chomsky’s theory of generative grammar. Finally, I will emphasize, in opposition
to Spinoza’s defense of the primacy of the noun, the fundamentally verbal and dynamic
nature of the Hebrew language.

HE COMPENDIUM GRAMMATICES LINGUAE HEBRAEAE, written by

Spinoza for his circle of students, was published in 1677 in the Opera Post-
huma, in a hasty and uncritical manner, and the text still requires some numerous
corrections.* According to Jarig Jelles, the Compendium was to have two parts, the
first dealing with etymology and the second with syntax, but the latter was never
written.* This work has been described as fundamentally eclectic, for example, on
the one hand, it describes the pronunciation of consonants in the manner of the
Portuguese,” whom he calls Lusitanians (Lusitani, living in the southwestern part
of the Iberian Peninsula), such as debarav (his words).° But on the other hand, it
reports the pronunciation of vowels according to the Ashkenazim, as described by
Buxtorf I (1564-1629), notably transcribing the vowel shureq,° whereas the Sep-
hardim pronounce it shurugq.

indicare).’ However, in line with Buxtorf I, he devoted an entire chapter (chapter
IV) to accents, which only concern the reading of Biblical text. He thus contradicted
his claim to want to write a grammar of the Hebrew language and not of Scripture.®

CONCLUSION

I began by pointing out the historical parallels that can be drawn between the project
presented by Spinoza in the Compendium grammatices linguae Hebraeae and the
theses developed by certain Jewish and Christian Hebrew grammarians of the Middle
Ages and the Renaissance. Through the analysis of these parallels, I attempted to
understand the roots of the secularization of Hebrew. I then sought to clarify the
controversial question of the theoretical relationship between the Ethics and the
Compendium. Thus, by comparison with the Grammaire Générale et Raisonnée of
Port Royal, { then attempted to uncover the foundations of a possible correspon-
dence between the Ethics and the Compendium. | thereafter emphasized Spinoza’s
projection onto Hebrew of categories specific to the Latin language. I afterwards
examined the relationship between Spinoza’s analysis and that of generative and
transformational grammar, by recounting the debates between Wallace L. Chafe and
Noam Chomsky concerning Spinoza’s thesis on the prevalence of the noun and the
verb. Finally, I stressed, against the primacy of the noun defended by Spinoza, the
fundamentally verbal and dynamic nature of the Hebrew language.
