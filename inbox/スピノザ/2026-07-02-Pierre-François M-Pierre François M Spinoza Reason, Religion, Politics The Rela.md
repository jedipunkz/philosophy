on 5-7 May 2017 at Princeton University, a conference that also served to celebrate

Introduction

Daniel Garber

Spinoza’s Ethics and his Tractatus theologico-politicus are, on the face of it, radically
different works. The one is written in Euclidean form, with definitions, axioms,
and propositions, while the other is written in chapters. The one is austere and
abstract and virtually without reference to other people or other thinkers, while
the other is filled with biblical exegesis and history and with discussions of
philosophers, theologians, and prophets. While the TTP claims to be addressed
to the “philosophical reader,” the presumed audience of the Ethics, it would seem
much more of relevance to the theologian and even the common person than to
the philosopher. Furthermore, as some of the chapters in this volume will discuss,
there are real tensions and even contradictions, real and apparent, between what
the one book teaches and what the other does. Yet there is a way in which the
same voice animates both works, the same intellectual curiosity and bold
imagination. The chapters in this collection explore the complex relations
between these two contrasting works by the same author.

The volume is divided into six parts. Part I, “Two Intertwined Texts,” begins with
a chapter by Piet Steenbakkers, “Parallel Masterpieces: Intertextuality in Spinoza’s
Ethics and Tractatus Theologico-Politicus.’ Steenbakkers starts the volume with a
discussion of the composition of both of Spinoza’s texts, emphasizing the way in
which their composition overlaps one another. The Ethics was started in late 1662 or
early 1663, but set aside between the summer of 1665 and the end of 1669, while
Spinoza composed the TTP. At that point, he returned to the Ethics, which was
finished by May 1675. Though the works have radically different styles and organiza-
tion, and are addressed to rather different audiences, Steenbakkers argues that the
two works are thematically interconnected and ultimately advance the same larger
program: bringing people of different sorts and temperaments to a collective
liberation through reason, revelation, and politics. Though distinctly different
projects, they are nevertheless expressions of the same philosophical spirit.

Steenbakkers’s chapter is nicely complemented by Pina Totaro’s contribution,
“Continuity and Discontinuity between the Tractatus Theologico-Politicus and
the Ethics.’ Her chapter explores the relationship between the TTP and the Ethics
by appealing to the dialectic between “letter” and “spirit.” On Totaro’s reading,
Spinoza’s biblical exegesis adopts the “sola scriptura” principle (adherence to the
“letter” to show how, behind the different narratives offered in the biblical texts,

the ethical meaning (or “spirit” of the text) remains unchanged. This same “spirit”
which Spinoza finds in Scripture, the product of a secularized reading of the text,
is also evident in the Ethics, where it is presented as a consequence of a philo-
sophical and political reflection on common human salvation and beatitude.

This part of the book ends with a chapter by Steven Nadler, “Spinoza’s Ethics as
a Theological-Political Treatise.” Nadler begins with the religious and political
context of the Dutch Republic of Spinoza’s day. After showing how the TTP fits
into that historical context, and directly addresses the way in which religion
penetrates into the public sphere, he goes on to show how the Ethics can be seen
to address many of the same issues about religion and politics so prominently
displayed in the TTP. In this way, Nadler argues that the Ethics is, in a way, a
theological-political treatise in its own right, despite its obvious differences from
the TTP.

In Part I of the book, we turn to “God and Atheism.” Mogens Leerke’s chapter,
“Prejudices, Common Notions, Intuitions: Knowledge of God between the Ethics
and the Tractatus Theologico-Politicus, confronts an apparent inconsistency
between what Spinoza says about God within the Ethics, and between the Ethics
and the TTP. In E2p47, Spinoza writes that “the human mind has adequate
knowledge of God's eternal and infinite essence.” However, in E2p47s, Spinoza
claims that “men do not have so clear a knowledge of God as they do of the com-
mon notions.’ Moreover, in the TTP, Spinoza writes that “knowledge of God
must be drawn from common notions certain and known through themselves”
[TTP 6 (G II 61/C II 130)]. Lzerke attempts to resolve the apparent contradic-
tions among these competing assertions. Lzrke’s chapter is followed by Yitzhak
Melamed’s examination of one of the most common complaints among readers of
both the Ethics and the TTP: that Spinoza is an atheist. In his chapter, “Spinoza’s
Atheism, the Ethics and the Tractatus Theologico-Politicus” Melamed examines
this question carefully, and arrives at what will certainly be a surprising conclu-
sion for many. After an extended discussion of what various readers meant when
they called Spinoza an atheist and the various grounds on which such judgments
rested, Melamed works carefully through the Ethics and the TTP to argue that
properly interpreted, Spinoza was, in his own way, a deeply committed theist. He
confirms this by showing the close ties between Spinoza’s conception of God in
his major works, and the conception of God and God’s relation to the world
found in Rabbinic Judaism.

Part III is concerned with “Laws: Natural and Divine.’ The first chapter in this
section is Edwin Curley’s “Descartes and Spinoza on the Laws of Nature.’ In his
first book, Spinoza’s Metaphysics: An Essay in Interpretation, Curley made
Spinoza’s views on the laws of nature in natural philosophy central to his meta-
physics and to his view of the world. After many years—and much discussion of
his views—Curley returns to this theme in his own work and in Spinoza’s as he
sees it. The piece is a personal reflection by one of the most influential

commentators on Spinoza in the English-speaking world on one of the central
themes in Spinoza’s thought, including the Ethics and the TTP, reflecting on his
past thoughts as well as those of others, his sources and his critics. Though the
focus is on laws of nature, it offers the reader a wide overview of the current state
of interpretation of Spinoza’s metaphysical thought from the mid-twentieth cen-
tury to now. Curley discusses the laws of nature, first in Descartes and then in
Spinoza, and the role that they play in Spinoza’s metaphysics. He draws on both
the Ethics and the TTP, but also on a wide variety of Spinoza’s other texts. The
chapter has implications for a wide variety of issues in Spinoza’s thought, includ-
ing the status of his definitions, his understanding of the substance-mode distinc-
tion, and the allegations that he was an atheist. The next chapter in this part,
“Divine Law’ in Spinoza’s Tractatus Theologico-Politicus” by Theo Verbeek, turns
from Curley’s concern with the laws of nature toward divine law. Verbeek exam-
ines the treatment of divine law, largely in the TTP, against the background of the
view of God Spinoza presents in the Ethics, where God cannot be conceived as a
lawgiver, and against the background of the Grotian conception of divine law as
the result of divine will. After discussing the importance of the denial of divine
will for Spinoza and the problems that it raises for a conception of divine law,
Verbeek turns to the way in which Spinoza tries to reinterpret divine law as a “rule
of life” a means for people to attain an end, some sort of highest good. Arguing
that divine law cannot be successfully understood either as Grotian voluntary
law, or as a rule of life, Verbeek explores its possible connection to politics, only to
conclude its irrelevance there too. In the end, he concludes that, in a way,
Spinoza’s expressed views on divine law were almost certainly just a smoke screen
to hide his real views. This suggests that Spinoza considered his real views too
dangerous for him to express openly. On Verbeek’s view, what Spinoza really held
but hid was “the idea that there is no Divine Law at all....” The last chapter in
Part III is Donald Rutherford’s “Divine Law and the Right Way of Living: From
the Tractatus Theologico-Politicus to the Ethics.” Rutherford is concerned with
Spinoza’s notoriously obscure and difficult ethical doctrine in the Ethics.To gain a
better understanding, he suggests that we appeal to the notion of divine law in the
TTP. More precisely, we should consider the connection Spinoza draws in TTP 4
between divine laws and the principle of living [ratio vivendi] which he calls a
universal ethics. In this way, Rutherford argues, we can see the TTP as constitut-
ing the hidden framework of the ethical doctrine of the Ethics.

Part IV deals with “Wonder, Final Causes, and Miracles.” Wonder was one of
the basic five passions for Descartes, central to his account of the passions. In his
chapter, “Is Wonder a Remedy against the Passions? Spinoza’s Struggle with
Descartes’s Legacy in the Tractatus Theologico-Politicus and in the Ethics? Andrea
Sangiacomo treats Spinoza’s ambivalent reaction to wonder over the course of his
writings. In the KV, Sangiacomo notes, wonder is barely considered. It is dis-
missed as a cognitive attitude rather than an affect, properly speaking. However,

in the TTP, wonder plays a much more important, though ambivalent role. It can
foster social cohesion in various ways but also disrupt society through the super-
stition that wonder can lead people to adopt. In the Ethics, by contrast, wonder
loses its positive valence and becomes unambiguously problematic. As Spinoza
comes to see sociability linked more and more to sharing a common nature,
Sangiacomo sees wonder as interfering with social connection and thus as a nega-
tive affect. In her chapter, “Finalism, Religion, and Miracles: From the Tractatus
Theologico-Politicus to the Ethics” Emanuela Scribano discusses Spinoza’s
account of the tendency people have to see purpose in nature and, in particular, in
the miraculous intervention of God. Scribano examines the evolution of Spinoza’s
views on miracles and related views on finalism from the TTP to the Ethics, from
something that arises from one particular group of people with their own special
interests to a characteristic of people in general: their tendency to see nature
acting for a purpose that corresponds to their special wants and needs. Her story
takes us from Spinoza’s account in the TTP of the Jews who invented final causes
to argue that they are especially loved by a God doing things to their advantage,
to the account in the Ethics where he sees the urge to posit final causes as a general
tendency shared by all peoples.

Part V of the book deals with central issues in the TTP, “Revelation, Reason,
and Salvation,’ issues which also have their reflection in the Ethics. The first chap-
ter is “Spinoza’s Views on Prophecy and the Prophets Revisited” by Ursula Renz
and Philip Waldner. Their interest is in how prophecy fits into the world that
Spinoza is trying to shape, particularly in the TTP. Renz and Waldner are fully
aware that Spinoza does not accept the literal claims of prophets, yet, at the same
time, on their view, Spinoza does not reject prophecy either. Rather, they try to
understand the broadly social and even political roles that prophets and proph-
ecy play in society for Spinoza. The second chapter in this part is Daniel Garber’s
“Philosophy and Theology, Reason and Revelation: The Ethics and the Tractatus
Theologico-Politicus.” Garber’s concern is Spinoza’s strong claims about the inde-
pendence of philosophy and theology in the TTP. He is especially concerned with
an apparent contradiction between the two. For Spinoza, philosophy is the
domain of reason, while theology is the domain of revelation and obedience. Yet
in the TTP, Spinoza argues that obedience requires that one be, in some sense,
committed to what he calls the “dogmata of Universal Faith” However, these dog-
mata involve a conception of God that seems blatantly inconsistent with the
philosophical conception of God Spinoza puts forward in the Ethics. In his chap-
ter, Garber proposes a way of understanding the conception of faith and obedi-

1. Introduction

Although the Tractatus theologico-politicus did enjoy great popularity among
freethinkers of all descriptions from the very beginning, a glance at the reception
of Spinoza’s thought shows that his place in the history of philosophy has always
rested first and foremost on the exposition of his system as given in the Ethics, his
masterpiece par excellence. Nor is this to be wondered at. As a book, the Tractatus
theologico-politicus is difficult to pigeonhole: is it a pamphlet, an exercise in bib-
lical philology, a political treatise? What is its philosophical status? Spinoza even-
tually entered the philosophical canon in the nineteenth century owing to his
popularity among the German idealists, who had a marked preference for the
metaphysics of the Ethics—a circumstance that will also have contributed to its
reputation as the single most important work Spinoza wrote. Only in the last few
decades has the Tractatus theologico-politicus been catching up, receiving unpre-
cedented scholarly attention. Much work has been done by French scholars like
Alexandre Matheron, Pierre-Frangois Moreau, and Jacqueline Lagrée. Edwin
Curley published two papers with the telling title “Notes on a Neglected
Masterpiece” Theo Verbeek, Paul Bagley, Steven Nadler, and Susan James
devoted full-length monographs to it,’ and there have been numerous other pub-
lications. The present volume is another sign of this significant shift in interest.
What is it that makes these two texts, the Ethics and the Tractatus theologico-
politicus, stand out in Spinoza’s oeuvre? One reason is that all his other works are
incomplete: his geometric digest of Renati des Cartes Principia philosophiae, the
early but never finished Tractatus de intellectus emendatione, the Hebrew
grammar, and the tantalizing text his death prevented him from accomplishing,
the Tractatus politicus—they all end in unison with the set phrase Reliqua

2. The Ethics and the Tractatus Theologico-Politicus: Chronology

3. Texts, Context, Intertextuality: Instances

I will now deal with a number of instances of intertextuality between the Ethics
and the Tractatus theologico-politicus in order to illustrate the various ways in
which these two texts are interrelated. On the level of the genesis of the works, we
can sometimes see what was on Spinoza’ mind when he transferred certain terms
or concepts from one to the other, looking at them afresh in a different context. At
times the interaction reveals something of his rhetorical strategies. As to the
philosophical implications, I advance the hypothesis that the Ethics and the
Tractatus theologico-politicus are systematically coherent, if one takes the differ-
ent contexts into account. An attentive analysis of the philosophical vocabulary of
the Ethics can guide us in understanding what Spinoza wanted to convey in the
Tractatus theologico-politicus and how that fits in with his own position.

(a) In chapter 6 of the Tractatus theologico-politicus Spinoza uses the concept

sophical view. He is well aware of the controversial issues involved, but does not
want these to overshadow the argument he makes here.

(f) Exemplar.’° One of the distinctive innovations of early modern mechanistic
philosophy was its rejection of final causes or, as it came to be called in the
eighteenth century, teleology. This is central to Francis Bacon’s critique of
Aristotelianism and of Scholasticism. It is also crucial to Spinoza’s metaphysics, as
he himself emphasizes in the appendix to Part 1 of the Ethics. There he argues that
the fundamental set of prejudices that gives rise to all other errors about nature is
a baleful combination of teleology, anthropomorphism, and anthropocentrism:
because human beings do everything for an end, they assume by analogy that
other things in nature, and even God, will act for certain ends as well, and they
take themselves to be the centre around which the world was created. Spinoza
rejects these biases and with them the notion of a divine creator.

The insistence on a rational life informs Spinoza’s metaphysics, ethics, and
political philosophy. The true life of the mind as a distinctive feature of a
genuinely human life is embodied in his ideal of a fully rational human being:
“an idea of man, as a model of human nature,’ set forth in the preface to Part 4 of
the Ethics:

approach nearer and nearer to the model of human nature that we set before
ourselves. By evil, what we certainly know prevents us from becoming like that
model. Next, we shall say that men are more perfect or imperfect, insofar as they
approach more or less near to this model.”

4. Conclusion

What do these instances of intertextuality teach us? Given the circumstances,
with Spinoza’s writing of the Tractatus theologico-politicus sandwiched between
two substantial stretches in which he concentrated exclusively on the Ethics, there
must have been a reciprocal spillover from one text to the other. Moreover, the
two parallel works are part of a single master plan—at least that is what I have
argued. These mutual influences should not, however, lure us into considering
the two masterpieces as equivalent in their philosophical content. The last
instance we discussed shows that precisely Spinoza’s original philosophical devel-
opment of the notion of essence in the Ethics can help us find our bearings here.

A popular image of Spinoza (and one with a long tradition) is that he was a
militant atheist, on a mission to undermine and even destroy all forms of religion.
While it is incontrovertible that he strove to curb the power of religious rabble-
rousers, and to defuse the repressive climate of fanaticism and sectarianism cre-
ated by superstition, he did not seem to hold that religion would ever go away,
nor that its disappearance was a necessary condition for peace, toleration, and
morality.’ According to the view Spinoza gradually developed while composing
the Tractatus theologico-politicus, religion was rooted in human imagination and
in their affects, notably in fear. On account of its affective origins, Spinoza saw
religion as an inexorable phenomenon: people will always be susceptible to fear
and distress, and therefore to a temptation to invent gods and invest them with
anthropomorphic features, seeing them as beings to whom they can turn for
comfort and support. Human beings cannot be absolutely free in Spinoza’s strict
sense of the word, i.e. guided solely by reason. Hence attempts to eradicate reli-
gion will be futile. It is wiser to strengthen the potentially constructive social and
moral functions true religion may have for society. And that, I submit, is what the
Tractatus theologico-politicus is about. In it, Spinoza demonstrates that a naive
belief in the truth of all stories in the Bible, and the disastrous superstitious prac-
tices this entails—such as the presumptuousness of religious leaders, the repres-
sion and persecution of critics and dissenters—is untenable for anyone who can
think even the least bit rationally. His definition of the essence of faith is an
attempt to salvage the possibility of a religious inspiration for social and moral
conduct, while it shows at the same time that superstition, sectarianism, and
fanaticism can derive no legitimate excuse from any religion, nor from the Bible.

The interpretation I suggest here is most emphatically not an esoteric reading
of the texts. I take Spinoza at his word, and I take both the Tractatus theologico-
politicus and the Ethics entirely seriously and (if I may say so) literally.

the Tractatus Theologico-Politicus

1. Introduction

In this chapter, I would like to show that despite the diversity of their subjects,
their structures, and their specific purposes, the Tractatus theologico-politicus
(TTP) and the Ethics are inspired by a single philosophical vision.’ The editorial
history of the two works, as reconstructed by Piet Steenbakkers, testifies to their
profound interweaving, connections, and reciprocal implications.” A provisional
draft of the first part of Ethics was completed by Spinoza before 1662. As Spinoza
conceived it at that time, the Ethics consisted of only three parts. A new but still
unfinished draft of the Ethics circulated among some friends after the publication
in 1663 of The Principles of Cartesian Philosophy (Renati Des Cartes Principiorum
Philosophiae pars I et II). But from the summer of 1665 to the autumn of 1669,
Spinoza set the Ethics aside and composed his Tractatus theologico-politicus,
which was published in 1670. In that same year he returned to the Ethics, refor-
mulating the text that he had considered almost definitive five years earlier, and
completed it in 1675. In this way work on the Ethics both precedes and follows the
composition of the TTP.

' In much of the critical literature, the contents and conclusions of the TTP and Ethics are con-
sidered to be profoundly different from each other. It is often claimed that the TTP and Ethics are two
completely different works, both for the topic, for their purposes, and also for the public to whom
they are addressed. On this reading, only the Ethics is directed to real philosophers and is exclusively
a work of philosophy, while the TTP deals with exegetical, political, and religious policy issues.
Understood in this way, the lector philosophus whom Spinoza explicitly addresses in the Preface to the
TTP is not conceived of as a “fully formed philosopher” but a “would-be philosopher,” or a “reader
who [...] begins their philosophical journey with the view that revelation is a source of knowledge.”
(See Garber, “Philosophy and Theology”; James, Philosophy, Reason, and Politics, 29; and Nadler,
Forged in Hell, 23.) In this way the purpose of the TTP might therefore be construed as attempting to
convince the philosopher to give up Scripture and revelation. (See Garber, “Philosophy and
Theology” and Strauss, “How to Study.”) (It should be noted that while Garber presents this as a pos-
sible reading of the TTP, he does not endorse it.) E. Curley, on the contrary, interpreted the TTP “as a
prolegomenon’” to the Ethics (Curley, “Neglected Masterpiece”).

My claim is that both these works are part of a single project, at once a theo-
logical, political, ethical, and philosophical project that deals with an interconnected
series of questions. But one central element dominates all these areas and both of
these works: Spinoza’s conception of God. The God of the “natural divine law” in
the Tractatus theologico-politicus is the same as the God of the “order and connec-
tion” of things and ideas in the Ethics.’ These same ideas also shape Spinoza’s
views on the foundations of religion, on the principles of ethics, and on the social
relations in politics. Spinoza’s God is not God the father, good and provident
toward his creatures, not the God of transubstantiation or of incarnation, nor is
he the God of miracles understood as the suspension of the order and laws of
nature. Spinoza’s God is not endowed with human characteristics, nor he is the
God of revealed religion. Spinoza’ God has no freedom of will because this
would presuppose a before and after, which would introduce finite temporality
into divine action, a concept of duration in time, something inconceivable in
God, properly understood. Spinoza’s God is instead comprehended as infinite,
eternal, unlimited in power, causa sui, a God who unfolds in his effects in accord-
ance with an internal logical principle. God is the cause of himself from which
everything comes: he is what is manifesting, evolving, expanding, unfolding.
True religion for Spinoza is universal religion, “religio catholica,’ catholic in the
etymological sense of the term, that is, universal. This religion is summed up in
only two imperatives: to love God, and to love your neighbor. These two impera-
tives are the foundation of Scripture, according to the TTP, while at the same
time they constitute the ultimate goal of the Ethics, the final step in the internal-
ization of divine law, and, at the same time, highest and most authentic
self-affirmation.

The idea of love, love of God and consequently love of neighbor, everywhere
recommended in the Bible, as Spinoza argues in the TTP,* comes to us from the
knowledge of the profound structure of nature, a structure that arises from an
infinite series of interconnected causes and ideas, as we read in the Ethics.’ These
relationships and connections constitute the very texture of which the universe
is made.

Inserted in the eternal and infinite order of the causes and perfections of God,
we attain freedom through the intellectual knowledge that brings with it liberation
from the violence of external causes that always threatens us. The TTP analyzes
some of the ways in which people have expressed their conceptions of God and

While the project of the two works is similar, the argumentative structures and

us to the intellectual love of God, the last link in the chain, returning us to the

interpret them in the right way, that is, through the method of interpretation that
Spinoza prescribes in chapter 7 of the TTP.’

It would be wrong to consider the TTP as a simple treatise of biblical exegesis,
just as it would be equally misleading to consider Ethics as a straightforward text
in moral philosophy or a text on philosophical anthropology. In the Tractatus
theologico-politicus and the Ethics, Spinoza attempts a general reformulation of
religion, ethics, and politics by way of an epistemological journey through the
three kinds of knowledge, a journey that begins with the senses and testimony
and ends in a full transformation of the self through the knowledge that consti-
tutes intellectual love of God. In this sense, we could say that a continuous striv-
ing pervades not only the TTP and the Ethics, but all of Spinoza’s work starting
from the Tractatus de intellectus emendatione and the Korte Vernandeling, striv-
ing toward achieving the full knowledge of which we are capable.* This complex
tangle of approaches and themes, though dealt with differently in the different
works, has one and the same goal.

2. Spiritus, Christus, and Spiritus Christi

The term “spirit” (spiritus) occurs often in Spinoza’s texts. In the very first chapter
of the TTP Spinoza discusses at some length the various meanings of the word
“ruagh (nn); Hebrew for spirit.” Spiritus is also used in the expressions Spiritus
Christi, Spiritus Dei, Spiritus sanctus, etc.

The full title of the TTP, including its subtitle, contains a virtual recapitulation
of the entire content of the text that follows:

Theological-Political Treatise, containing several discussions showing that the
republic can grant freedom of philosophizing without harming its peace or
piety, and cannot deny it without destroying its peace and piety. [TTP, title page
(G III 3/C 11 65)]

But the quotation that follows on the title page, a verse from the First Epistle of
John, is also very significant, and presages some of the themes that will be of
importance not only in the TTP, but also in the Ethics:

1 John 4:13: By this we know that we remain in God and that God remains in us,
because he has given us of his Spirit. [TTP, title page (G III 3/C II 65)]"°

Of particular relevance here is the term Spirit (spiritus) and the expression “he
has given us of his Spirit” (de Spiritu suo), indicating something shared and
something in which we participate. The verse of 1 John that immediately precedes
the verse Spinoza quotes is also significant here:

1 John 4:12: No one has ever seen God. Yet, if we love one another, God remains
in us, and his love is brought to perfection in us.

This, together with the verse that follows, draws a connection between spiritus
(pneuma) and “love,” because only through love are we able to see God: “No one
has ever seen God, yet if we love each other, God remains in us,’ and only in this
way can we enjoy and recognize his “presence” (his inhabitatio) in us.

3. Law and Obedience

Further aspects of the close relationship between the TTP and the Ethics can be
understood by considering Spinoza’s treatment of law in these two works.

Law is first discussed in the important chapter 4 of the TTP, which is devoted
to divine law. In TTP 4, Spinoza notes that as it is usually understood, law is
closely connected with obedience. “Law,” he writes, “is generally taken to be a
principle of living prescribed to men by the command of others” [TTP 4 (G II
59/C II 127)]. When a law is established by those who have the authority to make
laws, we are obligated to obey: “obedience consists in someone's carrying out a

command solely on the authority of the person who commands it...” [TTP 5
(G IH 74/C II 144)].” But Spinoza returns to the question of the law in TTP 16 in

reward. But Spinoza introduces a new model of political obligation. Our obliga-

thought.”*

things which pertain to the mind’s duration without relation to the body”

4. Conclusion

In this chapter, I have emphasized some of the philosophical concerns that unite
the Tractatus theologico-politicus and the Ethics into one program. But there is
much more to say on this theme.

From the TTP to the Ethics, the concept of law as system of obligations, applied
to a global conception of nature-God—law as natural law, as connections and
relationships in the order of causes—is embedded in a reflection on the human
condition, passing from the plan of the universe to everyday particulars. The pas-
sage from geometric order to the human sphere implies the transformation of the
law from a system of universal relations to the personal experience of us as indi-
vidual human beings and the collective experience of groups. This process, which
results from the transposition of propositions demonstrated with mathematical
certainty into norms of behavior and practical life, translates the eternal order of

Spinoza’s Ethics as a Theological-Political
Treatise

Steven Nadler

It is obvious to any reader who makes it through at least Part 4 of Spinoza’s Ethics
that, on top of its metaphysical, epistemological, psychological, and moral elements,
the work also has a significant political dimension. One need only look at E4p37,
and especially its long second scholium, to recognize that Spinoza’s account of
human nature and the conclusions about reason and virtue that follow from it
engender, in his mind, a particular theory of the origins of political society, the
nature of justice and the grounds of obligation to the state. These political
remarks, with their unmistakable Hobbesian overtones, were no doubt added to
the original three-part composition that Spinoza had put aside in 1665 to work on
the Tractatus theologico-politicus (TTP) only after he had had occasion to read De
Cive (published in 1642, a copy of which he owned) and Leviathan, the latter in
either its 1667 Dutch or 1668 Latin edition.’

But it is one thing for a work to be a political treatise, and quite another for it to
be a theological-political treatise. Not every instance of the former is also an
instance of the latter. John Locke’s Second Treatise on Government, for example,
as well as Spinoza’s own Tractatus politicus are arguably straightforward political
works, while Hobbes’s Leviathan, John Milton’s Areopagitica and, of course,
Spinoza’s TTP are stunning examples of treatises that fall under both categories.
For among the many topics these latter works address is the vexed question of
what ought to be the proper relationship between religious authority and the civil
sovereign, between Church and State.

I argue, however, that while the Ethics is obviously a work with political
dimensions—and those all-too-brief remarks in E4p37sl-2 receive detailed
elaboration in the latter chapters of the TTP and in the Tractatus politicus—it is
also undeniably a theological-political treatise. And I suggest it is a theological-
political treatise not because of the explicitly political themes in those propositions
of Part 4, but, oddly enough, because of Spinoza’s metaphysics, and especially
his metaphysics of human nature. More specifically, I argue that Spinoza's

1. The Theological-Political Question: The Dutch Context

Let me begin by clarifying what I mean by a “theological-political treatise”. The
seventeenth century witnessed a good deal of theoretical attention paid to the
role of religion in political society. In the competition between civil and religious
authority for state power, as well as for the hearts and minds (and bodies) of the
people, thinkers such as Hugo Grotius, Hobbes, Milton, Pieter and Johan de la
Court, and, of course, Spinoza raised the question as to what ought to be the
proper relationship between the theological and the political.* Should the polit-
ical be subservient to the theological, with a nation ultimately governed by its
clerics and its laws restricted, even commanded by theological principles? Or, on
the other hand, should a polity’s religious life, like its other aspects, be controlled
by the secular authorities? Should the church rule the state or the state rule the
church? Or, in fact, should one have nothing whatsoever to do with the other?
The theological-political question was particularly acute in a nation like the
United Provinces of the Netherlands (the Dutch Republic), a new nation whose
birth occurred in a time of heavy religious warfare. Having only recently liberated
itself from Spain’s Catholic rule, the seven Reformed (Calvinist) provinces of the
northern Low Countries not only continued to debate what form of government
would be best for them but also had to decide what was going to be the place of
religion in their society. Article thirteen of the Union of Utrecht (1579), which
served as a kind of founding constitution for the Dutch Republic, explicitly stated
that “each individual enjoys freedom of religion, and no one is persecuted or

2. Spinoza’s Theological-Political Project

Spinoza’s TTP—with its extended argument that the separation of philosophy
and religion, reason and faith, politics and piety is essential for the well-being of
the state—is directed at this attempted appropriation of political, social, and
moral authority by the clergy. In Spinoza’s view, the solution to the theological-
political problem is to undercut the influence of ecclesiastics over the mechan-
isms of the state and especially over people’s minds. To counter this nefarious
tendency, Spinoza must do some serious debunking of dogmatic pillars of the
religious establishment. If the preachers’ power over politics and persons derives
its sustenance from the superstitious beliefs that they find and encourage in
people, then the most effective strategy to counter that power is to cut it off at its
source by undermining those superstitious beliefs themselves. Spinoza needs to
refute or at least illuminate the true meaning of those fundamental notions that
are manipulated by ecclesiastics to gain influence over public and private lives.
Thus, the deflation of prophecy to the product of vivid imagination by ethically
superb individuals (chapters 1-2); the denial of the possibility of miracles (chapter 6);
and especially the de-sacralization of the Bible, the book wielded by preachers to
reinforce their prerogative, and its reduction to a “mutilated” work of human
literature whose “divinity” consists only its capacity to morally edify readers and
inspire them to virtuous action (chapters 7-13)—all these elements of the TTP
are geared toward weakening the authority of the ecclesiastics and reducing the
influence they have over people’s beliefs and behaviors.

To see how this is supposed to work, let us first consider those superstitious
beliefs. In the preface to the TTP, Spinoza offers a kind of natural history of reli-
gion. As Spinoza sees it, our natural response in the face of the slings and arrows
of outrageous fortune is superstition. As long as things are going well, we are con-
tent to rely on our own resources; a person who is satisfied with his lot does not
generally seek supernatural aid, or even the help of other mortals. “If men could
manage all their affairs by a definite plan, or if fortune were always favorable to
them, no one would be in the grip of superstition” But once their hopes are

On Spinoza’s account, behind the major organized religions and their power
over people lies a certain convenient but ultimately irreverent and harmful
conception of God. The superstitious rites and ceremonies of Judaism and
Christianity, calculated to win God’s favor and avoid His wrath, rest on the false
assumption that God is very much a rational agent, endowed as we are with a
psychological life and moral character. God is, in other words, supposed to be a
kind of person, possessed of intelligence, will, desire, and even emotion. The
Abrahamic deity is a merciful, wise, and just God, a transcendent providential
being who has purposes and expectations, makes commands and judgments, and
is capable of great acts of forgiveness and vengeance.

It is precisely this traditional religious picture of God that Spinoza, in the
Ethics, rejects as foolish anthropomorphism. In Elp15sl, he inveighs against
“those who feign a God, like man, consisting of a body and a mind, and subject to
passions. But how far they wander from the true knowledge of God, is sufficiently
established by what has already been demonstrated” [Elp15sl (G II 57/C I 421)].
Having established that Nature is an indivisible, infinite, self-caused, substantial
whole—in fact, the only substantial whole; that outside of Nature there is noth-
ing; and that everything that exists is a part of Nature and is brought into being
by and within Nature with a deterministic necessity through Nature’s laws,
Spinoza concludes that God and Nature—the substantial, unique, unified, active,
infinitely powerful, necessary cause of everything—are one and the same thing.

sive Natura is totally unsuitable for the role that the Abrahamic deity is ordinarily
called upon to play. Because of the necessity inherent in Nature, there are no pur-
poses for or within the universe, outside of the projects that human beings may
set for themselves. God or Nature does not act for any ends, and things within
nature are not created for the sake of anything. God or Nature does not do things
to achieve any goals. The order of things just follows from God’s (Nature’s)

manipulate the hearts and minds of the people and gain not just moral power but
political power as well.

Another metaphysical element of the Ethics that contributes to the theological-
political project is the absolute necessity that, according to Spinoza, governs
Nature and all its phenomena. Everything, he claims, follows “from the necessity
of the divine nature” [Elp16]; “all things have necessarily flowed, or always follow,
by the same necessity and in the same way as from the nature of a triangle it fol-
lows, from eternity to eternity, that its three angles are equal to two right angles”
[Elp17s1]. But if, as Spinoza demonstrates, “in nature there is nothing contingent,
but all things have been determined from the necessity of the divine nature to
exist and produce an effect in a certain way” [Elp29], then any divine interrup-
tions or violations of the law-like course of nature are thereby ruled out in prin-
ciple. In other words, miracles are impossible, and the belief in them grounded in
ignorance. And if miracles are evidence of God’s providence, of a divine plan to
which the preachers have privileged access, then Spinoza thereby removes
another weapon from their arsenal. Spinoza is clearly aware of the dangerous
position he is taking here: “One who seeks the true causes of miracles, and is eager,
like an educated man, to understand natural things, not to wonder at them, like a
fool, is generally considered and denounced as an impious heretic by those whom
the people honor as interpreters of nature and the Gods” [Elapp (G II 81/C I 443)].

Thus, we have here in Part One of the Ethics, with the revisionist conception of
God and the necessity governing all things, two elements of Spinoza’s metaphys-
ics that clearly contribute to his overall theological-political project, each by
undermining in its own way superstitious beliefs that buttress the authority of the
ecclesiastic class. But these are easily picked, low-hanging fruit, since it is fairly
obvious how the God of the Ethics and the necessity of nature inform the TTP. We
need only look at the discussion of divine providence in chapter 3 of the TTP,
where Spinoza claims that “God’s decrees and commands, and consequently his
providence, are really nothing but the order of nature” [TTP 3 (G III 89/C I
160)]. And then there is the expanded analysis of miracles in chapter 6, which
dovetails nicely with the doctrines of the Ethics.

3. Immortality as a Theological-Political Problem

However, there is more to the relationship between the Ethics and the TTP than
Spinoza’s conception of “God or Nature” and the political discourse of E4p37s. In
fact, considering the two works together can help us make sense of what is one of
the most notoriously difficult doctrines of the Ethics—the doctrine of the eternity
of the mind, presented in Part 5. By the same token, a failure to consider the
theological-political context of the Ethics and its relationship to the TTP can lead
one to fail to grasp the significance of, and even too hastily dismiss, Spinoza’s dis-
cussion of “those things which pertain to the mind’s duration without relation to
the body” [E5p20s].

I assume that what Bennett thinks is “unmotivated” is not so much disaster per
se, but the propositions concerning the mind’s eternity. And yet, the motivation
behind these propositions is precisely the same motivation that is behind
Spinoza’s anti-anthropomorphic conception of God and the denial of miracles—
namely, a theological-political project whose goal is to undermine the supersti-
tious beliefs that nourish the power that ecclesiastics have over people’s lives and
public policy. Let us see how this is so.

The propositions on the eternity of the mind in Part 5 constitute the core of
Spinoza’s assault on the religious doctrine of the immortality of the soul and on
the eschatological beliefs that accompany it. On his account, there is no robust or
meaningful sense in which human beings enjoy personal immortality or an after-
life in some world-to-come. Since I have argued for this at length elsewhere,’° let
me here simply summarize what I take to be the salient facts about Spinoza’s
account of the eternity of the mind.

In E5p23, Spinoza notes that “the human mind cannot be absolutely destroyed
with the [human] body, but something of it remains which is eternal” The formal
essence of the human mind, and its essential core during its durational existence,
is the idea of the formal essence of the human body. This idea is an eternal idea in
the infinite intellect under the attribute of Thought, corresponding to the eternal
formal essence of the body under the attribute of Extension. It is nothing but a
knowledge of the human body sub specie aeternitatis.

Now the human mind enjoys actual durational existence when the formal
essence of the human body is instantiated with an actual durational existence
(E2pll, E5p23c). During that durational existence, the human mind acquires, on
top of its essential core idea, many sensory and imaginative ideas that reflect the
affections of the body as it interacts other bodies. But when the body comes to its
durational end, those sensory and imaginative ideas come to an end as well. What
is left, after death, is simply the eternal idea of the essence of the body. But notice
that what is true of the human mind and body is true of everything in nature: the
eternal idea of the eternal formal essence of a body is there both before and after

that is, that part of the mind that is eternal. Spinoza does say that “the eternal part
of the mind is the intellect” (E5p40c). But what he means is not the intellect as
some personal faculty. “The essence of our mind consists only in knowledge,
Spinoza claims (E5p36s). The intellect is simply a collection of ideas—or, more
precisely, the idea of the formal essence of the human body, along with whatever
other true and adequate ideas have been acquired in this lifetime—and that is all
that remains.

This is not a doctrine of the immortality of the soul, at least not in any reli-

remains eternally of the mind. It may be that the body whose formal essence is
the object of the idea that is the essential core of the mind individuates that eter-
nal idea from other eternal ideas, but it certainly is not an extension of my self in
this lifetime. There is no continuity within consciousness, since there is no
memory; Spinoza explicitly ties memory to the durational existence of the body
(E5p21). In fact, the eternal part of the mind does not constitute a conscious self
at all—it is simply a body of eternal ideas.

In other words, for Spinoza there is no personal immortality, no afterlife. There

The doctrine of the immortality of the soul may in fact be the most pernicious
of all the superstitious ideas that religious authorities encourage in their follow-
ers. The belief in an immortal self leads inexorably to a life governed by the pas-
sions of hope and fear: hope for eternal reward and fear of eternal punishment.
These emotions about the fate of one’s soul in some world-to-come will be
extremely strong and will dominate our affective lives. A person will do
anything—anything!—to earn that divine reward and avoid that divine punish-
ment. Meanwhile, preachers and rabbis say that they know precisely what one
needs to do to achieve the desired eternal outcome. The result of this combustible
combination of desperation among ordinary folks, superstitious beliefs, and
deception perpetrated by ministers is uncritical obedience, even servility by the
former to the latter. People throw themselves at the mercy of clerical authorities
and allow them to dictate their behavior and run their lives and even their polity.
The upshot is not only psychological bondage to the passions, where desire is
guided not by reason but by the emotions of hope and fear, but a social and polit-
ical bondage as well. Moses Mendelssohn seems to be expressing this same
insight when he says that the “inadmissible idea of the eternality of punishment
in hell” is “an idea the abuse of which has made not many fewer men truly miser-
able in this life than it renders, in theory, unhappy in the next.”

Spinoza’s point is that there is no afterlife, there are no eternal rewards or pun-
ishments. Virtue does indeed have its rewards, but they are entirely in this dur-
ational life and they are perfectly natural: happiness, equanimity in the face of
fortune, and “true peace of mind” (E5p40s). A person who sees things through
reason or intellect, and who thereby knows the necessity that governs all things in
nature, including his/her own mental life, will experience a weakening of the pas-
sions and a greater degree of activeness, freedom, and self-control. As for “bless-
edness” and “salvation,” these consist not in our selection for assumption to
another realm, but simply in the intellectual love of God and the joy that cogni-
tive condition represents in the here and now (E5p40).

bondage that keeps us from lives of freedom. By undermining the belief in

4. Conclusion

Despite efforts by some scholars to treat the Ethics and the Theological-Political
Treatise as only tangentially related works, even going so far as to ignore one in
dealing with the other, the two treatises are essentially complementary parts of
the same theological-political project. The TTP, in effect, draws out in a more
explicit manner the theological, religious, and political implications of what the
Ethics has established about God, nature, the human being, and society. What
Spinoza wants to see is a politics of hope (for eternal reward) and fear (of eternal
punishment) replaced by a politics of reason, virtue, freedom, and true moral
behavior. The TTP and the Ethics, while written in very different styles and
addressed to somewhat different audiences, each makes its own particular contri-
bution to this goal. It is thus not so much a new project that Spinoza has taken on
when he put aside the Ethics in the summer of 1665, but rather a continuation of
the same overall mission albeit from a different direction and by different means.”

1. Introduction

In E2p47, Spinoza states that “the human mind has adequate knowledge of God’s
eternal and infinite essence,’ a point he reiterates in the associated scholium in

intuitive. How can Spinoza seriously claim that God’s essence is known to “all”?
Both the Judaism he was raised in and the Calvinism he was surrounded by are,
each in their fashion, deeply attached to the idea of an inscrutable divinity, the
idea of a God unknown. Perhaps, then, he did not refer to what others call God,
but only to his own conception? The claim appears only more extravagant for it.

this is what God is.

not knowing what God is, but in the fact that we have been distracted from this

example, we “wander from the true knowledge of God” when “feign[ing] a God,

wherein they acquire new, adequate meaning.” Throughout the Ethics, Spinoza
thus deploys his own terminological framework within the structures of the basic
geometrical demonstration formed by the definitions, axioms, propositions, and
demonstrations. The scholia, by contrast, are frequently polemical, and engage

4. The Gestalt Switch: Most Simple Notions and the
Intuition of God

The consideration of Spinoza’s linguistic strategies does not, however, suffice to
solve all difficulties in E2p47 and its scholium. As already noted, Spinoza’s con-
tention that human beings do “not have so clear a knowledge of God as they do of
the common notions” (E2p47s) suggests not only, as shown in the previous sec-
tion, that much of our discourse about “God” refers to an imaginary, inadequate
idea. It also suggests that whatever true, adequate idea about God we can acquire
will not be a common notion, but an intuition. This is hardly surprising, for it is
difficult to see what God could really have something in common with given that
God is unique (Elp14). Moreover, it is not clear how there could be any common
notion of God’s constituents, the attributes, since Spinoza establishes from very
early on that those attributes “have nothing in common” (Elp2). But if God is not
known adequately by a common notion, this only leaves one option, namely, that
God is known adequately by intuition. The point finds some further confirm-
ation in E2p47s, when Spinoza declares that, from the fact that we possess an
adequate idea of God, it follows that we can deduce “a great many things which
we know adequately, and so can form that third kind of knowledge of which we
spoke in P40S2 and of whose excellence and utility we shall speak in Part V”
According to E2p40s2, this third kind of knowledge—i.e. intuitive knowledge—
“proceeds from an adequate idea of the formal essence of certain attributes of
God to the adequate knowledge of the essence of things” (E2p40s2). Spinoza, of
course, does not explicitly say that the knowledge of the essence of God's attri-
butes from which the third kind of knowledge proceeds is itself of the third and
not the second kind. But given that knowledge by reason and common notions is
never knowledge of singular essences (E2p37), and that Spinoza here speaks of
knowledge that proceeds from the essence of God to the essence of things, the
knowledge of God in question here must be intuitive. Similarly, the “adequate
knowledge of God’s eternal and infinite essence” that Spinoza has in mind in
E2p47 and E2p47s must therefore be intuitive.”* God simply cannot be known by
reason and common notions.

the necessary and self-evident qualities of mathematical knowledge,” but also
hints at the role that geometrical exposition can play in restructuring the ways we

Now, the common notions we have of extension or thought as universal, sim-

believe that knowledge of extension and thought is knowledge of God in some
sense, because both extension and thought express God’s essence as attributes of
the unique substance.** However, when considered as common notions, the
notions of extension and thought procure us knowledge of the properties of
things, namely knowledge of simple properties that all things have in common.
And yet, as Spinoza puts it in ADN 6, we can somehow “connect” these simple
notions to “those pertaining to the divine nature” (G II 252-3/C II 156). The
challenge is to understand exactly what that “connection” consists in, and how we
get from knowledge of things to knowledge of God.

My suggestion is that the “connection” in question is established by the short-
est inferential path possible because the difference between the two kinds of
notions is merely an aspectual one. The notions of extension and thought qua
simple properties of things, on the one hand, and the notions of extension and
thought qua expressions of the essence of God, on the other, are exactly the same
only perceived under two distinct aspects, namely, under the aspect of reason and
common notions, and under the aspect of intuition and singular essences. Under
one aspect, extension and thought are simple common notions in the sense of
simple, universal properties through which all things are conceived. This is how
extension and thought are grasped under the aspect of reason, as knowledge of
the second kind pertaining to what is common to all things. Under another
aspect, however, thought and extension are attributes conceived through them-
selves that express the essence of God. On this understanding, all things are con-
ceived as infinitely many modes that follow from this unique essence. This is how

5. Conclusion

Knowledge of God can be inadequate but is in that case imaginary knowledge,
and knowledge of God only in name. Or knowledge of God can be adequate and,
in that case, it is intuitive knowledge of the divine essence. In E2p47, Spinoza
claims that all human beings possess such intuitive knowledge of God, although,
in the accompanying scholium, he does allow that we do not always correctly
identify it, distracted as we are by prejudices about the divine nature. God, how-
ever, is never known by reason. This is because reason is knowledge of common
notions and since God is unique, there cannot be a common notion of God.*®
And yet, in order to overcome our prejudices about the reference of the name
“God, and eventually “connect” with our intuitive knowledge of what God really
is, we must go via reason. For it is by means of reason and common notions that
we obtain the kind of knowledge about natural things that will ultimately allow us
to grasp the nature of the being that produced them all. E2p47, in its seemingly
extraordinary epistemological optimism, thus points to a path for the intellect
seeking to reconnect with the true knowledge of God it in fact already possesses,
but does not yet know how to recognize. In order to achieve that, the use of lan-
guage must first be purged of the common prejudices lingering in its very struc-
ture, in the imaginary ways in which we assign meaning to words. Only in this
way will we come to realize that what we usually call God and that we cannot
really know, is in fact not God. Instead, something that we all know but usually
do not call God, is in fact God. Second, we must pay attention to the simplest and
most common notions we have about things because it is them, and not notions
about the extraordinary and the supernatural that we tend to emphasize when
speaking of God, which will bring us closer to God. These most simple
