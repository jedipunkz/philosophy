Introduction

At the heart of Spinoza’s Tractatus Politicus (TP) is an apparent tension
On the one hand, he criticizes those political philosophers who write about
men not as they are but as they wish them to be. On the other hand, he
writes a treatise on politics with the goal of improving the collective
condition of men. The goal, presumably, is to write about men as they
actually are and use this knowledge to make politics as effective as possible
But how is it possible to achieve this goal without projecting at least some
ideals? The issue is complicated by a question about Spinoza’s own political
biography. His earlier work the Tractatus Theologico-Politicus (TTP) was
an attempt to intervene in a political crisis in the Netherlands. Although
it was a plea for toleration, it failed to achieve its goals. The book was
immediately attacked — mainly due to the claim that its early enlight-
enment critique of religion undermined the foundations of moral and
political virtue — and the political party he supported was ultimately over-
thrown. Spinoza worked on the TP in the period leading up to and after
the collapse of the republican government. It appears to be less of F
intervention and more of a sober account of the nature and aries
political authority. Was Spinoza’s criticism of idealistic politics in the T’
a form of historical self-critique, looking back at the TTP’s failure? Or Wis
the TTP’s political theory already consistent with this insight? In order :

The first problem arises in the opening sections of the TP when Spinoza
criticizes the political philosophers who “conceive men not as they are, but
as they wish them to be.”’ Politicians tend to be too fearful of the men they
lead, but overall their writings are superior to those of philosophers because
they are at least based on experience (1.2). Spinoza implicitly asks the
question of how it is possible for a philosopher like himself to write
about men as they actually are, something that seems to require a very
different kind of knowledge from that which he possesses.

The second problem arises in the beginning of the fifth chapter, when
Spinoza reminds us that “man is most his own master when he is most led
by reason, and hence ... that that Commonwealth is most powerful
and most its own master which is founded on and directed by reason”
(5.1; I11/294-5)- However, while the civil order is based on the right of the
sovereign, which in turn follows from the coordination of individual
natural right, Spinoza is quite clear throughout that this right is not
based upon reason. Hence there is a tension between the organization of
a state according to right — what is — and the organization of the state
according to reason — what would be optimal [optime], or, in Curley’s
translation, “the best way” (5.1; II/295). This general problem or tension is
repeated throughout the remaining chapters, each devoted to a discussion
of a particular constitutional form. Spinoza appears to discuss the civil
orders not as they actually are, but rather as they ought to be, outlining
what A. G. Wernham calls “ideal constitutions.”* Is Spinoza not violating
his very own injunction to speak of politics realistically, in terms of men as
they actually are and not as we would want them to be?

Avery difficult problem arises: How is it possible for men in the state of
nature to create a covenant and overcome the collective action problem
produced when self-interested men are asked to transfer their power to an
entity more powerful than any one or more of them for the sake of their

what he judges to be good, unless he hopes for a greater good, or fears

elsewhere, this does not solve the problem by itself.’+ The difficulty is that
changing individual calculations about self-interest requires the very
thing that the process of contracting is supposed to produce: namely, the
existence of a stable authority that coordinates actions through enforcing
tules or laws. This difficulty points out an important limitation in the
social contract account. Although it models the ideal outcome, it does not
explain how the transformation takes place. More on this in a moment.
