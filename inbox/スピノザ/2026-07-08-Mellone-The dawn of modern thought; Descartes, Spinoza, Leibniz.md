INTRODUCTORY NOTE

THE hundred and twenty years that intervened between
the birth of Descartes and the death of Leibniz may
fairly be said, in the realms both of philosophy and
mathematics, to have witnessed the dawn of modern
thought. It is true that the recent researches of Pro-
fessor Etienne Gilson and others have shown that
Descartes’ philosophy was not the entirely new de-
parture which it has sometimes been supposed to be,
but owed much to the Schoolmen; and that Professor
Roth has been able to prove a large debt of Spinoza to
Maimonides. But no research can impugn the essential
originality of these thinkers. Every reader feels the
freshness of Descartes’ outlook, of his spirit which
questions everything not for the sake of questioning or
of scoring dialectical victories over dogmatism, but in
order to see what truths there are which survive all
questioning and can be made the starting-points of
philosophy. Every reader can see, too, that in him the
Platonic type of thought which moves on the borderline
of philosophy and mathematics makes discoveries of
surpassing originality and importance. And though the
materials of Spinoza’s thought may perhaps all be found
in his predecessors, the white heat of his intense spirit
has fused them into a whole which is genuinely new,
and which still remains and perhaps will always remain
the most classic expression of monism. Leibniz again
carried on to new and dazzling triumphs the half-
philosophical, half-mathematical thought which had
been characteristic of Descartes, and becomes in his
turn one of the most classic exponents of pluralism.
The three thinkers, together with distinguished Car-
tesians like Arnauld and Malebranche, make the seven-
teenth century one of the great periods of philosophy,

and one apart from which the later developments of
philosophy cannot be understood.

Dr. Mellone’s book offers to general readers in-
terested in philosophy, as well as to University students,
a sympathetic and at the same time critical account of
the leading activities and ideas of these great thinkers,
such as may prepare the way for more elaborate com-
mentaries and criticisms; and his bibliography tells
them where they may look for further guidance. A
book like this, which limits itself to giving a moderately
full account of a particular period, should appeal to
many readers who find the effect of general histories of
philosophy confusing and benumbing.

In this book we shall describe the essential achieve-
ments of three great thinkers, whose work belongs to
‘the century of genius’. ‘The seventeenth century is not
unjustly so named. It is the one century which through-
out the whole range of human activities provided
intellectual genius adequate to the greatness of its
opportunities. The harvest of the gradual transition,
from the outlook of the Middle Ages to that of the
modern world, was ripe for the ingathering; and men
like Francis Bacon, Harvey, Kepler, Galileo, Descartes,
Pascal, Newton, Locke, Spinoza, Leibniz, arose to
carry on the work.

During the fourteenth, fifteenth, and sixteenth
centuries, the European nations were learning to throw
off the influences which hitherto had guided their
religious, their scientific, their political life. The
religious authority of the Church, and the intellectual
authority of Aristotle as expounded by the great
theologians of the Church, appeared no longer as
welcome guides but rather as encroaching tyrannies.

This spirit, strengthened by the re-discovery of the
literature and art of classical antiquity, and by the
impulse to national self-assertion, was crossed by and
partly mingled with the religious movement represented
by Luther. But the influence of Luther’s movement
went much farther than the aims which Luther himself
had at heart. He advocated the abolition of institutions
for the cultivation of formal asceticism because to him

i. His Life and Personality.

RENE DescarTeEs was born on March 31st, 1596, at La
Haye, a small town in Touraine, on the River Creuse,
about midway between ‘Tours and Poitiers; but his
childhood was chiefly spent in Brittany, where his father
was Conseiller au Parlement at Rennes. His compat-
riots reckoned him a Breton, although his family tree
was firmly rooted in Poitou, and he considered himself a
Poitevin. Never was there a man for whom such details
were less important. No theories of ‘heredity’ or ‘en-
vironment’ can do anything to explain that indepen-
dent, abstract, yet eager mind.

Descartes, however, was born of a mother apparently
consumptive who died young during his infancy,
leaving him a legacy of enfeebled health, so that the
doctors half despaired of rearing him. His father,
anxious to surround the child with every care, entrusted
his education to the Jesuits, who brought him up in
their newly established College at La Fléche. Of the
education there given, of the equality maintained among
the pupils, and of their free intercourse, Descartes at a
later period spoke in terms of high praise.

He was a brilliant scholar, learning with ease and
avidity and carrying all before him. Having left school, he
appears to have intended to follow his father’s profession
of the law; but nothing came of it. After four years in
Paris—partly occupied with social life and partly with
solitary work at mathematics—he set out, at the age of

twenty-one, for the Netherlands, and took service under
Prince Maurice of Orange, son of William the Silent, and
one of the greatest generals of the age. Soon afterwards,
desiring to see more of the world, he joined the army of
the Duke of Bavaria, and later of the Emperor, and
followed more than one campaign. But he found no
satisfaction in military service, and fell into a state of
despondency in which nothing seemed worth while;
until one day, reading on a wall a placard advertising a
mathematical problem—according to the custom of
those days—and challenging any one to solve it,
Descartes solved it almost at sight. ‘The pleasure of the
discovery dispelled his dull depression: and soon after-
wards a night of strange dreams, which he believed to be
heaven-sent, confirmed him in his resolve to seek truth
within and not without. He left the service of the
Emperor in 1621, but did not immediately abandon his
travels, although the whole perspective of his life was
changed. He visited Rome, and lived for some time in
Paris; then he sold his estates in Poitou, and in his
thirty-third year settled at a quiet country house in
Holland, where he might live undisturbed; for he was
persuaded that his principal duty was to think. His
work betrays no sign of interest in history, literature, or
art; he looked only to the actual aspects of human life
and the phenomena of nature, and his wisdom grew out
of his own reflections and experiments, calmly yet
ceaselessly pursued.

Between the ages of thirty-three and fifty-three
Descartes lived almost entirely in Holland, moving
more than once from one to another of the Dutch .
manors that he loved. He never separated himself from

His second great work was published in Paris in
August 1641 under the title Meditationes de Prima
Philosophia. In it he expounded more in detail the

the priority of mind to body, and on the absolute

Principia Philosophiae, appeared at Amsterdam. Passing
briefly over the conclusions arrived at inthe Meditations
it deals in its second, third, and fourth parts with Laws
of Motion and the general principles of what we should
now call Physics. ‘The work exhibits some curious marks
of caution; the Copernican theory, for example, is re-
jected in name but retained in substance.

Shortly after the death of Descartes his friends

Principia, and entitled Les passions de ’adme. 'This is an

During these years of concentrated activity, a number

u. The Cartesian Method

The result of the mathematical studies to which
Descartes had been led by temperament and genius—

and in particular the result of his careful reflections on
the method of geometrical proof—was that he proposed
to introduce the same method into scientific inquiry in
general. It is essentially the method which aims at
knowing exactly what its initial assumptions are and
what they mean, and at deducing their consequences
exactly and in the right order.

The rules given in the second section of the Discours
de la Méthode are of oracular brevity; and even in their
expanded form in the later Regulae ad Directionem
Ingenu they must be read in the light of the author’s
original and independent mathematical studies, and in
particular of the Géométrie, which was published in the
same year (1637) as the Discours, though it was the result
of much earlier work.

Long before the publication of the Discours, Des-
cartes had made known to his friends the principles of
his new Analytic Geometry, in which he reduced form
to magnitude. As in geography we refer places on the
earth’s surface to the equator and a definite meridian,
so he referred every point in a curve to one or more lines
whose position is given. For example, in a circle, the
perpendicular to the diameter from any point in the
circumference, and the distance of that perpendicular
from the centre, are two lengths which vary for every
different point in the circumference, but have a definite
relation to each other which is the same for all such
points. This constant relation can be expressed in the
form of an algebraical equation, and then serves as a
definition of the curve, from which its properties can be
deduced. Analytic Geometry thus provides a great gain
in simplicity and generality, and forms an example,

though only one example, of the general nature of
mathematical science.

The fundamental idea of mathematics Descartes
finds to be that of order. In other words, the objects of
this kind of knowledge fall into series, of which the
members are known by means of one another. In every
such series or group there is a dominant or constitutive
element, simple and irreducible, the standard on which
the rest of the series depends, and hence ‘absolute’ so far
as that group or series is concerned. ‘The other mem-
bers of the group are dependent, and only to be under-
stood as in various ways subordinate to the dominant
principle. Now we may abstract the relation of order
from the special subject-matter under investigation, and
thus we may construct a universal mathematics, a most
general science of order, dealing with all its implica-
tions, and superior in generality to the particular
branches of the subject as usually treated. Descartes
gave the title Géométrie to the Essay in which he laid
the foundations of this general science; but he deals
with principles whose applicability is not limited to
geometry. In fact he thought of them as not limited to
mathematics at all, in the ordinary meaning of the term,
but as the foundation of an all-embracing science. Not
only mathematical objects but all the objects of our
knowledge fall under the conception of Order as defined
above. \|It is clear that the primary problem is to
recognize the fundamental or constitutive element in the
group or series before us. It is recognized by its
intuitive or self-evident character; in other words, it is
given by ‘the evident conception of a healthy and
attentive mind, so clear and distinct that no doubt

remains’) Having discovered the fundamental element
in the group, we proceed to investigate the different
ways in which the other members of the group enter
into relation with it and depend upon it. Here deduc-
tion comes into play to show the dependence of one
term upon the others; and in the case of a long chain of
intervening links, the problem for intelligence is so to
grasp every element, and so to repeat the connexion,
that we may finally grasp all the links of the chain in one.
In this way we bring the fundamental or causal factor
and its remotest dependent factor immediately together,
and attain a systematic knowledge, derived from know-
ledge that is primary and intuitive.

This idea of Intuition is vital to the understanding of
the Cartesian method, and is the basis of the Rules given
in the Discours and the Regulae. ‘Things differ in
complexity and simplicity, in their dependence and in-
dependence, in derivation and immediacy. When we
have found something which as it were resists decom-
position, something apprehended so clearly and dis-
tinctly that the mind cannot divide it into any parts
which can be more distinctly known, then we have
found a primary, fundamental, and causal factor. Such,
in the Cartesian view, is extension, in the case of
corporeal things, with its modifications of figure and
movement. ‘These ‘simple natures’ are seen by a direct
vision exempt from illusion and error; and from these
we can in our understanding reconstruct the more
complex objects before us.

One in its nature, always identical with itself in its
procedure, the method is one in its applications: it will
not differ whether it is concerned with mental or

material facts. All the sciences collectively are nothing
else than the intelligence of man, always one and the
same, however various are the objects to which it is
applied. Sciences the most diverse are only factors in a
single organic whole. Nevertheless, in the Cartesian
view, the unity of the method does not necessarily imply
the fundamental unity of things: far from it. If at the
end of the analysis the mind finds itself in the presence
of a plurality of simple notions, irreducible to one
another, then it will really be in the presence of things
irreducible and distinct.

iii. The Material Universe.

The next stage, beyond the simplicity of general
mathematics, is that of general physics. Descartes finds
that ancient and medieval physics neglected the con-
ception of natural Law, and sought to explain pheno-
mena by super-phenomenal principles, such as ‘sub-
stantial forms’. In his view, this was really to assume a
second world, a useless doubling of the world of
perception. On the other hand, the scientific investiga-
tors of his own age, Galileo, Harvey, Vesalius, and
others, had indeed made very important particular dis-
coveries and formulated laws on this basis, but there
was not yet a general theory of physics.

We have seen that the Cartesian method requires
the resolution of every object into the most simple,
clear, and distinct conceptions beyond which the mind
cannot go. In the case of corporeal things, this is to
resolve them into the mathematical elements of exten-
sion, figure, and movement. In his third Meditation
Descartes explains fully how he is led to this conclusion.

THE MATERIAL UNIVERSE 23

In the experience of corporeal objects he finds only a
limited number of conditions which he can conceive
clearly and distinctly: these are, magnitude, or rather
extension, in three dimensions, figures formed by
limiting this extension, positions or situations which
bodies of varying figure hold in relation to one another,
and movement or change of position; ‘to which may be
added, substance, duration, and number.’ As to the
other qualities, light, colour, sound, odour, taste, heat,
cold, and other qualities which enter into our experience :
they involve (when thought about) so much obscurity
and confusion that he is not prepared to say whether
they are true or false.

This conception of matter as consisting essentially of
extension in three dimensions has far-reaching con-
sequences in the Cartesian philosophy of Nature. In
some directions it led Descartes to take up positions
now long since abandoned; in other directions it led
him on to brilliant anticipations of theories now generally
accepted as sound.

There can be no such thing as empty space. All that
we mean when we speak of a place as empty is, that it has
not its usual contents. A cup, for instance, is said to be
‘empty’ when it has ‘nothing in it’ though it is full of
air. Descartes dwells on such considerations in support
of the theory, deduced directly from his definition of
matter, that a vacuum is inconceivable. In the same
way he concludes that matter, as identified with
extension, is not resolvable into ultimate atoms, but is
infinitely divisible; that it is homogeneous throughout
the universe; and that its apparent varieties are dis-
tinguished only by the different division and movement

During the Reunion discussions, Leibniz, being, as

or, as Spinoza might have said, the adequate Idea of
each person, contains in itself the reasons for every
event that happens through him or to him, and the
reasons for the order in which these things happen.
What he will do at any time is certain to the mind of
God, not merely by a miraculous foreknowledge but
because there are ‘sufficient reasons’ for the act.
Nevertheless Leibniz urges repeatedly that these
‘sufficient reasons’, though they incline the agent to
the choice which he makes, involve no compulsion of
necessity. Arnauld on the other hand declared that
this is a doctrine of fatalism; the individual’s whole

and Leibniz argued the question with him at great

complete exposition of the rules or laws by which these

called Symbolic or Mathematical Logic.

iv. The Monadology.

We are now in a position to state the essentials of the
famous doctrine which Leibniz afterwards called the
Monadology. Accepting the view that Substance can
be conceived only as Energy and, after the analogy of
our own conscious psychical life, only as a living
individuality; and calling any such individuality a
Monad, as Leibniz afterwards did—this term being
preferable to ‘Substance’ and free from misleading
association—we find the conclusion to be that an
unbounded multiplicity of such Monads constitutes the
basis of reality, the fundamental being of the whole
physical and spiritual universe. Just as the mind does
not consist of ‘parts’ separated from one another by
something which is not mind (as islands are by water),
so the Monad has no ‘parts’ capable of separation or
division from one another. No dissolution of it is
possible. It can only come into being or come to an end
by supernatural creation in the one case or supernatural
annihilation in the other. As living individualities, each
capable of evolving its nature and experience from
itself, no two Monads are exactly alike; for two beings
to be exactly alike in every respect, and yet remain two,
would (according to Leibniz) be inconsistent with the
plan of a rational world.

contradiction. It is easy to be unjust to Wolff, as many
historians have been. His work showed little specula-
tive power, and its methodical and systematic form
does not conceai the lack of depth and insight. His
ideal, however, was a fine one, to make philosophy
embrace in its survey the whole field of human know-
ledge; his insistence on clear and systematic forms of
exposition, and his confidence in the power of reason to
reduce all subjects to this form, are merits of no mean
order. He had many ‘disciples’, and his philosophy
held almost indisputed sway in Germany until it was
displaced by the new way opened up by Immanuel
Kant.

CONCLUSION

In these pages we have surveyed the work of three
master-builders, ‘whose mighty designs’, to adapt a few
words of John Locke, have left ‘lasting monuments to
the admiration of posterity’. These famous construc-
tions appear in a different light, according as we take
them for works of speculative genius, or as we examine
their logical foundations.

In the hands of Descartes, Spinoza, and Leibniz, the
perennial problems of philosophy are given a form and
setting distinctive of modern thought; and their
treatment of these problems brings to light the funda-
mental divergences which have to be reconciled in any
rational view of the world. Above all is this true of the
contrast between the monism of Spinoza (qualified by his
assumption of the conatus as the secret of individuality)
and the pluralism of Leibniz (qualified by his assump-
tion of the ‘pre-established harmony’). The task of
reconciliation is necessarily a difficult one, and fails to
produce any result which is strictly conclusive in the eye
of reason. But it does not follow that we ought to
abandon the pursuit of philosophic truth and content
ourselves with science and its applications to practice,
even when science is conceived in its widest sense; and
the thinkers of the seventeenth century inspire us to
resist all attempts, whether in the interest of a positive
or of a negative creed, to fetter the speculative impulse
which is inherent in the higher races of mankind.

When, however, we examine the logical foundations of
these works another aspect of the situation comes to

light. The historical forms which these systems
actually took, and the methods actually used in working
them out, were at bottom various expressions of the
entire confidence of Descartes in the ‘clearness and
distinctness’ of our Ideas as a criterion of truth. ‘This
confidence tempted his successors to philosophical
constructions of a kind which forced on thoughtful
minds the previous question—the question of the
validity and extent of the knowledge contained in such
clear and distinct ideas. ‘This was the question dealt
with by Locke in view of the practical pre-occupations
of his cautious English mind, and by Kant in view of
the problems raised and not solved by the over-con-
fident rationalism of his age. Thus was opened up a
new channel for the speculative impulse of the Western
European mind, based upon a more adequate analysis
of the foundations of knowledge.

BIBLIOGRAPHY

THE literature on Descartes, Spinoza, and Leibniz, is of
immense extent. The books named below are selected as
being for the most part of comparatively recent date, and as
themselves providing guidance for further and more elaborate
study.

DESCARTES

An historical account of Cartesianism, by E. Boutroux, will
be found in the Cambridge Modern History, vol. iv, ch. xxvii;
and much biographical and historical information is given
in Descartes, his Life and Times, by Elizabeth S. Haldane
(1905). Prof. N. Kemp Smith’s Studies in the Cartesian
Philosophy (1902) is a valuable introduction; and we have an
English translation, The Philosophical Works of Descartes, by
E. S. Haldane and G. R. T. Ross (2 vols., 1911-12).

The best critical work on Descartes, in recent years, is in
French. We may mention: Le Systéme de Descartes, by
Octave Hamelin (Paris, 1911); Index scolastico-cartésien
(Paris, 1913), by E. Gilson (specially interesting as showing
the links between Descartes and medieval thought); La
Liberté chez Descartes et la Théologie (Paris, 1913), by E.
Gilson; La pensée religieuse de Descartes (Paris, 1924), by
H. Gouhier; René Descartes, Discours de la Méthode, texte et
commentaire (Paris, 1925), by E. Gilson.

The standard edition is Les Giuvres de Descartes, edited by
C. Adam and P. Tannery (12 vols., Paris, completed 1910);
of which vol. xii contains a full biography by M. Adam.

Arnauld’s collected works occupy 42 volumes, chiefly theo-
logical. Among his philosophical writings, the work Des vraies
et des fausses Idées (published separately, Cologne, 1683) is
of interest in connexion with present-day realistic doctrines
of sense-perception. From the writings of Malebranche we
have an English translation of the Entretiens Métaphysiques
by M. Ginsberg: Dialogues on Metaphysics and Religion (1923),
with Introduction by G. Dawes Hicks.

SPINOZA

Prof. A. Wolf’s translation of the Short Treatise on God,
Man and his Welfare (1910) contains a biography as well as
critical notes, and forms a useful introduction to Spinoza’s
later thought. Additional biographical information is given
in the same writer’s edition (1927) of The Oldest Biography
of Spinoza. An English translation of The Correspondence of
Spinoza, with introduction and notes, by Prof. Wolf, was
published in the same year. The best English version of the
Ethics is by W. Hale White, revised by A. Hutchison Stirling
(fourth edition, Oxford and London, 1910). The best French
translation (based on recent studies of the text) is by Ch.
Appuhn, Les Giuvres de Spinoza (second ed., 3 vols., Paris,
1928-9), not including the Ethics, published separately by
M. Appuhn with Latin text and French version. The best
German translation is by C. Gebhardt and others (3 vols.,
Heidelberg, 1922). Among the many critical studies we may
mention: H. H. Joachim, A Study of the Ethics of Spinoza
(1901); Leon Roth, Spinoza, Descartes, and Maimonides
(1924) and Spinoza (1929); R. Mackeon, The Philosophy of
Spinoza (1928); L. Brunschvicg, Spinoza (1906) and Spinoza
et ses Contemporains (1923); R. Lévéque, Le Probléme de la
Vérité dans la Philosophie de Spinoza (1923). The most elab-
orate work on the man and his thought is by J. Freudenthal,
Spinoza, Leben und Lehre (second ed., edited by C. Gebhardt,
2 vols., Heidelberg, 1927).

The standard edition is Spinoza, Opera, edited by C.
Gebhardt, 4 vols., Heidelberg, completed 1924), with apparatus
criticus.

LEIBNIZ

A convenient introduction to the study of Leibniz will be
found in Leibniz, the Monadology and other Philosophical
Writings, translated, with annotations, by Prof. R. Latta (new
edition, Oxford and London, 1925), together with Prof.
H. W. Carr’s Leibniz (1929), in which the relation of the
Leibnitian system to recent physical science is discussed. For

German, Leibniz, by W. Wundt (Leipzig, 1917, on the bi-

A fuller historical account is given by Kuno Fischer, G. W.
Leibniz, Leben Werke und Lehre (Kéthen, 1902). Among the
