## PDF Text

Daniel Conway Nietzsche’s On the Genealogy of Morals: A Reader’s Guide (New York, New York: Continuum, 2008). Simply put, Conway’s Nietzsche’s On the Genealogy of Morals: A Reader’s Guide, is the best commentary on Nietzsche’s work of the same name in the secondary literature. Conway’s deep knowledge of Nietzsche in general, and of The Genealogy in particular, is evident as Conway leads a majestic excursion on each section of the German philosopher’s most systematic and argumentative work. The book is well-organized and is the ideal companion for the advanced undergraduate or graduate student. Conway’s tour de force not only provides readers with an appreciation of the general theses of The Genealogy as a whole but, also gives students a clear, detailed elucidation of the goals, arguments, and purpose of each section in particular. Conway is brilliant in showing how every section of The Genealogy is put to work towards a more general strategy to destroy, in the words of Conway, “… Christian morality and inaugurate the extra-moral period of history.” (7) Another helpful feature of the work, especially for undergraduate students, is the Study Question sections that appear at the end of each chapter. These questions help to extract the key takeaways from each of the three essays that make up The Genealogy, as well as provoking students to apply the many lessons of The Genealogy to their own lives. For example, in the Study Question section of essay two, Conway poses the following query: “How might Nietzsche’s readers turn their ‘bad conscience and ascetic heritage to their own advantage?” (100) Probing questions like this one force Conway’s audience to appreciate that reading The Genealogy is not just another “knowledge-gathering routine” (19) but is, rather, a truly educational, transformative and edifying experience. Conway’s commentary is incisive and illuminating: he is very good at revealing nuggets of insight one might miss if not for his penetrating analysis. A few sections stand out as exemplary
interpretations that reveal Conway’s deep reflection on the text and his mastery of Nietzsche’s corpus. One of the more impressive feats is Conway’s analysis of Essay I section 16, a passage, that very often is read at face-value: “The symbol of this struggle, (nobles vs. slave values) inscribed in letters legible across all human history, is Rome against Judea, Judea against Rome.” (GM: I 16). A face-value, non-nuanced interpretation would entail that Nietzsche is espousing a reductive view of history where, according to Conway: “the ideals and active forces of ‘Rome’ have been exemplified, in succession, by the Roman Empire, the Renaissance, the counter-reformation, the ancien regime in France and Napoleon; while the ideals and reactive forces of ‘Judea’ have been furthered by Christianity, the Reformation, the French Revolution and its aftermath.” (49) The temptation, Conway explains, is that, given Nietzsche’s prior analysis of noble and slave cultures, one may infer that this sign, Rome vs. Judea is to be thought of as a fitting symbol, a graffito, which one may truthfully emboss on Western history itself. However, this would be a mistake, so thinks Conway. This interpretation is not what Nietzsche’s is advancing in this section. Rather his intention may be, Conway surmises, to waylay certain readers who prove themselves ineducable to the deeper teachings of the genealogical method. These teachings, (all of which are well-explained by Conway) include the attention to the bottomless gray of history, the importance of oppositional difference and, perhaps most important, the rule of conceptual transformation, which, as Conway illuminates, is “a concept denoting political superiority always resolves itself into a concept denoting superiority of soul.” (32). The true meaning of this symbol, Conway speculates, may be to demonstrate the ontological tension between what appear to be separate, distinct and ineradicable forces but which are really co-constitutive and co-relational points existing on the same continuum. As Conway
instructively writes in this regard: “The slave revolt in morality may have originated with the priestly Jews of Judea, but it did so only at the instigation of the Roman imperial presence.” (50) Another practical feature of Conway’s analysis of essay one is his helpful and substantively robust listing of the chief characteristics of slave and noble moralities. Typically, commentators on Nietzsche do not go into great depth when it comes to explaining the qualities of these two moral types. At best they might claim that noble types are active, spontaneous and singular individuals who act on their instinctual drives whereas slave values are reactive; they represent an inversion of noble values and are held by the herd and do not originate from discrete individuals. Not so for Conway. Conway carefully and systematically explains what each group values as well as why they value it. For example, Conway exclaims that whereas noble morality exists and expresses itself independently of the condition of the world it encounters, the slave morality “always first needs a hostile external world.” (40). Conway insightfully continues, “As a result, the slave morality can never credibly wish for, much less work toward, a cessation of hostility directed against it.” (40). This last point is not immediately obvious upon reading Nietzsche’s analysis of these two groups. In making this point clearly, Conway concretizes to his audience, in no uncertain terms, the full measure and consequence of slave morality. I would like to draw the reader’s attention to an intriguing analysis of two rich passages: Conway’s interpretation of sections 12 and 13 of Essay III. The commentary provided by Conway is incredibly revealing, and best exemplifies Conway’s piercing and enlightening interpretative analysis. In a much commented on section of Nietzsche’s Genealogy, Nietzsche expounds his metaphysical and epistemic position of perspectivism. He writes: “There is only a perspective seeing, only a perspective “knowing”; and the more affects we allow to speak about one thing, the more eyes, different eyes, we can use to observe one thing, the more complete will our “concept”
of this thing our “objectivity”, be. (GM: III: 12). Much ink has been spilled detailing the various epistemic and ontological commitments that such a view entails and I shall not elaborate here on the many different ways in which perspectivism is construed in the secondary literature. To Conway’s credit, he does not wade too deeply into these muddy waters, but prefers, like Nietzsche, to get in and out of a potential philosophical quagmire quickly. What Conway gives us is a refreshing elucidation regarding the educational and genealogical value of perspectivism. He notes five ways in which the method can be and is employed by Nietzsche. Perhaps the most important insight is that under Conway’s reading of perspectivism, one must not simply take up a “Pro and Con” in a rather doxastic manner, but rather embody various Pros and Cons–different and conflicting perspectives. Conway explains: “He (Nietzsche) thus urges his fellow philosophers to experience–that is, to embody– ‘Pro and Con,' and then to control and dispose of them.” (116) Conway elaborates on how Nietzsche embodies a different perspective by providing a detailed exposition of Nietzsche’s physiological interpretation of the ascetic ideal in the next section (GM: III: 13). It is important for Nietzsche to view the ascetic ideal through the perspective of physiology because, prior to sections 12 and 13, he viewed the practice and value of asceticism, as one contrary to Life: “the ascetic life is affectively invested in the destruction of its own precondition, moreover, it harbors what Nietzsche elsewhere calls a will to nothingness.” (114) But, if this interpretation stood alone, without further correction, then Nietzsche’s efforts to provide a naturalized depiction of morality would be questionable because it would mean that a schism exists within life itself: the ascetic ideal is one that wills its own destruction but how, one might ask, could life produce entities and forces that act contrary to the very goal of life? Would this schism not lend credence to the idea that there really are two very different forces at work in the world, namely biological and spiritual?
Taking up a different perspective, namely the physiological, extricates Nietzsche from this paradox, so thinks Conway. From that perspective, Nietzsche can affirm that the ascetic ideal “is an artifice for the preservation of life” (GM III: 13) “…because life itself has dispatched this ideal in an effort to preserve sickly beings…” by providing them with meaning and resolve to continue their miserable existence. (117) Conway’s work offers the richest, most profound reading of Nietzsche’s Genealogy in the secondary literature. Conway’s commentary is deep, reflective and, given Conway’s obvious mastery of Nietzsche’s oeuvre remarkably constrained: in no way does the reader ever feel that the text is groaning under the strain of some ill-conceived interpretation. Nor does one get the sense that the author is providing a commentary on the Genealogy that acts merely as a backdrop to the reviewer’s peculiar, idiosyncratic view of Nietzsche. Conway provides a sober, level-headed, clear, charitable and remarkably coherent reading of the text so as to communicate, faithfully, the virtues, spirit, and theses of the Genealogy to genuine, serious readers of Nietzsche’s philosophy. Brian Lightbody Philosophy Dept. Brock University St. Catharines Ontario, Canada
