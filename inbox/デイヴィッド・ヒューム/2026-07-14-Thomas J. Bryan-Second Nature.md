PREFACE

The collection has already traced one of the two great doctrines of the modern world to its source. A
previous study followed the revolutionary lineage — from the Jacobin republic of virtue through Marx
and the canonisation of the science of history to Lenin’s vanguard and the state it built — and showed
how a situated insight became an inherited scripture that read its every failure as the unfaithfulness of
others. This volume performs the same work on the other doctrine. It is a genealogy of the market
church’s scripture: a tracing of where the idea came from that private property and the free market are
not arrangements some power built and holds, but the natural condition of humankind, the neutral
ground that appears of its own accord when force and custom step back.

That idea, the volume argues, is the deepest belief the market order possesses, because a doctrine
that has passed itself off as nature need not be defended as a choice and cannot be contested as an
imposition. And it has a history. Across five centuries the warrant for property and the market mutated
through eight forms — the gift of God to Adam; the dictate of a reason that would bind even if there
were no God; the fruit of labour and improvement; a useful human convention, admitted and then
forgotten; an iron law of population that naturalised the poverty the arrangement produced; a
mathematical equilibrium settling like a body to rest; an order grown rather than designed; and at last a
self-correcting creed — while the function never changed. The function was always to convert a made

arrangement, resting in the last instance on force, into the order of nature, beyond the reach of politics.

A word about scope, because the method is easily mistaken for a debunking and is not one. To show
that property is made rather than found is not to show that it is bad, nor that it should be abolished, nor
that any arrangement of it is unjust; a made thing may be a good thing, and an evolved institution a
beneficial one. The genealogy refuses not property but its naturalisation — the move that removes a
contestable arrangement from the realm of things a free people may argue over and installs it in the
realm of things they must simply accept. The target is the warrant, not the thing it warrants. The
method is the collection’s: load-bearing specifics verified before assertion; the genuine goods of each
thinker credited; the strongest objections — including the charge that genealogy commits the genetic
fallacy — held at full strength; and the conditions under which the verdict would fail published at the

close. The genealogy’s gift is not to abolish property but to return it to politics. Tested, not revealed.

A NOTE ON THIS COLLABORATION

This volume, like the rest of the collection, is a collaboration between a human author and an artificial
intelligence. The framework is Thomas J. Bryan’s: the reading of every durable order as a made
arrangement rather than a nature, resting in the last instance on a floor of force; the recognition that the
chief work of government is to declare who owns what, so that nearly everything else is downstream of
that primary act; the insistence that private property and the rule of law backing it are man-made
concepts and not a neutral base; and the synthesis — built across the collection’s studies of the two
churches and the petrodollar’s lever — that the market doctrine’s claim to naturalness is the oldest and
most effective of managed beliefs. The historical reconstruction, the verification of every figure, date,
and quotation, and the drafting are Claude’s (Anthropic), working under the author’s direction and to
the project’s standing rules. The truth of no religion is adjudicated; the analysis is of how each warrant
functioned. The suffering the warrants licensed — the dispossessed frontier, the workhouse, the famine
read as natural law — is held with gravity, and counted, in the collection’s discipline, as a human fact
and not an illustration.

I. The Question Under the Floor

The collection has already traced one of the two great doctrines of the modern world to its roots —
followed the revolutionary lineage from the Jacobin republic of virtue through Marx and the
canonisation of the science of history to Lenin's vanguard and the state it built, and shown how a
situated insight became an inherited scripture that read its every failure as the unfaithfulness of others.
This volume performs the same work on the other doctrine. It is a genealogy of the market church's
scripture: a tracing of where the idea came from that private property and the free market are not
arrangements that some power built and holds, but the natural condition of humankind, the neutral
ground that appears of its own accord when government and custom and force step back and let men
be. That idea is the deepest and most effective belief the market order possesses, because a doctrine
that has succeeded in passing itself off as nature need not be defended as a choice and cannot be
contested as an imposition; and the burden of this volume is to show that the idea has a history — that
it was made, and remade, by particular thinkers facing particular problems, and that its appearance of
timeless naturalness is the most historical thing about it.

The place to begin is the question that the whole doctrine answers and rarely admits it is answering:
who owns what, and by what right. Every durable order must settle this question, because the
settlement of it is the settlement of nearly everything else — who may use which land, who must work
for whom, who eats and who does not, what the law will defend and what it will let perish. The
founder of the modern doctrine said so plainly, and the volume will return to his admission because it
is more candid than most of what came after him: John Locke held that government has no other end
than the preservation of property, that the great and chief reason men leave the state of nature and
submit to a common authority is to have their possessions secured. Everything else a government does
is, on this account, downstream of that primary act — the declaration and defence of ownership. If that
is right, then the rules of property are not one feature of the social order among many but the floor on

which the rest of it stands; and the question of where those rules came from, and whose interest they

II. A Warrant That Keeps Changing Its Name

The genealogy that follows has a single shape, and naming it at the outset will let the reader follow the
long procession of thinkers without losing the thread. At every stage, the same made thing — the
private ownership of the world's land and goods, and the market through which they are exchanged —
is handed a warrant: an account of why it is legitimate, why it is binding, why it is the way things
ought to be or simply are. And at every stage the warrant does the same work, which is to convert the
made thing into a natural thing, to take an arrangement that some power built and that rests in the last
instance on force, and to present it as the order of nature, prior to and independent of any human
choice. What changes across the centuries is only the content of the warrant — the name under which
the naturalness is claimed. The arrangement was natural, first, because God gave the world to Adam
and his heirs. Then because reason, not God, dictated it, and would dictate it even if there were no
God. Then because labour created it, mixing the self into the soil. Then, in the hands of the doctrine's
subtlest critics, it was admitted to be no nature at all but a useful human convention — and then the
admission was forgotten. Then the poverty it produced was naturalised as an iron law of population.
Then the market itself was rendered as a mathematical equilibrium, a system that settles to its natural
state like a physical body coming to rest. Then as an order grown rather than made, evolved like a
language and tested by survival. And finally as a self-correcting creed, the efficient end-state of human
economic life, the neutral baseline from which every departure is an interference. Eight warrants, one
function.

A word is owed at the outset about what the genealogy does and does not claim, because the
method is easy to mistake for a debunking and it is not one. To show that property is made rather than
found is not to show that it is bad, nor that it should be abolished, nor that any particular arrangement
of it is unjust. A made thing can be a good thing; an evolved institution can be a beneficial one; the
convention of property may be, as one of its sharpest analysts believed, enormously advantageous to
nearly everyone who lives under it. What the genealogy refuses is not property but the naturalisation
of property — the move by which a contestable human arrangement, backed by force and serving some
interests more than others, is removed from the realm of things a free people may argue about and
installed in the realm of things they must simply accept, like the weather or the tides. The target,
throughout, is the warrant and not the thing it warrants; the aim is not to tear out the floor but to make
it visible as a floor that was laid, by someone, for some purpose, and may therefore be examined,
amended, and held to account. With that distinction fixed, the procession can begin, at the warrant that
came first and named itself most boldly: that the world, and the right to own it, was given by God to a

Man.

ADAM

The theological warrant in its two great forms: the English doctrine, given its fullest statement
by Sir Robert Filmer, that God granted Adam dominion over the world and made him the first
king and sole proprietor, so that property and sovereignty descended together as a divine
inheritance; and the Catholic and papal form, by which the vicar of Christ donated whole

force beneath it was holy — 1455-1680

I. Adam the First Proprietor

The first warrant for property was the boldest, because it claimed the highest possible author. In the
England of the seventeenth century the doctrine received its most thorough statement from Sir Robert
Filmer, whose Patriarcha, written in the 1620s or 1630s and published in 1680, argued that the whole
structure of ownership and rule descended from a single divine grant. God, in creating Adam, had
given him dominion over the earth and everything in it; Adam was therefore not merely the first man
but the first king and the sole proprietor of the world, holding both sovereignty and property by the gift
of his Creator. From Adam the grant passed to his heirs, through the line of the biblical patriarchs,
down to the kings of the present day, so that Charles I of England ruled, on Filmer's account, as Adam's
eldest heir, holding his crown and the lands of his realm by the same unbroken divine descent. Property
and political authority were one thing in this doctrine, both originating in God's gift to the first man,
both inherited rather than made, both placed by their divine origin entirely beyond the reach of any
human consent or contract. There was nothing to argue about, because the arrangement was not a
human arrangement at all; it was the working-out, across the generations, of a settlement God had
made at the creation.

The doctrine is easy to dismiss now, and Locke would dismiss it with contempt within a few years
of its publication, but it is worth taking seriously as the purest instance of the move the whole
genealogy tracks, because in it the naturalisation of property is total and undisguised. The warrant
claims, in so many words, that ownership is not a human institution — that it was instituted by God
before there were any humans to institute anything, and that the present distribution of land and rule is
the direct continuation of that divine act. A man who accepted this warrant could not coherently ask
whether the rules of property were just or unjust, whether they served some and harmed others,

whether they might be arranged differently; the question would be a kind of blasphemy, an attempt to

revise at the human level a settlement made at the divine one. The force that actually held the
arrangement up — the king's armies, the gallows, the dispossession of those who resisted —
disappeared entirely behind the warrant, becoming merely the lawful defence of a God-given order
rather than what it was, the floor of coercion beneath a particular distribution of the world. This is the
function every later warrant would perform in subtler form: the conversion of force into legitimacy, of
arrangement into nature, of a contestable settlement into a fact no subject could question. Filmer

simply performed it with the bluntest instrument available, the direct invocation of God.

II. The Donation of a World

The Catholic and papal form of the warrant was, if anything, more consequential, because it was not
merely a theory of why kings ruled but a working instrument for the appropriation of continents. The
reader raised it precisely: that the Pope, as the vicar of Christ, spoke for God, and that God's authority,
channelled through the Church, anointed the prince and sanctioned his dominion. The instrument was
the papal bull, and its most famous exercise was Inter Caetera, issued by Pope Alexander VI on the
fourth of May 1493, the year after Columbus returned from the western ocean. Invoking the authority
of Almighty God, the bull granted to the crown of Castile the exclusive right to the lands discovered
and to be discovered west of a line drawn through the Atlantic, with the lands to the east reserved, after
the Treaty of Tordesillas of the following year, to Portugal. A pope, claiming to speak for the Creator,
donated whole hemispheres he had never seen to the Christian princes who would conquer them —
and the bull was not an isolated act but the culmination of a series, including the Romanus Pontifex of
1455, by which Nicholas V had granted Portugal a monopoly of the African trade and authorised the
enslavement of the peoples it found. From these donations descended the Doctrine of Discovery: the
principle that the act of Christian discovery conferred a real title to the land, and that the peoples
already living on it held, at most, a right of occupancy that the discovering power could extinguish at
will.

The structure here is the same as Filmer's, transposed from the question of who shall be king to the
question of who shall own the New World, and it shows the warrant doing its work at the largest
possible scale. A made arrangement — the seizure of two continents by European powers,
accomplished by the floor of force the conquistadors carried with them — was warranted as the
execution of a divine donation, so that the conquest appeared not as the armed appropriation it was but
as the lawful taking of what God, through his vicar, had given. The force was total and the warrant
rendered it holy; the dispossession of the peoples of the Americas proceeded under a document that
claimed the authority of heaven, and the Requerimiento, the legal text read aloud to uncomprehending
native populations demanding their submission to the Church and the crown, made the warrant into a
ritual performed before the killing began. It will matter, two parts from now, that this theological
warrant did not simply vanish when the natural-law jurists and then Locke replaced God with reason
and labour: the conquest continued, and the new secular warrants took up the same work the old holy
one had done, justifying the appropriation of a world on grounds that had merely changed their name.
But the genealogy must first follow the warrant through the lawyers who began, even within the

Catholic tradition itself, to put nature where God had been.

I. Vitoria and the Dominion of the Indians

The first crack in the theological warrant came, remarkably, from within the Catholic tradition that had
issued the papal donation, and it came because the donation could not survive contact with the
conquest it was meant to justify. The Dominican theologians of the School of Salamanca, foremost
among them Francisco de Vitoria, lecturing in the late 1530s, confronted the question of by what right
the Spanish crown held dominion over the peoples of the Americas, and reached a conclusion that
undercut the bull of 1493. The papal donation, Vitoria argued, was no valid ground of temporal
dominion at all: the Pope had no secular authority over the whole world to give away, and the peoples
of the Americas were, under natural law, the true owners of their lands and the holders of genuine
political authority over themselves — possessed of real dominion, real property, real rights, which their
unbelief did not forfeit. This was a profound limitation on the theological warrant, and it has rightly
been honoured as an early statement of the idea that rights inhere in persons by their nature rather than
by their faith. But it was not the end of the matter, and the genealogy must be honest about what came
next, because Vitoria did not conclude that the conquest was therefore unlawful. Having removed the
papal warrant, he supplied natural-law warrants in its place: a natural right of the Spanish to travel and
to trade among the peoples of the Americas, a natural right to preach the faith, and a right to make war
upon those who wrongfully obstructed these — so that the conquest, denied its theological
justification, acquired a natural one.

This is the first appearance of the mutation that the whole genealogy traces, and it is worth dwelling
on because it shows the pattern in its purest form: the warrant does not disappear when its current
name is discredited; it changes its name and carries on. The force on the ground — the appropriation of
the Americas — was a constant; what changed was the account of why it was legitimate, which

migrated from the donation of a pope to the dictates of a natural law discovered by reason. Vitoria

II. Grotius — Right Even If God Did Not Exist

The decisive secularisation came with Hugo Grotius, the Dutch jurist whose De Jure Belli ac Pacis of
1625 is commonly called the founding text of the modern natural-law tradition, and whose central
move detached the whole edifice from God with a single sentence. Writing amid the catastrophe of the
wars of religion, Grotius sought a foundation for law and property and the relations of states that could
command assent across the confessional divide — that would bind Catholic and Protestant alike, and
so could not rest on any contested revelation. He found it in human nature and human reason, and
stated the consequence in the famous passage of his Prolegomena: what he had argued about natural
law, he wrote, would hold "even if we should concede that there is no God" — a thing he added could
not be conceded without wickedness, but the concession was made, and the point was made with it.
Natural law, and the property and obligations it grounds, would be valid even on the supposition of
God's non-existence, because its source is not the divine will but, in Grotius's phrase, human nature
itself, the rational and social character of the creature who needs the rules in order to live with his
fellows. With that sentence the warrant for property completed its migration: ownership was no longer
a gift heaven bestowed but a right reason discovered, written into the nature of things and binding by
its own rational necessity, independent of any divine grant.

It is one of the genealogy's quiet ironies, and a confirmation of how the warrants build on one
another rather than simply replacing each other, that the very sentence by which Grotius cut natural
law loose from God appears to have been borrowed from a scholastic of the School of Salamanca — so
that the seed of the secularisation lay already inside the Catholic tradition the secularisation would
supersede. Grotius's settlement was carried forward and systematised by Samuel Pufendorf, whose
great work of 1672 built the most influential modern system of natural law and natural property,
explicitly secular and anti-metaphysical in method, and through Pufendorf and Grotius together the
new warrant passed into the mainstream of European thought and onto the desk of the Englishman who
would give it its most enduring form. For Locke read Grotius and Pufendorf, took from them the
framework of a property grounded in nature rather than divine gift, and then did something neither had
quite done: he located the precise act by which a man converts the common stock of nature into his
own private possession, and in locating it he gave the market order the warrant it would carry, in one

form or another, for the next three centuries. The genealogy turns now to him.

I. The Refutation and the Replacement

John Locke enters the genealogy as the man who killed the theological warrant and built its
replacement, and the two acts belong together, because the replacement was shaped by the demolition.
The Two Treatises of Government, published anonymously in 1689, open with a sustained and almost
contemptuous destruction of Filmer. Across the whole of the First Treatise, Locke proceeds nearly
sentence by sentence through Patriarcha, denying that God ever gave Adam the sole dominion Filmer
claimed, observing that even had He done so there could be no way of identifying Adam's heir among
the billions of his descendants thousands of years later, and dismissing the entire doctrine as an affront
to common sense — "glib nonsense," in his phrase. With the theological warrant in ruins, the question
Filmer had answered stood open and urgent: if kings do not own their realms and rule their subjects by
divine descent from the first proprietor, then where does property come from, and what makes any
man's possession of any thing legitimately his? Locke's answer, set out in the fifth chapter of the
Second Treatise, is one of the most consequential arguments in the history of political thought, and it is
built entirely on the secular, natural-law foundation that Grotius and Pufendorf had laid: property is not
given by God to a chosen line, but generated by every man, continuously, through the application of
his own labour to the world.

The argument begins from self-ownership, which Locke takes as the bedrock: every man has a
property in his own person, which nobody has any right to but himself, and from this it follows that the
labour of his body and the work of his hands are properly his own. When a man takes something out of
the common state in which nature has left it — gathers the acorns, tills the field, draws the water — he
mixes his labour with it, and by mixing into it something that is indisputably his own, he annexes it to
himself and excludes the common right of others, making it his property. The thing becomes his not
because a king or a pope or a god has assigned it to him, but because he has, quite literally, put part of
himself into it. This is a genuinely new warrant, and the genealogy must credit its power and, in the

II. The Labour, the Provisos, and the Money That Dissolved Them

Locke himself saw that the labour theory, left unlimited, would justify a man in engrossing the whole
world, and so he hedged it with two provisos that are as important as the theory itself and that the later
tradition would quietly let fall away. The first is the famous condition that one may appropriate from
the common only where there is "enough, and as good, left in common for others" — so that my taking
deprives no one, the world being abundant enough to leave each as well provided as before. The
second is the prohibition on spoilage: a man may appropriate only as much as he can use before it
perishes, for to take more than one can use, letting it rot, is to take more than one's share and to injure
others who might have had it. Within these limits the labour theory is modest and even egalitarian, a
warrant for the smallholder's right to what he has worked and no more, bounded by the rights of all the
others who must also live from the common stock. Had property stopped here, it would have warranted
a world of roughly equal cultivators, each owning what he could work and use, none able to engross
the holdings of the rest.

But Locke did not stop there, and the move by which he passes beyond the provisos is the hinge of
his whole theory and the point at which the warrant for the smallholder becomes the warrant for
unlimited accumulation. The provisos bind, Locke argues, only in the state of nature before the
invention of money. For money — gold and silver, things that do not spoil — allows a man to sell the
surplus he cannot use before it rots, converting perishable goods into imperishable metal, and so to
accumulate without violating the spoilage limit; and because men have, by tacitly agreeing to put a
value on money, consented to this, they have consented also to the unequal possession of the earth that
it makes possible. With the introduction of money the spoilage proviso falls away, and with it the rough
equality the provisos had secured; the "enough and as good" condition follows, as a world of unlimited
accumulation fills up and the late-comer finds the common already enclosed. By this argument the
labour theory, which began as a warrant for the modest holdings of the man who works the land,
becomes a warrant for the vast and unequal accumulations of a commercial society — and it does so
while still presenting the whole arrangement as natural, the unequal distribution of the world appearing
not as a political settlement that might have gone otherwise but as the rational consequence of a tacit
consent men gave when they agreed to value gold. And it was on this foundation, the warrant of labour
and improvement, that Locke built his declaration that government has no other end than the
preservation of property — the candid statement, from the founder himself, that the chief business of
the political order is to secure the unequal distribution his theory had naturalised.

I. Waste and Improvement

Locke's labour theory carried within it a second concept that proved even more consequential than the
mixing of labour, and that reveals the force the warrant of nature concealed: the concept of
improvement. For Locke, it was not labour in the bare sense that conferred the strongest title but labour
that bettered the land — that cleared, drained, enclosed, planted, and cultivated it, raising its yield and
its value above what it had been in its natural state. Labour, he wrote, puts the difference of value on
everything, and an acre of cultivated land yields many times what an acre of the same land lying waste
in the common would yield; it is the improvement, the husbandry, that makes the far greater part of the
value. From this it followed, with a logic that seemed to Locke and his readers simply rational, that
land left in its natural state, neither enclosed nor cultivated, was waste — unimproved, its value
unrealised, its potential lying idle — and that the man who took such land and improved it not only
acquired a title to it but did the world a service, turning waste into wealth. The argument has an
innocent agricultural face, and in the English context of enclosure it did the work of justifying the
conversion of common fields into private farms. But its reach was far larger, because the world was
full of land that Europeans judged unimproved, and full of peoples whose relation to that land did not
take the European form of enclosure and tillage.

The genealogy must be precise here, because this is the point at which the secular warrant of nature
is shown doing exactly the work the theological warrant of conquest had done, and doing it more
effectively for being dressed as reason rather than revelation. The peoples of the Americas, hunting and
gathering and farming in ways that did not enclose and improve the land in the European manner,
appeared through the lens of Locke's theory not as the owners of their territories but as wanderers
across a waste that no one had yet made into property. Their land, being unimproved, was in the
relevant sense vacant — available to whoever would mix his labour with it and make it yield. Locke
himself drew the connection, observing that in the beginning all the world was America, a great

II. The Force at the Frontier

The legal doctrines that grew from this argument carried it into the law of empires and gave the
dispossession its enduring form. The principle of vacuum domicilium — empty dwelling — held that
land not occupied in the recognised European sense of settled cultivation was legally empty and open
to appropriation; the broader doctrine of terra nullius, no-one's-land, held the same of whole territories,
treating the lands of non-agricultural or non-European peoples as belonging to no one and therefore
available to the first improving claimant. These doctrines, descended from the improvement argument,
justified the appropriation of continents — North America, Australia, much of Africa — on the ground
that their existing inhabitants had not, by European lights, made them into property, so that the colonist
who enclosed and farmed them was not taking what was another's but making property where none had
existed. The warrant of nature thus accomplished, across three centuries of empire, a dispossession as
vast as any in history, and it accomplished it while presenting itself as the opposite of force — as the
peaceful and rational transformation of idle waste into productive property, the improver's natural
reward.

This is the genealogy's central demonstration about the warrant of nature, and it is why the volume
insists, against the doctrine's own self-image, that the made arrangement rests in the last instance on
force. The improvement argument did not abolish the floor of force beneath the appropriation of the
world; it concealed it, by relocating the act of taking inside a theory that made the taking look like
making. Behind the natural and rational acquisition of waste land stood, at every frontier, the same
force the conquistadors had carried — the militia, the treaty broken, the people removed or killed, the
prior occupants whose own relation to the land was not recognised as ownership because it did not take
the form the theory required. The warrant of nature was, at the edge of empire, a warrant for conquest

which the whole study turns: that the appearance of naturalness is precisely what hides the force, and
that a made arrangement called natural is a made arrangement whose force has been rendered invisible.
The Scots who came after Locke would see this more clearly than he did — would recognise that
property was made and not found — and the genealogy turns now to the strange fact that the tradition's

own subtlest minds were the ones who knew its naturalness was a fiction.

I. Hume — A Relation Not Natural but Moral

The deepest mind of the Scottish Enlightenment, and one of the deepest in the whole tradition the
market church would later claim, held that property is not natural — and he is the genealogy's most
powerful witness, because he is a witness for the prosecution who stood inside the very movement
from which free-market thought descends. David Hume, in the third book of his Treatise of Human
Nature of 1739, set out a theory of property that flatly denied it any natural existence. A man's
property, Hume wrote, is some object related to him, but the relation is "not natural, but moral,"
founded on justice — and justice, on Hume's account, is not a natural virtue written into the human
constitution but an artificial one, a convention that human beings invent to solve a problem nature sets
them. The problem is scarcity and the instability of possession: humans face numberless wants with
slender means, and there is nothing in nature to fix any object as belonging to any person, so that
possession is perpetually insecure and the fruits of industry perpetually at risk. To remedy this, men
enter, by a slow and tacit convergence rather than any formal contract, into a convention to stabilise
possession — to leave each in the peaceable enjoyment of what he has acquired — and it is this
convention, and nothing in nature, that creates property. The origin of justice, Hume wrote, explains
the origin of property; the one artifice gives rise to both. Property is a human invention, devised to
meet a human need, with no existence prior to the convention that constitutes it.

The significance of this for the genealogy is hard to overstate, because Hume is not an opponent of
the market order but one of its formative thinkers, and he reaches the volume's own conclusion by his
own road and a century before the doctrine hardened into the creed that would deny it. Hume thought
the convention of property enormously beneficial — held that the whole system of property-respecting
behaviour, though no single act of it need advantage the agent, is infinitely advantageous to society as
a whole, and defended it accordingly. His point was not that property should be abolished because it is
artificial, but that it should be understood for what it is: a made thing, a convention adopted because it

II. Smith — The Stages, the Hand, and the Merchants

Adam Smith, Hume's friend and the supposed founding saint of free-market economics, was subtler
and more critical than the canon that claims him has generally allowed, and on the question of property
he too refused the naturalising move. Smith historicised property through the theory of the four stages,
which he developed in his lectures on jurisprudence and which structures the argument of the Wealth of
Nations of 1776: human societies pass through four modes of subsistence — hunting, herding,
farming, and commerce — and property and law evolve with each, the forms of ownership and the
very functions of government changing as the mode of subsistence changes. Property, on this account,
is not a fixed natural right but a historical institution, taking different shapes in different stages of
social development, its content determined by the material conditions of the society that holds it. Smith
observed, with a bluntness that sits awkwardly in his free-market reputation, that civil government, so
far as it is instituted for the security of property, is in effect instituted for the defence of those who have
property against those who have none — a near-echo of the Lockean candour, and a recognition that
the property order is a settlement in the interest of the propertied rather than a neutral nature serving all
alike. His celebrated "system of natural liberty" is real and is his, but it is hedged at every turn: it
operates only within the bounds of justice, which is to say within the made framework of law, and
Smith allowed numerous exceptions to it — for defence, for public works, for education, for the
gradual and humane management of the transition away from the old mercantile restrictions.

And the merchants whose interest the later free-market doctrine would identify with the public
good, Smith regarded with a cold and steady suspicion that is the opposite of the apology his name is
now invoked to make. He warned that men of the same trade seldom meet, even for merriment, but the
conversation ends in "a conspiracy against the public," or in some contrivance to raise prices; that the
masters combine, constantly and silently, to keep down the wages of labour; and that the interest of the
merchants and manufacturers is in some respects always opposed to that of the public, so that any
proposal of theirs ought to be examined with suspicion and never adopted until long and careful
scrutiny. The "invisible hand," the phrase on which a whole ideology would later be hung, appears
exactly once in the Wealth of Nations, and was for Smith a modest observation about unintended
consequences rather than the organising principle of a self-regulating natural order. The genealogy
dwells on the real Smith because he is the strongest evidence for its thesis: that the naturalness of
property and the market is not the considered view of the tradition's founders but a later simplification,
and that the founders themselves — Hume openly, Smith in his historicising and his suspicion of

capital — knew the arrangement to be made, conventional, historical, and arranged in someone's

I. The Polemic Against Perfectibility

The nineteenth century opened a new front in the genealogy, for it extended the warrant of nature from
property itself to the poverty that property produced, and it did so in a work written explicitly to defeat
the opposite claim. Thomas Malthus published his Essay on the Principle of Population in 1798 as a
direct polemic against the radical optimists of his day — against William Godwin and the Marquis de
Condorcet, who had argued that human misery was not fixed in nature but was the product of bad
institutions and unjust arrangements, and that the perfection of those arrangements could progressively
abolish poverty. Malthus set out to prove the contrary: that poverty was natural, written into the iron
relation between population and food, and therefore beyond the reach of any reform of institutions. His
argument was the famous disproportion of the two growths — that population, unchecked, increases
geometrically, doubling upon itself, while the food supply can increase only arithmetically, by steady
increments — so that population must always tend to outrun subsistence, and the surplus of mouths
over food must always be removed by what he called the positive checks: famine, disease, and war, the
mechanisms by which nature restores the balance that human fecundity destroys. Poverty and
premature death were thus not the failures of a remediable social order but the operation of a natural
law as fixed as gravity, and the radical dream of abolishing them by better institutions was a category
error, an attempt to repeal a law of nature by an act of legislation.

The genealogy must, in its discipline, credit what was real in Malthus and be precise about where
the error lay, because his case was not foolish and the naturalisation it performed was not a simple lie.
Malthus had identified a genuine dynamic — the real tendency of populations to press against the
limits of their subsistence, a pressure that had in fact shaped much of human history before the
agricultural and industrial transformations that would partly escape it. His mistake was not in seeing

the pressure but in naming it nature and thereby placing it beyond politics: in concluding, from the real

II. The Iron Law and the Workhouse

The naturalisation of poverty was carried into the heart of classical economics by David Ricardo,
whose work of 1817 gave the doctrine its most rigorous form and contributed the proposition that came
to be called the iron law of wages: that the wages of labour tend inexorably toward the minimum
required for subsistence, because any rise above it, by Malthusian logic, only multiplies the labourers
and drives wages back down, while any fall below it reduces their numbers and drives wages back up
— so that the labourer is locked, by a natural law of population and a natural law of the market acting
together, at the edge of survival. The classical economics that descended from Smith but hardened in
Malthus and Ricardo became, in the phrase of the age, the dismal science precisely because it rendered
the misery of the labouring poor as a natural necessity that no benevolence could relieve and no reform
could escape. The whole apparatus of the new political economy testified that the distribution of the
social product — subsistence to the worker, profit to the capitalist, rent to the landlord — was
governed by iron laws, natural in their operation and inexorable in their results, against which the
moral objections of the humane were simply the protests of those who did not understand the science.
The cruelty this naturalisation licensed is the genealogy's grimmest demonstration of what is at
stake in the conversion of arrangement into nature, and it took concrete legislative form within a
generation of Malthus's essay. The New Poor Law of 1834 was built on Malthusian and classical
premises, and its harshness flowed directly from the naturalisation of poverty: if misery was nature's
doing and not society's, and if relief to the poor only encouraged the population growth that was
poverty's natural cause, then generous relief was not merely wasteful but actively harmful, an
interference with a natural mechanism that would only multiply the suffering it sought to ease. The law
therefore withdrew outdoor relief and confined assistance to the workhouse, deliberately made the
workhouse a place of deterrent misery so that only the truly desperate would enter it, and broke up
families and stigmatised the pauper in the name of a science that taught that poverty was natural and
relief was folly. Here the warrant of nature did its lethal work in the open: a made arrangement — a
labour market that paid subsistence and a poor law that denied relief — was warranted as the
obedience of policy to natural law, and the suffering it imposed was rendered not as a choice the
society had made but as a necessity nature had imposed, against which compassion itself was
unscientific. The collection's framework names this the metabolic harm of a doctrine that cannot read

its own cruelty as a choice; the naturalisation of poverty is its purest nineteenth-century instance.

I. The Subjective Turn

The 1870s produced a transformation in economic thought that handed the market a new and powerful
warrant, the warrant of mathematics, and it arrived almost simultaneously from three directions. Within
a few years of one another, and substantially independently, Carl Menger in Vienna, William Stanley
Jevons in England, and Léon Walras in Switzerland worked out the theory of marginal utility, which
solved a problem that had vexed classical economics — the paradox that water, essential to life, is
cheap, while diamonds, useless, are dear — and in solving it overturned the classical theory of value.
Value, the marginalists argued, derives not from the labour embodied in a thing, as Smith and Ricardo
and, on the other side, Marx had held, but from its marginal utility — the usefulness of the last unit of
it to the consumer who values it — so that the worth of a thing is set not by the toil that produced it but
by the subjective valuations of those who want it, the diamond dear because its marginal unit is scarce
and craved, the water cheap because its marginal unit, in a world of plenty, is barely wanted. This
subjective turn relocated the source of economic value from the objective process of production to the
psychology of the consumer, and in doing so it dissolved the labour theory of value that had been
common ground from Locke through Smith to Ricardo, replacing it with a theory in which the market's
prices reflect the aggregated desires of the people transacting in it.

The genealogy credits the real intellectual achievement here, because the marginal revolution was a
genuine advance that solved real problems and the project's discipline is to grant the goods of every
doctrine it analyses. The subjective theory of value is, within its domain, illuminating; it explains
prices that the labour theory could not, and it gave economics a tool of great analytic power. But the
warrant it supplied was not merely analytic, and this is what concerns the genealogy: by rendering the
market as a system in which countless subjective valuations resolve, through the mechanism of price,
into a determinate outcome, the marginalists made it possible to model the whole economy as a system
of equilibrium — a structure of interlocking equations in which supply and demand, across all goods at

once, balance to a unique and natural set of prices. Walras in particular built the theory of general

II. Equilibrium Mistaken for Nature

The warrant that the mathematisation supplied lay in the quiet slide from a model to a nature, and the
genealogy must mark it precisely because it is the subtlest of all the warrants and the one that most
thoroughly governs the modern imagination of the market. The equilibrium of the general-equilibrium
model is a property of the model — of its idealised assumptions of perfect competition, perfect
information, perfect rationality, costless transaction, and the rest — and within those assumptions it is a
real and demonstrable result, a genuine achievement of mathematical reasoning. But the assumptions
are not the world, and the equilibrium they yield is not a fact about any actual economy but a feature of
a highly idealised representation of one. What happened across the late nineteenth and twentieth
centuries was the migration of equilibrium from the status of a model's property to the status of the
economy's natural state — the coming to seem obvious that a market, left to itself and freed of
interference, would tend toward its equilibrium as a pendulum tends toward rest, finding the natural
prices and the efficient allocation that the mathematics described. The elegance of the equations lent
the market the authority of physics: as a physical system obeys the laws of mechanics and settles to its
lowest-energy state, so the market obeyed the laws of supply and demand and settled to its equilibrium,
and the role of policy was simply to refrain from disturbing the natural settling.

This is the warrant of nature in its mathematical disguise, and it is more powerful than its
predecessors precisely because it does not appeal to God or to a contested natural law but to the
apparent neutrality of mathematics, which seems to describe rather than to prescribe. Yet the
prescription is smuggled in with the description, in the choice of assumptions and in the treatment of
the model's equilibrium as the economy's natural goal. The actual economy is not a frictionless system
of perfectly informed rational agents; it is a thing of power, ignorance, monopoly, manipulation, crisis,
and force, in which the conditions for the model's equilibrium are nowhere met and the equilibrium
itself is a counterfactual idealisation. To call the market a self-equilibrating system, and to treat its
departures from equilibrium as interferences with a natural process rather than as its normal condition,
is to perform once more the move the whole genealogy tracks: to take a made arrangement, full of
power and force, and to present it as a natural mechanism obeying natural laws, so that the
arrangement's outcomes appear as nature's results and the force within it disappears behind the
equations. The mathematics was real and the achievement was real; the naturalisation was the warrant,
and it would pass, refined and qualified, into the most sophisticated form the doctrine ever took — the
Austrian conception of the market as an order grown rather than made.

I. Grown, Not Made

The most sophisticated warrant the market ever received, and in important respects the most honest,
came from the Austrian school, which descended from Carl Menger — the same Menger of the
marginal revolution — through Ludwig von Mises to Friedrich Hayek, and which conceived the
market in a way that neither the theological nor the simple natural-rights nor the crude equilibrium
warrants had managed. The market, for the Austrians, was neither a thing God ordained nor a state of
nature that obtained when institutions fell away nor a machine settling to a calculable equilibrium; it
was a spontaneous order — an order that arises not from anyone's design but from the uncoordinated
actions of countless individuals each pursuing his own ends, generating, without intending it, a
structure of immense complexity that no mind planned and no mind could plan. The model was
language, which no one invented but which emerged from the speech of millions and serves them all;
or the common law, which no legislator designed but which grew case by case into a coherent body; or
money itself, which Menger showed could arise spontaneously from barter without any sovereign
decreeing it. The market, on this view, is grown rather than made in the sense of designed — an
evolved institution, shaped over centuries by a process of trial and survival in which the practices that
coordinated human cooperation effectively persisted and those that did not fell away, so that the
resulting order embodies a wisdom no individual possesses and represents the accumulated, tested
experience of the species.

Hayek made this the most formidable case for the market ever framed, and he made it by way of the
knowledge problem, which the collection has examined in its study of the two churches and which
deserves its full weight here. The knowledge that a complex economy requires in order to function —
the particular knowledge of time and place, of local conditions and shifting wants, dispersed across
millions of minds and never available to any one of them — cannot be gathered into a central planner's
bureau, because it exists only in the scattered and tacit form in which the individuals who hold it use it.

The market's price system, Hayek argued, is the only mechanism that can marshal this dispersed

each can coordinate his plans with the plans of others he will never meet — a marvel of unconscious

the attempt to redesign the economic order from above, on a rational plan, was not merely difficult but

command the knowledge that only the evolved and distributed order contains.

II. The Most Honest Warrant, and Its Limit

This is the warrant the genealogy must engage most carefully, because it is the strongest and because it
honestly concedes much of what the genealogy contends. The Austrian does not claim that the market
is natural in the crude sense the earlier warrants asserted; he grants that it is made — made not by
design but by evolution, grown rather than decreed, an institution and not a fact of physics. He does not
pretend the order fell from heaven or obtains automatically when force withdraws; he insists, on the
contrary, that it is the product of a long historical process and must be cultivated and defended. He
even supplies, in Hayek's image of the social scientist as a gardener who tends a growth he cannot
command rather than a craftsman who shapes dead material to his design, almost exactly the posture
the collection itself urges against every revealed doctrine. The Austrian warrant is, in short, the tested
rather than the revealed form of the market doctrine — a case for the market that argues for it as an
evolved and beneficial institution rather than asserting it as a nature beyond argument — and to that
extent it stands outside the procession of naturalisations the genealogy has tracked, and the volume
credits it as the honest exception.

And yet it does not finally escape the genealogy's point, for two reasons that the synthesis will press
and that must be marked here. The first is that an evolved order is still a made order, and being grown
rather than designed does not make it natural in the sense that matters — does not make it a neutral
baseline prior to politics, because it remains an arrangement that distributes power and advantage and
that rests, like every property order, on a floor of force. The common law evolved, but it is enforced by
the state's coercion; money emerged spontaneously, but its modern form is sustained by the central
bank and the law of legal tender; the market grew, but the property rights that constitute it are
defended, in the last instance, by the police and the prison and, at the frontier, by the army. That an
institution evolved tells us it was not arbitrarily decreed; it does not tell us it is just, or neutral, or
beyond the reach of the politics that might amend it. The second is that the Austrian warrant, honest in
Hayek's own hands, did not remain in his hands; it passed to heirs who converted the careful claim that
the market is an evolved order embodying dispersed knowledge into the cruder claim that the market is
a self-correcting natural mechanism whose outcomes are efficient and just and whose every regulation
is a fatal interference — and in that conversion the tested warrant became, once more, a revealed one.

I. The Council and the Creed

The final warrant was assembled by a deliberate and organised movement, and the genealogy insists on
this because the movement's own self-image — that it merely defended the natural order of liberty
against the artificial impositions of planning — is precisely the naturalisation the volume exists to
expose. In April 1947, in the village of Mont Pélerin above Lake Geneva, the economist Friedrich
Hayek gathered thirty-nine like-minded thinkers, among them Milton Friedman, George Stigler, Karl
Popper, and Ludwig von Mises, to found the Mont Pélerin Society and to organise the intellectual
defence and propagation of market liberalism against the planning and collectivism that dominated the
post-war world. The historians of the movement have given it its accurate name — a thought
collective, a deliberately constructed transnational network of scholars, foundations, and institutes
whose purpose was to develop a doctrine and carry it, over a generation, from the academic margins to
the commanding heights of policy. The doctrine they carried took Hayek's careful evolved-order
argument and, in the hands of Friedman and the Chicago school and the wider movement, hardened it
into a creed: the doctrine of the self-correcting market, the efficient market that allocates resources
optimally if left alone, the market as the near-natural baseline of economic life from which every tax,
every regulation, every public provision is a departure and an interference, a friction to be removed so
that the natural order may resume.

This is the warrant of nature in its creedal form, and it is the scripture of the market church the
collection's study of the two churches anatomised — the doctrine that, after the collapse of its rival in
1991, would declare itself not merely victorious but vindicated, the final form of human economic
organisation, the end of history's argument about how an economy should be run. It is the warrant in
which the naturalisation is most complete and most consequential, because it governed the policy of
the most powerful states and institutions on earth across the decades the collection's other volumes
tracked: the doctrine beneath the dollar order of The Note, beneath the shock therapies and structural

II. Polanyi's Rejoinder — The Planned Market

The decisive answer to this final warrant was written before the warrant reached its full creedal form,
by an economic historian who saw the naturalisation for what it was and demonstrated its falsehood
with the historical record. Karl Polanyi, in The Great Transformation of 1944 — the same year the
dollar order was assembled at Bretton Woods — laid down the proposition that the whole genealogy
converges upon: that the self-regulating market, far from being the natural state that emerges when
planning and interference cease, was itself the most deliberately planned construction in the whole of
economic history. The free market, Polanyi showed, did not arise spontaneously when the state stepped
back; it had to be created, deliberately and often violently, by an enormous and sustained programme
of state action — the enclosures that made land a commodity, the abolition of the old protections that
had embedded economic life in social obligation, the legislative manufacture of markets in land and
labour and money that had never before been treated as commodities to be bought and sold. Laissez-
faire was planned, in Polanyi's exact and devastating phrase; the natural order of the free market was
the most artificial of constructions, brought into being and held in being by the constant, deliberate
intervention of the state, while the social protections that later arose to defend society against the
market's ravages were the unplanned, spontaneous reactions of a society defending itself. The market
that the creed presents as nature is the planned thing; the resistance to it is the spontaneous thing — the
exact inversion of the warrant.

Polanyi saw, too, what the market doctrine had become in the hands of its zealots, and named it in
the register the collection's study of belief requires: laissez-faire, he wrote, had been catalysed into a
drive of uncompromising ferocity, an economic movement turned almost into a religious creed. This is
the market church recognised by one of its sharpest historians as a church — a body of belief held with
the fervour of faith, propagating a doctrine its adherents experienced not as a contestable arrangement
but as the structure of reality itself. The genealogy's procession ends here, at the moment the made
arrangement achieves its most complete naturalisation and a historian stands ready to show that the
naturalisation is exactly backwards — that the market presented as found was in fact the most
thoroughly made of all economic orders, and that the warrant of nature, in its final and most powerful
form, is the managed belief that lets the most planned construction in history pass for the spontaneous
order that appears when no one plans at all. What remains is to draw the genealogy's threads together,
to state with care what the whole procession does and does not establish, and to hold, at full strength,

the strongest objections to it.

The synthesis and the verdict: that across five centuries the warrant for private property and the
free market mutated through eight forms while its function never changed — to convert a made,

I. The Constant Function

The genealogy can now be drawn together, and its shape stated whole. Across five centuries, the
warrant for the private ownership of the world and for the market through which it is exchanged
mutated through eight distinct forms, each replacing the last as the last lost its power to persuade. It
was, first, the gift of God to Adam and his heirs, and the donation of a vicar of Christ to Christian
princes. Then, as the theological warrant cracked, it became the dictate of a natural law that reason
discovered and that would bind, in Grotius's phrase, even if there were no God. Then, in Locke's hands,
it became the fruit of labour, the world made one's own by the mixing of the self into it, and the reward
of the improvement that turned waste into wealth — a warrant that, at the frontier, did the conquering
work the papal donation had done. Then, in the subtler minds of the Scots, it was admitted to be no
nature at all but an artificial convention, useful and beneficial but made — an admission the tradition
would forget. Then the poverty the arrangement produced was naturalised as an iron law of population,
beyond the reach of relief. Then the market itself was rendered as a mathematical equilibrium, a
system settling to its natural state like a body coming to rest. Then, most honestly, as an order grown
rather than designed, evolved like language and tested by survival. And finally as a self-correcting
creed, the efficient and near-natural baseline from which every departure is an interference. Eight
warrants — God, reason, labour, convention forgotten, iron law, equilibrium, evolved order, creed —
and beneath the changing names a single, constant function: to take a made arrangement, resting in the
last instance on force and distributing the world in some interests more than others, and to present it as
the order of nature, prior to politics and beyond its reach.

The constancy of the function beneath the variety of the warrants is the genealogy's central finding,
and it explains a great deal that is otherwise puzzling about the market doctrine — above all, its
remarkable capacity to survive the refutation of each successive justification. When the theological
warrant became unbelievable, the arrangement did not fall; it acquired a natural-law warrant. When the

proved embarrassing, money dissolved them. When the Scots exposed the convention beneath the
nature, the exposure was forgotten and the iron laws of the nineteenth century renaturalised what
Hume had denaturalised. When the iron laws grew cruel, the mathematics of equilibrium lent a cleaner
authority; when the equilibrium proved an idealisation, the evolved-order argument supplied a subtler

that the arrangement is natural and necessary — is fixed in advance, and the warrants are merely the

II. The Verdict, the Counter-Readings, and the Return to Politics

The verdict must be stated with the precision the subject demands, because the genealogy is easily
mistaken for more than it claims, and the discipline of the collection is to claim exactly what the
evidence supports and no more. The genealogy does not show that property is bad, or that the market is
illegitimate, or that any particular arrangement of ownership is unjust; it shows that property and the
market are made rather than found, arrangements rather than natures, and that their long appearance of
naturalness is the most historical thing about them. The crucial distinction, which the volume has
insisted on throughout and must now make explicit, is between made and arbitrary. To say that
property is made is not to say it could be anything we chose, or that it is held in place by nothing but
caprice, or that it could be abolished without cost. An institution may be made and also evolved, made
and also beneficial, made and also the best arrangement available on the tested evidence — the
Austrian warrant, in its honest form, is right about this, and the cruder conclusion that a made thing
may be remade at will without consequence is the very high-modernist error the collection condemned
in the planners of its other studies. The genealogy's target is not property but its naturalisation; not the
arrangement but the warrant that places the arrangement beyond argument.

Three objections stand against the genealogy at full strength, and the volume holds them. The first,
and the most serious, is the charge of the genetic fallacy: that to trace the historical origin of an idea is
to say nothing about its truth or value, since a belief may have a disreputable pedigree and still be
correct, and the warrants’ having served power does not make the arrangement they warrant unjust. The
volume grants the logic of this entirely, and concedes that the genealogy refutes neither property nor
the market — that one could accept every word of the history and still hold, on independent grounds,
that private property and free exchange are the best institutions humanity has devised. But the
genealogy does not claim to refute the arrangement; it claims to disarm one specific and powerful
defence of it — the defence from nature, the claim that the arrangement is not a choice at all but the
neutral baseline, beyond politics, that obtains when interference ceases. That defence the genealogy
does refute, because the defence is a historical claim and the history is against it; and disarming the
defence from nature returns the arrangement to the status of a choice, which is the whole of what the
genealogy seeks. The second objection, the evolved-order defence, holds that the market is grown and
tested rather than designed and arbitrary; the volume grants this in full and answers, as it answered in

The third, the indispensability defence, holds that property and markets are simply the best means we

The conditions under which the genealogy's verdict would fail follow from this, and the volume
publishes them in the collection's discipline. Evidence that property and the market are not in fact
backed by force — that they would persist in their present form with no court, no police, no
enforcement, no army at the frontier — would undo the claim that they rest on a floor of force and are
therefore political rather than natural; but the evidence runs the other way, force being visible wherever
property is contested. Evidence that the successive warrants were not recruited to defend a fixed
conclusion but were genuinely abandoned when the arrangement they warranted was found wanting —
that the doctrine revised the arrangement when a warrant failed, rather than merely replacing the
warrant — would move the finding away from the revealed pattern toward the tested one; but the
record shows the arrangement persisting through every change of warrant, which is the revealed
signature. And the genealogy's actual gift, the thing it offers in place of the naturalness it takes away, is
not the abolition of property but its return to politics. To show that the arrangement is made is to show
that it is the kind of thing a free people may argue over, amend, hold to account, and choose differently

insists on its naturalness and counts the costs it imposes — the dispossessed frontier, the workhouse,
the lost decade, the captured president of the collection's other volumes — as the price of an order that

available choice, and accepts revision when it fails. The whole burden of this genealogy, and of the
collection it belongs to, is the difference between those two postures. Tested, not revealed: the floor
was laid by someone, for some purpose, and a free people may ask whom it serves, and lay it
differently.
