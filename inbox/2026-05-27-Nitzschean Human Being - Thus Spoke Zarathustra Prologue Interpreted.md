## Abstract

The goal of this paper is to interpret Friedrich Nietzsche's prologue to Thus Spoke Zarathustra to extract from within the nietzschean meaning of Human Being. This is done by reading through and interpreting the whole text whilst systematically understanding that which is being told through the story, as it may, too, include necessary contextual information. The reading is done in order and the interpretation is directly written whilst reading. Through the main nine pages of the paper, a subjective view of the text will be provided as the author is not an expert on Nietzsche or has done any previous reading neither of the book nor of any publications available interpreting it. No generative AI’s have been used to the creation of this text or the interpretation of the original text. I do not condone any use of generative AI’s.

Interpretation of the Prologue to Nietzsche’s Thus Spoke Zarathustra by Marga Jackson
Initial Statement
Here I intend to present my interpretation of the prologue of Friedrich Nietzsche’s Thus Spoke

Zarathustra in a means to understand what he represents as “human beings” or otherwise, what

“humanity” means to him.
This will be done by reading through and interpreting the whole text to also systematically

understand that which is being told through the story, as it may, too, include necessary contextual

information
The reading will be done in order and the interpretation is directly done whilst reading.
As a reminder, too, this work is not objective, as interpretation carries personal subjectivity.
I am by no means an expert on Nietzsche nor say I speak the truth.
No generative AI’s have been used to the creation of this text or the interpretation of the original

text. I do not condone any use of generative AI’s.
Thank you
Lain
and
Costel
for your support.
Thank you very much for reading.
09/Feb/2026
1/1 – The Wake of Zarathustra
Zarathustra wants to become like the sun: an object of worship, or alternatively, an object that

indirectly provides wisdom, warmth, or otherwise comfort.
This object of worship, though, isn’t without those that are “to see it”: «What would your happiness

be if you had not those from whom you shine?». For it to be a god, it requires of worshippers.
This wealth he “wants to bestow” is, possibly, the wealth of knowledge, the same Zarathustra

developed in solitude with his spirit.
This development of wisdom through solitude in a cave is what ensured his “going down”, viz., it

was the isolation of that which is common that allowed him to be a superhuman.
Here, “flow over” may, too allude to the “bestowing”, the giving, similarly to the sun, the blessing

of wisdom.
In this giving of wisdom, the “cup empties”, namely, gives everything they hold.
My main issue is with the “Zarathustra wants to become human again”.
Is it that he’s pretending to “save the nihilists” by turning “them back”, and thus it’s not Zarathustra

“becoming human” but rather than the nihilists become more like Zarathustra?
Or is it that in the overflowing, he loses that superhuman nature and, therefore, returns to common?
1/2 – Descending the Mountain
Could we call the Saint a half-awoken character? He understands the difference between nihilist

humans, him, and Zarathustra, but it is plain to see that Zarathustra is of different nature to the

Saint.
Whereas the Saint intends to “Love God”, Zarathustra has become a god on earth.
From this we can imagine he “loves mankind” because he sees the potential of all mankind to

become god-like, or otherwise because he is to become the new God amongst men, considering the

“blessing of the sun” that he wants to become and bestow.
We must consider here the translator’s note on how one should consider human beings as part of

animals and therefore nature.
Does this possibly reinforce the idea of “becoming a god to men”? Or is it a consideration on how,

by turning “back into humans” as previously mentioned, one becomes a more natural version of

humans, one that is not “divine” and “divine-given” but rather one that has removed God from its

being and become back into a natural being.
We get more imagery on Zarathustra’s change, and through his “descent” through the valley,

carrying these flames of knowledge or maybe wisdom, he is named a «child»…
Maybe as means of saying pureness, namely, that it’s yet to be “corrupted”, in a Rousseauian way,

by society and that which is common.
We see in the Saint the disdain for humanity, maybe as means of self-protection: «love for human

beings would kill me». They consider humans untrustworthy and mistrustful, viz., not open to

change or new frameworks.
Here, we see what part of the gift really is: «God is dead!», a direct connection to «Let me leave

quickly before I take something from you!».
I wonder if the Saint isn’t human anymore. If the telling that “God is dead” is part of this gift

-negative nihilism so one can develop positive nihilism and then become a super human-, and if

Saint were human, Zarathustra would have given them the “gift”.
Maybe in his love for God, the Saint has lost his quality as human. Maybe they’ve lost it when they

developed said disdain for other humans.
1/3 – The Market
Zarathustra is not calling the humans “apes” or “worms” as means of mockery, it’s actually

otherwise: they have abandoned that which made them animals, worm and ape, therefore the human

is a «painful embarrassment» for that which is natural, and so is that the
overman
is the same for the

everyday man.
The everyday man who believes «those who speak to you of extraterrestrial hopes», is one that is

unnatural because it’s given itself to idealism, particularly Christian idealism. The cure is, thus,

becoming the
overman
, one that goes beyond and further than that which is idealistic, back to

nature.
«Once you were apes, and even now a human is still more ape than any ape» meaning that, even

though the attempts at “giving extraterrestrial hopes”, namely, to make humans idealists, has very

much been effective, there is still nature in humans.
Now that Christian idealism and its desecrators are gone, one is forced to return to earth, but “old

habits die hard”, and now humans are desecrating earth.
Statements reinforced by the following paragraph: «The soul wanted to escape the body and the

earth», but it was but the soul’s need for physicality, therefore, it was impoverished.
Therefore, as one becomes the
overman
one “cleanses” the contempt of the soul towards the body.

The human stops providing for that which is ideal, and begins questioning their lives as to receive

“what makes me happy”, which should suffice to «justify existence itself».
The next paragraph is the contrast of rationalism and, especially, idealism.
In the same logic, justice and virtue are shown as tools provided by human nature, where the latter

is too constructed by that which was left by reason, and therefore idealism.
The first, though, is what makes humans «ember and coal». Considering that Zarathustra is himself

regarded as “he who carried the flames into the valley” before being “ashes” that he “carried to the

mountain”, we could thus consider justice the one that makes humans “ember and coal” of a similar

potential: practically ashes, or un-burnt fuel that can become fire again.
To put it clearly, justice is, potentially, that which can make us become the
overmen
if we are to

want to become one.
Lastly, there is pity, but his conceptualisation of pity is separated from religious pity, like the one

with the death of Christ, therefore removing the aspect of pity from that «poverty and filth and

pitiful contentment» that is origin from reason and by extension Christian idealism. Does this mean

there is a transformation from the “pitiful” nature of human as idealistic to in turn become a pity of

oneself for not being the
overman
?
It seems that religious pity is transformed into another kind, but it is unclear to me of what nature.
There, human beings are terrified of sinning, «the stinginess even in sinning», but this is naught but

a cry for help, a cry «out to high heaven», hoping for some kind of resolution or betterment, maybe

as a return to nature or physicality, maybe as a means of being “saved” once one is not an idealist or

a nihilist anymore.
This thunder, this madness becoming the
overman
promises, it is that answer to the cries.
Regardless, and just like the Saint explained, they are not with open ears and hearing eyes to

understand that which is being told to them, but the tightrope walker, they want to «see him too».
They want to see the
overman
.
1/4 – Zarathustra’s Love
The opening statement connects with what was previously assumed. A rope may be connected, but

human beings are walking over the rope, therefore they are not within the trajectory of the rope

themselves. This walking on the rope is, well, dangerous in all means, as one is completely

disconnected from the rope but their feet, and nothing but walking suffices.
It is though that disconnection from the rope, «they are a bridge and not a purpose», that can allow

them to “cross over” and “go under”.
I am unsure if this means for Nietzsche that one must first be a human being in this state before one

can be an
overman
or if one could reach the state by just “becoming the rope”.
I wonder though… He was amazed at
what
of them? Their behaviour? Their response? Their

difficulty to hear?
Those Zarathustra loves, can be categorised in three sections: a)
Those who go under:
They are the ones that, based on their circumstances, become the

overman
, reach the end of the rope.
From their characteristics they doubt what’s common, seek, discover, create, and ultimately

sacrifice themselves for that which is natural and for nature to become the
overman
.
They build the path safer, and further, so that everyone can reach.
These are too the ones that have nothing else to lose, or have everything to lose: their natural

nature, their freedom, their “soul”.
b)
Those who are virtuous:
These understand that virtue is only one, «one virtue is more

virtue that two», and that there are other virtues that may be poison, more “hooks” on which

«doom may hang». They see their virtue as a constant that they must share no matter the end

goal, leading to their doom, and sometimes reaching the end of the rope as a spirit of his

virtue, namely, after having given that virtue to the rest.
c)
Those that will perish
: Many who give all they have and distrust their fate and life. They

are doomed to perish, either by their god or their contemporaries, giving everything these

cannot take: all their love.
Lastly, there is that who heralds: Zarathustra.
It is important to note, these categories, they are all human nature, a behaviour necessary to become

the
overman
, but these are Zarathustra himself too. These categories characterise him entirely.
He then calls himself «A herald of the lightning and a heavy from from the cloud»,«but this

lightning is called
overman
». This makes me wonder, does Zarathustra not consider himself the

overman
here?
Is he, by returning from the mountains, to the valley, and then the town, “lowering himself” to the

status of human?
Is he, to put it in a Platonic way (oh! How you would have hated this Nietzsche!), he who has seen

the sun and returned to the cave?
I also wonder, are these naught but his process of awakening? One that doubts, seeks, discovers, the

path forwards, becomes virtuous and understand “real” virtues of “false” virtues, separating itself

from the idealism of humans, eventually reaching the state where he must give everything, as a

herald, and perish, hopefully building a future for his soul and for earth to become the
overman
.
1/5 – Human Contempt, Human Pride
In the introductory lines to «the last human being», we see Zarathustra understanding how

education may become that which “blocks” the ears of the “herd”, thus he intends to attack their

pride: a personal attack.
He envision a time where humans are incapable of, possibly, cultural creation, or otherwise of

developing all that is understood as human.
Sooner than later, Nietzsche fears human beings not capable, interested, or allowed to seek a better

version of themselves, whether it is natural or philosophical (ethical, judicial, political, teleological,

etc…), and therefore become a soil that is «poor and tame».
Zarathustra thus envisions chaos
1
as a the originating force for “birthing” this human ambition, one

that is a form of contempt, contempt for oneself’s contentment.
This “last human being”, as I’ve anticipated, is one that is not capable of life without reductionism,

but not a reductionism of beings, rather, a reductionism of meaning.
«They abandoned the regions where it was hard to live» because searching for a meaning is

uncomfortable: it has to be, either because one finds there is no finalising meaning, or because the

meaning they find is not to their liking.
They thus return to that which is “warm”: a herd of “neighbours”, and a «bit of poison» provided as

the church’s idealism and moralism, where they’re all the same, and no-one rules, as that who rules

is dead.
This has created a society of norms where all do the same, and where whenever something or

someone is different, it is treated as insane, similarly to how this society of norms believed “the

former world” as insane: it did not follow these.
Even that which is bound to knowledge, and supposed to be to develop oneself as a better man, an

overman,
has turned into a framework to support the norm.
But ultimately, «one honours health», even when they have «a little pleasure» every now and then:

this normative society is charade.
Therefore, they indeed, invented happiness: that happiness that is «poverty and filth and a pitiful

contentment». One is content not because one has developed a meaning, but rather because one has

remained there were it is comfortable.
And they blink. Does it mean “they do as if nothing matters”? “as if nothing’s changed”?.
Here, we feel Zarathustra’s pain when he understands they want naught but to be these “last human

beings”. The public, the crowd, seek this normativeness because, as said, it’s comfortable.
Zarathustra believes this the case because he’s separated himself too much from that which is

human, and therefore he does not speak the language of “the herd”. This develops a difference of

perception.
He might believe all similar to him, of the same capacities, but it is that he’s become unintelligible
This hate, when the crowd laughs, it may indicate that, there may be a basic or maybe whole

understanding of Zarathustra’s message, but they refuse it, they think it “insane”, as it violates

normativity and the safety of apparent normality.
1
It could be that he uses chaos in a hesidodian way, maybe even, in an anaximandrian way, where chaos is a
“substance that initiates cosmogony/theogony”, seen on how it is capable of «give birth to a dancing star». For
more information about chaos and the Apeiron, I recommend reading Paul Seligman’s “The Apeiron of
Anaximander: A Study in the Origin and Function of Metaphysical Ideas”.
1/6 – Death as Liberation
We return to tension with the main protagonist of the scene: the tightrope walker.
He’s emerged from a little door, and this door makes me wonder, is there anything to it? I imagine,

perhaps, it could signify the difficulty of evolution towards intelligence, or towards an
overman
.
Soon after, the “Jester” appeared.
It too makes me wonder: was this a part of the show? Considering the horrible outcome. Our answer

may be soon after. He pushes the walker, with word, threat and force, as they «block the way for

one who is better than you», where we can understand either a fight of human against human,

attempting to become
overman
or otherwise, considering the appearance of the Jester, another being

against man’s race to overcome its self-imposed limits.
The threats become reality once the Jester reaches our protagonist, and there he understands it’s a

little too late, and thus, the human falls.
Before we move on, I want to address the towers pictured. They obviously represent the starting and

end point: animal to human.
When the Jester speaks out «You should be locked away in the tower», do we necessarily mean

only the starting point? Or could it be that “both towers” are to be considered: if there is nothing

better than the
overman
therefore reaching the end point would mean “getting locked into it”.
In the same way -and it’s easier to imagine-, the Jester refers to staying as an animal, without the

purpose of development of a man, which is what the crowd has done: “become cattle”.
The fall was foreseen by Zarathustra, the death of humanity by idealism, where the human hits the

ground faster than whatever it’s constructed: «He threw away his pole and plunged into the depths

even faster than his pole».
This brings chaos to the market, and everyone scrambles away, orderly but thoroughly, like «he sea

when a storm charges in», leaving a hole in the middle, where Zarathustra stays and where the body

hits the ground, Zarathustra is actively fore-telling the events happening.
There is this imagery of humanity not being dead yet once it falls: it crumbles instead, bones and

all, to regain consciousness soon after, yet still grasping that which made it human.
And so, the Walker fears that which was normative, right before death: «The devil would trip me

up. Now he’s going to drag me to hell». And seeks of Zarathustra the role of a priest: «Are you

going to stop him?»
Here we see humanity’s attempt to retain that which made one comfortable: the poison and warmth

of normativity. It is asked of Zarathustra to be one that provides comfort, “you will go to heaven”.
Otherwise, Zarathustra decides to follow, not first without saying “all that you believe isn’t real, but

your death is”: There is no tortuous path, no sin, no ideal world or ideal penance.
The wake of nihilism arrives: «Then I lose nothing when I lose my life. I am not much more than an

animal». We recognise the struggle of humanity through history of differentiating themselves from

animals, something Nietzsche is trying to shatter, and a base of Christian theology, where from the

scriptures, we are God’s preferred creature done to perfection by Him.
This is not just a differentiation from animals, but too from nature, as too, fear of death, something

idealism solves by separating the soul and the body.
Zarathustra assures the Walker, the soul will be dead, but that does not mean one loses its meaning.
Here we see negative nihilism, the loss of belief, become positive nihilism, the construction from

the nothing, the building that character and justice, that morality outside the bounds of idealism and

Christian moralism, through Zarathustra’s words: what you’ve done is what brings value to your

life. Meaning is made by oneself.
The Walker understands, and gives in death, too late to be the
overman
, but early enough to know

there’s more behind the curtains of norm.
1/7 – Night Falls on Zarathustra
The darkness, the cold, they feel but synonyms of abandonment, of social distance between those

that are normative and those that are not, this is why «the curiosity and terror grow weary»: there’s

nothing else to see but a corpse, a reminder of humanity’s mortality, and perhaps something even

worse, a reminder of the meaning of life.
It seems, though, the Walker is no human, at least not anymore, maybe due to his conversation with

Zarathustra or otherwise because he overcame his humanity.
A corpse, though, doubly so as a body, and as the remainder of an image left by the death of

humanity, and by Zarathustra’s failure. Here he reflects: how void and worthless a human can be

that even the slightest breeze may knock it? A mere joke, a Jester.
The images of the potentiality of humans return as these dark clouds that lightning the
overman
, but

Zarathustra is incapable of presenting this in a way they can understand: they are abstracted of

themselves and their lives.
For this reason Zarathustra is not but a fool, or might as well be a corpse, perhaps, too, a Jester.
Zarathustra reflects himself on his «companion» and this is that cold, that night.
He is burying himself. Maybe his own love for humans? Maybe his hopes to gift them the
overman
?
1/8 – The Walk of the Weary
He carries the corpse: nothing more than that which he reflects himself into, nothing less than that

which one grows into when they learn. So, he was a child, but now he’s grown.
The weight of the corpse isn’t in one’s walk, but rather in that which they learn, and what they give.
Here, the Jester reappears, as the voice of the town, but the role of this character is different: it no

longer represents that which is threatening to humans, rather, it may be that the Walker was never to

be a representation of a human, but one in transition being pushed by those who are humans too.
Thus he warns Zarathustra of the same he seemed to already know: his message was not liked, he is

dangerous to normativity, to faith, to the multitude. They don’t see his words as truth, but rather, as

a threat to that safety they required, and threatening this poisoned comfort would be a death

sentence, therefore he’s warned «or tomorrow I shall leap over you, a living man over a dead one».
Note the difference in wording when regarding the Walker. He is now a «dead dog».
This too is noticeable after they encounter the gravediggers. They shine their lights as means of

exposing him the same way society shines the light on those that are considered, buffoons, jesters,

crazies, insane.
The dehumanisation of the Walker is to mean something: perhaps the human-to-be-more, before

Zarathustra, that died trying, perhaps to show the utter disgust the “herd” feel towards difference.
He is now doomed to carry the dead, both the weight of his past, “dead weight”, and the recently

“assassinated” idea of the
overman
the Walker was to represent. The process of becoming.
From here, we can see the allegory to the roast and the devouring devil as Zarathustra’s own process

of ouroboros, eating itself, or rather, its own ideas.
«If only the devil were not a better thief», if only the idea were stronger than the person, «he’ll steal

them both», he would envelop them over their individual person, «he’ll devour them both!», and

remove them.
It is thus fortuitous that Zarathustra rescued the body from the devil, perchance, that he saved the

Walker from a fate -to those that intend to give to the world- worse than forgiveness, removal.
Woods, and especially swap are difficult terrains to walk within, the same goes for those refused

ideas for betterment or change: they may get stuck, or soiled, used incorrectly, or with a horrible

purpose. And then, Zarathustra grows “hungry”, “like the wolves”.
Before entering in what these wolves may be, I too want to point out the difference between dog

and wolf in this context.
One is perceived as aggressive, destructive, evil, overpowering, threatening. The second is

otherwise. There is no animal as human as a dog: comfortable, dependant, anthropocentric, almost

like the dog and the human require each other. For no other reason they’re called “man’s best

friend”. Perhaps this comparison to a “dog” comes as a perception of Zarathustra, that which

accompanies him faithfully. Perhaps it’s a term for diminishing the value of the corpse.
These wolves, they too can be multiple things.
As aforementioned, they show threat, destruction, persecution, reason why they follow Zarathustra,

as he hears their “hungry” growling as a constant threat for his life. These are the people, the

normativity, chasing that who is different, or just generally non-conforming.
The wolves may also be Zarathustra’s fears, insecurities, anger that hungers. A hunger of vengeance

perhaps, one of retaking that which was lost, whether it is his pride, his ego, or his truth, his

comfort, his purpose. This hunger too may be a hunger for more: more knowledge, more

information, hunger for change, for actualisation, reason why «it falls upon me like a robber», it’s a

hunger to take. This idea too can be supported with following phrase, where his hunger grows «in

woods and swamps [...] in the deep night» or so to say, whenever he’s weary and has transited

difficult terrain, and the lack of vision, the lack of direction, that he finds «at a lonely house in

which a
light
was burning».
Zarathustra wonders where his hunger was, where was this fuel to act before, but he did not need it

as he had eaten before, and soon after released in into town. Now that he was challenged,

reprimanded, he needs to know where has he “gone wrong”.
And so he reaches the Hermit’s cabin, where his hunger is tested.
Here food is nothing but comfort, too. Perhaps comfort in knowledge, as the Hermit himself says

«That is why I live here. Beast and human being come to me»: nothing more than to feed the

hungry in the badlands.
The Hermit signifies the classics of philosophy and literature, the knowledge many do not have or

don’t access by simple means as this would mean traversing the difficult terrain that signify the

search for truth -possibly to find there is no truth whatsoever. He offered Zarathustra release of his

needs, and so he offered to feed the dead.
Zarathustra believes the dead not capable of being nurtured by that which the Hermit provides.
The Hermit does not care and forces the feed upon the corpse.
This perhaps is a critique introduced by Nietzsche on the study of the classics and the obligation of

learning them, especially when he considered many of them “lies” or generally “useless”, force

feeding “themselves” even in those ideas and movements that are already dead, lost.
Regardless, the text seems to imply Zarathustra prefers refusing the food over feeding the corpse,

and thus continues his walk.
He finds feed on the small, dim light of the stars, but they eventually disappear from the sky as

dawn comes, leaving him stranded.
Dawn is nothing but “a new day”, the new attempt, perhaps even a new invitation to rest and

“awake again”, just like he did before, but now knowledgeable of the world.
He left the body, his weariness, into the hollow of a tree, hidden so the wolves can’t eat it, and he

himself decides to rest «down head first at the tree, upon the earth and the moss.» connected back

with nature, «weary in body but with a calm soul», as there is no longer a dead weight on his back,

but the tiring of the way still remains, an effort now to be redefined.
1/9 – The Awakening of Zarathustra
His rest determines another period of digestion, where what was learned is now processed, or

discarded, perhaps in a cartesian manner where nothing is kept, nothing is worthwhile but one’s

own truth. Don’t mistake this for rationalism though, as this process is extremely experiential.
He found himself as the woods, silent, this silence from leaving the corpse behind, from digesting,

and finally understanding: He’s not to be the one that leads the herd, nor the one to combat

shepherds, as these will forever hate him, he who brings the destruction of their values, laws, and

morality. He is to share and cooperate with the creative ones, companions, who will make new

values, new laws, and new morals, and in unison attract more who create.
The movement is from sheep to creator. From one that follows the faith, or the truth, the laws and

their comfort, to one that goes beyond their comfort to know, to create, to live.
Companions are not corpses, not anymore, as corpses weight, they slow the “wearer” down.

Corpses are static, are finished, there is no more creation to them. Otherwise companions create and

«harvest» the fruit of change, of development, and the creation of the
overman
.
Zarathustra understands these, too, will go through a similar path to him: persecuted by wolves,

carrying their corpses and hungering for more, «they shall be called annihilators and despisers of

good and evil», where they go against comfort.
But once they find other companions, just like Zarathustra, they will harvest and celebrate.

Celebrate maybe a victory over the wolves, or the shepherds, but surely a victory of becoming the

overman
, the human that has gone beyond.
No longer will he carry the weight of the dead, as he leaves the corpse in the hollow, affirming that

«I shall not be a shepherd, nor a gravedigger». Here he rids himself of his past, of his knowledge,

that which was offered to him by the Hermit: a food not even the dead need.
This way, he becomes the one that gives to the Hermit, and the one who creates, as the hermit

reaches those that are hungry, and the hunger can be satiated with Zarathustra’s happiness of finding

a new truth.
This whole chapter is Zarathustra’s abandonment of the old and the normative to a new, renewed

way of delivering the truth. One that’s developed by oneself and his creation, which is truly free.
1/10 – Reunion
Therefore we reach the end.
The connections with the animals with wisdom and pride seem obvious; what escape me are

possible double meanings brought up by their possible nature.
It seems that the sun did, indeed, grant his blessing, as these appear «under the sun», the “golden

bliss”.
And so the animals were “scouting”. Does this mean Zarathustra really did go anywhere at all? Was

he really separated from his wisdom and his pride at any time? Did these ever really went different

ways as he promises his heart? «May I be wise from the ground up […] I ask instead of my pride

that I always walk with my wisdom! And if some day my wisdom abandons me [….] may my pride

then fly away with my folly!» and so if one day he is neither wise, nor proud, may he lose that

which gives him understanding, may he too become human.
The Nietzschean Human Being
From here we can work on a short description of what the human being is to Nietzsche by

reconstructing the work.
It seems, humans are a reservoir of huge potential, one to become that which is free and true: this

overman
, though seems to prefer the comfort of others, the company of the same within a system

that feeds them lies, and rules, ones that have escaped the focus on nature and freedom as means of

attaining not having to think for themselves, or rather, not having to seek for the meaning of a life.
We find a human that denigrates itself with his contentment to contempt, and that does recognise

the bases of their failings but prefers them and openly invites them.
These normative societies of poisoned men are charade, they all do what they’re not to when

hidden, and they are all quick to shun those who break the rules entrenched in their behaviour,

hunting these rule breakers like hungry wolves with threat and misery, and punishing them with

suicide or death.
But here too we see another facet of humanity: the humanity that Zarathustra loves. It is not just its

potentiality, but also its fearlessness to conflict, to reinventing themselves, their will to leave a mark

and a better world for the rest.
This is the Zarathustra that goes to town, that talks with the Saint, the Crowd, and most importantly,

the Zarathustra that, despite his status, carries the Walker to his grave, carrying the weight of the

horrid and the exhaustion.
Too is the Walker a demonstration of human that differs from the Crowd. He is a creator, an artist,

willing to die for his passion, knowing, even within the bounds of his possible normativity, that he

was to be chased and eventually thrown to his death, so like Zarathustra chants on as qualities the

people he loves have.
These creators, they are humans too. They are to help become the creation of the
overman
by

supporting each other in the set-up of a new norm outside the bounds of Christian idealism, herd

mentality, and the comfort of an undisputed life.
Unlike shepherds, who have learned to follow rules set by none, as they don’t want to lead

themselves, but uphold these ethics, virtues, that are beyond what’s human, what’s natural.
Last but not least, human being is inherently natural for Nietzsche. Dogs, Wolves, Sheep, Worms,

Chimps: they all serve as allegories to that which we come from, not evolutionarily, but

systematically.
Becoming the
overman
means becoming natural in a way we’ve only been before once: between

belief and reason, where all possibilities lie. For this reason the chimp is a joke to humans, but the

humans are a joke to
overmen
. They think:
“How come humans had become such ghostly thus?
How come humans had reinvented life thus?
How come their lives had to be strict and directed thus?” And they smile.
For it was naught their contempt what lead this questions, but rather that which fuels curiosity.
Wisdom and pride are as human as contentment and contempt, so is fear, comfort, laughter, evil.
We are constituents of what we experience, whether it is a sense or a feeling, they form us within

this potentiality.
Some are due to be sheep, some are due to be shepherds and lead through fog with tainted vision,

some are made to be creators and reshape earth and humans alike, and some, the very few, can

become
overmen.
