## Abstract

This essay develops Hannah Arendt's concept of natality as an ontological structure of forgetfulness rather than mere biological or political metaphor. Through sustained engagement with The Human Condition (1958), Between Past and Future (1961), and On Revolution (1963), alongside critical readings by Peg Birmingham, Dana Villa, and Adriana Cavarero, I demonstrate that natality names the capacity for metabolic rupture, the positive power to release the determining hold of the past. This analysis reveals natality as structurally identical to the forgetfulness enabling individual cognition (through figure-ground withdrawal) and epistemological transformation (through paradigmatic rupture): in all three registers, withdrawal constitutes the enabling medium for unprecedented appearance. The essay concludes by examining how contemporary algorithmic governance threatens to eliminate natality through total memory systems that foreclose the temporal gap where political freedom appears.

The

Perpetrators

Through

to

The

(backward-looking)

7).

This
