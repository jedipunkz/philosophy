Foundational Definitions:

These definitions are so central to this work, that they must be established at the outset of
this document. The reader must understand and recognize their true and legal meanings to
prevent either genuine misunderstanding or the deliberate distortion by bad-faith actors.
Any attempt to refute or circumvent the conclusions of this Treatise by redefining these
terms will be ignored out of hand; to do otherwise would demand the rejection of the idea
that words have meaning. Such an action would remove all coherence and is contrary to
the nature of discourse, rendering such discussion mute and impossible.

Many ideas have origins with Political Philosophers such as Rousseau and Lock, and some
documents of American origin, such as the Federalist Papers and Declaration of
Independence. While these are not held in the same esteem under law as the Constitution,
they are foundational to the American Idea. They are to the U.S. Constitution as the
concept of numbers is to Calculus. Calculus makes no sense without the concept of
numbers, and could hardly be said to be possible to develop without them. Just as the
ideas and phrases in the Constitution make no sense without Rousseau, Locke, Federalist
Papers, and the Declaration of Independence. The Constitution could not have been made
in any recognizable form without them. To argue the merit or usefulness in this is to argue
against that of the Constitution itself.

Rights: A set of protections and liberties inherently held by individuals, organizations, or
subordinate political entities which are guaranteed under a system of government. These
are conditions which may not lawfully be violated by the State. They ensure the protection
of life, liberty, and property and act as the foundation of lawful governance. Any violation of
which by the State constitutes a breach of the Social Contract. Rights are recognized and
protected by the State, but do not originate from the State. Rights exist without regard to the
existence of a State. They are innate, inalienable, naturally occurring.

Social Contract: a document or Constitution which is the foundation for lawful
governance over a People, under which terms they have collectively consented to be
governed. It defines the legitimate authority of the State and establishes the mutual
obligations owed between the People and the State.

Under the Social Contract, the State is bound to protect the Rights of the People, including
their lives, liberties, and property. In exchange, the People owe obedience to the laws
which are enacted with their collective consent, and pledge to defend one another and the
State against external invasion.

The legitimacy of the State rests on its continuous adherence to the Social Contract. The
People, having established the Social Contract amongst themselves to create the State,
always retain their Sovereign Right to withdraw their consent and dissolve the State if the
State violates its duties and obligations under this agreement. If the State breaches its
terms under the Social Contract, especially in ways that cannot be lawfully remedied
within its framework, the People have the Right and obligation to dissolve the Contract and
reconstitute a new one which secures their Rights and restores lawful governance.

State vs. state: a State (capital “S”) is a community or society politically organized under
one independent government within a definite territory and subject to no outside control. A
State has Universal Jurisdiction, Absolute Sovereignty, and a monopoly on the legitimate
use of violence or force within its established territory.

A state (lower-case “s”) is a political subdivision within the United States of America (ex.
California, Texas, Virginia). These entities posses their own constitutions, governments,
and legal systems with certain powers and Rights under the Constitution of the United
States. A state in the United States is similar or equivalent to a province or region in other
systems of government.

Jurisdiction: The authority under an established legal framework granted to a body of
government to enforce laws, enact justice, and exercise power over individuals or other
legal entities.

Universal Jurisdiction (domestic): Supreme and comprehensive jurisdiction a sovereign
State possesses within its defined territory and all people within that territory. Unless
lawfully limited by Treaty, Constitution, or mutual compact, the State has ultimate
authority to legislate, adjudicate, and enforce laws on all persons and activities within its
borders without permission by external powers. All States possess Universal Jurisdiction
and Absolute Sovereignty as prerequisites to their lawful existence. Without Universal
Jurisdiction within its territory, no State can be said to have sovereignty or legitimacy.

Sovereignty: The supreme authority to make binding decisions over all persons, actions,
and legal entities within a defined territory. It grants the State the exclusive Right to
legislate, enforce laws, administer justice, and define its own institutional structure. In a
republican form of government, Sovereignty originates from the People and is conditionally

delegated to the State through the Social Contract. The legitimacy of this authority
depends entirely on the State’s adherence to the terms of that Contract.

Absolute Sovereignty: The indivisible power of a sovereign State over all persons and
actions, and judicial matters within its territory. lt encompasses all legislative, judicial, and
executive powers, constrained only by the State’s own Constitutional framework and Social
Contract. Treaties, compacts or federalist delegation may also limit this power so far as
they are consistent with the Constitution. Absolute Sovereignty, with Universal Jurisdiction,
is a prerequisite to a State’s lawful existence.

Republic: the name of any State that is governed by Laws rather than by the will of
individuals (Rousseau). A law is only a law if it is legitimate. A law’s legitimacy comes from
its creation and public declaration from the Legislature in accordance with the procedures
established in the State’s Constitution and Social Contract, and equally applied to all.

The Legislature is composed of either the whole of the population, or those
Representatives who are duly elected and consented to by the People of which they
represent. The Legislative power belongs to the People, and can belong to it alone
(Rousseau). Laws created by any other but the Legislature are no more than decrees or
edicts by an individual, and are not legitimate, nor are they legitimately or lawfully
enforceable.

General Will: The Will of the People collectively towards their shared interests and
common good, with general application and consideration of the law to all, universally.
Takes no consideration of private desires and governs all as equals under a just and legal
order.

Specific Will: The Will of a specific individual or faction of individuals in pursuit of a private
interest, contrary to or notwithstanding the General Will. It may coexist within a republic
when constrained by law. When it dominates or takes the place of the General Will it
necessarily becomes a source of injustice, corruption, and tyranny.

Legitimacy: The Right to govern and exercise power, as recognized and consented to by the
People and in accordance with established legal frameworks of a Constitution, Social
Contract, and laws. Distinguishes lawful governance from unlawful occupation by force.
Not synonymous with power, force of arms, or mere ability to impose one’s WILL.

Consent of the Governed: The idea and Principle that all legitimate power comes from the
voluntary and informed agreement of and by the People, which enables them to be
governed under the specific terms of the Social Contract and Constitution. Consent is an
ongoing event and may be withdrawn if the State violates its terms of the Social Contract.

Rule of Law: The idea that all People, organizations, and the State itself are subject to and
accountable under the law. That laws are created generally and applied equally to all,
including to the State and those acting on its behalf. Laws are and can only be created by
the Legislature, validated by the Judiciary, and enforced by the Executive. No body or
individual may create laws except for the Legislature.

Despot: One who sets themselves above the laws (Rousseau). Replaces rule by consent
and legal authority with the rule of arbitrary and Specific WiLL.

Tyrant: One who aggregates to themselves the Sovereign authority without having a Right
to it; A individual or group of individuals who govern violently without regard for justice or he
law; acting in contradiction to the laws. Tyrant and Usurper are interchangeable terms,
those who seize or exercise power without legitimacy (Rousseau).

State Suicide: A condition when breaches by the State of its own foundational legal
framework are so severe, systemic, and unremedied that no lawful mechanisms remain to
correct or constrain its actions. Technicalities are not remedies; if no lawful mechanisms
remain in practice, then neither do they remain in fact. The State voids its own claim to
legitimacy once it no longer binds itself to conditions by which their obedience was once
owed. The People are thus no longer bound by duty or consent to the State. A State’s self-
termination of legitimacy and authority to rule over free People.

Zombie State: A political entity which continues to operate and exert force over a People
once the State has irreparably severed its own legitimate and Constitutional foundations. It
is no longer a lawful government but a decaying vestige of what once was a State; animated
by bureaucratic inertia and force rather than lawfulness and consent of the People. It may
retain outward appearances of legitimacy while functioning outside the bounds of the
Social Contract and lawful Constitutional framework which once established and bound
its original existence. It is the consequence of State Suicide.
