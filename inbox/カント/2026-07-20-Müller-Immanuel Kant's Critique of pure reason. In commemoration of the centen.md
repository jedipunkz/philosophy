Our  reason  (Verninift)  has  this  peculiar  fate
that,  with  reference  to  one  class  of  its  knowledge,
it  is  always  troubled  with  questions  which  cannot
be  ignored,  because  they  spring  from  the  very
nature  of  reason,  and  which  cannot  be  answered,
because  they  transcend  the  powers  of  human
reason.

Nor  is  human  reason  to  be  blamed  for  this.
It  begins  with  principles  which,  in  the  course  of
experience,  it  must  follow,  and  which  seem  suffi-
ciently confirmed  by  experience.  With  these  again,
according  to  the  necessities  of  its  nature,  it  rises
higher  and  higher  to  more  remote  conditions.
But  M'hen  it  perceives  that  in  this  way  its  work
remains  for  ever  incomplete,  because  the  questions
never  cease,  it  finds  itself  constrained  to  take

experimental  application,  and  nevertheless  seem  so
unobjectionable  that  even  ordinary  common  sense
agrees  with  them.  Thus  reason  becomes  involved
