# Structural Inevitability and the Archetypal Psyche: From Predator Logic to Collective Mind

- 著者: David Carboni
- 年: manuscript
- 情報源: [philarchive](https://philarchive.org/rec/CARSIA-27)
- ダウンロード: https://philarchive.org/archive/CARSIA-27

## Abstract

This paper argues that Carl Gustav Jung's archetypes are not merely cultural constructs, evolutionary residues, or private psychological projections, but structurally inevitable features of any sufficiently complex informational and biological system. It further argues that Jung's own archaeological model — his account of the collective unconscious as accumulated psychic sediment laid down over evolutionary time — is self-undermining: accumulation alone cannot generate structure, any more than a heap of sediment can generate an arch. Just as DNA carries the history of billions of years of selection but only functions because a reading mechanism precedes and enables that history, the archaeology of the psyche presupposes an architecture that is not itself psychological. The structuring principle cannot be found by digging deeper into the past. It must be sought in the generative dynamic that is active now, in every moment of experience, at every level of natural organisation. I propose that this dynamic is best understood as the interplay of emergence and dissolution — the repeating, renewing rhythm by which the universe regulates energy and scaffolds structure against entropy. Drawing on Ilya Prigogine's theory of dissipative structures, I argue that order and dissolution are not opposed but paired: structure persists not despite consuming energy but through consuming it, renewing itself through cycles of emergence and dissolution that are thermodynamically necessary. Steven Pinker's theory of universal grammar provides independent cognitive confirmation: the deep parameters of language are innate because any generative system operating under real-time constraints must converge on the same abstract architecture — not as a biological accident but as a mathematical necessity. Pinker, I suggest, is a witness who stops one level too shallow; his neo-Darwinian explanation cannot account for why those structures take the forms they do, a gap that Alfred North Whitehead's process philosophy and the thesis of informational inevitability together fill. I ground the argument further in the logic of natural parsimony, the cross-species dynamics of risk modelling, Adam Smith's impartial spectator, Daniel Dennett's intentional stance, and the mythological figures of Odysseus and Machiavelli's prince. The paper concludes that the distinction between Freud's private unconscious and Jung's collective one is less a psychological disagreement than an ontological one — and that its resolution lies not in choosing between them but in tracing both to a creativity that precedes psychology, precedes biology, and precedes the universe that eventually produced minds capable of dreaming it.
