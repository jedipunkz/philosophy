## Abstract

This paper presents a structural reading of Nietzsche’s Thus Spoke Zarathustra as a theory of the self, contrasting it with John Bowlby’s attachment theory. It argues that Zarathustra embodies an agency derived from insecure-avoidant relational structures, characterized by unmodulated resolution, unilateral assertion, and isolation. While aesthetically compelling, this model appears structurally unsustainable when considered as a general guide to human development. By highlighting the absence of secure relational grounding and affective co-regulation, the analysis clarifies structural limitations in Nietzsche’s conception of self and suggests a possible integration of relationally moderated agency with the pursuit of self-overcoming. This reading is structural and interpretive; it does not intend to offer a historical or biographical psychological diagnosis of Nietzsche or his characters.

1. Introduction

2. Theoretical Background

2.1 Nietzsche and the Self

Zarathustra embodies the will to power, conceived as the principle of
selfaffirmation and

creative expansion. In Nietzsche’s words:

“I teach you the overman. Man is something that shall be overcome.
What have you done to overcome him?” (
Zarathustra
, Part I,
“Prologue”).

The figure of Zarathustra operates in a radically solitary and unilaterall y
assertive mode: resolution is absolute, coregulation with others is
absent, and vulnerability is largely suppressed. As noted by
commentators such as Young (1995) and Solomon (2003), the
existential and aesthetic force of the work derives in part from t his
intensity, even as it abstracts from ordinary relational embeddedness.

2.2 Bowlby and Relational Agency

Attachment theory emphasizes the development of self in relational
context. A secure attachment provides a base of safety, enabling
exploration, reg ulated agency, and flexible engagement with others
(Bowlby, 1969). In contrast, insecureavoidant attachment tends toward
isolated agency, attenuation of affective signaling, and defensive selfsufficiency, potentially generating structural vulnerabilities

in longterm
relational functioning (Mikulincer & Shaver, 2007).

3. Structural Analysis of Zarathustra

Zarathustra exemplifies a form of agency that can be structurally
compared to insecureavoidant relational organization:



Unmodulated resolution:

decisions and actions are not adjusted
in response to relational feedback.



Isolation:

engagement with others is predominantly declarative
rather than reciprocal.



Absence of secure relational grounding:

agency unfolds without
mutual affective scaffolding.

N
ietzsche explicitly valorizes this intensity:

“You must be ready to burn yourself in your own flame: how could you
rise anew if you have not first become ashes?” (
Zarathustra
, Part II, “On
the Sublime Ones”).

From a structural perspective, such an arrangem ent appears unstable
as a generalized model of human development. The overman is
internally coherent and philosophically powerful, yet it lacks the relational
modulation that contemporary developmental psychology identifies as
central to sustainable agency
.

5. Discussion

Philosophically, Nietzsche’s ideal remains coherent and aesthetically
formidable. It articulates a vision of radical selfovercoming that resists
dependency and celebrates creative autonomy.

Psychologically, however, sustaina ble selftransformation appears to
require integration of potency with relational modulation. Attachment
theory clarifies why coregulated agency
—
anchored in secure relational
structures
—
tends to exhibit greater developmental resilience.

The overman can
thus be interpreted as a form of intensified agency that
abstracts from relational foundations. This abstraction may illuminate
both the dramatic force and the structural vulnerability of Nietzsche’s
model.

6. Conclusion

Thus Spoke Zarathustra

presents a
powerful vision of agency and selfovercoming. Structurally considered, however, the figure of Zarathustra
exemplifies the limits of agency that unfolds without secure relational
grounding: absolute, unmodulated, and developmentally unstable.

By applying a ttachment theory as an analytical lens, this reading clarifies
both the coherence and the fragility of Nietzsche’s conception of self. It
further suggests that integrating relationally grounded agency with the
pursuit of selfovercoming may yield a more su stainable model of human
potency
—
one that bridges existential intensity with psychological
realism.
