A fatalist—if there is any such—thinks he cannot do anything about the future. He thinks it is not up to him what is
going to happen next year, tomorrow, or the very next moment. He thinks that even his own behavior is not in the
least within his power, any more than the motions of the heavenly bodies, the events of remote history, or the

political developments in China. It would, accordingly, be pointless for him to deliberate about what he is going to
do, for a man deliberates only about such things as he believes are within his power to do and to forego, or to affect
by his doings and foregoings.

~ Richard Taylor, “Fatalism,” 1962

Nietzsche's notion of amor Fati

Nietzsche wrote of amor fati (the love of fate) as though it were a healthy psychological
orientation toward the perceived inevitable. Mike Archbold will start us off by considering
Nietzsche’s subjective approach to fate. See his slides here: “Nietzsche: Will and Fatalism, a
Tension.”

Intro

Then we will discuss an account of the objective situation that subjective fatalism of the
Nietzschean type, for example, is a reaction to: namely, the influential one put forward by
Richard Taylor in 1962: the conclusion that fatalism best describes reality, no matter how we
may or may not feel about it. This is known as “logical fatalism”—it is the view that “we are
powerless to do anything other than what we actually do,” and it appeals only to “logical laws
and metaphysical necessities...”

Along the way, we will untangle fatalism from determinism and its presuppositions and
consequences. Fatalism and determinism are not the same. Fatalism is a simpler metaphysical
idea involving certain basic “truths” of logic, time, and knowledge. It has a leaner metaphysics
than determinism. Determinism complicates matters by requiring additional assumptions about
the way the world is independently of our empirical limitations or rational capabilities. It posits
extra layers of metaphysically lavish notions like materialism and “causation.” Moreover, there
is an interesting argument owing to Robert Holton suggesting that even if causal determinism is
true, it may lose some of its mojo because its truth can be decoupled from any implications about
the “predictability” of the future. Determinism, he argues, does not entail foreknowledge. In
other words, determinism may accurately describe reality (assuming “causation” more
enlightening than philosophers suspect it
to be) while being rather useless to any
project we may have.

Two philosophers on free will and determinism

Might these two both be wrong?

Part of the appeal of fatalism is its relative elegance. It is sparse in its assumptions about what
there is and the way they work. If determinism’s metaphysics is complicated by causation, that
of free will is complicated by the metaphysics of agency.

David Hume three centuries ago cast suspicion on the notion of causation, suggesting we had no
reason to think of it as anything but an induced psychological proclivity. Nature does to us what
Pavlov did to his dog. What we actually experience is one damn thing after another with
regularity. We call it causation. See this often enough and we abstract “natural laws” from the
individual experiences. Causation is an organizing principle. Useful because of our
environmentally tweaked evolution. Like a paperclip. How useful would a paperclip be if we had
never developed hands?

And what are these things, these “agents,” that exempt themselves from the laws that constrain
everything else in the universe? (Or every thought about anything in the universe?) Are agents
super dense concentrations of hubris?

The description fatalism offers of the universe owes nothing to these assumptions and problems
they raise.

It is also important to say something about the optional relationship between pessimism or
optimism to fatalism. Whether or not fatalism is a viable theory’ objectively, the subjective
attitudes of optimism and pessimism are only contingently associated with it...

Think fatalism and pessimism go together? Think again: consider Trump. He repeatedly
identifies himself as a fatalist, and is and always has been certain he was destined for great
things, and he is indefatigably optimistic that the rest of the world, too, will come to recognize
this in time. Why else should America (or anything else) “be great”? (See articles in Resources
below.)

And, finally, maybe (if not on this occasion on a subsequent one) we’ll touch on ancient classical
—and as Nietzsche called it, “joyful”—cynicism, from which, we think, he took inspiration. For
Nietzsche—as for the classical cynics like Diogenes, Hipparchia, and Crates—to love fate is an
imperative... We don’t love fate because it is a lovable thing. We love it—if we love it—because
we do not want to be embarrassed by our total powerlessness...

Logical Fatalism

There is an objective argument for the truth of what fatalism is a reaction to quite apart from
whether a subjective orientation (such as Nietzsche’s recommends) appeals to you or not. It
involves only basic notions of truth, logic and time.

But, first, fatalism is not determinism. That’s a different notion relying on at least one other
philosophically troublesome concept, namely, causation. Fatalism is consistent with both
determinism and its opposite, indeterminism. Determinism obviously is not. If the fatalist thesis
correctly describes the nature of experience, then the convictions driving both determinism and
free will are wholly psychological needs, if even that. Useful, perhaps, as coping mechanisms,
but metaphysically idle. Truth, logic and time are more simple than the notion of causation,
which assumes, in addition to these, an entire cosmos of the material.

Fatalism is not the same as determinism and it does not necessarily entail pessimism. It may spur
and ground, as Nietzsche suggests, a psychological orientation, a natural or assumed reaction
toward life events.

Or it can be held as a basic fact about the world irrespective of objective or subjective truth—as
basic as a law of logic. Deny it at your own peril. Indeed, it can be held as tightly as a law of
logic because it can be shown to be founded on little else but widely accepted conceptions of
truth and time plus basic logic. Dismissing fatalism may entail dismissing a law of logic
commonly taken as fundamental to rational thinking.

Under a common understanding of what it means for a statement to be true, if the statement is
true in the present tense, then it must be true. You cannot say the opposite without contradicting
yourself. You cannot now say you are not reading this. Or five minutes from now that you did
not read it. But a fatalist believes this is as true of future statements as we agree it is about
present and past statements. If they are true at any time, they cannot become false with the
passage of time. If you come to learn that a statement you formerly regarded as true is false,
that’s because you mistook a falsity to be true in the first place, not because it was true and then
somehow magically became false. If something caused a statement about a future event to
become false, you were wrong to ever think it was true in the first place. If nothing caused a
statement about a future event to become false, you were still wrong to ever think it was true in
the first place. You were in error in your prediction, not that the statement changed its truth
value. True statements don’t become false. Or vice versa.

Note that we are not talking about the knowledge-state of any knower entertaining the truth or
falsity of a statement. Knowledge of the truth value of a statement is a very different thing from
the truth value of the statement. Knowledge implies a knower with contingent powers of
knowing (except in the case of an omniscient being, if there is any such thing).

Truth, under one of the simplest definitions, is a correspondence between what a statement
claims and a state of affairs external to the claim. The statement is true when what it claims
corresponds with the state of affairs it refers to. False, when it doesn't.

If a statement describing a state of affairs is true now, then if you had expressed yesterday that
this statement would hold true today, you would have been correct. Likewise if you make a
statement now about some event tomorrow and it happens that we learn tomorrow that it is true,
then it is true now that it will be true tomorrow. And if it will be true tomorrow, then it is true
now to say it will be true tomorrow. And it would have been true a billion years ago had there
been anyone around to say it then.

Time does not make statements true. Their inevitability makes them true. Time merely informs
us of future truths when the future they describe becomes a “now.” There is no logical way in
which a true future statement could turnout not to be true. When the time ceases to be tomorrow
and becomes a present or past statement, it will be true because it always was true, not because at
one time it was not and then became true. Or that it is required that someone exist to know this
truth. Likewise, if it turns out to be false, then it was always false. You were mistaken to think
otherwise if you thought that way today—or at any time.

Knowledge of the truth or falsity of a statement is irrelevant to the truth or falsity of the
statement. In making this claim we are relying only on two assumptions: one about the nature of
truth and one about the laws that govern intelligibility of any statement, namely a basic law of
logic or reasoning.

For example, suppose dinosaurs existed 67 million years ago. And that no dinosaur then existed
who could formulate the proposition that “I, a dinosaur, exist.” And so could not know they
themselves existed. And further suppose that 66 million years ago an asteroid struck the earth
and eliminated dinosaurs and indeed most life on earth. And thus none of our evolutionary
ancestors survived, hence we never evolved, and the earth were today as barren of life as it
appears Mars is. Under these circumstances, would propositions such as “once dinosaurs
existed” and “an asteroid struck the earth at a certain moment in its history” be true?

Under one common conception of truth, the answer would be “yes.” A statement’s truth does not
depend on there being anyone around to know its truth. (There are other stranger conceptions of

truth under which this, in fact, may not follow, but we’ll return to those in a moment.)

Some assumptions made by the logical Fatalist argument:

¢ A statement’s truth value does not require cognitive appreciation.

¢ A statement’s truth value may or may not be determined by prior events, but the passage
of time, by itself, does not alter truth values. Time is not, in itself, efficacious.

¢ A statement’s truth value—provided the statement is well-formed, that is, is grammatical
and semantically able (i.e., not nonsense)—‘is either true or it is false. There is no third
possibility. This is an ancient law of logic known as the law of excluded middle (tertium
non datur). In other words, statements do not first appear as empty containers needing the
passage of time to fill them with a truth value, like a drinking glass in a cupboard pre-
exists empty and only awaits being filled with drink.

Ways to escape Fatalism and their price

The correspondence theory of truth may be incorrect. The truth of a proposition may not always
map onto anything in reality. A “true” future prediction may not refer to any future state of the
world because these future states lack reality. Dumping this widely held theory of truth is one
way to escape fatalism. But at what price?

Or, our theory of time may be off. Fatalism seems to assume what is called the block or “B”
theory of time, in which past, present, and future events are all equally real and equally
susceptible to being pinned down by a true proposition. But perhaps instead one or other version
of the “A” theory of time is the correct one.

One version of the “A” theory, called the “partial block” theory of time, is one in which the past
and present have reality, but not the future. The latter remains open or fluid until the present pins
it down. The other more radical version of the “A” theory, “presentism,” takes the present
moment—and only the present moment—as the gold standard of reality: the past (through recent
memory), and the future (through immediate anticipation) may partake of a measure of “reality”
but this measure fades with distance in time from the present moment. Both the future and the
past, to a presentist, look the way a path may appear to a near-sighted person without corrective
lenses. Things emerge out of obscurity and into it again as time passes (or we move through it).

Or, suppose the principle of bivalence—which insists that every proposition must either be true
or false but not both—is false. It makes no reference to past, present, or future statements. Some
non-standard, non-Western logics, indeed, do permit other valences such as “indeterminate” or
“neither” or even “both” as truth values. We can say of propositions describing future events that
they are neither true nor false but indeterminate, for instance.

But dismissing the principle of bivalence has some strange consequences. It means, among other
things, that if I had predicted yesterday that I would be writing this now it could have not been
said then that the prediction is true. In fact, no prediction can ever be true except in hindsight.
But what is the point of a prediction if you always have to wait to see if it is true. You can do that
without making any predictions at all. What is the point of them? So will we have to dispense
with a coherent concept of “prediction” to avoid fatalism?

Logical fatalism, determinism, and free will
A good introductory lecture surveying the philosophical concepts of fatalism, determinism, and
free will by Prof. Rami El Ali.

“Fate,” by Richard Taylor (from Metaphysics, 2nd ed. 1974) is the classic modern exposition on
the subject. See also the original more technical account, “Fatalism,” in The Philosophical
Review 1962. Taylor revived in contemporary analytic terms the ancient argument for fatalism
found in (and rejected by) Aristotle.

“Fatalism and the Metaphysics of Contingency,” M. Oreste Fiocco. In Steven M. Cahn &
Maureen Eckert (eds.), Freedom and the Self: Essays on the Philosophy of David Foster
Wallace. Columbia University Press. pp. 57-92 (2015). This essay is an excellent exposition and
defense of Taylor’s argument.

“Logical Fatalism with Alicia Finch.” A podcast interview with Alicia Finch in which she
explains in detail the argument for logical fatalism, its incompatibility with free will, and various

possible responses.

Despite the title “Metaphysics: The Problem of Free Will,” Richard Holton in this short video
presentation highlights an often ignored but serious epistemological problem for determinism.
Maybe we don’t have free will because determinism is true, but, if so, determinism may promise
more than (it is commonly assumed) it can deliver: it cannot give us foreknowledge. His paper

“From Determinism to Resignation; and How to Stop It” expounds on the problem in more
detail.

“Fatalism,” by Hugh Rice in the SEP surveys the problem and attempted solutions.

“Future Contingents,” Andrea Iacona’s entry in the IEP further explores some of the subtleties
involved in the logic of fatalism.
