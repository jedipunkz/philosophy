FOREWORD

This study of four 19th-century autobiographers was written more
than fifty years ago (1968-1971) as a doctoral thesis. A shortened
version was included in my Reading Relations (1982). The overall
approach and main argument were fairly innovative at the time and
still seem to me of interest and relevance today and therefore worth
now putting into print.

One considerable change since the 1960s is that the autobiographies
themselves are now easily available, as reprints or new editions, and as
downloads on the internet. So it is now possible to read the writings I
then commented upon without needing access to specialist collections
in university libraries. 1 wish that had been the case fifty years ago.

I think the autobiographies are enjoyable and interesting for several
reasons and my analysis tried to respect them as writings rather than
simply raid them for historical ‘evidence’. The issues they raise are still
with us.

The Afterword provides some further context but I would suggest
simply reading the four studies, without even necessarily reading the
autobiographies first, though I hope you will be sufficiently interested
to do so at some point. I have not included the 90 pages of footnotes
and references, though a selected bibliography is provided. The now-
dated footnotes primarily indicated the historical context of the
autobiographies which to a large extent the autobiographies can
themselves provide.

The main text as it stands was taken from a 50-year-old typescript
which was scanned as the basis for optical character recognition
software and I have retained some of the typing conventions of the
1970s. The old-fashioned quotation marks ". ." usually indicate
actual phrases or passages from the autobiographies themselves
whereas the more up-to-date quotation marks ‘. .’ are used for other
quotations and terms. I also retain my excessive use of semi-colons
rather than short sentences with full stops. Page references are to the
original editions which may differ from recent reprints

My main debt while actually writing the thesis was to my Supervisor,
Arthur Sale, of Magdalene College, for his great patience and
sympathetic encouragement. Above all, I enjoyed the community of
scholars in many fields which constituted the very helpful context in
which most of this work was done: St. Edmund’s House, Cambridge.
Despite such debts, I acknowledge that the argument and the
limitations of this thesis still remain my own responsibility.

BS.

INTRODUCTION

There appears to be agreement on one essential: that the type of
social criticism and intellectual history currently in fashion must be
superseded by a composite discipline which will be a structural
investigation into the relationship between theory and practice.
Many literary intellectuals, while recognising the necessity for a
new approach, are somewhat overwhelmed at the prospect of it.
Lacking the philosophical training of their German colleagues and
the sociological expertise of the French . . they are simply
unqualified technically for the new intellectual experience. And
thus their real talent, empirical research, has tended very often to
be overshadowed by their relative neglect of other fields.
TLS, 5th March 1970

This study is not as comprehensive as I would have wished. It proved
impossible, within sensible limits of length, both to present the
individual analyses in some detail and to provide an adequate context
of methodological elaboration and cultural history. For example, a
detailed account might also have been offered of the historical
development and significance of the autobiographical mode; the
theoretical problems of delineating an adequate approach to a
sociology of literature might have been investigated; some middle-class
autobiographies might have been included for comparison; a larger
selection of working-class autobiographies could have been analysed;
the notion of ‘class’ might have been given a more extended
examination. Some of these themes have been touched upon, in
drastically compressed form, in the Introduction and Conclusion, but
my main purpose even in those sections has been to trace a clear line
of argument in the text.

7 selection of texts

The subject of this study is a small selection of autobiographies. The
method of analysis is basically a form of ‘close reading’, of literary
criticism. That method is used within a framework derived from a
‘sociology of literature’ approach, and the content of the chosen
autobiographies is more familiarly met with in the context of studies in
labour history. The connections between each of these aspects of the
study are outlined in this Introduction, but a preliminary note is
required on the criteria of selection.

Since the study began with a primarily methodological emphasis, as
an attempt to see how far a method of literary critical analysis could be
usefully applied to texts not normally regarded as_ literature
(autobiographies), no rigid criteria of selection were employed: the
thesis to be tested was, simply, whether such texts could be
illuminated by that kind of analysis

The choice of ‘nineteenth-century British working-class political
autobiographies’, broadly defined as autobiographies by people more
immediately associated with a working-class background than with any
other social position, and connected at some stage with some form of
political event or movement, was dictated by personal preference and
interest, though also to some extent by the theoretical considerations
outlined in section (ii) below.

The number of autobiographies falling within that broadly-defined
category proved to be fairly large. The choice of the particular texts
analysed in Part Two was arrived at on the grounds of reputation and
accessibility, in the case of Bamford and Somerville, and on the
opposite grounds of relative obscurity and apparent irrelevance (a
‘beggar boy’, a minor journalist) in the case of Burn and Frost.

If the method of analysis were indeed useful, it should, I thought,
prove to be equally applicable to texts generally taken, for whatever
reasons, as outstanding examples of their kind and to apparently
petipheral or ‘ordinary’ examples.

Other autobiographies, of both kinds, were considered for inclusion
but since adequate analysis of each example has required considerable
space, it seemed preferable to concentrate on a small selection of
reasonably comprehensive studies than to include a large number of
over-compressed and unconvincing sketches. Moreover, since it
would somewhat contradict the whole mode of analysis adopted to
refer simply to a passage in an autobiography, out of context of the
whole work and of its historical ambience, I have not included
comparisons with any autobiographies not examined in detail. For the
same teason, I have not attempted to ‘summarise’ an analysis for any
autobiography except the four treated at length.

Even so, none of the individual analyses included is exhaustive. The
chapter on Bamford is the most comprehensive in terms of
examination of the complete text, partly as an example of a fairly full
analysis and partly because Bamford is probably the best known of the
autobiographers dealt with. Each analysis is presented as a self-
contained study of a particular text and author.

The more general conclusions and connections finally drawn
emerged only after the individual studies were completed and are
offered with an even greater decree of tentativeness than the results of

each single analysis. That such a relatively random selection of texts
proved to be linked, however, in suggestive ways is itself interesting
and may indicate that an adequate elaboration of the method and its
application in appropriate detail to other examples could provide a
further vindication both of the general intention of the study and of its
conclusions.

a problems of method

Some connections between the two major facets of this study —the
material (autobiographies) and the method (sociology of literature) can
be indicated briefly. Sociology of literature was indirectly linked, in its
modern origins, with an interest in autobiographies. Wilhelm Dilthey
placed great emphasis on biography and autobiography as an element
in historical understanding; his pupil and eventual editor, Georg
Misch, provided the first major study of autobiographical writings, and
another pupil of Dilthey, Gyérgy Lukacs, has been perhaps the most
influential figure in the development of sociology of literature.

Lukacs’s main early theoretical work, Hestory and Class Consciousness
(1923), tried to establish that the emergence of the proletariat as a self-
conscious class resolved in terms of praxis the problem of the subject-
object dialectic posed by German Idealism, arguing that the class-
consciousness of the proletariat was the historical or materialist
equivalent of the Hegelian self-knowledge of the Zeitgeist, but Lukacs
maintained that the relevant class-consciousness was not the ‘actual’
consciousness of individual members of the proletariat, but rather the
‘imputed’ consciousness of the class—i.e. that ‘consciousness’ which
was historically appropriate to the class as a whole. This distinction has
strongly influenced Lukacs’s and others’ subsequent studies in the
sociology of literature. Particularly in the formulation emphasised by
Lucien Goldmann, that of ‘world vision’, ‘imputed class
consciousness’ has been employed as one mediation, between society
and literary artefact, the work of art being regarded as an expression of
that ‘world vision’ appropriate to the class of which the individual
author is a member or with whose interests he is allied.

This study examines, therefore, a number of writings which seem
peculiarly central to the problems raised by Lukacs’s original argument.
By analysing the autobiographies of some individuals who can be
broadly classified as members of, or allied to, the working class during
an important period in the formation of its political self-consciousness,
I hope, in part, to clarify the distinction between ‘actual’ and ‘imputed’
‘class consciousness’ during that historical period. The earliest
autobiographer examined was born in 1788, shortly before the
establishment of the London Corresponding Society, and the latest

autobiography considered was written in 1886, a few years after the
foundation of the Social-Democratic Federation. The period involved
therefore covers a number of important phases in working-class
political history.

Clearly, then, this study also encroaches on the field of history, and
in particular the sub-discipline of labour history. The development of
social history and labour history in the mid-20th century has attempted
to deepen and enrich previous modes of historical writing
(constitutional, legal, narrowly ‘political’, economic, etc.), by providing
an insight into the experience of the ‘ordinary’ strata of society. It was
perhaps Collingwood, also greatly influenced by Dilthey, who most
forcefully alerted a generation of historians to the necessity of thinking
themselves back into the problems and lived experience of any period
under study, and this emphasis has been strongly present in much
work done recently in the fields of social and labour history.

Yet ‘Labour History’ too has had difficulty in defining an
appropriate method. Asa Briggs, in a paper at the inaugural meeting of
the Society for the Study of Labour History, in 1960, outlined five
different traditions of Labour History (reminiscences, the Hammonds’
approach, Marxist, official histories of Trades Unions etc., the Webbs’
method) and after listing various areas of future research commented:
‘All these studies demand a human as well as an analytical approach,
insight and imagination as well as discipline.’ Over ten years later, in
reviewing the second volume of Essays in Labour History, Eric
Hobsbawm could still argue that the nature of the ‘labour movement’
was not easily amenable to ‘institutional’, narrative or simple
biographical approaches, and remarked:

If the subject of this book had not been the labour movement but,
say, the Tudor period, its monographs would have found
themselves more easily classified with Neale and Elton than with
Lawrence Stone or Keith Thomas.

Part of the problem here can be gauged from the methodological
tension in Hobsbawm’s own writings: a juxtaposition of statistical and
‘economic history’ studies with attempts to convey the lived
experience of a generation or group. The debate about the ‘standard of
living’ in the Industrial Revolution was in part the result of a related
clash of methods: what the ‘objective data’, about wages and price
fluctuations, seem to show, as against what it ‘felt like’ to undergo the
complex changes of that period. Other aspects of this methodological
unease can be seen in recent studies of ‘the crowd’, in eighteenth and
nineteenth century England or in the French Revolution, which

Richard Cobb has criticised for still treating the actual participants as
faceless units.

Perhaps the most sustained attempt in recent labour history to
combine ‘insight and imagination as well as discipline’ is E. P.
Thompson’s The Making of the English Working Class, and part of the
intention of that book can be seen in the introductory comment:

I am seeking to rescue the poor stockinger, the Luddite cropper,
the "obsolete" hand-loom weaver, the "Utopian" artisan, and even
the deluded follower of Joanna Southcott, from the enormous
condescension of posterity. . . Their aspirations were valid in
terms of their own experience; and, if they were casualties of
history, they remain, condemned in their own lives, as casualties.

This study attempts, in its own way, to emulate that intention. By
using a literary critical approach, I hope to get close to the "lives" of
some participants in the long making of the ‘labour movement’, and by
employing that approach within a sociology of literature framework I
intend not to lose sight of the nature and, particularly, some of the
problems of that ‘movement’.

It is not accidental that Thompson’s work in part overlaps with,
parallels, or draws upon some aspects of literary criticism. Thompson
has written, for example, a major study of William Morris, and even in
The Making of the English Working Class an interest in the creative
literature of the period is strongly present. One specific parallel with
the work. of a literary critic can be usefully instanced here. Thompson
formulates an important conclusion of his work in the comment: ‘By
1832 there were strongly based and self-conscious working-class
institutions, . . working-class intellectual traditions, working-class
community patterns, and a working-class structure of feeling’. The
term ‘structure of feeling’ is apparently derived from Raymond
Williams’s The Long Revolution, where Williams writes:

The most difficult thing to get hold of, in studying any past
petiod, is this felt sense of the quality of life at a particular time
and place: a sense of the ways in which the particular activities
combined into a way of thinking and living. .. The term I would
suggest to describe this is structure of feeling: it is as firm and
definite as ‘structure’ suggests, yet it operates in the most delicate
and least tangible parts of our activity.

Williams’s work, too, crosses the boundaries between literary
criticism, cultural history and history in general, yet the sensibility

operating in his work is primarily that of the literary critic (a position
for which Thompson has criticised him). Williams’s approach, indeed,
parallels a great deal of Lukacs’s critical method, and one brief
example from Lukacs might illustrate the grounds for expecting
connections to emerge between literary criticism and labour history

In his The Haostorical Novel, Lukacs writes of the difficulty, for the
novelist, of presenting an historical period not just in terms of material
details but in the psychological depiction of character; he praises Scott
for conveying exactly ‘the quality of the inner life, the morality,
capacity for sacrifice, steadfastness, etc., peculiar to a given age’, for his
‘ability to translate elements of economic and social change into
human fates, into an altered psychology.’ Clearly, we have here
something akin to the combined interests of the historian and the
literary critic — an interest both in the material ‘facts’ of a period and
(to use Williams’s term) the structure of feeling of that period. The
analyses which follow, in Part Two of this study, also attempt to
combine both interests, but by almost reversing the procedure Lukacs
sees as characteristic of Scott. My own approach could perhaps be
formulated as trying to ‘translate’ the ‘human fate’, the ‘psychology’, of
these individuals, as expressed in their autobiographies, into “elements
of economic and social change’.

This, however, raises again the problem of method, the mode of
translation. For what is common to Lukacs and most of his disciples
is a curious lack of what, in England at least, has been taken to be the
primary task of literary criticism: a close attention to, a detailed
reading and exposition of, the actual language of the literary text. And
there is perhaps a complementary absence, in some of the work done
recently in social history, in the use generally made of autobiographies:
although memoirs, diaries and autobiographical writings have been
extensively cited as sources for the understanding of and knowledge
about a period, little attention has been paid to the kind of document
an autobiography is.

Autobiographies have tended to be taken by historians as basically
narratives of what happened, more or less biased or inaccurate, or as
providing anecdotes of ‘human interest’ to enliven a dull page. But
they have not often been recognised as constituting a rather peculiar
‘genre’ in their own right. This is partly, perhaps, because there has
been very little work done on the nature of autobiography in general
and until recently much of the work available has lacked any adequate
method or awareness of the problems of critical method: ‘close
reading’, for example, has rarely been practised in relation to
autobiographies, and no satisfactory criteria have been established for
critically assessing an autobiography. The emphasis has been on

somewhat skimpy surveys, and to some extent on theoretical
considerations. Moreover, the theoretical assumptions underlying the
vatious surveys have been implicit rather than articulated. These
problems arise partly because autobiography is a form of writing which
seems to slide between standard categories: it hovers between ‘history’
and ‘creative literature’, ‘non-fiction’ and ‘fiction’.

As such, of course, autobiography would seem to constitute, a priori,
an area of inquiry which invites a sociology of literature approach: a
form of writing which is explicitly concerned with recounting the ‘life’
(and, often, ‘times’) of an author perhaps offers itself as an area in
which the connections between that life, those times and the written
work itself might be closely established, or at least usefully explored.
This ambivalent status of autobiography need not necessarily make the
application of literary-critical techniques inappropriate.

Recent studies in psycho-analysis and sociolinguistics, for example,
and even in political philosophy, have illustrated the amenability of
rather unexpected discourses and texts to a close reading approach: it
is increasingly clear that what is said and how exactly it is said can
reveal a great deal, in very precise ways, about a person’s unconscious
problems and self-understanding, and even his or her class-position. I
do not feel competent, or confident, enough in the techniques
emerging from these fields, or in the more technical attempts at linking
criticism with methods developed in linguistics, to want to employ
them in any directly derivative way, but at least a close reading of
autobiographies might help to counter-balance some of the less
closely-focussed treatments of autobiographies by historians. Indeed,
the more traditional historian’s approach to this body of material can
be left to others. I have tried to concentrate upon the actual text and
to relate the self-portrait expressed in the text as a whole to wider
contexts, rather than directly to the ‘real’ biographical details of each
author’s life — a point I am concerned to stress.

The method I have adopted, then, has been to concentrate upon
what is actually said in each autobiography as a whole: to try to
understand and make clear any significant pattern or unity in the work.
This has involved a close analysis of any characteristic features of the
writer’s style and an examination of the overall structure of each
autobiography. I have also tried to suggest ways in which style and
structure are related both to each other and to other facets of each
author: the political positions he (my examples were all men) depicts
himself as holding, the general position he occupied in the social

structure, and some features of the time, and sometimes place, in
which he lived.

A further connection or ‘homology’ which emerged in the course of
comparing the results of each particular analysis is the correlation
between these various features and the problems of political
otganisation facing the working class at different stages during the
nineteenth century. The significance of this last point is discussed in
Part Three, the Conclusion, together with some further comments on
points raised in this Introduction.

There are, of course, other areas upon which this study might be
seen as impinging —for example, those of literary and cultural history.
Autobiography as a genre seems uniquely involved in a great many
aspects of nineteenth-century English culture. The term itself only
comes into general use in the early part of the century. Both
Wordsworth and Coleridge eventually conclude their respective
searches for a major philosophical poem and a magnum opus in
autobiographical form, in The Prelude and the Birographia Literaria.
Hazlitt’s notion of "sympathy" is directly appropriate to the process
involved in reading another’s account of their life. Carlyle constructs
Sartor Resartus as a fragmented autobiography of Herr Teufelsdrockh,
and even coins the phrase ‘our auto-biographical age’ to characterise
his own period. The development of the dramatic monologue has cleat
affinities with the autobiographical mode — as Robert Langbaum has
remarked, ‘the ‘poetry of experience’ must to some extent be
autobiographical for both reader and author.’ The development of the
complex autobiographical novel, from Hoge’s Confessions of A Justified
Sinner to 'Thackeray’s History of Henry Esmond is a marked feature of the
century. The debate about ‘realism’ in the novel overlaps with an
interest in other people’s lives and includes at times an insistence upon
authors writing only from their own experience. The demand for
‘sincerity’ becomes generalised, and is often interpreted in the narrow
sense of writing from personal experience. The interest in various
forms of ‘alien’ life — peasants, foreign countries, provincial life, etc.
— can include and provide a context for a developing interest in the
actual lives of ‘the working classes’, whether mediated through others,
in novels, Blue Books or Mayhew, or directly recounted by workers
themselves in their autobiographies. None of these connections can be
traced in detail within the bounds of this study, but that general
context is worth bearing in mind as the complex background against
which the autobiographies analysed were written and first read.

Finally, I emphasise that the contribution of the literary critic in
these interrelated fields can only be tentative, and _ that,
correspondingly, my debt to non-literary students of the period,
particularly to historians, is very great indeed. In taking the results of
their work and seeking to make some new connections within and

across the period, I hope I have not distorted their findings.

The analyses that follow are deliberately presented as separate pieces
of work, as examinations of specific texts. The conclusions suggested
in the third part are attempts simply to indicate some points which
may be relevant to the labour historian, to those interested in some of
the problems originally raised by Lukacs, to literary critics concerned
with problems of method and with the nature of ‘writing’ itself — for
example, the question of the boundaries which delineate ‘fiction’ from
other forms of writing. .But despite the brevity of this study, I hope
that the ramifications which I think are implied have not been left too
implicit.

7: aspects of the historical context

The great changes which have taken place in the nature of
employment in the manufacturing districts of South Lancashire,
since the publication of Collier’s “View of the Lancashire Dialect’,
have not only caused the old appearances of the country to pass
away, but they have altered nearly everything appertaining to, or
resulting from, the life and condition of man. At the time when
Tim Bobbin was spending his jovial and facetious days at
Milnrow, such a thing as a cotton or woollen factory was not in
existence ... working hours, whether at the loom or on the farm,
were, as compared with those of modern operatives and labourers,
spent in leisure. There was often great irregularity in their
observance of working hours, and their duration varied much,
according to the wants, or habits, of individual workers, or of
families.

But soon a change was destined to come over this scene of
homely labour and plenteous living. In 1769, a patent was taken
out for a machine to spin cotton by rollers; in 1770 the spinning-
jenny was patented; in 1785 appeared improved carding, drawing,
and roving frames; after which came the willow, the scutching-
machine, and the lapping-frame. In 1779 the mule-jenny was
invented, and in 1785 Watt had completed his steam-engine.
Then came a wonderful facility of production, and a proportionate
decrease of the cost, whence followed an increase of demand, an
increase of employment, an increase of population, a crowding
towards the great hive, of many people of all industrial classes, and
from all parts of the kingdom and the world. Next, as a
consequence, followed the breaking up of old associations and the
formation of new ones; the abandonment of old habits; the giving
up of old customs; new modes of dress became common; new
modes of living were adopted; new subjects of thinking were
started; new words for the expression of thought were introduced,
and from that time the old dialect, with the old customs of the
country and the old fashions, has been gradually receding towards
oblivion.

Bamford’s compressed account, in his Dialect (pp. iii, vili-x), of the
many and interconnected changes in South Lancashire at the end of
the 18th century can be supplemented and paralleled by the comments
of contemporary observers and later historians, though he makes the
essential, rich, connections.

12

Bamford was born in Middleton, near Manchester, in 1788; in 1789
there were no power-looms in Manchester and only one spinning mill
driven by steam; the first power-loom was introduced into Manchester
in 1790. The next few decades saw the immense expansion of the
cotton industry: in 1781, 5 million pounds of raw cotton was
imported; by 1820 the figure was 150 million pounds. England as a
whole was still predominantly rural and agricultural, but though the
new industrial developments advanced fairly slowly, Middleton—
Bamford’s birth-place—was one of the areas in which, by 1812, they
were concentrated.

The new factories first replaced the older modes of spinning rather
than weaving: in 1819 in the Manchester area there were 66 spinning
mills, employing 20,000 workers (2,000 male operatives, with 18,000
female and child labourers) but also an estimated 40,000 hand-loom
weavers still working in cottage-industry conditions. Individual
factories were relatively small—even by 1832 the average number of
employees in each was only 400, though two mills employed more
than 1,000—but the size of the total population in the local towns had
increased dramatically. Contemporary accounts of the combined
population of Manchester and Salford give 19,839 in 1757, 27,246 in
1773, over 50,000 by 1788, between 65,000 and 74,000 by 1791— the
faltering later estimates themselves indicative of the sense of
uncontrollable expansion.

Whatever the exact figures, an estimate that Manchester’s population
increased sevenfold between 1750 and 1820 would probably not
exaggerate too wildly. The total population of England was increasing
at about 15% each decade in the early 19th century; by 1811 almost
one-sixth of the entire population of England lived in the textile areas
of Lancashire, Yorkshire and Cheshire, with Lancashire second only to
Middlesex in population, and Manchester and Liverpool the only
English towns apart from London (already 1 million) to number 90-
100,000 inhabitants.

Those who flooded into the Manchester area during this expansion,
many of them Irish, were, of course, ‘strangers’ to one another and to
the local inhabitants. The sudden presence of numerous ‘strangers’
could always alarm the authorities, but the permanent immigration of
so many newcomers naturally caused deep difficulties also for the local
workers.

We can grasp one aspect of these difficulties by tracing the history
of local agitation. Manchester had been generally regarded as Jacobite
in the 1740s, but from 1760 to 1790 the predominant local feeling was
Tory. During these years there were local food-riots, for example in
1757, and some traditional ‘collective bargaining by riot’ and machine-

13

breaking, but from 1789 local clashes began to take on a mote directly
political character, with the formation both of the Church and King
Club, against the Dissenting petitioners for the repeal of the Test and
Corporation Acts, and of the Manchester Constitutional Society.
Donald Read describes the main shift thereafter as from loyal mob in
1792 to anti-loyal mob by 1819, but as well as the transfer of allegiance
we can mark the movement from ‘pre-industrial crowd’ to a relatively
organised opposition movement. There were traditional food-riots in
1797 and some attempts at organisation in 1799 in response to the
flour-shortage from 1796 to 1800, but when the next bad harvests
came, in 1808-12, we can see a new element in the presence of Major
Cartwright and Lord Brougham at the trial of the Manchester
‘Luddites’ of 1812.

In 1808 the weavers had shown further signs of self-organisation: in
protest against the dropping of the minimum-wage Bill, 10,000
weavers had assembled peacefully on St. George’s Field to
demonstrate, only to be dispersed by troops. By 1811-12 there was an
increasing number of local working-class meetings, to petition
Parliament on various issues, to meet delegates from Ireland, even to
discuss a general ‘turn-out’; ‘secret committees’ of workers were also
reported to be at work.

In 1816 the organised groups established the first provincial
Hampden Clubs, at Royton, near Oldham, in August, and in
Manchester on October 28th; on December 15th a meeting of
delegates at Middleton sent out ‘missionaries’ to other northern areas;
by March 1817 there were forty Hampden Clubs in the Manchester
area, with about 8,000 members, and a local radical newspaper, the
Manchester Political Register, had been founded. On March 10th a mass-
meeting of 12,000, prelude to the Blanketeers’ March, was cleared by
dragoons. In 1818 the agitation concentrated on immediate economic
issues: 20,000 Manchester and Stockport spinners struck on July 18th,
and their strike lasted till the arrest of the spinners’ committee on
August 29th and an attack on Gray’s factory in Manchester on
September 2nd. On August 31st the weavers also struck but despite
well-attended and organised meetings on September 1st, 2nd and 3rd,
the weavers’ strike fizzled out on September 4th, when a march of
strikers from Manchester to Ashton met Sir John Byng’s troops and
immediately ‘lowered their colours and went home’

The spinners, under their radical political leaders, Bageuley and
Drummond, attempted to form a General Union, but the weavers,
rejecting Bageuley at a mass meeting, remained a-political. Only in
1819 did the weavers take up a political stance: at a mass meeting on
June 21st, called to consider mutual assistance for relief or emigration,

14

their own leaders, Saxton and Walker, persuaded them to consider the
need for Parliamentary reform as well.

By that time there were enough local ‘Political Protestant’ societies,
modelled on the Methodist-style ‘class’ structure of the Stockport
Union for the Promotion of Human Happiness (founded October
1818), for a delegates’ meeting representing twenty-eight societies to
be held on June 7th, and for a number of mass meetings to be
organised throughout the area. In August 1819 the radical Manchester
Observer reached its highest circulation (4,000 copies) and had a
network of northern agents.

The loyalists were naturally also active: on July 16th they formed an
Armed Association; on July 24th Wroe, the Manchester Observer editor,
was charged with seditious libel. On August 16th the two forces met—
at ‘the Massacre of Peterloo’.

After Peterloo the radical movement in the area split and declined,
to revive again briefly in 1826 and then in the 1830s. In 1831, with
average wages down to less than 12/- a week, the Manchester mill
operatives turned again towards politics; even the hand-loom weavers,
by then declining in numbers, could be described as inclined towards
‘revolution’ rather than reform. The stage was set for Chartism in

Manchester.
*

There are a number of points to be emphasised here. Throughout the
period, there is a shift from a small, relatively ‘known’ and
homogeneous working community to a large, new kind of
conurbation, in which many of the inhabitants were, in a double sense,
strangers. By 1841 the number of Irish in Manchester, for example,
was equal to its total population in the 1770s. For any radical
movement to emerge in that context there had to be development of
otganisational and tactical forms: the older forms, of relatively
spontaneous riot and communication by word of mouth, were
replaced, in a long process, by reliance on disciplined and co-ordinated
meetings, organisational committees, and newspaper-circulation—one
of the earliest signs of the emergence of local Chartism was the
transformation in September 1830 of the Manchester and Salford
Aldvertiser into a working class radical paper. But at the same time the
local authorities had to re-organise their responses to agitation. The
local ‘police-force’ was inherited from a parish-situation: in 1812, for
example, Oldham had only two regular constables.

Apart from the building up of a regular police, the authorities had
two main tesources in times of trouble: an attempt to match numbers
with numbers—in May 1812, for example, General Maitland in

15

Lancashire commanded 79 infantry companies, 18 troops of horse,
and two artillery detachments, totalling 5,500 infantry and 1,400 horse,
while in the Salford Hundred alone 1,500 special constables were
enrolled, about ten per cent of the adult male population. Or,
secondly, an attempt to infiltrate the radical groups with spies and
informers, relying precisely on the fact that so many people in the area
could no longer know the background or credentials of a stranger.
And of course the loyalists, while organising their own associations,
attempted to prevent the development of the working class attempts at
organisation—in 1813, for example, Hay, chairman of the Manchester
and Salford district bench, proposed a Bill to regulate Friendly
Societies.
i: a representative life?

That, among so many thousands in the Manchester area, Samuel
Bamford should have produced the autobiography by which we know
him itself requires some kind of explanation. In 1814, for example,
another Samuel Bamford was shot dead in a Luddite raid at
Nottingham; he is merely a name, otherwise unknown, whereas
Samuel Bamford of Middleton is referred to over thirty times in
Thompson’s The Making of the English Working Class.

Why autobiographies are written is never fully amenable to
explanation, but at least we can partly grasp how Bamford became a
point at which the general process described above received individual
expression. For many of the currents and strands in that complex
history intersected in Bamford’s life.

His two paternal great-grandfathers were James Bamford and
Samuel Cheetham, respectively a small farmer and a watch-maker (ED,
p. 12); the former was deprived, claims Bamford, of his right to the
Bamford Hall estate (ED, p. 11); the latter was chiefly remembered for
his violent support of the King when the Scots army reached
Manchester in 1745 (ED, p. 15). The same year, Bamford’s maternal
grandfather, a master shoemaker named Jeffrey Battersbee, was
arrested on a High Treason charge for helping the Pretendet’s forces
(ED, p. 21). His paternal grandfather, Daniel Bamford, was by that
time a small farmer and weaver (ED, p. 12); when Bamford himself
was born his father, also a Daniel, was simply a weaver, though of
muslin, ‘at that time considered a fine course of work, and requiring a
superior hand’ (ED, p. 1) In his immediate lineage, then, we can trace a
representative decline from independent farming to cottage weaving,
cut across at different generations by marriage with the daughters of
relatively superior craftsmen. The move from rural to urban districts is
parallel: from Thornham to Middleton and, soon, to Manchester.

16

A radical element can also be traced: the Bamford family was split in
the Civil War, and Bamford claimed descent from the brother who
fought against the king and was therefore dispossessed at the
Restoration (1, p. 52); Jeffrey Battersbee’s clash with the king of his
day occasions the remark from Bamford: “The rebel blood, it would
seem, after all, was the more impulsive; it got the ascendancy—and I
was born a Radical’ (ED, p. 22). But apart from a consciousness of this
strand in his ancestry, Bamford had the immediate example of his
father and uncle Thomas: in the 1790s, under the influence of ‘Pain’s
[sc] Rights of Man’, his two relatives were members of the ‘small band’
of Middleton ‘Jacobins’ who were ‘supporters of Parliamentary
Reform, as it was then advocated by the Duke of Richmond, Mr. Pitt,
and other distinguished characters’ (ED, p. 43), and the attacks on
such local radicals were among Bamford’s earliest memories (ED, pp.
44-48),

His father’s influence was certainly operative in another important
area: he was, briefly, a schoolmaster (ED, p. 4), and Bamford proudly
describes how his father was ‘considerably imbued with book
knowledge, particularly of a religious kind; understood arithmetic; had
some acquaintance with astronomy; was a vocal and instrumental
musician, singing from the book and playing on the flute; he was
likewise an occasional composer of music, and introduced several of
his pieces amongst the religious body with which he was connected; he
was also a writer of verses’ (ED, p. 2), and ‘the author of several moral
essays in verse’ (ED, p. 7). Bamford even quotes a hymn and a
political song written by his father in the 1790s (ED, pp. 8-10).

The religious element mentioned is strong in his ancestry: his
grandfather Daniel’s home was, Bamford claims, ‘the first that opened
at Middleton for the reception of Methodist preachers; and John and
Charles Wesley, John Nelson, Thomas Taylor, and many of the first
promulgators of their doctrines, had addressed their humble and
simple hearers on the floor of that ruined dwelling’ (ED, p. 13); his
father became a Methodist after an ‘irregular youth’ (ED, p. 3), but in
his Jacobin phase ‘left the society of Methodists, but to the doctrines
of John Wesley .. continued an adherent as long as [he] lived’ (ED, p.
43); Bamford’s cousins remained Methodists ‘of the old primitive
earnest cast’, ‘the old John Wesley caste’ (ED, pp. 71-78, 100).
Whatever the overall interaction between Methodism and radicalism—
and the issue is complex—Bamford’s Methodism, as we shall see,
probably did contribute something to his autobiographical account of
his personal radicalism.

Bamford’s own early life continues some of the trajectories visible
here. He was born just at the start of what William Radcliffe called

17

‘the golden age’ of the weaving trade, 1788 to 1803, and about 1797
he himself became a weaver (ED, p. 115). But for Bamford that was
part of a return to his origins: in 1793 his father had been appointed
manager of a ‘manufactory of cotton goods’ at Strangeways
Workhouse, and soon after Workhouse Governor (ED, p. 50f); a
small-pox epidemic in 1795 killed Bamford’s mother, uncle Thomas
and his younger sister and brother (ED, p. 61f); after his father’s
subsequent re-marriage Bamford moved, back to Middleton to live in
his uncle William’s family and ‘never resided’ again with his father
(ED, p. 96). While in Manchester he had already attended Manchester
Grammar School, but his father prevented him entering the ‘latin
class’; as Bamford remarks: ‘Had the threshold of the classics been
once crossed . . a course of life, far different from the one I have
pursued, would have been marked out before me’ (ED, p. 79f, 92, 93).
But the return to Middleton also meant a return to the simpler forms
of education he had known earlier—local Methodist schools and
Sunday Schools (ED, p. 79, 106f). Though many of the Manchester
Sunday Schools were still intended ‘to teach the duties of humility and
submission to superiors’, at least Bamford’s brand of ‘real old
Armenian Methodists . . thought it no desecration of the Sabbath to
enable the rising generation, on that day, to write the Word of God as
well as read it’ (ED, p. 106).

In 1803, as the ‘golden age’ ended, Bamford left the mixture of
weaving and schooling of his stay at his cousins, and went to live with
his sister in Salford, again attending briefly John Holt’s Methodist
School in Oldham Street. Shortly after, he began a succession of jobs
as a watchouseman in Manchester, with an interlude as a stableboy on
one employer’s farm in Prestwich (ED, pp. 187f, 200f). The ease with
which that particular employer, Mr. Robinson, was simultaneously
manufacturer, wholesaler and farmer, spreading his interests from
Bury to Manchester and Prestwich, is to some extent paralleled by the
ease with which Bamford, living in Greengate and working in
Macdonald’s Lane (now Lower Cannon Street) or High Street, in the
centre of Manchester, speaks of his recreation at this time entirely in
terms of the countryside, ‘lonely walks in those pleasant indulating /s7/
pastures’, ‘country strolls at eventide’ (e.g. ED, pp. 196f, 207, 211).
The swelling Manchester was still almost an intrusion upon a rural
setting.

Nor did Bamford find it difficult to move back to Middleton and set
himself up as a weaver when ‘trade was going remarkably well’ (ED, p.
225) or to return to warehousing when he tired of a sailor’s life after
‘some half dozen trips betwixt Shields and London’ in 1807 (ED, pp.
246, 276). He remained a warehouseman in Manchester till 1813, even

18

after settling with his wife, Mima, in Middleton after their marriage in
1810. Between 1813 and 1816 he was a putter-out to the Middleton
weavers, and even dabbled in bookselling (ED, p. 311), but by the time
he became involved in the Hampden Clubs he was settled as a weaver.

What is significant for this study, however, is not these bare bones
of his life, but his own experience and expression of them. Before we
examine the actual form that Bamford’s autobiographical writings
took, we must first analyse the elements of his experience that are
given particular emphasis in his work and see how these relate to the
general historical experience sketched above.

In this brief exchange the contrast of dialect with ‘standard’ English
is an index to a deeper contrast; the old farmer lives in a known
community, its paths and inhabitants familiar and taken for granted;
for him it is difficult to recognise that a man may be a stranger,
unfamiliar with that scene, and even more difficult to grasp that
London is not a similarly “known community’ for its inhabitants. For
the stranger, London is the focus of a quite different community:
England as a whole, with the capital as ‘the town’ and such places as
Lancashire as ‘the manufacturing districts’; for the farmer, Rochdale is
‘the town’ and London is merely some other Rochdale. That clash of
experiences, separated out in this dialogue, was in Bamford’s case an
element in a continuing internal dialogue.

Bamford began by inhabiting a known community. For example, of
a motley group of seven prisoners he can remark that ‘three of the
men were personally known to me; a fourth I knew only by name; and
the others I had never seen before that morning’ (I, p. 98; cf. p. 94),
while a prisoner suddenly thrust into his carriage is simply introduced
to the reader as ‘George Howarth, for that was the name of my new
companion, was a decent, labouring, married man, of Royton, and was
about six feet four inches in height’ and is thereafter simply "George"
d, p. 84); when he lists the Middleton victims of Peterloo it is as a
group of people personally known to him (I, pp. 224-5). The local
forces of law and order, and his employers, are known people, not
distant figures (e.g. I, pp. 80-1, 202). He had once clashed with the
local authorities over non-payment of a maintenance order (ED, pp.
230-2) and his own family were intermarried with local employers
(ED, p. 5); moreover, the radical meetings and perhaps too the joint
political meetings of masters and men may have helped to extend
Bamford’s own known community. But the overwhelming sense
conveyed by the way Bamford presents Middleton to the reader in
Passages is as a shared known community — even dates are almost
taken for granted while he is writing about activities in Middleton or
Manchester: the full date is given when he recounts his departure for
London (‘Sunday, the 30th of March, 1817’, I, p. 95) but most dates
before then omit the year or are in the form of, for example, ‘the
following Monday’.

Early Days is mote explicit about the nature of Middleton, describing
the personal relationships of masters, putters-cut and weavers (ED,
pp. 117-125), and emphasising Bamford’s deep home-sickness for
Middleton when he moved to Manchester (ED, ch. viii), but perhaps
the most telling indication of the nature of the community that
Bamford knew comes in an incident after Peterloo:

I found Redford’s mother bathing his wound with warm milk and
water, and to please her, he said it was easier. It was a clean gash
of about six inches in length and quite through the shoulder blade.
She yearned, and wept afresh, when she saw the severed bone

God not to visit that sin upon the head of him who did it, but to
change his heart and bring him to repentance. That prayer had
well-nigh touched my heart also, but Tom rapped out one of
another sort, to which I incontinently, as may be supposed, added
my "Amen". (I, pp. 214-15)

Like the farmer in Wa/ks. Bamford at times seemed to presume an
extension of his own experience — he lists ‘Mr Johnson, of Smedley
Cottage; Mr Moorhouse of Stockport; Mr Carlile of London; Mr John
Knight of Manchester’ (I, p. 205) as if Smedley Cottage were the same
kind of place as London, or even Manchester; he suggests that an early
local informer, William Wilson, who ‘left the country . . and was next
heard of as a police officer at London’ might well be “a man of his
name, whose marks were, $.38’ serving in the London force ‘recently’
—that is, about twenty-three years later (I, p. 10). He attributes his
own kind of personal knowledge to Hunt during the large Palace Yard
meeting and demonstration on January 28th, 1817: ‘He seemed to
know almost every man of them, and his confidence in, and entire
mastery over them, made him quite at ease’ (I, p. 19); he was
correspondingly surprised at first to find that he, Bamford, had to
identify himself to the London relief committee— it had to be
explained to him ‘how necessary it was, in a great place like London, to
be quite certain as to the persons with whom they transacted business’
(I, pp. 49-50).

Bamford, however, had not only to learn that other places were not
‘known communities’; he also had to negotiate the breakdown of his
own. He can recognise that he himself may be a ‘stranger’ to the
London radical meetings and need to be informed as to who ‘two of
the most influential leaders of the London operative reformers’,
Watson and Preston, were (I, pp. 24-5), but when ‘a stranger whom
[he] did not know’ becomes one of the ‘chief movers’ ‘at work during
[his] absence’, ‘exciting to, and carrying on, private meetings, and
suspicious intrigues in our neighbourhood’, then Bamford feels
distinctly uneasy (I, p. 155). Even after Peterloo he finds it natural to
react in a meeting by arguing that:

I had sent for men whom I knew, but they came not; strangers
came whose faces I had never seen before, and I could not act
with such; neither was it to be expected that I should. (1, p. 219)

In part, Bamford’s reaction was justified by the fear of strangers being
informers — but that fear itself could only be generated in a situation
where the community was ceasing to be closed

It is easy, in fact, to overlook the elements in Bamford of sheer
inability to grasp others simply as strangers— a facility long since
acquired by the present urbanised population. He will not speak till
spoken to by strangers, even though on one occasion he realises that
they are ‘some of the London politicians of the working class’ (II, p.
136); in London he was ‘half-inclined to believe also, that the people
[he] met seemed as if they knew [he] was pennyless’ (II, p. 140). The
experience of London he recounts in Early Days remained for a long
time a shaping factor in his outlook:

. the men and women themselves formed an apparently
unceasing current of human beings, the ever-changing hue and
character of which, confused my head—wearied my attention—
and made me wishful to see no more. (ED, p. 241)

In Passages he can still use "Londonish" simply as an adjective for ‘the
people, their houses, and their manners’ (II, p. 132); he was particularly
appalled by the London slums and underworld and his immediate
reaction was to wish (I, pp. 151-2) :

Oh! let me live afar from scenes like these,

Where the winds bend the giant armed trees;

Bask on my own dear banks of new blown flowers,
When thirsty sol, hath supp’d the morning showers.

The quality of the poetry hardly captures the quality of the experience.
In later life he managed to live in London for a few years, as a minor
clerk at Somerset House (1851—55); by that time he had learned to
live, to some extent, as a ‘stranger’, and before we examine the
underlying political importance of this element in Bamford, some
aspects of this process of learning must be noticed.

A central feature in Bamford’s experience of his known community
was his reliance on ‘friendship’: he asserts friendship to be one of the
characteristics of his own class (I, p. 50), and the experience of the
power of friendliness had been an early part of his make-up (ED, p.
70); his natural reaction on meeting again a co-delegate whom at first

22

he scarcely recognises is to refer to him very soon after as his ‘old
friend’ (I, pp. 156-7). But his friendships gradually soured: some
‘friends’ spread the rumour that he was a spy (L, p. 160f); Hunt left his
co-defendants to their own resources at the trial, snubbed Bamford,
and slandered Carlile (I, pp. 72f, 88f, 85f); Bamford’s fellow local
radicals left him almost without financial assistance for his trial (I, p.
104f), and the London radicals mainly forgot their imprisoned
colleagues (II, p. 165). Even in prison with Healey and Johnson,
quarrels, arguments and suspicion ‘at length, entirely put a stop to all
confidence and friendly feeling betwixt [them] and rendered [Bamford]

. a stranger to the society of [his] two fellow-prisoners’ (II, p. 180).
He had already had the experience of being forced, briefly, to act as a
‘stranger’ in his own neighbourhood, in disguising himself after
Peterloo (I, p. 213), but to acknowledge all men as basically ‘strangers’
was a difficult process. We can see the recognition emerging in his
realisation that the ‘great men’ of the country, the Members of
Parliament, might be ‘some three or four hundreds of the most
ordinary-looking men [he] had ever beheld at one view’, while he
himself, as a prisoner, could become the centre of attention for both
ordinary people and the ‘genteel’ and ‘respectable’ (cf. I, pp. 26, 99,
275). His description of Manchester as a play comes close to an
awareness that roles in urban society are based not on personal
knowledge but on how we appear to one another, through endless
mediations:

If any one wishes to see a play performed he has only to walk the
streets of Manchester, or any other of our large towns, and he may
behold the perfection of either tragedy or comedy enacted by
performers who need neither prompter, call-boy, nor rehearsal;
but all coming and going as regularly as if the piece were a play
"got up" and "put on the stage", as the phrase is, "put on ready for
presentation". The scenes are admirably painted—the machinery
perfect in its operations, of wonderful construction, and
sometimes of most awful effect. The actors might have been made
for the performance of their several parts, so aptly do they go
through them; whilst the dresses, decorations, and all the
accessoties of the piece, are sure to be wonderfully befitting. And
with such a stage as this, with its ever-varying realitie [sz] before
our eyes, who can require sham repetition as an after-part? Not I
at any rate. (ED, pp. 209-210)

He is quite explicit at one point about the need to play a role where
one is a stranger: after being refused lodgings at an inn, he advises that:

A foot traveller, if he is really desirous to obtain lodgings, should
never stand asking about them. He should walk into a good
room,—never into the common tap room,—put his dusty feet
under a table,—ting the bell pretty smartly, and order something
to eat and drink, and not speak in the humblest of tones. He will
be served quickly and respectfully . . After his repast, he should
take his pipe or cigar if he be a smoker, and whether he be or not,
he should drink, chat, and make himself quite at ease until bed-

ask her to light him to bed. That will be done as a matter of course

.. (Ul, p. 120)

Later he makes a firm distinction between the role he is prepared to
play among strangers and the identity he demands within a close

I said it was not a sudden resolve on my patt, for I never intended
to enter Lincoln with the chains on. I cared but little how I
appeared in London or the country through which we had passed,
and where I was, as I should probably remain, a perfect stranger;
but I knew the consequences of a first appearance in a seemingly
degraded state, before persons with whom we must remain twelve
months. (II, p. 169)

Such deliberate role-playing already acknowledges that friendliness and
direct personal relations may have to be abandoned outside the known

each man was thinking, acting, reading, resting, singing, praying,
eating, drinking, weeping, or smiling for himself, and his own
concerns; just as in the wide world outside. (II, p. 164)

wv: a limited politics
There is, of course, already a political ‘position’ implicit in some
features of Bamford’s allegiance to a known community and the way

that allegiance crumbles, but we can make his political stance more
explicit—bearing in mind that his writing is retrospective and includes

24

a critique of his early politics.

The most obvious political options adopted by Bamford are
negative: his opposition to the Blanketeers’ March, to the plans for
"making a Moscow of Manchester" and later for assassinating the
Cabinet, and in general to proposals for "revolution" (I, pp. 30, 38, 76,
122-3). His opposition is often linked to a suspicion of planted
agitators and informers (I, pp. 10f, 38, 76f) but this at times shades
into a distrust of all ‘professional’ agitators (I, pp. 25, 36, 167). Yet
there is a tension in Bamford’s attitude on this point: he excuses his
own phase of radicalism, in contrast to the Chartist phase, by arguing
that

We had not any of our own rank with whom to advise for the
better,—no man of other days who had gone through the ordeal
of experience; and whose judgement might have directed our self-
devotion. (I, p. 153)

But in fact he rejects the approach of Thomas Bacon, the
septuagenarian involved in the Pentridge rising (, p. 156f) and
dismisses Watson, Preston, Hooper, Benbow and Evans as "the blind
leading the blind", as ‘wordy and intemperate’ (I, pp. 25, 126). It is not
so much experienced leaders that Bamford asks for, as leaders who
will give his own kind of political advice. The passage quoted above
continues:

..and have instructed us that, before the reform we sought, could

be obtained and profited by, there must be another, —a deeper
reform,—emerging from our hearts, and first blessing our
households, by the production of every good we could possibly

What is significant in this change is the model of community

governed are not those between groups or interests, but between
individuals related by personal knowledge; the belated adoption of the

mechanism that might have, and in practice tried to, overcome this
problem, Bamford rejects: the formation of a political movement or

v : politics or autobiography?

The limits of Bamford’s political position reflect a dilemma facing the
whole radical movement in the ‘transitional’ phase of the 1790s to
1830s. The values that the radicals sought to maintain were often, in
one sense, regressive: they looked back to a stable community, closed
and face-to-face; the forms of organisation they adopted rested in part
on that kind of relationship, often inherited from the small
organisational forms of religious dissenters. But at the same time, the

30

need was increasingly recognised for new forms of organisation,
approptiate to the generalised relationship of a national radical
presence; out of that struggle would begin to be created a new
perspective, an elaborated politics, a different version of ‘community’
to be worked towards.

We can grasp one of the roots of this dilemma, in the specific case
of Bamford, if we examine more closely a possible ‘break-through’
point in his career already glanced at above. Chapter XXXVI of
Passages vol. I, recounting the close of the day of Peterloo, ends with
the sentence:

. and now we spent the evening in recapitulating the events of
the day, and in brooding over a spirit of vengeance, towards the
authors of our humiliation, and our wrong. (1, p. 231)

That bleak word, "wrong", concludes the chapter on a threatening
note; Peterloo marked a watershed: to experience that day as a
"humiliation" was a different political option from interpreting it as a
"wrong". The overtones of the latter gradually come to the fore in the
next chapter: ‘it was reported that thousands of pikemen were oh the
way to Manchester, from Oldham, Middleton, and other surrounding
districts’, ‘all the working people were athirst for revenge, and only
awaited the coming of the country folks to attempt a sweeping
havock’, proposals for the ‘immolation of a selected number of the
guilty ones’ were being discussed, and when Bamford reached
Middleton he found that:

Many of the young men had been preparing arms, and seeking out
articles to convert into such. Some had been grinding scythes,
others old hatchets, others screw-drivers, rusty swords, pikels, and
mop-nails: anything which could be made to cut or stab was
pronounced fit for service. But no plan was defined,—nothing
was attranged,—and the arms were afterwards reserved for any
event that might occur. (I, pp. 214, 216)

On that note the chapter ends, in suspense.

Bamford’s own attitude has been carefully presented during the
chapter; earlier he had favoured the limited use of defensive cudgels
for the meeting, but had been willing to be overruled (I, pp. 192, 197);
now two incidents prepare for a possible return to an armed stance.
The passage in which Bamford attends the wounded Redford and
wavers between the two responses—of neighbourly forgiveness and
vengeance—is in this chapter, as is the episode in which Bamford, in

St

disguise, becomes a temporary stranger to his own community (I, pp.
214-15, 213-14; cf. above). Together, they indicate the ground for an
adoption of violent tactics: to accept violence is necessarily to cease
relating to one’s enemies as neighbours, as known persons, to cease
regarding local loyalties as overriding. It is a difficult process to live
through or to express. When Bamford returns from his disguised
sortie, having heard some proposals of direct personal revenge that
‘filled [him] with horror’, he simply says:

What these men sought would not do; and I retired, and put off
my dress, more thoughtful than when I took it up. (I, p. 214)

Before the end of the chapter half-a-dozen of the local radicals,
including Bamford, have ‘agreed to hold a larger meeting the day
following’ to discuss what is to be done. Bamford’s response, as he
recounts it in the next chapter, is becoming clearer:

The day following, I attended on the hill with a trusty friend.
Notices had been sent to Oldham, Rochdale, Bury, and some
other places, but at the time appointed no one appeared. We
waited for hours, until the afternoon waned, but no one came; and
then we went down to Royton, to ascertain the disposition of the
reformers of that place. Some had been severely wounded, but
most of the people were carousing, and there did not appear to be
any disposition to retaliate the outrage we had suffered, by force
and arms. I called on William Fitton, but he gave no
encouragement to such an idea. I went to John Kay, in Royley-
lane, but he was as usual, imperturbably placid. He was one of the
least impassioned men I ever knew. (1, pp. 216-217)

It is clear from this that Bamford, almost alone, is impassioned enough
to consider seriously an armed reaction. The passion and the
desperation emerge clearly in his argument to Kay:

After introducing the cause of my visit I asked his opinion, and in
order to obtain it frankly, I spoke the more so. "If the people were
ever to rise and smite their enemies, was not that the time? Was
evety enormity to be endured, and this after all? Were we still to
lie down like whipped hounds, whom nothing could arouse to
resistance? Were there not times and seasons, and circumstances,
under which the common rules of wisdom became folly, prudence
became cowardice, and submission became criminal? and was not
the present one of those times and seasons?" It was astonishing

that men could eat and sleep; that "the voice of their brothers
blood, crying from the ground," did not make them more
miserable." (I, p. 217)

But Kay’s counter-arguments eventually convince Bamford. Kay puts
forward a series of points: that those guilty of Peterloo are already
remorseful; that indiscriminate massacre and assassination ate clearly
ruled out; that to march against a whole army would be impossible;
and finally that any use of violence would be used retrospectively to
justify the use of pre-emptive violence at Peterloo (I, pp. 217-218).
Bamford shortly after rehearses the same arguments to a meeting of
delegates, but the initial emphasis is quite different:

I told them briefly, that I would not take part in a delegate meeting
to discuss the taking up of arms. That I saw not any prospect of
succeeding, and if I did, they were not the men with whom I
would act. I had sent for men whom I knew, but they came not;
strangers came whose faces I had never seen before, and I would
not act with such; neither was it to be expected that I should. (I, p.
219)

The double blockage is really one: precisely because of the refusal to
work with "strangers", to break beyond the small known community
into a different form of relationship appropriate to larger numbers, it
was impossible that any direct confrontation with the existing forces
of law and order could be successful. Without such a breakthrough,
the only options left are submission and defeat or withdrawal into the
a-political realm of the domestic. The chapter ends, appropriately:

Some days after, I was informed of the arrest of Joseph Healey, at
Lees. I began to expect something of the sort myself, and told our
constable that if he got a warrant, and would let me know, I would
go with him any day, or night, to Manchester, and there should be
no fuss; no one would be the wiser. He said he would take that
course, should he have a warrant; and I attended my business as
usual. (I, p. 219)

That a different perspective on the appropriate interpretation of the
conflict focussed in Peterloo was already available, is clear from the
reaction of another prisoner: James Murrey, captured by the drilling
radicals while spying on them. In his official deposition, quoted by
Bamford, Murrey relates that (I, p. 194):

Examinant [Murrey] begged they [the radicals] would not murder

him."—-Examinant said, "you treat ME very differently to what

Suppose that Iam an enemy, you ought to treat me as a prisoner."

If we remember that the 12,000 troops deployed against the
Luddites was a far larger army than Wellington’s in the Peninsular
campaign of 1808, and recall that Disraeli’s Sybz/: or The Two Nations
was to appear only five years after Bamford’s Passages, we might
conclude that Murrey’s interpretation was more accurate than

The limits of Bamford’s political analysis and position are thus
clearly related to his adherence to the experience of a known
community; though that basic sense of himself did collapse, of the two
options then open—into individualism or into a more organised
national radical politics—Bamford basically chose the former.

Can we now attempt to connect these various related features to the
actual style and structure of his autobiographical writings, particularly
Passages? That there is a consonance between the elements so far
sketched and the fact of Bamford’s writing an autobiography at all
should now be fairly clear. Writing, of any kind, is, as a political act, an
option for persuasion over any more active strategy; and the choice
stems naturally from an analysis that bases itself on the factor of
involuntary ignorance. To write about oneself is the natural response
if the ignorance in question is taken to be ignorance of your kind of
people and of their condition, the absence of that element of personal
knowledge so important to Bamford’s politics. Moreover, to write an
autobiography is simultaneously an act of withdrawal into one’s known
base and an attempt to extend that known community, to include
one’s readers in it, to have a ‘personal’ relationship to unknown people
who yet remain strangers. Writing an autobiography was almost the
only way in which the tensions in Bamford’s position could be both
maintained and apparently overcome.

wt : a passive style

But how was he actually to write it? Bamford seems to have intended
his readership to include all classes (cf. H, chs. XLVI, XLVI, but if
the purpose of the autobiography was, at one level, to persuade, the
self-portrait had to attract the sympathy of those readers who might be
his political opponents. As we shall see, this involved Bamford in
presenting himself passively, as someone almost forced into his

34

political activity; it meant playing down any element of conscious,
willed and deliberate action, of decision-taking.

But the characteristically passive presentation is not simply tactical; it
reflects the central features of Bamford’s actual life: the experience of
living through a process of immense social change, which seemed
beyond immediate and adequate human control and which he could at
best only minimally influence. Much as he opposed it or personalised
it in interpretation, Bamford was nevertheless right to recognise that,
in some respects, ‘the present distress of the country arises from
unavoidable circumstances’ (I, p. 148); all the changes of the industrial
revolution may have ultimately stemmed from specific human actions
and decisions, but the result was—for those living within the
process—strictly un-avoid-able. Moreover the high-point, the defining
centre, of Bamford’s own life was the experience of imprisonment, of
being caught up in a judicial process from which he could not escape
and which forced him into confined inactivity; arrests, trials and
imprisonment occupy the overwhelming majority of pages in his
Passages. That he should present himself passively is no accident.

To demonstrate that Bamford’s passive self-presentation influences
even certain characteristics of his style will require detailed analyses.
We can approach these by tracing the more explicit elements of
passivity. Bamford quotes, in Passages I, ch. XIV, a great deal of the
prosecuting counsel’s speech at the York trial, opening with ‘Mr.
Scarlett’s description of us’. Hunt is regarded as sufficiently well
known to require no introduction, but "the others were obscure; they
were very little known, and he [Scarlett] should therefore state who
they were"; each defendant is then given a line or two of description,
including occupation and residence, until the list ends: ‘Of Robert
Wilde he knew nothing, save that he lived near Ashton-under-Line.
Samuel Bamford and Robert Jones were individuals in humble
circumstances’ (II, pp. 62-3).

Bamford and Jones are given no other description; even among theit
co-defendants they are the most obscure, the least important. At this
high-point of his life, Bamford is scarcely noticed. It is no surprise that
the adjective "humble" is the one most frequently used by Bamford of
himself. When, later in the trial, a quarrel nearly breaks out between
Hunt and himself, as Hunt snubs him, Bamford swallows the insult,
commenting: ‘I considered the cause too great, too holy—to be
prejudiced in the least, by any circumstance affecting one so humble as
myself.’ (II, p. 89)

His own defence speech ends:

35

"Humble as was my situation in society, I would not condescend
to beg the boon of mercy from any man or set of men, however
exalted their situation. I would disdain to receive that from their
pity, to which justice entitled me." (II, p. 154)

Here Bamford is rejecting what elsewhere he will indicate by the term
"humiliation", a refusal of the dignity and respect due even to one in
"humble" circumstances (cf. I, p. 156f; I, p. 213). But even at the
end of his account, in the additional chapters of Passages, he will still
remark that:

When "Young England", the other day, met "Young Manchester",
in the Free Trade Hall, many rich men, members of Parliament,
and exalted characters, gave additional interest to the meeting by
their presence. I also was there, and though undistinguished
amongst that vast and gay assemblage—too humble indeed to be
so—I could not but reflect with satisfaction, that I was one of
those who made the mind, which made both Young England and
Young Manchester.

"Humble" is always an adjective he is willing to use of his own
condition—for example, of the lodgings he preferred (II, p. 47)—but
he baulks when the description "humble" seems imposed upon him:
then it has different overtones, as when Sir Charles Wolseley invites
him to stay, but not in Wolseley’s home, only in a nearby inn; on that
occasion, Bamford concludes: ‘Sir Charles was always kind and affable,
without pretensions, but still I could not but feel that in his house, I
was, only a very humble guest’ (II, p. 27).

It is this sense of disparity between subjective self-definition and
external description that underlies some of Bamford’s political
problems: in his defence speech he objects strongly to the way in
which radical meetings were represented in the press —‘uniformly
styled . . mobs, riotous, tumultuous and disorderly mobs . . ridiculed as
illiterate, dirty and mean’ (II, p. 79). An awareness of being observed
is a constant element: he ‘believed ministers had eyes to see, and ears
to hear, and tongues to whisper whatever recurred’ (I, p. 157).
Bamford’s reaction is to turn such observation into an asset: in
preparing for the Peterloo meeting

It was deemed expedient that this meeting should be as morally
effective as possible, and, that it should exhibit a spectacle such as
had never before been witnessed in England . . we determined that
. . we should disarm the bitterness of our political opponents by a

36

display of cleanliness, sobriety and decorum, such as we never

before had exhibited. (I, pp. 176-77)

But such reliance on spectacle, on exhibiting order, could backfire:
Bamford quotes a magistrate’s comment on Peterloo, ‘that "the party
with the blue and green banners came upon the field in beautiful
order!" adding, I think, that "not until then did he become
alarmed" (I, p. 180) What is felt from within as an expression of
responsibility is seen from without as an organised threat, dangerous
precisely because organised. The difficulty arises as part of the
struggle, already examined, to establish an ordered opposition to an
already existing order, a social order composed of the "humble" and
the "exalted" and in which, to use Scarlett’s revealing phrase, "weavers,
and shoemakers, and other artisans, in this kingdom, . . were destined
to earn their bread by the labour of their hands" (II, p. 66).

That "destined" marks an assumption of overriding, supra-human,
unchangeable order, in the face of which the natural response is to
submit. Bamford’s political options, for non-violence and relatively
small-group activity, often amounted in practice to submission: he
offers no resistance to arrest, or (initially) to being chained, counsels
against provocation or retaliation, advocates patience and surrender of
passions and prejudice, and argues that "unoffending manners" will
help "restore the natural balance of society" (1, pp. 81, 104, 199; II, pp.
19, 90).

In these passages he is, of course, accepting and appropriating the
role that those he opposed would want to impose upon him: to remain
humble and deferential, even to accept the blame for any hostility that
may arise. It is no solution then to argue that ‘the degradation was
with those who offered the insult, and not with those who were
compelled to receive it’ (I, p. 166).

important incident. The devices he uses, briefly noted here, can all be
seen as operative in other passages of his work. In describing, for
example, the mass-meetings of 1819 Bamford relates that:

Bamford’s choice of passive and impersonal constructions instead of
active forms at important moments in his narrative is significantly
related to the other features of passivity, particularly political passivity,
sketched earlier. If we look at some episodes where active and passive

We can conclude this analysis of Bamford’s characteristically passive
forms of describing political action, by noting that, in one sense,
‘passivity’ was almost the only means of ‘control’ that a working class
tadical movement could have exercised at that time over the national
situation. When Bamford describes at length the drilling exercises
preparatory to Peterloo we can sense some of the difficulties that had
to be overcome before a disciplined movement could be forged:

When dusk came, and we could no longer see to work, we jumped
from our looms, and rushed to the sweet cool air of the fields, or
the waste lands, or the green lane-sides; we mustered,—we fell
into rank, we faced, marched, halted, faced about, counter-
marched, halted again, dressed, and wheeled in quick succession,
and without confusion. (I, p. 178)

Next would follow a long drill, in squads; and so expert were the

the praises of the old campaigners. Then, when they broke for a
little rest, would follow a jumping match, or a race, or a friendly
wrestle, or a roll down the hill, amid the laughter of others sitting
in the sun. Some would be squatted on the lee of a bush of gorse

The contrast here between marshalled order and spontaneous
relaxation is an index to the general cultural and behavioural shifts and
tensions of the industrial revolution as a whole. To oppose the
increasingly interlocked and homogeneous nature of an emerging
industrialised situation required an increasingly co-ordinated and wide-
spread counter-force. But if a "Spencean" strategy of insurrection was
excluded, the only viable action open to such a counter-force was,
basically, a withdrawal of labour, solidarity in strike-action, sustained
and patient in its very in-action.

On a small scale such tactical passiveness had already appeared as a
local weapon in e.g. the Manchester strikes of 1818; at a national level,
once an extensive opposition movement had been formed in the early
Chartist phase, such tactics were to be proposed as a complete
strategy, in the "National Holiday" so much disliked later by Bamford.
It was not so much his innate passivity that limited Bamford’s political
outlook — the circumstances engendered passive response of one
kind or another — but his restriction to a small-scale horizon within a
known group of political allies that made it impossible for him to
connect passivity with any grouping adequately large, and therefore
‘impersonal’, enough to make passivity more than submission.

Perhaps a final comment on the difficulties both of the problems
Bamford faced and of analysing their linguistic expression can be made
by examining a single odd sentence. Bamford’s departure ftom
Manchester after Peterloo is recounted in the sentence:

I rejoined my comrades, and forming about a thousand of them
into file, we set off to the sound of fife and drum, with our only
banner waving, and in that form we re-entered the town of
Middleton. (1, p. 213)

That "comrades" is used here (the first instance in Passages) of a
thousand people is itself notable, but even more inviting of analysi is
the unlocated subject of "forming": is it "I", "them[selves]" or "we"; is
the implied verb active, passive or reflexive? One might say that here
Bamford, instinctively, avoids the inadequacy of attributing the action
of "forming" solely to himself as ‘leader’; the action can only be that of
a collective subject, for which there is no appropriately democratic
grammar available. Linguistic analysis cannot convince at this point:
other explanations are easily enough to hand —poor education, slip of
the pen, hypercritical reading. But the sentence still reads oddly, and
perhaps symptomatically—in the sense that Freud’s analysis of
grammatical slips often indicate a deep-seated tension or problem.

vii : elements of form and relationship

If we move now to an analysis of some of the structural or formal
features of Passages the difficulties increase—primarily in the area of
knowing the criteria upon which to make the judgements that follow.
We can first link the characteristics of style examined above to one
element of the content of Bamford’s autobiographical writings, as a
bridge to a more tentative analysis. Bamford’s writings ate notable
among working class political autobiographies for the attention given
in them to the subject of women. Where the main emphasis in such
writings is political involvement, wife and family, and emotional
relations generally, tend to be taken for granted.

In Early Days. of course, the emphasis is more natural: he remarks
that he ‘can scarecely [sé] recollect a period of [his] life when the
society of females was not very agreeable to [him]’ (ED, p. 169), and
the book certainly supports this. As a child in his father’s workhouse,
he was captivated by a beautiful Irish girl of seventeen, whom he takes
a full page to describe (ED, p. 71); his relationship with Mima, whom
he eventually married, is traced in detail, including its adolescent
embartrassments; at school he ‘took quite as much notice of certain
pretty figures which sat in the girls room opposite . . as [he] did to
those in [his] book’ (ED, p. 185), and though, ‘Mima . . was not
forgotten’ (ED, p. 185), he constantly notes other girls, during work,
recreation and travel (ED, pp. 189, 197, 199-200, 221, 256, etc). In
Passages too he includes in the narrative many incidental mentions of
girls he sees or meets (e.g. I, pp. 20, 56, 178), and some more lengthy
accounts of particular encounters with women (e.g. I, p. 115f; cf.
below). His proposal that women should vote at radical meetings is
another indication of a special interest (I, p.165).

One could perhaps offer a specific psychological root for this
relative emphasis—his loss of his mother at the age of six, the
appointment of a new governess in place of his mother, and his
father’s re-marriage were all formative events (ED, pp. 58f, 66, 96); but
the appropriate point seems to me different.

Bamford himself recognises, in his 1817 Account, that his personal
relationships, especially in the case of his illegitimate children, might
have political significance at the level of providing ammunition to his
opponents, but there is perhaps another level at which they are
politically more significant. Considerable work has been done in
recent years on the connection between neighbourhood environments
and modes of personal relationship; the general conclusion seems to
be that relationships in a ‘traditional’ working class district are
characteristically passive, the codes assumed and lived into without

49

great personal tensions; but this means that the social skills necessary
actively to create new personal relationships in a changed
environment—a new housing estate, for example—can be learned
only with difficulty. If we grasp Bamford’s experience of negotiating
the move from known community to a world of strangers, as involving
similar problems of relationship, then we can see that his emphasis on
encounters with women is not fortuitous.

Indeed, his love-life in Early Days is actually polarised round two
main relationships: that with Mima, following a comparatively easy and
‘natural’ path, with accepted codes of courting, and that with
Catherine, accidentally begun and pursued intermittently through
confusions and difficulties, only to end without satisfaction (cf. ED,
pp. 205-7, 212-15, 221f, 283-87). Mima is almost ‘the girl next door’,
known from childhood; Catherine comes from a different class, a
strange world, and Bamford’s responses are complex: faced with her
mother’s economic conditions for a suitor, Bamford says: ‘I felt
abashed, I thought I was sure enough presumptious /si/, and that I
had not any right to stand in the way of the old mother’s expectations
on behalf of her daughter’ (ED, p. 207). His reaction to losing
Catherine is to attempt to bury all emotion, ‘and never more to give to
woman the power of inflicting pain’ (ED, p. 216). In this mood, he
has an encounter which clearly underlines the connection between his
sense of a known community and his difficulties in reacting in such a
way as to deny the patterns and modes of personal relationship
engendered within such communities:

took the dangerous resolve of hearing what she had to say, and of
observing what she would do. I therefore suffered her to continue
her conversation, and she led me into less frequented streets, and

His refusal here to enter into an impersonal relationship is part of

autobiography, as was noticed earlier, presented some of the same
problems: to establish a personal relationship with a reader who
remains a stranger is difficult, and to grasp the way Bamford tried to
solve that difficulty brings us close also to some of the problems
involved in writing ‘literature’, in becoming a novelist.

The following example shows one aspect of the problem. Passages
II, chapters XHI to XX are concerned with Bamford’s trial at York,
chapters XXIX and XXX relate the final proceedings at the King’s
Bench, London. Between these two phases of his trial, Bamford tells
the story of his journey down to London (chs. XXI to XXVIII).
These intervening chapters may seem at first ‘mere’ narrative,
essentially unrelated to the overall narrative. But if we examine their
content more attentively, a different impression emerges.

The second volume of Passages is, as has been indicated, very much
concerned with Bamford’s politically important realisation that
personal friendship is an inadequate basis for a radical movement; it
might be argued that these chapters help, in fact, to chart his
confusion and progress towards disillusion on this matter. Ch. XXI

All these aspects of Bamford’s writing break through the limits of
‘straight’ narrative, employing what would normally be regarded as
‘literary’ devices. At the other extreme from such deliberately stylish
writing as the invocation to the reader quoted above, there is
Bamford’s frequent use of documentary material, from State papers,
newspapers or reports of his trials. His own account at times too takes
on the stark character of court-room verbatim reporting. It is worth
recalling, of course, that the work of Gaskell and Dickens, at about the
same time as Bamford’s Passages, also shows influences from
documentary evidence and verbatim reportage. But the point to grasp
is that Bamford’s writing employs a wide range of ‘styles’, or perhaps
more strictly, modes of écriture, intricately tapestried. Almost any
passage of any length would reveal, in analysis, constant shifts of kinds
of writing—the first three chapters of Passages, for example, would
repay close attention, in their moves from personal comment through
impersonal omniscient history to ‘autobiography’. But one long
episode in particular awaits examination: the concluding movement of
the whole work, where many of the devices so far noted are brought
into play.

The phrasing of those last words is exact. Throughout this long

As we saw earlier, Bamford’s political allegiances were based on
personal relationships; an earlier section, analysed above, showed him
meeting various kinds of breakdown and confusion in personal
relations; now the climax has come: radical political "friendships"
cover only the same kinds of egotism, corruption and self-seeking as
other, apparently opposed, forms of politics. To see through that
"veil", finally, is for Bamford, a tremendous "gain": he ceases, in his
own eyes, being "a passive instrument"—a precise description of his
mode of political activity (cf. iv, vi, above). From now on the only
personal relationship on which to build a life is that of married love —
even Hunt’s false (in Bamford’s view) relationship with his mistress is
rejected. But pure domesticity is a-political. This realisation is a kind
of conversion for Bamford.

The point of the analyses above has been to show that Passages does
have an unusual form, that it verges on, without ever quite clearly
becoming, a work our responses to which are controlled and guided by
literary techniques more often to be expected in novels and
imaginative literature generally than in apparently simple narrative.
Bamford, it should now be clear, is not simply writing a report of his
experiences; by selection, by thematic patterning, by indirect or near-
allegorical commentary and illustration, by parallels and juxtapositions,
he is trying to overcome the barrier between his experience and that of
readers who would not have shared that kind of experience but who
might, by their response to his anecdotes and apparently irrelevant
inclusions, find an echo, a resonance. It is perhaps impossible to judge
how consciously shaped his writing is; but the same might be said of
other writers to whose work we would not hesitate to apply some of
the interpretative techniques of criticism employed above. Perhaps, as
Bamford remarks in an appropriate context, "reality is always
romantic, though the romantic is not always real" (I, p. 230).

Account.

However, the main point to be emphasised here is that Passages is a
half-way house. The writing —the style and structure— is a fusion of
historical documentary, personal narrative and imaginative literature —
if such category-terms can be allowed to stand. Bamford does not
transmute his experience fully into fiction, into a novel, where the
relationship to his material would have required a different kind of
skill or capacity: an imaginative creation of, and inwardness to, ‘other’
characters, a breaking beyond the bounds of his own actual
experiences, a distancing from self. To some extent, perhaps, his
meagre education provided some of the limits in that direction. But it
also seems to me that there is a complex relation between his political

74

position (reflecting, as we saw, his social experience, and reflected in
turn in some aspects of his style) and the mode of his autobiography.
Just as the limits on his political stance were concerned with the
difficult transformation of a close, known personal community of
relationships into extended and ‘impersonal’ forms of relation to
unknown co-operators, to ‘strangers’ —a transformation Bamford
eventually refused in his virtual retreat into domesticity— so the limits
of his self-expression are deeply connected with his inability to enter
fully into a mediated relationship with his experience and his audience.

One can perhaps put the point more clearly another way: to read a
novel is to enter another community, to penetrate others’ experiences,
to leave the horizon of one’s own ‘world’; in the process, one’s
horizon is opened, one’s community re-defined, one’s experience
reflected back and refracted, prepared for judgement. To write a novel
seems to require the same kinds of openness; to read an autobiography
also does. But to write an autobiography does not demand openness
in the same way: the horizon remains the closed circle of one’s self,
even if concentric citcles of self-identification, chronologically
traceable, appear within it.

The terms used above to describe the process of reading a novel
have a clear correlation with a possible description of the political
mode necessary for a fully democratic participation in a social world. I
tried earlier to indicate the positive reasons why to write an
autobiography was a decision homologous with Bamford’s limited
political position; but to seek an elucidation of the limitations of his
actually writing an autobiography is perhaps to arrive at a threshold of
currently answerable literary questions—and political horizons.

If at Peterloo banners demanding ‘parliamentary reform’ mingled with
others advocating ‘repeal of the Corn Laws’, the two movements
represented by those slogans soon diverged. In the 1840s Chartists
and Corn-law repealers were distinct and often antagonistic groups.
Bamford may have only dabbled in corn law repeal agitation, but
Somerville was closely involved, employed for a period by the Anti-
Corn Law League itself. He also touched, at important moments, the
insurrectionary elements in working-class agitation of the 1830s and
1840s, but his relation to them was hostile. His political position was
clear, a commitment to the kinds of organised pressure-group activity
exemplified by the ACLL.

This chapter cannot present an exhaustive analysis of Somerville. I
want simply to focus on one element in his autobiography, as revealing
an underlying homology between his connection with the CALL, and
through that with certain features of English society in the mid-
century, and the structure and content of his Aufobiography of a Working
Man (1848). That element is the importance, in various ways, of
names. The analysis is not strictly of ‘style’, but parallel perhaps to the
analysis of the role of women in Bamford’s autobiography (cf. above,
chapter 1, sec.vii ).

The title-page of the autobiography includes no name: The
Autobiography of A Working Man. by "One who has whistled at the plough".
The opening pages (ostensibly addressed to "My dear boy" — the
author’s son) give no family surname, recounting his ancestors simply
in terms of "your grandfather and grandmother, my parents" even
though the second paragraph is largely occupied with a comment
about names (pp. 1-2):

One of them, Lawrence, died at Perth within my recollection. I
was to have been called Lawrence after him, but a change was
made, and I was named after my mother’s brother, a collier, living
at Square, near Berwick-upon-Tweed - a worthy man, to whose
name I may not have done all the honour I might have done. In
my boyhood I used to regret that I had not been called Lawrence.
I then thought my own name was a shabby one. Perhaps my
dislike to it arose from its being so very common in Scotland.

Common or not, the reader is not told the name: we are given the
name of the author’s father’s horse ("the common equine name of
Dick"- p. 2) but not his father’s. An uncle’s Christian name and the full
name (John Orkney) of Somerville’s maternal grandfather are given,

author’s full name from the auto-biography until its climax is reached.
On page 277 a. sentence of court-martial is quoted:

The Court, having found the prisoner ‘guilty’ of the crime laid to
his charge ... sentence him, the prisoner, Alexander Somerville, to
receive two hundred lashes ...

This long silence, though odd, might be explained by the convention
of Somerville writing his autobiography as letters to his son; but that
convention only rarely intrudes and within a few chapters effectively
ceases to operate. Moreover, the preface acknowledges that in its
published form the work is actually addressed to the public at large (p.
iv). Before an alternative explanation is suggested, the role of names
in the autobiography has to be analysed.

Other names are omitted from the autobiography, their place taken
by initials and/or dashes. Some of these omissions ate connected with
a girl he refers to as "the nameless ONE" (e.g. p. 263), whom he
courted unsuccessfully and therefore whose name and those of her

(cf. pp. 347, 348, 354, 394, 412), or personal friends (e.g. pp. 37, 149,

Somerville himself offers:

In consequence of this Autobiography not having been written to
be published in my life-time, nor so soon as the year 1848, had I
died before, I find in it several names of persons which for the
present must be omitted. (p. 230)

Sometimes, however, it is the inclusion of a name that is interesting:
he gives the names and characters of the family cows (pp. 46-48), of a

78

farm-horse (p. 62), and of his plough-horses (p. 96). In his youth,
Somerville lived in a world of known people and things — even
more so than Bamford — so familiar and personal to him that on
running away from home he says an individual good-bye to the corn-
chest, the pump, the wheelbarrow, the parritch-cart, Kidley the cow (p.

our family who had herded cows in those woods had carved the
initials of their names, with the dates and other memoranda. He paid a
visit to that tree, and once more put [his] name on it, and then turned
away" (p. 77). When the three brothers return home together, years
later, Somerville again notes the "trees on which all three, with years
between, had carved [their] names" (p. 368). His early world is one in
which people are distinguished not by their surnames but by a fusion
of name and character: six casual workers named Michael are
differentiated as "old Michael, young Michael, big Michael, wee
Michael, singing Michael, and Michael the laird" (p. 71); we finally
learn his father’s Christian name only because "old Bonar, the orry
horse" is so like "old James, the orry man" (p. 62).

Somerville remembers launching a local nick-name: "In my effort to
call the old woman Mary Edgly, I called her Essel, which name
everybody else took up; she lived ten or twelve years after that time,
and was always called Essel" (p. 14). Mary Edgly’s husband, Thomas
Brown, (as Somerville notes, "it is the custom for married women, in
Scotland, to retain their maiden names, a custom which sounds
strangely in English ears", p. 41), used to tell Somerville fairy tales,
and, "as Thomas Brown had known the fairies personally, [Somerville]
had as complete a belief in their existence as [he] had that of the
people who lived at Stobby Castle, or at Edinburgh, or anywhere else
beyond Thriepland Hill and Branxton", about whom other people had
told him (p. 16). The very naming of people and places creates their
reality, if they exist outside the child’s immediate experience.

As a boy Somerville shared in another old man’s world, a world
created by names: blind James Dawson, "a man of strong imagination"
who "had read history and geography extensively", "used to sit down
and call to Sir Walter Raleigh, Essex, Burleigh, and other courtiers of
Elizabeth to come to him, and when they came he sent them to fetch
her majesty. He would then go into political arguments with them
about Philip of Spain and the other personages and subjects of
Elizabeth’s reign. He would listen as if some one spoke into his ear,
for their observations, and would interrupt them at times impatiently,
if they did not seem to be holding a sound argument. Intermingled
with such converse he would speak aside to some shepherd or farmer
whom he had known in his younger days, and ask him what he

79

thought of Burleigh’s opinions. The next minute he would address me
by name, and ask a question as to what I thought of Queen Elizabeth’s
dress."(p. 39)

It is in this setting that the author is first given any name: "J, in the
usual way, asked, "Well, James, how are ye thi’ day?" he said, "Man,
Sandy, I’m glad I’ve met ye .. you must go this minute to the Empress,
and tell her that Frank Horne must not be made a slave" (pp. 39-40).
Since the "Empress" is the imagined Empress of Russia, whereas
Frank Horne is actually a "lad from Branxton" we cannot be fully
assured of the status of "Sandy" until the name is later repeated — the
only name given Somerville till the court-martial.

Old James Dawson’s imaginary world is not far removed from
Somerville’s own, in which "old Bonar", the horse, can be regarded as
having, not just his own language, but a degree of religious and
supernatural insight (p. 62f), and in which the repetition of the names
of threatening things can magically ward off danger: "’m not feared
for thunder, nor the man of the moon, nor wull cats neither" (p. 60).

So far we ate in a world similar to Bamford’s known community,
where a name naturally referred only to someone within that
community, and which was also permeated with superstitious beliefs.
But Somerville is aware, in writing, that others — even his son — do not
share that world, and he is prepared to describe it in some detail: its
work-processes, its dialect, and its peculiar names for places and things
(cf. eg. pp. 9-10, 446 48£, 55, 74, 97-104, 140-141, 366). It is not his
origins in a community of familiar names, places, etc., that concerns us
here, however, but his continued interest in names outside that
community. When he leaves home he passes his mother’s home-village
and seeks out his grandfather’s grave and that of "two brothers and a
sister" who had died before Somerville was born; he tries to conjure
up "what kind of a man he had been" and what the dead children
might have become (p. 79). Later, that scene is echoed, in a different
graveyard: Somerville and a brother, James, "were like younger sons of
Old Mortality, scraping the moss from obliterated letters, to read the
names of men and women long dead, of whom we could know
nothing. But once in possession of these unknown names, we
moralised on the probable characters of those who had once answered
to them in days that now belonged to the dark past" (pp. 118). In
Edinburgh his "greatest entertainment of an intellectual nature" was
attending debates of the Synod of the United Secession Church, "not
so much from the interest attached to any single topic under
discussion" but because "many of the debaters were ministers of
whom [he] had heard; whose reputations for piety and ministerial
ability stood high, and whose very names were always uttered by [his]

80

father .. with .. veneration; and whom [he] could now look upon" (pp.
109-110).

Somerville’s introduction to politics is, of course, through the names
of politicians: when the Grey government is announced in a
newspaper, Somerville and his work-mates "read over the list of the
new ministry. Some of the names were unknown to us, and some
familiar names that we thought should have been there were not there,
the name of Hume especially" (p. 143). Names are for Somerville an
almost effective substitute for the real person: of a friend he says, "I
hardly ever write his name, or look upon it when it is written, or speak
it, or listen to it when it is spoken, but the echo of that friendly
counsel, and the form and image of that disastrous time, come back
upon me" (p. 184), while in the case of "the nameless ONE", "To me
there was a charm about every one who bore that name" and he even
starts drinking heavily to have "the daily satisfaction of associating
with one or more of her nearest relatives, and with two or three other

Some of the instances where names play a role in the autobiography
indicate underlying social reasons for this constant interest. For
example, Somerville relates an amusing anecdote on the disparity
between apparent social importance and actual social role
(interestingly, the case of a newspaper editor given priority in
Canning’s office), revealed only in an exchange of "cards" — the
unknown name suddenly given importance by the attached
professional description (p. 112); on one occasion, he obtains a paper
signed with Cobbett’s name as an introduction to London contacts (p.
341); both these episodes illustrate, of course, the (continuing) role of
the visiting-card in 19th century society.

One of Somerville’s early jobs was the commonplace task of trying
to collect the names of subscribers for a newspaper (p. 182). Two
other incidents show how, in a period of social and geographic
mobility, a name could often be the only element of continuity in
relationships: in 1847, as a newspaper correspondent, he meets some
of the officers he knew fifteen years before in the army, and, by not
introducing himself, accompanies them unrecognised (p. 226); after
the same time lapse, even the offer, eventually, to introduce himself is
enough to jog the memory of a publican who had entertained him
without knowing "the stranger" (pp. 342-3). But these are very minor
examples of a (now) familiar experience. Somerville’s concern with
names has deeper roots, and we can now begin to trace them.

The victimisation is clear; so too is the overall context of military

traditional, but it is worth emphasising that this was precisely the
situation in which Somerville achieved national notice as a named
individual (cf. ii below).

As a soldier in Spain in 1835-37, Somerville encountered this aspect
of army life at its most de-humanising: he "made out the returns of
sick and dead men at Vittoria, every day, where, during the first winter,
we lost nearly four thousand men" (p. 432), "kept our own company’s
accounts ... and knew what every man’s credit was when he died" (p.

comment that such deaths left "blanks in the company’s roll, in which
some of the bravest and best were scored out, with the words written,

‘killed in action, 5th Hay’, or “6th June’,"” (p. 438), underlines the extent
to which his experience of warfare presented individual men to him as
mere names on a list.

In fact, Somerville fought against this reduction: "knowing more
about the dead men, of every regiment, than any other individual, and
the time at which most of them lost their lives ... [he] was applied to by
their friends, on returning to Britain, to give information about their
fate, and the debts due to them by the Spanish government" (p. 436).
This knowledge, and the publication of his Narrative of the British Legion
in Spain (1837), brought him "daily ... twenties, forties, and sometimes
one hundred letters" (p. 451) from relatives seeking advice about the
recovery of pay and compensation; but in taking up these cases,
Somerville entered a Kafka-esque legal world in which names,
signatures, lists, identifications and his own signed guarantees became
so much paper for financial speculation, legal quibbling, and
government bureaucracy, and in which the original bearers of the
names involved were soon a secondary consideration (pp. 450-66 :
Appendix III). As a youth Somerville had already, traumatically,
encountered the purely legal significance of a name - he had been fined
for "coming into town with a cart which had no ticket of the owner’s
name and residence on it", even though the town officer "knew [his]
master very well, and .. the cart, though it had no name on it, was his
cart" (p. 65).

That early incident is already perhaps an indication of the kind of
changes overtaking the known relationships of a small community, but
in the affair of the Spanish certificates, where names of strangers
became all-important in a network embracing the _ legal,
communications and security systems of the country, we begin to
grasp on a major scale the shape and model of a new kind of society.

ai: 1832 and alienation

Somerville’s central insertion into that new kind of society began, not
inappropriately, with the sending of an anonymous letter to a
newspaper. In May 1832, during the feverish nine days of uncertainty
about the eventual reaction of the House of Lords to the Reform Bill,
Somerville, then a soldier in the Scots Greys, stationed in Birmingham,
was one of a group of soldiers who decided "that the best means of
preventing a collision with the reform movement and the national will,
as expressed by the House of Commons, was to give circulation to the
fact that [the troops] were not to be relied upon to put down public
meetings, or prevent the people of Birmingham from journeying to

83

London, to present their petitions, and support the House of
Commons by their presence" (p. 245).

This group "caused letters to be written and sent to various parties
in Birmingham and London, to that effect. Some were addressed to
the Duke of Wellington, some to the War Office to Lord Hill, and
some were dropped in the streets. Those letters were necessarily
anonymous" (p. 246). Somerville also, however, wrote a letter to a
London newspaper affirming this position. Its publication, on May
27th, led to his court-martial (ostensibly on a charge of disobedience in
a tiding-school incident) and flogging; a Court of Inquiry into the
legality of the court martial aroused national interest. The Times
sensationally, even replaced some columns of its normal front-page
advertisements with an account of its findings.

The narrative of these events is clear enough. It is their impact on
Somerville that concerns us. From the time of his flogging, Somerville
became a local, then national celebrity, but not as a known individual
person, simply as a name to be used as a slogan. Again and again,
Somerville recounts experiences of this divorce between himself and
his name.

After release from hospital after the flogging, Somerville is in a pub
writing another letter to the press to publicise the court martial, when
the drinkers inquire about "that soldier that was flogged a week ago";
Somerville declines to offer information, and is then upbraided for
cowardice by comparison with the writer of the letter of May 27th (pp.
301-3030. In this incident, there are three Somervilles: himself in the
pub, the flogged soldier, and the brave letter-writer — none identified
with the others. Once his second letter is published, the public can
name him "Somerville", but Somerville himself remains unknown:

Nobody in the crowds knew me; but many of them inquired
eagerly for Somerville. "What are they doing to him?" "Why don’t
they allow him to come out?" They thought the gates were closed
to keep him in. . none of them knew that they were talking to him"

(p. 304).

While being escorted to Coventry and again on the return to
Birmingham, Somerville meets the same unwitting queries about
"Somerville" (pp. 305, 309). He even has beer thrown over him in a
pub for refusing to drink to "Somerville" — an act of intolerant
imposition that contradicts the toaster’s profession of admiration for
independent opinion (pp. 307-8). While waiting to appear at his Court
of Inquiry he is enrolled, as an unknown "soldier standing in the way",
to polish the boots of the judges (pp. 325-6). During a celebration

84

procession for the Reform Act, Somerville is on sentry duty outside
the barracks:

Not one of the many thousands knew me personally, but each
band ceased to play as it came near the barrack gate; each trade or
section of a political union, halted in front of the gate, as pre-
arranged by a master of ceremonies, and three cheers, loud and
long, were given for "Somerville for ever!" They had not the
remotest suspicion that I was the sentry, with my carbine on my
arm, standing in the gateway looking at them. "For ever!" they
shouted in connection with my name. (p. 340)

That name soon became a profitable commercial commodity: "full,
true, and particular accounts" of his case are hawked around, none of
them authorised by him, and mostly "inventions of some enterprising
printer, or flying stationer" (p. 303). Madame Tussaud’s offer him £50
to exhibit "Somerville the soldier" (p. 346); a theatre-benefit is
proposed; he is offered payment "to lend [his] name to be used in a
newspaper" (p. 346); collections, both genuine and fraudulent, are
made for him and in his name (pp. 345, 347f); he is urged to "invest
the money [collected for him] and the use of [his] name, in a public-
house" (p. 372); he finally agrees to use his name to help launch a
literary journal, but even then he is left in debt for "the expenses ...
incurred in [his] name" (p. 373).

Somerville’s name also became a political weapon: at first, "People
took it into their heads to pass resolutions at public meetings, and to
send these formally written out" (p. 306); later, he himself is required
to attend political meetings or, on being recognised at a meeting, is
urged to speak: "I was observed; some one called me loudly by name;
there was shouting and clapping of hands, and a cry for me to go to
the platform" (pp. 349-350; cf pp. 344, 354). But all these
"impositions committed on the public in [his] name" (p. 348) only
provoke Somerville’s repugnance: he has no wish to be a celebrity and
feels that his motives are misunderstood and misrepresented; he
experiences a total divorce between his private existence and his public
image, between Somerville and "Somerville":

There was not, in all the metropolitan wilderness of streets and
houses, and it is a wilderness to those who are alone, a more
lonely being than I was, at that time. I saw my name every week
on the bills of newspapers, saw and heard people reading those
bills at street corners; heard my case and myself discussed in the
patlours of public-houses, occasionally by persons who professed

85

to know me intimately; none of which persons I had before seen
in all my life. I felt that I was not the kind of man that everybody
expected or believed me to be. They depicted to themselves a
person of flashy exterior, fluent in address, able and ready to talk
and speak anywhere, at any time; and so well-skilled in the ways
and usages of free and easy society, as to take a hand at cards, play
billiards or bagatelle, or crack jokes, crack nuts, or crack heads
with equal readiness. I knew of no quality which entitled me to
respect, except that I had a strong right arm [and] a determination
not easily turned aside. (p. 350)

In a strict sense of the term, Somerville’s name had become
alienated from himself. The direct parallel to his experience of his
name in its new status is his relation to the money collected for him:

There was a moral space, a chasm between me and that money,
which made it different from any other money; I had not worked
for it. I never loved it as my own. I never had confidence in it ..
My brothers warned me that I was losing it; but they did not see it
with my eyes, nor feel it to be an alien treasure, as I felt it. (pp.
372-3)

The parallel suggests the near-contemporary analysis of Marx:
names, like money, were increasingly becoming an abstract, an emptied
yet powerful medium of relationship; the older forms in which
"Singing Michael" was an adequate designation were being replaced, in
Somerville’s experience at least, by a world in which names were units
on a list or rallying-cries divorced from the actual, living persons who
bore them. This was, of course, an ambivalent social change: on the
one hand, it enabled Somerville to become available, through letters
from total strangers, to many people he could help, as in the Spanish
certificates episode; but it also, for example, allowed Somerville to be
imposed upon by a correspondent writing under his personal name,
while concealing his real interest as a journalist (p. 306f).

In the area of political organisation the ambiguity is acute. Chapter
XXIX recounts Somerville’s involvement in an insurrectionary plot in
1834. On the strength of his reputation as "Somerville the soldier", he
is invited to attend a secret meeting, where, he says,

I was hurried to a private room, but not before some persons had
come forward to shake me by the hand as a brother unionist, and
to introduce others whom I did not know. Some of these last
were foreigners, who spoke English with difficulty. But they

86

seemed to be quite familiar with my name, and with the affairs,
military and political, in which I had been engaged, and shook me
by the hand warmly; one of them advanced to embrace me as a
brother democrat. (p. 402)

The revolutionaries are willing to work with a man known only, or
mainly, by name; Somerville’s response recalls Bamford’s: "I expressed
unwillingness to be engaged in any enterprise of that kind, with
persons whom I did not know" (p. 404). Eventually he notifies the
authorities and newspapers, by letter, about the plot — without
revealing the names of those involved. The trust the plotters placed in
aman known by name and reputation is betrayed.

ii : a structure of identifications

We can move from this analysis of the role of names, and Somerville’s
own alienated name particularly, to an examination of the structure of
the autobiography as a whole, by focussing on one particular episode.

Among the many letters Somerville received as a result of the
publicity about his case (cf. p. 306), was one from his brother Peter’
(pp. 323-4), with whom he had lost contact many years before. They
miss each other in London, but hope to meet at home in Scotland. At
Glasgow, on his way home, Somerville is met by another brother,
James, who says "that a gentleman who travelled with him from
Edinburgh, had a very strong desire to see" Somerville.

I at once put on my hat, and went with him. We entered an
apartment at the inn, where sat a personage with a weather-beaten
face, whiskered, respectably dressed - something like the mate or
master of a ship that has been on a long voyage. We bade each

ww: The Anti-Corn Law League

Clearly, the interest shown in names in the autobiography itself is part
of a wider problem for Somerville which is partly reflected in the
structure, incomplete and muddled, of the whole work. But the
importance for Somerville of names and of fusing different public and
private identities is a reflection of something wider still.

One could, for example, compare the latent structure of the
autobiography with a very familiar device in Victorian novels: the early
appearance of a ‘stranger’ who is eventually identified, in the
denouement, as the mysterious wealthy patron or long-lost relative,
parent or heir. Or one could note Thackeray’s use of various
pseudonyms to avoid being identified by the public as the single
author of such widely different works, published in periodicals of
varying prestige. It is, however, the significance of Somerville’s
connection with the ACLL that I now want to develop.

That the League was a new political phenomenon has often been
emphasised. The QOvxarterly Review even tegarded it as an
unconstitutional conspiracy, though some of its tactics were fairly
traditional. What worried contemporaries and has fascinated later
historians was, however, the League’s superb efficiency and
organisation. It was this that gave them the edge over their opposition.
What has not, however, been sufficiently stressed is that the strength
of the League came from its existence, tactically and organisationally,
in a world of names. Its early reliance on travelling propaganda
lecturers was a failure. Only when the League concentrated its energies
in a new direction did it become a significant pressure group. The

patty-political priorities.

v : aspects of the historical context

The political exploitation of names and addresses by the Anti Corn
Law League can be placed in a wider context of social and
administrative changes in the 1830s and 1840s. The Registration Act
of 1836, requiring the systematic recording of births, deaths and
matriages under secular auspices, provoked the complaint in
Parliament that by divorcing the naming of a child from its baptism,
the religious rite would be forced into disuse and oblivion. By 1851
even the religious practices of the population had become the subject
of centralised statistics, under the more extended scope of the 1851
Census. In 1835 the Merchant Seamen’s Act established the first
national register of labour, and by 1842 the names, addresses, and
characters of 258,000 seamen were listed at the Admiralty. In 1839 the
Penny Postage Act set a seal on the development of a national
network of postal correspondence. In 1837 a new method of sending a
message to a distant name and address was patented: the electric
telegraph; and by 1852 the 4,000 miles of telegraph cable in Britain
linked not only every major town but stretched across the Channel
too.

Such equipment had obvious political uses: the 1846 Act establishing
the Electric Telegraph Company included a clause reserving use of the
telegraph to the Home Secretary in periods of emergency, and during
the Kennington Common demonstration of 10th April 1848 all

94

messages for transmission had to be accompanied by a note stating:
‘Despatch the message delivered by Bearer. G. Grey’.

The development of the telegraph system was closely tied to the
initial stages to the expansion of the railways. By 1848 1,800 miles of
railway had telegraphic equipment, and the first land-sea telegram, in
1849, was sent under the direction of the South Eastern railways’
electrician. The railways, too, had obvious political uses, for the rapid
transportation of troops to "disturbed areas” - and most railway
companies employed ex-Service officers as engineers, while the official
inspectors came from the Royal Engineers.

The social and commercial effects of railway expansion, reaching its
peak in the mid-1840s, have been extensively studied, and here we need
only recall the basic figures: in 1834 about 200 miles of line were open,
by 1850 the figure was 6,500; between 1845 and 1847 8,592 miles of
railway were sanctioned by Parliament. From 1844 the amalgamations
between railway companies multiplied, and between 1840 and 1848 the
main trunk system developed from a rudimentary network to a fairly
comprehensive coverage of major towns. In 1844 the report of a
Select Committee emphasised the need for a planned national system
of railway communication serving the entire country and in the same
year the Quarterly Review described "the present system" as "developing
itself to such an extent —penetrating all districts -superseding all other
communications—affecting every species of public and private
interests, and acting as the life-blood arteries of the empire."

In Samuel Smiles’ view, in 1849, the railways had so shrunk the
effective size of the country and so eroded the distinction between
town and country that "by their means has Great Britain become as
one Great city, the streets of which are the iron roads which now
stretch across it in all directions."

When, however, the Quarterly Review compares the rail-network with
the traditional system of Roman roads and "streets", we are struck by
an interesting point. Instead of such labels for main trunk routes as
"Watling Street", the rail-network is composed of such units as "The
York, Newcastle and Berwick", "The Manchester, Sheffield and
Lincolnshire", "The London and Birmingham", "The Leicester and
Swannington", "The Bristol and Gloucester". Many of these names
became merged into amalgamated companies ("The Midland", "The
London and North Western"), but for most of the 1840s the well-
publicised railway companies indicated by their very names the
connections between distant places: the geography of England could
almost be summarised in a list of names of companies and, conversely,
the regions and towns of England became the names of familiar public
institutions.

95

Names played a minor, but again interesting, role in the other great
processes of amalgamation occurring in the 1840s, in the commercial
and financial sectors. The Bank Charter Act of 1844 centralised nearly
all note-issue, making the signature of the chief cashier of the Bank of
England almost the sole visible guarantee of national currency. Yet the
effect of this restriction was reduced by the further development of
the private cheque system in the late 1840s — one’s own signature
playing an increasing role as a form of currency.

Also in 1844, the Joint Stock Companies Registration and
Regulation Act required companies (with some exceptions) to file
"particulars of name and objects, with the names, addresses and
descriptions of the promoters" with the Registrar of Companies.
Previous Chartered Companies Acts of 1834 and 1837 had required
the naming of only two officers for liability purposes, prompting a plea
in 1837 by the Manchester Chamber of Commerce to the Board of
Trade, following Bellenden Ker’s Report on the Law of Partnership,
that a register might be kept containing the place of business, the
names, professions and addresses of every individual partner.

The practice of merely "lending" one’s name as a "sleeping partner"
helped, of course, to raise acutely the problem of limited liability in
this period, particularly after the commercial crisis of 1847. During the
crisis itself part of the credit imbalance arose from the continued
willingness of discount houses to take over semi-fictitious bills "simply
because they were guaranteed by the joint-stock bank’s signature."

These specific features of the legal, administrative, financial and
commercial developments in the 1840s all indicate ways in which a
new kind of society was emerging, characterised by an increased sense
of the unity and uniformity of the country effected through more
elaborated systems of communication and transport. Both people and
places were being connected in new ways, and personal knowledge of
both was in many cases being extended and changed. The status of
personal names was perhaps, in that context, undergoing a curious
shift, which it is difficult to recapture as an experience. It is difficult
enough to delineate accurately the extent to which broader
administrative developments during the period effected, or were meant
to effect, an increased uniformity or centralisation of government
control, as recent debate indicates.

Contemporary opinion was certainly not unanimous in either
approval or disapproval of the change which, however, was generally
recognised. In 1844, for example, Charles Buller, M.P., argued against
railway legislation because "the system of centralization tended to
make men machines instead of making them powers; it made them

96

slaves instead of free men." In 1867, Bagehot commented in the
conclusion of his The English Constitution that:

We look on State action, not as our own action, but as alien
action; as an imposed tyranny from without, not as the
consummated result of our own organised wishes. I remember at
the census of 1851 hearing a very sensible old lady say that ... if
Government might be thus inquisitorial .. what .. might they not
ask and what might they not do?

In 1861 John Stuart Mill had offered a useful distinction between
those last two questions:

The principal business of the central authority should be to give
instruction, of the local authority to apply it. Power may be
localized, but knowledge, to be most useful, must be centralized;
there must be somewhere a focus at which all its scattered rays are
collected.

In Little Dorrit (1855-57, ch. X) Dickens had, of course, created a
parody of that "focus" in the Circumlocution Office, but we can see an
earlier and actual symbol of the "revolution in government" in the
extensions to Somerset House in 1835 and 1856, to enable that
versatile Government building to house the centralised statistical
knowledge of the society. Perhaps the insurrectionaries of 1816 were
perversely prophetic when, as Somerville noted, they singled out
Somerset House as an appropriate revolutionary headquarters.
Ironically, the rebel of 1819, Samuel Bamford, became in 1851 a minor
clerk in Somerset House.

Somerville’s insertion into that new form of society can be traced
not only in his receiving numerous letters from strangers because of
the publicity given to his name, or his work for the ACLL, but also in
his explicit interest in questions of banking and national finance: he
wanted to integrate the Bank of England, taxation, the National Debt,
minting, insurance and savings-banks into "one national institution".

Such experiences and concerns are of a radically different kind from
those of the closed and local community of the Lothian family into
which he was born. In the new social world, he could no longer simply
term himself "one of my faither’s Simerels". In this new world his
name had a public existence, to be protected, written to, registered,
exploited, sold and alienated. It is that experience — the subjective side
of the social developments exemplified in the ACLL methods and the

processes traced above — that is reflected in the content and structure
of Somerville’s 1848 The Autobiography of a Working Man.

vit : Roger Mowbray

In 1853 Somerville published a short book entitled The Life of Roger
Mowbray, Merchant Prince of England, and Member of Parliament. \f we
check the names of M.P.s and merchants for the relevant dates, there
is no such ‘Roger Mowbray’, and Somerville in any case warns us that
"Mowbray" is the subject’s mother’s maiden name, and that "the real
name of her husband and children is for the present withheld, her
eldest son, Roger, being now one of the merchant princes of England,
and so eminent in the country for which he is a member of parliament
that it might be inconvenient to say more directly who he is" (p. 2).
This trick of revealing only the mother’s maiden name, while seeming
to hint at later full identification, is also used in the Autobiography.
Other names in the book seem to offer clues. "Sir John Lombard, of
London, the great banker" (p. 4), whose son eventually marries
Mowbtray’s sister, and "Mr. Vanderlint, of New York, the eminent
capitalist" (p. 103) sufficiently echo the names of actual historical
bankers and capitalists (the Lombards, the Vanderbilts), while the
information given about Samuel Slater positively invites historical
verification: born in Belper in 1768, apprenticed to Strutt in 1782,
emigrated to New York in 1789 and, in partnership with "William

102

Almey and Smith Brown", erected "the first cotton-mill on the English
models . . in the United States of America", thanks to Slater’s
experience with Strutt and Arkwright. As "the originator of cotton-
spinning in America" and "the ‘father of American manufactures’”,
Slater has a town named after him, Slaterville near Pawtucket, and
Somerville can even quote from an "American biographer (Rev. Dr. L.
Blake, of New Jersey, formerly rector of the church in Rhode Island,
of which Mr. Slater was a member)" (cf. pp. 89, 91, 95, 88, 98, 94).
The outline of Slater’s life, in its intersections with actual phases of the
cotton-trade and its connections with historical events (cf. e.g. pp. 98,
89-90) is convincing enough — except that the said "Samuel Slater",
like "Sir John Lombard" and "Mr. Vanderlint", did not in fact exist
under the name given. Yet the representativeness of their careers (like
Mowbray’s own in some respects) prompts the reader to try to identify
the real people Somerville had in mind.

The invitation is almost explicit: Somerville remarks, in connection
with Slater, "Strange are the results sometimes of a search after facts
and names" (p. 89). Other features of the book, however, clearly
denote it as fictional: a fantastic episode involving underground caves,
or the obviously ‘morality-tale’ structure of ‘goodies’ and ‘baddies’.
Names ate, however, significant in the book not just as partially
confusing its genre; there is a clear recognition of the commercial
importance of names:

The firm was now Vanderlint, Mowbray Brothers and Co., it being
deemed inexpedient to withdraw the old name entirely. But as a
considerable sum of money was to be paid annually for the use of
that name, and Roger being sensible that the major part of all the
business was now transacted at Liverpool, with Birmingham,
Sheffield and the Staffordshire Potteries, and that his name was
that which was best known in those towns, he reconstructed the
firm in 1824, as "Mowbray Brothers and Co." (p. 105)

Later commercial reasons lead to another change of name, to
"Mowbray Brothers, Vanderlint, & Co." (p. 128). The ‘bubbles’ of
1824 and 1825 affect the firm, "As name after name of projectors and
directors were discovered to be names of penniless adventurers" (p.
106), but Mowbray’s integrity is revealed in his declaration: "I am here
to discharge my brother’s liabilities of every description, upon proof of
his name being responsible — bubble scrip and all; nothing shall remain
with the name of Mowbray on it unpaid" (p. 116). He is threatened
with bankruptcy by a joint-stock bank with the Dickensian name, the
"Mersey Muddle-Puddle Joint-Stock Banking Company" (p. 117):

103

"your ‘bills were refused two days ago - your name blown upon - your
credit done for: think of what may happen to yourself, Mr. Mowbray";
but noble Roger retorts: "May my name and credit perish!" (p. 126).

Earlier, Slater’s name had been associated with the older forms of
local bank credit (". . whose name was so long familiar to American
eyes on the bank notes circulating in the eastern states", p. 88); by the
end of the book, Mowbray’s name is also a source of credit:

I tell you that there is not a breeze or tide moving in the British
seas, but carries out or floats inward some ocean-going ship of his;
that round the circle of the globe the sun never sets upon his
fleets; that in all the civilised world where bales of British
merchandise have a market, the name of Roger Mowbray passes
like gold from the Mint. (pp. 178-9)

This weak echo of the opening pages of Dombey again reminds us of
the connection between names and money, and that the passage
purports to come from a speech proposing Mowbray as an M.P.
indicates another fusion: of industrial capital with political power, a
mutation already latent in the ACLL and soon a. significant
phenomenon in English parliamentary politics.

Certain specific elements in Roger Mowbray clearly come direct from
Somerville’s own experience: the episode of the hawk, the meeting of
the brothers, the Government offer of credit to a Liverpool firm in
1837. But the point to be emphasised is that the "biography" as a
whole, in its content and structure, reflects, like the Autobiography and
Dombey and Son, a wider commercial and political context of which the
Bank Charter Act and Company Act of 1844, the repeal of the Corn
Laws in 1846, the commercial crisis of 1847 and the collapse of
Chartism in 1848 are some of the constituent elements. The
underlying connection between names and money in all three texts,
examples of different genres of writing, is also an element in that
overall situation of the 1840s and early 1850s.

viti : conclusion

We can now, in conclusion, both locate some of Somerville’s other
work briefly in that same context and return finally to his
autobiographical writings. Somerville’s collected newspaper articles of
the 1840s and his "Letters from Ireland", published as The Whistler at
the Plough in 1852, his Free Trade and the League: A Biographic History (2v.,
1853) and such short pieces as the superb A Cry From Ireland (1843) are

104

all characterised by an emphasis on the specific case, on the living
people involved. The strength of the ‘Whistler’ articles comes from the
embedding of Somerville’s economic analyses in particular conditions,
specified farms, named landowners; the history of the League is
constructed as a series of vignettes and mini-biographies of "the
pioneers of freedom of opinion, commercial enterprise, and
civilisation"; A Cry from Ireland is an individual cry, the detailed and
moving case of one particular tenant persecuted by his landlord. And,
in similar vein, Somerville finally offered his own life as an individual
example.

The 1860 Canadian edition of his autobiography has the unexpected
title: Conservative Science of Nations (Preliminary instalment) Being the first
complete narrative of Somerville’s Diligent Life in the Service of Public Safety in
Britain. It was intended in part to clear his name of vatious accusations
by a journalist and to "introduce" the author to a Canadian public after
his emigration, but it was also part of a wider propaganda for what
Somerville called "Conservative Science", the main tenet of which
asserted "that the human being is prime constituent of public wealth;
and that the guardianship of human happiness is the true function of
any Political Economy, worthy of being called a Conservative
Science."The purpose of the "Diligent Life" was "to retrieve Political
Economy from a chaos of crudities, in its austere materialism . . to
demand, that as guardian of public wealth, it obey the absolute logic of
its position, and take cognisance of the prime element of wealth, the
human being."

This view of political economy Somerville dates from 1848; he
quotes a letter from John Bright about Somerville’s proposal for a
series of tracts, and comments:

In the forgoing, Mr. Bright said: "If you succeed in convincing
them (the workers) that non-interference by law with /abour and
with prices, whether of /abour or of other commodities &c." In
these words he confounded two very different things as one. He
meant by /abour the person, — the personal health, life, and limb of
the factory worker, as well as the skill which brought forth the
products of the spindle and loom at which the worker was
employed. I separated the person of the worker from work and skill.

In this "economic heresy", as Somerville calls it, he is close, in
feeling at least, to Dickens (Paul recognises that money cannot save
the life of his mother), to Ruskin (for whom the only true wealth is life
itself) and even to Marx, who analysed both the alienation of the
worker from his own work and skill and the unique nature of labour as

105

a matket-commodity. And though Somerville was, for example,
drastically confused about the nature of capital and its specific forms,
using the same term to cover very different phenomena, his emphasis
at this point is a necessary one in political economy. In a society
increasingly grasped in abstract terms, where money dominated the
modes of economic exchange and the people as a nation were coming
to be connected as a network of mere names and addresses, to be
exploited for bureaucratic political and commercial purposes, it was
important to re-assert the value of the specific, whole, living human
person. It was precisely that point which the concentration on the
individual case in his newspaper writings was designed to make, as
vividly as possible, and it was that concern which Somerville singled
out when, in Conservative Science (p. 22) he summarised his life:

As for Radicals, Whigs, Tories, and any such party alliances, I
never was of them. Mine has not been a life of small politics.
Much of my literary life has been spent and my brain worn to
incapacity for literary labour, in rescuing the science of Political
Economy from the soulless materialism which had made it, in
mouths of Whigs and Radicals, odious to the People. It has been
my self-imposed task to humanize and christianize Political
Economy. I assert man to be the primary element in national
wealth.

The analysis earlier of his written account of his life in the
Autobiography allows us to see how that particular "literary labour"
reflected, in a curious way, some of the problems of mid-nineteenth
century society which a "soulless materialism" was only partly, and
uneasily, aware of.

Perhaps we can conclude that just as Wordsworth and Coleridge, at
the beginning of the century, finally found the most amenable and
appropriate form for their projected philosophical poem and
‘Organum vere Organum’ respectively to be an autobiographical one,
so Somerville, unable to engender an adequate work on Political
Economy in terms compatible with mid-century theory and practice,
could only finally offer the lesson of his own life instead -— as
someone who had not only whistled at the plough but had also
experienced in an acute fashion the alienating distance between public
role and private person increasingly possible in a new form of social
world.

As Somerville himself remarked: "Strange are the results sometimes of

a seatch after facts and names."

7: journeys and families’

J. D. Burn’s life seems, from his Awtobiography of a Beggar Boy (1855) to
have been one of constant movement, first with his mother and
stepfather, as a travelling beggar and itinerant trader (e.g. p. 16), then,
after breaking with his parents (pp. 48-50), as a wanderer in search of
work: 54 miles in one day (p. 101), 62 miles another day (p. 84), 300
miles with only 2/3’/sd to support him (p. 98), 1,400 miles for a new
job (p. 119). Even after his marriage, he travels: for the Oddfellows
Society (e.g. p. 145) or for trade journals (e.g. p. 175). In his sixties he
uptoots his family and spends three years in America (cf. TY and
1882, pp. 256-349). In his seventies he finds stable employment for
ten years, the longest continuous job of his life - but, almost
symbolically, as a clerk with the Great Eastern Railway Company
(1882, p. 442). So constant is the impression of movement in the
autobiography that the reader is surprised at Burn’s claim to have lived
in and around Glasgow for nearly twenty years (p. 163).

The trite description of life as a "journey" (p. 20) has for him a
specific accuracy. He can wryly accept his affinities with the
"Wandering Jew" (p. 135) and, looking back, claim to have been "one
in five hundred thousand, if not a million, of old George the Third’s
subjects who had made the grand tour of England and Scotland!! " (p.
177). Though at this point he "flatters himself" with that claim, other
reactions are at times equally strong: he can "be ashamed" of his
unstable position (e.g. p. 84), or recognise that his vagrant past makes
others suspicious of him (p. 104), but occasionally he argues that if he
"had adopted the motto of Ne sutor ultra crepidam ... you would not have
had the benefit of my enlarged experience" (p. 174).

The motto was, in any case, inappropriate: Burn at first had no trade
to abandon, and his travels were not only territorial He moved
through one occupation after another: in rapid succession, for
example, tinker, miner, farm-labourer, smuggler, harvester, shearer,
office-boy (pp. 83-4). When he finally served the apprenticeship he
longed for, it was the "second grand crisis" of his life (p. 104) and
always looked back to as "the grand turning-point" (p. 198), but
though he became a time-served hatter, that trade rapidly underwent
basic changes (p. 144; cf. 1882, suppl. p. 508), restricting his chances
of employment, and again he turned to a variety of jobs, for example
dock-labouring and freelance lecturing within the same week,
meanwhile selling copies of his own History of Odd Fellowship (Glasgow,
1846) to meet immediate needs (p. 165).

The social disparity between the personae he adopted on that last
occasion highlights the tension he constantly tried to overcome: his

108

repeated aim was to "be looked upon as an honest member of society"
(p. 82), to find a "resting-place" that he "might become a recognisable
member of society" (p. 83). One can trace the roots of this in part to
his early experience of isolation even within his own family - an urge
to be accepted that was never quite met: he describes himself as a
"stranger" (pp. 41,82) in his own family - or, rather, families, for he
was, he says ironically, "blessed with three fathers and two mothers"
(p. 82); his mother and step-father transferred him to his real father
and the latter’s new wife, in Ireland (p. 39), and on meeting his mother
again he finds her with a third ‘husband’ (p. 82). He recalls being
kidnapped by a stranger, and comments that his "affectionate mother",
in refusing to accept £100 in exchange for him, "stood between me
and the flood which leads on to fortune" (p. 24) - which hardly
communicates an enthusiastic sense of belonging to his original family.
He remembers his mother and stepfather casually transferring him to a
strange family for three months, and his sense of being "cast adrift
upon the world" (p. 37). His own break with his (Irish) family is
equally casual: sent out to gather turf, he finds himself on the main
road to Belfast and, "after standing reflecting for a few minutes",
"turned my face towards Belfast and my back to a friendless home",
from that time "tearing two syllables from my name" to "sever the
only remaining link that bound me to my family" (p. 47). His relation
to his real father had in any case been one of hatred, even before
knowing him, to such an extent that he records later wanting him to be
drowned (p. 43). The ramifications of these family relationships could
be tracked further - in the emphasis for example that he later placed
on his own domestic life (e.g. p. 113) -but they are also part of a wider
pattern, with various facets.

ii: a sense of class

Burn’s comment about being a "stranger" in his own family, for
example, can be seen as one instance of a general theme in his work,
for the notion of "stranger" occurs often, sometimes with a positive
value - being helped and befriended by total strangers (pp. 88, 97,
101) - or, in a later work, as the character he gives his imagined reader,
a stranger to be introduced to the towns described by Burn (CE., pp.
57,62,69,101,117, etc.). The impression given in that later work, that
Burn is intimately and confidently familiar with a variety of towns,
their history, people, firms, trades, geography, etc, and is therefore able
to act as guide for his reader, is noticeably different from his
description of his first visit to London, which communicates a sense
of frightened isolation: his reaction to this new experience is to

109

"wander along the dull, hazy streets" for two days and nights.

Through the course of the second day, I continued walking on,
and sometimes unconsciously found myself at the same place
from which I set out. I had no aim, and must have been looking
for a miracle. The second night came, and I shivered along the
long, cold, dreary streets [passing] young men, haggard and
prematurely old, creeping along the streets in genteel rags.

Burn describes himself on that occasion as "wandering to leave time
behind [him] in the loud silence of the night in the largest city in the
world" (p. 96). The disturbing novelty of the experience is felt in the
uncharacteristically tangled mixture of metaphors:

I soon found out a mystery which has been solved by thousands
before me; namely, that London was far too large for me. I was
fairly lost in a wilderness of human beings; I was a mere atom in a
huge mountain of humanity I and as it were an unclaimed particle
of animation - a thing that belonged to nobody. In fact, I looked
upon myself as one of the outside links in the chain of civilised
society. If I could have become a part of the monument
somebody would have looked at me, and have set their wits to
find out my use. (pp. 95-96)

This is the same kind of experience that fascinated Charles Dickens,
to whom the 1855 autobiography is dedicated. The astounding extent
to which Burn later made himself familiar with minute aspects of
English (and .American) society seems part of a continuing attempt to
feel ‘at home’ in a world in which he inherited no firm place. The
supplement to the 1882 edition of the Awsobiography. for example, gives
micro-histories of envelopes, buttons, farming tools, needles, bicycles,
tinned food, shop-windows, advertising, photography, as well as more
obvious features such as railways and population changes.(1882,
suppl. pp. 510, 512, 519, 523, 537, 540, 545, 547, 589, 530)

This lack of an inherited "place" (p. 83) is not simply geographical
(national as well as local: he was born of Irish parents in Scotland, yet
lived often in England, with three years in America), or a matter of
having no secure trade. It also includes a shifting sense of which social
group he belongs to: in 1858 he presented himself as both a working
man and an employer (CE., pp. 98-99); in 1865 he described himself as
"one of their own order" when speaking of "the working classes"
(TY., p. ix); in A Gémpse. published after 1867 (cf. G., pp. iti, 77) but

not otherwise dated, he remarks in the context of comments on Trade

110

Unions that "however unpalatable my remarks may be, they are given
with the honest intention to serve the class of which I am a humble
member" (G., p. 124) and later he claims that he has been discussing
issues "from a working man’s stand-point of view" (G., p. 153); in
1882 he saw himself as neither working-class nor with the financial
means to be above that class (1882., p. 243). Nevertheless, in the
autobiography of 1855, he is lucidly conscious that there are class
differences which permeate most aspects of life, and that those from
one class can rarely penetrate the sense of life felt by those from
another:

Like a large number of my own class, I was born in poverty,
nursed in sorrow, and reared in difficulties, hardships and
ptivations. It is only such as have passed through the various
substrata of civilised society who can justly appreciate the feelings
and sufferings of the thousands who continually live as it were by
chance. ... The man who can dine is very differently situated to
the poor wretch who, after he has had one meal of victuals, has no
idea where or when he may be blessed with another! Those
members of society who are blessed with a regular supply of food
and raiment may be said to be antipodes to the accidental feeders,
and their modes of thinking, in every sense of the word, as
opposite as are their ways of living! (p. 2; cf. p. 128)

It is in the context of an "accidental feeding" that Burn later quotes a
"good old gentleman" who, "soliloquising to himself . . said, ‘Poor
bairn! Poor bairn! One-half of the world does not know how the other
half lives!" (p. 54). Perhaps with such gentlemen in mind as his
readers. Burn elsewhere remarks: "If you will imagine to yourself a
number of people obliged to live upon short allowance of food, and
forced to receive an additional member without a corresponding
addition of victuals, you will be able to form a pretty correct idea of
my feception in the congenial home of my father" (p. 39). Another
well-meaning gentleman’s efforts receive the comment: "As a proof
that the colonel had no idea of how we were living he purchased me a
splendid suit of clothes . . Poor old man! he little thought he was
dressing me like a puppet for the charity market!" (p. 19).

The difference between groups is not confined to material
circumstances or financial means (Burn frequently notes his own
wages, e.g. 1/- a week - p. 105, and also details election expenses
totalling £190,000 -p. 110); it extends to their moral codes: "I have
found that nearly every class of people in the kingdom have a moral
code of their own" - including pickpockets (p. 17). But in the case of

111

morals, argues Bum, the difference covers a basic similarity: "I have
seen a beggar barter his wife for a pot of ale, and I have known a
nobleman who got clear of his better half for 40s damages" (p. 17).
Fortunately, according to Burn, it is goodness that is common,
ultimately: "Although I have had to fight my way through a busy
world, where all classes of society were continually engaged in looking
after their own affairs, 1am happy to bear my humble testimony to the
general diffusion of that god-like feeling which so closely allies man to
his Creator", sympathetic charitableness (p. 51).

To some extent, Burn analyses political differences in the same way,
except that charitableness is a hope expressed rather than a fact
described. He comments on the agrarian "excitement" of 1830, and
though he calls "midnight incendiary" activity "worse than madness"
and an ambush on special constables "diabolical" and "infernal", he
prefaces his condemnation with the explanation: "When large bodies
of men are reduced to suffering from the want of even common
necessaries of life, it cannot be supposed, that they are in a condition
to be reasoned with by men whose stomachs are well lined" (p. 120).
Comparing the Luddism of 1830 with that of 1819, he elaborates
further: "Let us suppose either the middle or the upper classes in
society exposed to the pangs of hunger, without the means of relief,
while surrounded by abundance, - I would ask what would they do?"
The rhetorical question is left unanswered; Bum draws only a moral
and philosophical generalisation, that "man’s nature will ever remain
the same, and that his conduct will be regulated by the various
circumstances of his position" (p. 121). In_.A G/impse, this emphasis on

This analysis, in terms of selfishness, is clearly a political as well as

iui : political involvement and attitudes

We can analyse in some detail Burn’s reactions to two political
moments, the 1832 Reform Act and Chartism, since he treats of both
at some length in the autobiography. Describing the 1832 Reform
agitation, he begins his account with: "In this year (1831) the agitation

112

for a reform in the House of Commons was gathering strength over
the whole of Great Britain" (p. 123). The spread of agitation is not
only geographical; every social class is represented: the numerous
meetings "though composed generally of the working classes, were
supported by several members of the aristocracy, many of the liberal
gentlemen, and more especially by large numbers of the influential
merchants and manufacturers." In a passage omitted in the 1882
edition, he then analyses the pre-Reform Act situation as one in which
the "good old self-willed Tories", with their control of "power and
patronage" - a power "frequently used to crush the rights and liberties
of the people" - were opposed by the Whigs, "numerically strong" with
"considerable moral power" and heartily supported by "the wording
population", yet lacking the "machinery" wielded by the Tories (p.
123). Against this background. Burn sketches the stages of his own
involvement: he was elected hatters’ delegate to the Trades Committee
of Western Scotland, then to the Radicals’ "Central Committee";
becoming "seized with a wild enthusiasm, and for a time politically
mad", he "took an active part in all the proceedings of both the Whig
and the Radical parties in Glasgow for several years" (p. 124), with
"rarely a single night in the week . . . not occupied, either in sub-
committees, or on the general committee" (p. 125). After six months
he was "elected a member of the standing committee of the Reform
Association", composed of "the resident gentry, merchants and
manufacturers of the Whig party" (p. 126). During 1832 he "had the
matshalling of the whole of [the] out-door displays" in Glasgow,
including one demonstration involving "200,000 people". The way
Burn envisages his role at this time is interesting: he speaks of the
Trades Committee as "an excellent school for young beginners in the
science of oratory and public debating" (p. 124) and of the
demonstrations as "a display of self-possessed determination", marked
with "solemn import" rather than "boisterous mirth" (p. 126); on the
largest meeting he comments "I was somewhat afraid that the leaders
had raised a power they could not subdue" (p. 127). In other words,
the mode of politics he at heart accepts is that of the debating
chamber and committee, with occasional emphasis given to a position
by massive numerical support, that support, however, hopefully under
the control of "leaders" who, initially "elected", are finally seen as
those who have "raised" the masses in support and ought to be able to
"subdue" them.

Given this version of politics, it is no surprise that Burn later
objected to a mode of political agitation that threatened to go beyond
debating-chamber forms, the Chartist movement. Before then he had
in any case, it seems, become somewhat disillusioned with Reform:

113

immediately after the Reform Act he campaigned on behalf of Sir D.
K. Sandford, who failed as a Whig candidate at Glasgow and was then
elected Tory member for Paisley, to make only one speech in the
Commons before being "crushed" into parliamentary silence by Daniel
O’Connell (p. 12). Omitting this incident in the 1882 edition of the
autobiography, Burn retained his generalised comment: "Since then
the country has had frequent opportunities of being amused at the Jim
Crow gyrations of high-sounding politicians" (p. 128). A more specific
judgement, however, closer in tone to Chartist accusations, he simply
excised in 1882: "After the passing of the Reform Bill the Whigs soon
assumed all the airs of the old Tory party, and their political conduct
was characterised, in many instances, by greater deception and
tergiversation than ever marked the conduct of their opponents" (p.
130). Despite this, in the General Election of 1837, he was again active
in the Glasgow Radical Association, writing on its behalf to William
Aytoun, Arthur Roebuck and Perronet Thompson, but the election
machinations further disillusioned him. Dr. John Taylor, whom he had
known since 1832 (p. 128), was suspected of accepting Tory overtures
to split the ‘Liberal’ vote, and Bum was involved in an acrimonious
public meeting where, before 5,000 people, he successfully defended
himself against counter-accusations from Taylor (p. 131). The fracas
alienated the Radical clientele of the tavern Burn owned, and he was
deflected into financial and other domestic problems.

By the next phase of Chartism, Burn’s first wife had died and he had
matried again within a year; he had also transferred his tavern business
from Glasgow to Greenock. The chronology of his account is slightly
puzzling, but what is important is his attitude. He is initially cautious:

"While I was in Greenock I had in some measure identified myself
with the Charter movement, but up to that time the agitation had
been conducted upon something like rational principles" (p. 137).

When, however, he describes the "People’s Parliament" and its plan
for a "sacred month" (a national strike) his language is virulent:
"satanic", "hellish suggestion", "infamy", "infatuation". He summarises
his speech from the chair of a meeting in Greenock, in which he
proclaimed that "one of the immediate consequences . . would be, to
let loose the whole vagabondage of the country, who would rob,
plunder, and murder the innocent and defenceless members of
society" - for saying which, he "had the pleasure of being branded
with the character of a renegade and a traitor to the cause" (pp. 137-8).
Yet, magnanimously, he does not blame the "working classes", only
the "mercenary horde" and "set of hungry knaves" who were the

114

"majority" of the National Convention. His particular disgust is
reserved for O’Connor’s Land Plan (p. 138), possibly reflecting in this
the direct experience or disappointment of a man who had lived in the
earliest two of the four Scottish ‘Districts’ in that plan. In sharp
contrast to his praise for the debating workmen of the 1832 Trades
Committee, the leaders in the Chartist movement are nearly all
dismissed with the comment: "if these men were honest, they must
have been mad, and if not mad, no conduct could have been more
infamous" (p. 138); only John Collins, whom Burn knew, William
Lovett and Henry Vincent escape censure - and Collins is, noticeably,
described as "respectable" (p. 139).

It is that "respectable" that gives the clue. Burn was, on his own
account, no minor figure in local working-class politics: his tavern in
Glasgow seems to have been a Radicals’ gathering-place (cf. above),
and it was "some political acquaintances" who persuaded him to re-
open a tavern in Greenock; in 1837 he can "call a meeting of the
Liberal party for the following evening" which 5,000 people attend (p.
131); at the Greenock meeting he "was pressed to take the chair" (p.
137); he was active also in Trade Union circles, first at local level, then
as Glasgow delegate to various national meetings of the Journeymen
Hatters’ union (pp. 128-9). He can not only claim to have known the
leaders of the great Glasgow Cotton Spinners Strike of 1837 but to be
"personally acquainted with many of the men whose names figured in
these exciting times" (p. 137), including the national Charter leaders.
Yet, writing in 1854-5, he heavily criticises these acquaintances and
even in 1882, when his attacks on the Tories and Whigs are toned
down or omitted, he revises his onslaught on the working class leaders
only to the extent of an explanatory footnote on "sacred month".

There are, indeed, traces of a tension in his attitude: one feels, in his
criticism of O’Connor’s Land Plan, that if the scheme had succeeded,
Burn’s opinion might have been different (p. 138), and something of
the same sense lingers over his comments on the "intended emeute of
1848": "The leaders upon that occasion were utterly contemptible, and
are not worthy a place in the history of small political events, or even
to be named with the insane but honest leaders at Bonny Muir. At all
events, they were not like Ossian’s heroes, ‘who never court the battle,
nor shun it when it comes’" (p. 139). But by 1855 he was explicitly on
the side of the "middle classes": the fifth chapter or "Letter" (cf.
below, sec. vi), concerned in part with Peterloo, ends with a eulogy on
"the extraordinary energy and directing power" of the "middle class
element" which "during the last thirty years" "have attained for it a
moral force unprecedented in the history of the country", so that
"whatever social advantages we now enjoy over those of the preceding

115

age, are in great measure due to the well-timed exertions of this now
powerful class" (p. 79). It is, then, no surprise to find, in the
penultimate, reflective Letter, that Burn erects a defence of hereditary
property and argues that more equitable distribution of wealth would
be detrimental to society; the haziness of his notion of class at this
point is clear when he further remarks that "it matters very little
whether the riches be held by one class or another, inasmuch as there
will always be a select few who will possess great wealth"; one’s real
consolation, he urges, is to be found in "friendship" and "the human
family" (pp. 184-5).

This support for middle class efforts and values has to be seen partly
in the light of the particular configuration of Glasgow Chartism during
these years. As in other Chartist centres, but perhaps more acutely,
there was a two-fold struggle within Glasgow Chartism, over the issue
of moral force versus physical force and over the role of middle class
allies.

These issues tended to become confused, but with no simple
demarcation of middle class Chartists supporting only moral force
policies; only rarely, however, as in the great demonstration of 21st
May 1838, was there a simple fusion of all the elements. Burn’s
particular hostility to O’Connor and his horror at violence, even
industrial violence, would seem to place him despite his friendship
with Taylor, the Scottish Feargus - on the side of the conciliatory
middle class groups who opposed O’Connor, and his approach to
Roebuck in 1837 and membership of John Bright’s Parliamentary
Election Committee in 1852 (p. 175) would support this interpretation.
So too, indirectly, would his concentration, from 1839 onwards, on the
Oddfellows Society rather than on political activity; pre-occupation
with ‘social’ efforts and co-operative and friendly societies was in any
case characteristic of one strand of Glasgow Chartism, a strand that
Burn certainly regarded as perfectly "respectable" (cf. p. 141).

ww : respectability?

The context for this pro-middle class attitude is also, of course,
personal; as was noted above, his autobiography reveals a constant
urge for a respectable "place" in society. A direct connection between
this personal urge and his political activities interestingly affects the
final section of his autobiography. He writes:

I am now about introducing you to the last scene in the shifting
drama of my truly chequered life, up to the present time. In the
month of May, 1853, I was offered employment from a gentleman

116

to whom I had been recommended by a mutual friend. The
conditions of the engagement offered were more liberal than I had
been accustomed to for some time; I was therefore not slow in
accepting the offer. The character of the business was perfectly
new to me; but I had every confidence in being equal to it, and
have since justified my own anticipations, and the expectations of
my employer. Since I have been in my present business, I have
travelled over the most of England and Scotland, and have
therefore passed over many of those scenes that were once
familiar to me; and have had many opportunities of comparing the
past with the present state of things. (p. 175)

In the last paragraph of the book he again alludes to the "present
business":

"For the last two years, I have held a situation of considerable
responsibility, during that time I have come in contact with many
of the first class commercial men in the United Kingdom" (p.
200).

What is interesting is that the nature of this "present business" is never
disclosed. A reasonable initial presumption is that Burn is preserving his
anonymity. But he also makes his employer anonymous - "a gentleman”.
The 1832 edition, however, adds the name "Mr. Hill" (1882., p. 220).
Later, it is mentioned that Mr. Hill was once editor of the Northern Star
(1882., p. 254). The supplementary survey of social improvements, etc.,
in that edition deals at one point with "trade protection" societies and
businesses, and mentions that from 1838 "a weekly list of Scottish
registered protest bills, applications for sequestrations, trust deeds, etc.,
was published in Edinburgh, and supplied to subscribers only." This
Black List was compiled by "Mr. William Hill" (1882, suppl, p. 555),
who in 1853 (the date of Burn’s employment by "Mr. Hill") "added the
English lists of weekly mercantile defalcations to his Scottish
publication, and opened offices in London, Dublin, and several large
towns"; this "new Mercantile Gazette was charged two guineas a year, and
when the debt-collecting was added the charge was three guineas"; this
"system of espionage” was eventually sold to a Mr. Stubbs in 1860
(1882, suppl, pp. 555, 554,556). With this further information one
suspects a rather different motive for not mentioning the nature of his
"present business" in 1855. In the final pages of the autobiography Burn
is claiming to have achieved his goal of respectability: he emphasises that
"My great struggle in the battle of life was to find my proper position in
society" (p. 198) and concludes "You have now before you an honest

history of my life up to the present time" from which the reader can
learn "what energy and determination ... are able to accomplish" (p.
199). This sounds impressive - but one wonders how many of his
contemporary readers would have accepted an agent of a "system of
espionage” and "debt-collecting" as "respectably" employed. Moreover,
his employer had not only, as the Rev. ‘William Hill, once edited the
Northern Star but had in 1842 been arrested for "uttering seditious

v : hovering between styles

Burn’s political opinions and his lack of a secure social position ate now
adequately clear, and we have seen one instance where both play a part
in affecting the autobiography. A detailed examination of the general
characteristics of his style also reveals a reflection of his social and
perhaps political position. One immediately noticeable feature of his
style is the constant use of deliberately misapplied words, most
frequently adjectives chosen to indicate their opposite, for ironic or
other purposes. Some examples are simple: the intended sense is clear,
and the effect is mildly wry or humorous, as in the various anecdotes
related on pages 26 - 27.

After a minor quarrel with Burn, his brother "had sufficient tact to
make the most of it in representing the matter to his father, as he knew I
should be well punished"; in fact, his father slashed at Burn three times
with a long Spanish knife on hearing the complaint. Immediately
following this, Burn relates how on another occasion, while enjoying a
truant swim, he "behind the gaunt form of my dear father, with a smile
of satisfactory vengeance curling about his mouth", striding towards
him; Burn runs away, naked, with father in pursuit; he comments:
"Before I could reach home I had to run fully three-quarters of a mile,
and to make the journey pleasantly exciting, one-half of the distance was
through the leading street in the town." In these cases it is obvious what
linguistic values are being overturned. But when Burn writes of his
foster father that his "health and constitution had been sacrificed before
the altar of patriotism and glory in the Peninsular war" (p. 3) the tone
hovers between sarcasm and piety. A somewhat similar hesitancy is

118

provoked in the reader when Burn, himself a beggar boy, describes
those beggars proficient in: heart-tugging stories and personae as
"artistes" who were "no mean ornaments to their honourable
profession" (p. 9). The exact tone of this is indecipherable, but it is
similar to later descriptions of begging, the intention of which is slightly
less implicit, for example: "The begging trade, with its gross deceptions

whether they left the business from conscientious scruples, or from a
feeling of independence, I really do not know, but . . we became
transformed into respectable travelling merchants, or . . pedlars" (p. 25),
or, of an overnight stay in Ely, "while there I had a very flattering
invitation to join two genteel young men in their regular cadging trade,
both of whom had successfully passed their probation in the profession.
After we had gone to bed . . the two youths fully initiated me into the
mysteries of their business" (p. 99). The choice of middle-class terms
("begging trade", "left the business", "respectable travelling merchants",
"profession", "business"; for what are decidedly not middle-class
"businesses" simultaneously hints a question-mark as to the difference
between "respectable" and disreputable ways of earning a living, and
suggests sufficient irony to allow the middle-class reader his instinctively
contemptuous dismissal. That the choice of vocabulary is deliberate
seems clear from the immediate switch, in the continuation of the last
passage, to blatantly slang expressions, "guod... tin ... dodge ... cove ...
nabbed", all italicized and with a parenthetical translation ("gaol") added
for "quod". ‘The relative uncertainty of tone and the vocabulary shift
both indicate Burn’s own uneasy position between two social worlds,
two sets of values.

A different context for a similar device is given in his description of

facetiously denominated Peg Walker. from the peculiar cohesive

uses middle-class phrases to expose, and challenge, middle-class
assumptions - for example, "When I retired from business, it was into
