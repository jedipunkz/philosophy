It keeps the sociology — that beliefs are installed, that institutions serve the

the difference between a politics that widens what a person can see and
returns the choice to him, and a politics that decides it knows his true
interest better than he does and acts on that knowledge against his will. The
first is reform. The second is the road that ran, in the twentieth century, to
the famine and the camp.

The premise is the doctrine of false consciousness — the claim that
beneath the self a person actually has, with its actual loyalties and its actual
loves, there lies a truer self with a truer interest, which the person cannot see
because the institutions of his world have veiled it from him; and that
someone who can see it is therefore entitled, even obliged, to strip away the
veil, by force if need be, and set the true self free. This paper argues that
there is no such self under the formation. Formation is not a veil over a self;
it is the self — the values a person weighs by, the field of what he can see, the
community he turns to for how to live. Strip it away and you do not reveal a
free rational worker who was trapped inside. You bereave a man, and leave
him standing in the wreckage of his world while you tell him he has been
liberated. And the one who does the stripping is never the impartial

liberator he believes himself to be; he is a man with his own formation and

Beneath class consciousness, holding it up, is false consciousness — the
doctrine that the workers have not woken because they have been put to
sleep: that the institutions of their world, the church and the school and the
press and the nation, have installed in them a false picture of who they are
and where their interest lies, so that they mistake a counterfeit self, handed
to them by their oppressors, for their real one. The Catholic worker thinks
he is a Catholic; the German worker thinks he is a German; the Jewish
worker thinks he is a Jew — and each is wrong, on this account, in the
deepest possible way, because each has taken an identity manufactured to
bind him to the order for the true identity, worker, that would set him
against it. False consciousness is what explains the missing class
consciousness. It is the floor on which the whole edifice rests, and it is, as we
shall see, where the edifice cracks.

And from the two together is drawn the third idea, which is not a claim
about the world but a licence to act upon it: the vanguard. The reasoning is
short and, granted its premises, almost irresistible. If the worker has a true
interest he cannot see, and if his blindness is the work of institutions
designed to keep him blind, then he cannot be expected to free himself —
the very faculty he would need has been disabled by the people who profit

from disabling it. Someone, therefore, who is not blind, who has seen

So the three are one structure, and the structure has a load-bearing
member. Class consciousness is the roof; the vanguard is the use to which
the building is put; but false consciousness is the floor, and everything
stands on it. This is why the argument of this paper concentrates its whole

show that there is no true self beneath the formed one, no veiled rational
worker waiting under the Catholic and the German and the Jew. For if there
is no such self, then false consciousness has nothing to be false about —
there is no truer consciousness it is failing to be — and the floor is gone.
And when the floor is gone the roof falls with it: class consciousness has

It gets out at a station the Marxist rides straight past, and there are three
things it leaves on the train. The first is the truth of the belief: The method’s
discipline is to analyse how a belief functions — what it holds up, what it
makes cheap, whom it binds — and never to rule on whether it is true,
because how a belief functions and whether it is true are two different
questions and running them together is the characteristic Marxist error. A
belief can be sincerely held, load-bearing in an order, and true, all at once;
that it props up an arrangement says nothing whatever about its truth. The
Jew’s Judaism may be doing real work in the social order he lives in and be,
for all the method can say, exactly true; that it functions as part of an order
does not make it a distortion, a veil, a lie. Marx’s false consciousness, by
contrast, is an error theory from the ground up: the belief is not merely
functional, it is false, a misperception of reality measured against the reality

the theorist claims to see clearly. The method never makes that move, never
