Sex, Lies, and Philosophy
A crash test for Kantian ethics

“...not that you lied to me, but that I no longer believe you.”
~ Friedrich Nietzsche, Beyond Good and Evil

“For the value of life, in so far as it consists of the enjoyment we get from people, is vastly overrated.”
~ wrote Immanuel Kant to Maria von Herbert.

“If you want to have love in your life, you’d better be prepared to tell some lies and to believe some lies.”
~ Clancy Martin

Is telling the truth always good? Is lying always bad? Is there something in between, something
not quite good but still permissible? Do circumstances matter? Does the relationship between the
liar and the lied to matter?

We will ask whether we have a duty not to lie in all circumstances? Kant was especially
concerned with this question as were, and are, his critics.' He is, in fact, hardly alone among
philosophers in taking this hard line stance. We will take it for granted that generally it is wrong
to tell falsehoods—all moral theories agree about that. But some ethical theories allow for
principled exceptions and some do not. We will focus on the most influential of the latter
theories, Kantianism or deontology,* and specifically on one of its most controversial fallouts:
that lying is always morally impermissible.

Kant and lying

Kantianism in its purest form prohibits the telling of falsehoods by one rationally autonomous
entity to another categorically. It does so because of the disrespect the act shows to what is
deemed the highest and most respect-worthy aspect of a rationally autonomous being. To be
autonomous is to be capable of making reason-informed decisions and acting on them, thus
“owning” them or taking responsibility for them. To treat someone so capable with respect
entails giving them truthful information so that they can then act fully aware of what they are

This preemption is the worst thing one rationally
autonomous agent (as such) might do to another. It is the
essence of immorality for a Kantian.

Behind that conclusion is a metaphysics. It goes something
like this: not all entities in the universe are moral
participants. Inanimate objects are not. Living things with
demonstrable interests such as plants and animals arguably
are to some degree’ but not as full participants. They might
be passive moral actors in the sense that full moral actors
such as adult rational humans may have conditional duties to
promote their well-being, conditional on some preservation
or enhancement of their own dignity as rational beings. But
there can be no reciprocal duties with passive moral actors or patients. Such moral patients do
not owe anything back. Only fully rational autonomous agents have unconditional duties to treat
each other in respectful ways. This is because respecting rational agency is what Kantian ethics
is all about. Such rationally-enabled entities are distinct from all other natural entities. Full on
morality applies only to them.

Lying prevents the very possibility of morality happening between two rational agents.

What is a lie?

But what exactly constitutes lying? Does it mean we are obligated to tell the truth, even the
whole truth, to others? (Assuming we know it and, if we don’t, are we under some obligation to
find it out in order to tell it?) Might telling the truth be too much to ask of us in some
circumstances? What are those circumstances? Or is it rather who we are, not quite circumstance,
that ultimately sets limits on what is permissible?

3. There are Kant-inspired ways of extending moral regard to other living things beyond patient-status to lesser

Agent-centered moral theories like deontology tend to interpret the act of lying as one with the
intent to deceive. This is not the same as not telling the truth. I may do any number of things that
do not convey the truth but are not intended to deceive (e.g., act in a play). Among them: I may
choose to withhold part of the truth or even the whole of it without saying anything positive
about what the truth is. Perhaps I am aware of how you might take it, perhaps not; but
whichever, I am not making false declarations. We call this reticence. Is reticence just as bad as
lying? It might be.

Are some questions as immoral as some answers?

An answer responds to a question. If some answers can be immoral for lack of respecting the
rational dignity of the inquirer, may some questions fail to be ethical for a corresponding lack of
proper regard for the inquiree? Or if not, why not? Since it is supposed to be all about respect for
a Kantian...

Reticence seemes to be morally permissible for Kant. In fact, it seems to act as his escape from
certain dilemmas. Kant scholar J. E. Mahon thinks that there is a moral difference between
reticence and lying. Other philosophers such as Rae Langton‘ think the distinction is overblown
or in the end morally irrelevant. She suggests that at least in some contexts to appeal to that
distinction to excuse less than full disclosure is disingenuous, and disingenuousness can be
tantamount to lying. I will argue that the distinction may or not be relevant. Reticence may not
be a moral option in some situations. Just when you need a moral way out of a Kantian dilemma
it may fail. It will depend on what is at stake, which, in turn, will depend on the nature of the
relationship between between the liar and lied to, which in turn may be affected by the kinds of
beings they are, by a change in their metaphysics.

I think what she learned—or should have learned—from her correspondence with Kant was that
whatever his moral philosophy had to offer it was beside the point as far as her predicament was

Here’s the deal: morality is all about respecting rational autonomy—presumably the rational
autonomy of individuals who are members of a larger community of such beings. Among the
relations that subtend among individuals in that community are a great many that can be
sustained only by respecting the separateness or individuality of the other with only his own or
her moral record to account for. Kant’s moral theory acquits itself admirably here. It has these
relations pretty well covered with its concern for impartiality and universality. Morality can be
formulated in terms of rights and duties.

But some relations of great normative significance do not fit this picture of right relations. Not
all specifically human relations with their animality intact can even in theory” be brought under
the Kantian picture.

Behind Kant’s morality is a plausible assumption that we are separate independent autonomous
beings who may choose or not choose to enter into agreements with other such beings. Good
information is necessary to wise choices. A respectful recognition that we are rationally
autonomous beings makes sense if we conceive of ourselves in the abstract Kantian realm of
