Book the First

All philosophy is a search for the unum in quo totum continetur.
Throughout the history of human thought this impulse has taken on
and been known by a myriad of names: Schopenhauer’s "will",
Kant’s "noumena", Plato’s "ideas", and, most generally— the word
"God". What has "God" meant? Either too many things or nothing
at all. Often, nothing more than an abstraction, a focal point, a
fancy of expression. Other times, not too infrequently, "God" has
meant something far more sinister: the total destruction of natural
laws, the overpower of will, the control over the whole material
universe. What remains true, however, is that the God of the
philosophers is not that of the poets, and is in fact far closer to that
of the people— though far sicker, far more unnatural. It takes a
certain degree of sick logic to assume the existence of a God
whose existence entails the baseline of the world. In the end,
despite the hate which the ancilla theologiae held towards
mysticism, it cannot be denied that the latter instituted the former’s
ideals in their truest form. The sufferings of St. John are merely the
result of the logic of the theologians — what they undertook with

the pen he undertook with the dagger of his heart — his blood has

the divine love for God to bless or blame. But perhaps no Saint of
any kind formulated this ideal, this twisted ideal, better than the
"pagan" Saint Plotinus through "The One". What is this wish for
God? Has it not been the wish for this "One"? And what is this
wish for "The One"? Has it not been the wish for control over the
entire material and spiritual universe, in the same way a child plays
with his clock to change time? The wish for total power by
returning to the womb? "The narrow path"? "The center"? Oh
sancta stultitia! But what am I even talking about? Was this not
meant to be an essay on Shestov? And salvation? What is
salvation? 7i eotiv owtnpia; I promise, my friends, it will all make

sense very shortly...
II

Who is Lev Shestov? I believe I had not heard his name, save for a
brief mention in Camus’ ghastly Myth of Sisyphus, before I
stumbled upon his great work: The Good in the Teachings of
Nietzsche and Tolstoy. | distinctly recall that fateful day of winter
where I, by most fanciful whim, decided to find parallels in the
thought of Nietzsche and Tolstoy. Whether this was part of a
project of thought or not I forgot later, for what I found perplexed
me, stunned me, sucked me in from the first few lines, bringing my

heart to its realms of profoundest doubt— in short, I forgot about

everything. I was struck with a deep sense of gravity unlike few
times before, and I saw here an attempt, above all, to understand
myself. This nonsensical suffering; where does it come from? I am
not speaking of suffering in general, but of this specific,
nonsensical suffering. Tolstoy and Nietzsche had wondered the
same, they had experienced the same— hallelujah, I am not
insane! But the way out? There was no way out. They began
preaching, teaching, manufacturing doctrines— but it all left them
unsatisfied, it all left them back where they had begun, only worse,
as a man who descends from the heights of intoxication. And so all
their toils were failures, all their efforts wasted, all their works in
vain. Terrible, terrible thought! But it gave me some comfort, for
this meant that I was not wrong in rejecting all these toils, that I,
indeed, needed something more. But enough about myself! What
nonsense, really. Was this not supposed to be a study in rhetoric?
Well then, let us first, in order to study rhetoric, get rid of it all

together!
Il

Well, did these men achieve something, or was it all truly just in
vain? About Nietzsche I am not sure, but Tolstoy’s inner life
torments me to this day. The impossibility of man to change his

fate — what fate? — that of the soul. Throughout his Good in the

Teachings of Nietzsche and Tolstoy Shestov presents us with a
picture of men totally abandoned by all human solace, all human
hope, all human striving at all. The second part, Dostoyevsky and
Nietzsche: Philosophy of Tragedy expands on the theme, rooting it
significantly more in the works of Dostoyevsky — though he is not
omitted from the first part — discussing a lot, but reaching pretty
much the same conclusions. And, so, Shestov ends the second
work thus, with a quote from Baudelaire: Aimes tu les damnes?
Dis-moi, connais-tu I ’irrémissible? Dostoyevsky, Tolstoy,
Nietzsche: these are all condemned men, men cast out from all the
safety nets of mankind— cast out from mankind. This meant not
only being cast out from mankind materially, socially— but from
the category of "man" as a whole, spiritually. Nietzsche, Tolstoy,
Dostoyevsky: those horrible names which re-echo as a humdrum in
the darkened rooms of the spirit— they were no longer men. This
meant that their moral orientation was no longer obvious, their
North Star no more shining, and, worst of all, the laws of the spirit
which the philosophers had so carefully crafted, or tried to keep in
check, were now gone. And where did this leave them? At the only
logical endpoint— at the furthest limits of the universe, at the
fringes of spirit— not only beyond man and time, but, worst of all,
beyond causality. A suffering without causality. Can there be

anything more horrible than this? Can there be such a thing as this

at all? And yet to have those horrible words "Ding an Sich" still
mercilessly trample upon you, to submit to a law in a place where
there are no laws— is this not an atmosphere too cold for a human

being to be in and still remain— a man?
IV

This, at any rate, is the way in which Shestov chooses to present
them to us. These great men, admired by everyone, venerated by
all— reduced to shambles? To a crying, sniffling, weakly ecstasy
of sickness? Truly, this is a great feat of literary mastery, this is,
dare I say, the best psychological novel of the 20th century.
Whether the inner world of Nietzsche, Dostoyevsky and Tolstoy
truly resembled what Shestov described of it is truly up for the
heartiest of debate, and something I will return to at one point or
other. Whether it is true or not is not as relevant to our current
examination, however. What matters is the craft; that Shestov, with
whatever material he worked with, managed to convey such
stunning feats of literary rhetoric. So, let us celebrate then— and
why not in song, in lyric, in some merry little verse? A little song

for Shestov, perhaps? Well then...

VI

Lev Shestov is a hypocrite: there is no way around it. Let us go
back to The Good in The Teachings of Nietzsche and Tolstoy—

every chapter, every page, every blot of ink in this /ibellum is

dedicated to destroying the idea of a "teaching", and of exposing
the futility of preaching to save a man’s soul. But let us then look
at the final line of this book (mind you, of this book, not the
Dostoyevsky and Nietzsche): "We need to seek something higher
than the good, we need to seek God"— Remarkable words! The
end of one preaching just turns out to be the beginning of another. I
felt this in my bones the first time I read it, but the cleverness of
seduction can lure all the other organs to simply ignore this
sensation. J gave him the benefit of the doubt. Oh, and what a
terrible doubt! That such a critique was grounded on such a
paradoxical, new affirmation, so vague, so dark, so indefinitely
murky— 1s sinister. What was Shestov trying to achieve here, what
sleight of hand did he pull? What ideal was he trying to sneak in
here? But oh, I’m getting ahead of myself. The answer to this
question is not quite clear in the books following Nietzsche and
Tolstoy. His Nietzsche and Dostoyevsky continues this mining,
under-mining of psychology. The underground man is exalted to
the heights of genius, and, as he will later tell us in his life,
Dostoyevsky possessed a second sight— a religious statement. But
for the time being, neither his Nietzsche and Dostoyevsky nor his
Apotheosis of Groundlessness make any of the imminent positive
philosophy of Shestov any clearer. His final word, or, rather,

penultimate word, is that unanswered questions and mysterious

sufferings lie dormant in the bosom of mankind, a problem which
philosophers, seemingly, ignore. Suffering is the penultimate
word— the penultimate word before what? Before the ultimate

word: God.
VII

Nietzsche, Tolstoy, and Dostoyevsky as sick men. A seemingly
unresolvable sickness as proof of a mysterious suffering. A
seemingly mysterious suffering as a proof of areas of life hitherto
ignored by philosophers: a perfect, untouched ground for
discovering new phenomena! Nietzsche, Tolstoy and Dostoyevsky
as proof— of what? There are areas of life where time is out of
joint, where there is effect without cause; this much has already
been established. What we are left with, essentially, 1s a void; and
out of this void Shestov will erect a monument so high, so
stunningly penetrating, floating in thin air, that we cannot help but
sense that he feels some pride (whether it is false pride or not I
dare not say) at his creation, to the point that we feel that this
insignificant writer may at any moment burst into laughter and
song, repeating the immortal lines of Horace: "Exegi monumentum

aere perennius regalique situ pyramidum altius — and he does.

Vill

It is not until Shestov’s Potestas Clavium that the philosopher’s
conviction appears on the scene. Yes, previously we had the word
"God". But did not Tolstoy also call his highest ideal "God"?
Nonetheless, this was rejected by Shestov as merely a hiding place
for something different, for "the good", which Tolstoy essentially
agreed with. This "good" which, in Shestov’s eyes, was merely a
trap for men— a deaf, blind and dumb system which merely
commanded without offering any options— a mechanical creation.
It is evident that there are as many "Gods" as there are people.
"God" means the highest ideal, but what this highest ideal is to
each person is a completely different question, and an obscure,
dangerous question, thick as the jungle at night. So what did
Shestov mean by God? It was not an abstract ideal such as "the
good", nor was it any form of Spinozian pantheism or Hegelian
world spirit. Shestov tells us that a true philosophy of religion must
be brought back to The Eternal Book; just as Luther said, just as
Luther did. It is clear that this is not a question of dogma, for the
spiritual tensions we frightfully witness on the page are not the
commonplace compromises of an accountant who was to submit to
his authority— but of deep, private desire. Yet, just as every claim
ad fontes is essentially an impulse towards re-interpretation, it is
obvious that we cannot merely take Shestov’s description of God

at face value. He defines God as boundless freedom, but that

should mean very little when his, apparent, enemy, Hegel, could
have used the very same words to describe his world spirit. In this
case, however, Shestov’s attempt at re-capturing the Biblical God,
the so-called living God and not the abstract God of the
philosophers, seems to be, though not without its nuances, fairly

accurate.
Ix

But God for what? God for whom? If we have established that the
God of every man, philosopher and beast is the making of his own
interpretation, the making of his own fancy, must we not, first,
ground our principles in the man making the interpretation, and,
most importantly, in his will? Kant, Spinoza, Hegel— all these
wrote seemingly without a will, but behind every prejudice which
comes with philosophy, such as assuming the existence of the / that
thinks, or of the cause with effect, or of the reality which is
intelligible, the fundamental instinct, the fundamental desire and
will of the philosopher becomes apparent. Hegel’s was wirklich ist,
das ist verniinftig is nothing but desire fulfilled. So, what is the
will of Monsieur Chestov? I am afraid we are going to have to
embark on a little expedition, a little boat-adventure or fishing
excursion of the spirit, though, in truth, I care very little for any

form of abstract psychologizing, any form of speculative

Geistwurst— I care for what is visible, provable— that is perhaps
my little vice, the little prejudice of this writer— I shall thus keep
strictly to the fact, the deed, the word, despite Faust’s heartiest
wishes. For, truth be told, in the realm of literature — or by its

more popular name: spirit— one can hardly speak of any deeds.

"The beginning of wisdom is the fear of God, not fear before
Nothingness. And freedom comes to man not from knowledge but
from faith, which puts an end to all our fears." — “Nothingness?
What does he mean by this?" Oh, please be patient my reader, all
things have their due time. Right now, I would like you to turn
your attention to the latter half of the sentence: freedom comes...
from faith. Remarkable words! And he continues: which puts an
end to all our fears. Oh good heavens, stunning! He has found it!
The philosopher’s stone! The potion of eternal youth! The unum in
quo totum continetur. Is this beginning to make more sense now? I
hope so— not out of a fear of obscurity, but because I hope for
intelligent readers. Nonetheless, clarity will come, but, for now, let
us please appreciate these magnificent words. In truth, one could
contemplate the words, have one’s eyes rove over the page back
and forth for an eternity and a day; so rich, so delicately precise

they seem, so striking... But let us not be lulled into wistful sleep

by mere seductions, let us use our sharp eye, some may even say
our evil eye. Despite all the high-flown religious language which
has been bestowed upon the word "faith", there is very little to
suggest that anyone really meant anything more than a rhetorical
gesture, exaggerated metaphor, as any lover is bound to do when
writing his first, admittedly embarrassing, volume of poetry. With
faith what is most often meant is not much more than trust. "T trust
you" "I have faith in you"— these tropes have been used quite
interchangeably, even to the point of substituting faith in God with
"trust" in God. Once again, it is very important to emphasize that
whatever emanates from God can be traced back to the specific
definition of said word. When faith in God is equal to trust in God,
God himself is nothing more than a watchful guardian, equivalent
to that great goddess Providentia. A God responds to the specific
instincts of a person in a time or a place, to their needs (an
important word which I will come back to later), this is why the
pagan Gods afford such a greater level of clarity than the
Abrahamic, for, wherein different needs were answered by
different Gods, now everything is instilled into one, confusing
mess, bafflingly named "God". Perhaps it was much more simple
to be honest then; each God answered a category of instinct, and

reflected it in its most crystalized, pure form— now, lacking this

15

clarity, all is confused, and thus psychology was invented. But I

digress.
XI

Providentia: providence. What most men want out of God is the
assurance of providence, which is almost always equal to the
bestowing of material needs. This is how all religion has worked—
pray and hope for the best. Faith, trust, hope— all means of
calming the nerves. Nothing more, nothing less. "Give us our daily
bread" they exclaim, and then go back to working— it’s not that
the prayer will give them daily bread, but, perhaps, it will make it
easier: just for good measure, have faith. You do your work, and
you have patience for things to hopefully get better, but you do
your work nonetheless. If faith were anything more than this, it
would mean that every prayer would be a call for Providentia to
reach down from heaven and fulfill your whim with the turn of her
hand. Whether this does or does not happen, I do not know, but I
do know that the instances in which a person does pray like this,
are rare. Faith for the most part is patience and calm, a way of
shutting off thoughts while you do your work. This is quotidian
religion, i.e. most of religion. But what Shestov, and, consequently,
we, are interested in here is not this quotidian optimism, but a faith

which ends al/ our fears. That is, we do not fear to walk and fall

off a cliff. Even the most faithful person will deny such a sick and

demented act, perhaps answering you with the Savior’s "thou shalt

not tempt The Lord thy God". But Shestov speaks of freedom, of

boundless freedom, of an end to all fears— is it not obvious? A

faith which moves mountains— not metaphorical mountains, as the

philosophers and even the poets are wont to say, but actual
mountains. The prophet tells us that we shall not tempt the lord,

but he also tells us that we can acquire a faith which moves

mountains— how are these reconciled? Shestov does not make this

clear— in fact, he makes nothing clear at all, repeating Pascal’s:
"qu'on ne nous reproche donc plus le manque de clarté, puisque
nous en faisons profession." Clarity, logic, has been wholesale
rejected; there must be something higher than reason, something
higher than the good, which, as he so often tells us, is nothing
more than the universal law which binds men and is stupid, deaf
and dumb. So, a mover who moves even the laws of the universe.
This is the God of Shestov, the boundless freedom which he says
philosophers often speak of, but end up trading with the eternal
law of causality and arithmetic. Is two times two able to equal
five? In the hands of mere men, of mere reason— no. But in the
hands of a being above reason? Above the natural laws
themselves? Definitely. But then the question remains: does this

being listen to the cries and wails of man? Does this being break

17

all morality and law to answer his will? Listen to Job— listen to
the cries of the man most deserted, most abandoned by necessity.
"I am loathsome to my own family" exclaims the poor man: sick,
degraded, disgusting, naught but a pile of dust. This man— might
he be no better than dead? How could such a man be counted
among the living again? How could such a man be a man again?
There is no point in crying— and, what is more, he has no right to
cry. He can just take the consequences of his unknown sin: lie
down, and die. Reason is silent, it offers no answer— an echo in

the abyss.
XII

And, yet, the cries of Job were answered. The whirlwind is made
manifest, the old God of the ancient Hebrews is made once again
into matter as the ancient tyrant of the heavenly lightning. Job gets
his divine tribunal. This is the proof which Shestov gives us of his
"faith". "The Book of Job, in which "cries" triumph over
"thinking," So, Job is right, for God tells Job that he spoke most
honestly of him. But let us be very clear in what way God praises
Job’s sermo: in his calling him a tyrant. God does descend as Job
wants, to stand before the divine tribunal, to have his say before it,
but this is only after Job himself was chosen as a cruel experiment

of God, when the divine tribunal had already laid its eyes upon

him. And does God not tell Job of the bounds of the earth, and the
stars singing in joy? What does Job know of all this? What does
Job know of anything? Job cannot know God’s divine plans. This
much is evident. And what is Job’s answer? "J spoke once, but I
have no answer— twice, but I will not say more". In the end, Job is
a moral story. Trust God’s plan, for you will never know it. There
is very little here of moving mountains, Shestov’s anti-moral,
anti-nomian "faith" comes crashing down, reduced, once again, to
the submission to law. Whether this law and law-giver is more or
less benevolent and arbitrary (voluntary) or not is hardly relevant.
Some terribly misguided scholars of Shestov have pointed out that
he would praise God even if he were a tyrant— this is false.
Shestov makes it clear over and over again that philosophy’s end
goal must be for man to reach God, so as to, once again, acquire
that primordial, boundless freedom. "Before the face of the Creator
there revives in man the authentic freedom created by God, that
freedom which is boundless possibility limited by nothing - like the
freedom of God Himself." Boundless freedom, unbound by law or
tyranny, a God which answers the cries of men. Whether this is the
explicit God of the Bible or not is not too relevant—it must
certainly be admitted that it is far truer to the spirit of the
Abrahamic faith. God as savior— that is the whole ordeal.

Whereas the savior in this case is not much more than a metaphor

for the patient faith we have spoken of, a mere psychotherapy for a
people too easily upset by nervous stimuli, Shestov takes this and
elevates it to its most absurd, absolute heights. God as savior, God

as boundless freedom. God as caprice.
XII

Seneca tells us "ipse omnium conditur et rector...semper paret,
semel jussit." The creator of all commanded once and obeys
forever. In the end, not even God himself, the creator of the
heavens and the earth, the father of the morning and evening star,
is free from subjugation to the merciless yoke of the laws of nature
and arithmetic— in short, of reason. What does this mean? God is
no longer free, reason is above God. God is no longer living, God
is dead. What can he do now that even his will is subjugated to the
mechanical iron law of mathematics? Nothing. The sensation of
the void between God as Gimeipov and God as semper paret has
never been more obvious. The boundless freedom of which
Shestov spoke of, which was promised by the prophet, that faith
which could move mountains— 1s dead, killed by the hands of
reason, or, what the Stoics more accurately call: necessity. What is
this necessity? It means that there are ungovernable laws by which
all things in the world act. That the trees move in the wind as they

do now in this exact moment, that my fingers are moving over this

20

plane of plastic and steel in this exact way; all of this is the result
of an endless chain of cause and effect, mechanical and determined
as clock-work. This is the meaning of semper paret. All things
happen in accordance with necessity, and necessity, more than
reason, is what has created this frightful, unbreakable, monstrous
world-order. Reason can do nothing but submit to necessity;
reason, with its impotence at opposing it. So, it is not reason which
the philosophers submit to when they subjugate themselves to the
mechanical, but to necessity, reason is merely the tool through
which they subjugate themselves. Thus reason, impotent, maybe
even indifferent to what it subjugates itself to — for all it can do is
bow down, whether it is a rational or irrational world-order does
not matter — cannot move one finger to upset this world-order—
what is more, it does not even occur to it. And, yet, there occurs to
a man, not infrequently, the thought that maybe this world-order is
indeed frightful, monstrous and terrible. Wherefrom may this
thought have sprung, then? Is not man a part of this mechanical
world-order? And, if he were, would not the thought of rebellion
not only seem absurd— but impossible? Yet man does, often, stick
out his tongue at the world, he does rebel. Mutiny... does this not
point to a will, however impotent, which is free from this supposed

"moral world-order"? From the seduction of the laws of nature and

21

necessity which no being, apparently, is free from? Does it not

point to man coming... from a higher origin?
XIV

Man is not free— reason tells him so, because necessity demands
it. The only thing in man which points beyond necessity is his
cries. Thus, man’s cries, irrationality and madness are the only
ways in which man may be made free. Only crying 1s divine, all
else is subjugation to necessity. I believe you are now beginning to
see what I am getting at, with what eloquent syllogism Shestov
turns us around to bless, not reason, but unreason. The only hope,
the last bastion of salvation for man and his freedom lies in time
breaking, as it were, out of joint. Of a force which upsets, or, at the
very least, works against the world-order. "All must be made
insane"— thus cries the typical Romantic (for Shestov was
intimately related to him). Indeed, all must be made insane. The
only enemy, the only hope for opposition which can be found
against the world-order is what those who laud necessity hate— if
they hated nothing their praise would have needed no saying, for
all would have already been perfect. And what do these lovers of
necessity, the Stoics, hate? Cries and wailing, madness, wnsinn.
Perfect, so there is still a part of this world that is free from

necessity, even if only in thought. Man imagines the hope of

22

freedom, of moving mountains. The only argument which the
philosophers have against this is the whole world as proof: torture,
starvation, blood. "Those who deny a contingent being are to be
exposed to torture until they concede that it is possible not to be
tortured." How terrible! How inhumane! A world-order which is
not well adjusted to man— could this be? Thus, Shestov puts
forward his greatest weapon: "When God created man, He said to
him that the whole world was his." The prophet says that sabbath
was made for man, and not man for the sabbath; the world was
made for Man, and not Man for the world. There is something in
man that longs elsewhere... therefore reason must be wrong, and

"man" must be right; man, the measure of all things...
XV

"Mangum miraculum est homo" exclaimed someone once— I do
not remember who. And, indeed, man is a great miracle, a creature
deserving of the highest admiration. This was the ideal of the
Greeks, this was the ideal of the men of the Renaissance— those
great men of immortal, passionate ambition. They could have
exclaimed with Protagoras "man is the measure of all things" and
been wholly justified in doing so. The right of man as the
inalienable first principle of humanity— this is humanism, a

humanism which exalts and wants man to become exalted, a

23

humanism which, in truth, is totally contrary to the humanism of
our day, which only seeks to diminish man. Yet, both of these
ideological enemies could be very comfortable using the same
name, even exclaiming simultaneously "man is the measure of all
things" and feel two completely different things thereby. Thus, let
us not be fooled when Shestov praises man and his "rights", his
right to "cry" as man’s ultimate triumph over necessity, over the
laws of nature and arithmetic, which, ultimately, shall render him
and his cries "the measure of all things". Humanism— this can
mean a million things. A million to the power of a million. What
differentiates the humanism of our egalitarian era from that of a
Pico or Borgia is the type of man which is praised. Is it the lower,
or the higher type which is praised here? This same question could
be posed, and positively should be posed, without the slightest bit
of malice, here, too. Which type of man does Shestov praise? Who
are his heroes? He does not hide this— it is the sickest of people,
the most downtrodden, the most broken by existence. He praises
the sick man, the man outside of nature, the man hateful of nature
and hateful to nature. This— is not man. This is an abortion, not in
the way a man without an arm is no longer a man, for clearly he 1s,
but in the way in which the most leprosy ridden creature, barely
human, longing for death, is no longer alive, but dead. Shestov

are often so fatefully, so terribly and perplexingly intertwined in
one another. It may be accurately said that beyond virtue and vice,
beyond good and evil, strength and weakness inescapably hold
another as the sunlit tree and its shadow. A drive may both exalt
and depress, depending on its direction; Nietzsche’s instinctual
skepticism turned suspicion and mortal doubt is the clearest
example of this. The most important thing, more than the
individual elements of his personality, are the proportions and
relationships between them. Nietzsche’s afflictions, mastered and
"turned around" may in fact have become his greatest strength—
but his afflictions still remain a fact. There was strength in him, a
certain untimely and atavistic strength which comes to overcome
the age, but the way in which this strength grows to such heights is

nonetheless in large part a product of chance. There may have

47

existed, and perhaps still exist, men of similar "virtues" as
Nietzsche— but the deep intimacy with decadence, sickness, and
the deeper understanding of the importance which the stronger
instincts have within one’s self— did this not come from sickness?
It is possible that, if it weren’t for the extent of the unlucky
circumstances which Nietzsche found himself in, he would not
have been able to understand, even less to formulate, the extent to
which "pity" was destructive, and the extent to which it needed not
only to be discarded, but totally annihilated. Nietzsche was a sick
ass, as were all the men of his age, and only by the great
magnitude of his burden was he able to know the danger of
decadence to its utmost extreme, only by being the most
thoroughly acquainted with it and its dangers was he able to
overcome it by through-coming. Hence, Nietzsche’s strange words
"nihilism thought through to the end" hence, the "great
down-going". Can there be any other way for men today? Only he
who suffers by every affliction can know what it is like to need to
be pushed through to the very end; one sickness less in the recipe
of what one is and one does not know the tensions whereby the
importance of the deed becomes necessary— the importance of not
merely remaining on strength, but of pushing this strength to the

very end, to the death of all pity.

Book the Third

There is enough ground to suggest that what we call "ideas" are, at
most, dulled sense stimuli. An idea composed of words is the
marriage of sound registered by our ear and image based on past
registers through our eyes. A connection, a parallel, a number or
logical line, may correspond to the movements of our hands, or
relations of physical movement. It seems that the more perfect, the
more pure, the more cold and objective an idea is as it presents
itself to us, the more it ceases to entice the movement of the heart,
the blood, or the sense organs, but rather appeals to the most subtle
discordant and concordant movements in our inner organs,
processed in the brain. In any event, the coldness with which ideas
are to be regarded are nonetheless never free from the movements
in the physical body, and to assume that we may think outside the
risk of further stimuli in a realm of pure spirit is simply absurd. We
are always vulnerable to the physical affects which may be
exercised on our sense organs, no matter how much we guard
ourselves against it with our coldness— yet we still need to be
cold. At one moment we may find ourselves in perfect

contemplation of ideal relations, in the other we suddenly fall on

60

an image which awakens sorrow, pity or admiration, and our body
is filled with the warmth of rushing blood. A book is no innocent
thing, and when we think we have escaped with it into a realm of
pure speculation, we may turn the page and suddenly find
ourselves stabbed in the back by a totally different affect. Often
these are noticeable and sudden so as to merely be discarded as the
author’s overt enthusiasm, but other times it builds up subtly,
gradually, from one side of the spectrum of emotion to the other,
and in the end the affect itself is intertwined with logic to the point
of laying itself upon the whole of our physiology, to the point at
which we, by prejudice of movements which we characterize as
"line of though", assume relationships between individual affects
to be causal, and we do not notice its effect. But this is how all
rhetoric has always worked, and how all images, intertwined with
often contradictory arguments, convinces us to a certain "truth", by
the seeming concordance between different affects, by the
superimposition of one over a totally different idea... No, a book, a
speech, a polemic— is no innocent thing! Through it we may be

convinced that an ant is, indeed, an elephant.
II

"Life is not literature", this does Shestov assure us in his

"Philosophy of Tragedy"— this does he, too, assure us to be the

61

theme of the Dostoyevsky he is primarily concerned with: Notes
from Underground. The story, if one can even call it such, of
Dostoyevsky’s novel is well known enough and we won’t spend
time repeating it here, but it can very well be accurately
summarized in the words "life is not literature". Dostoyevsky’s
underground man is almost overtaken by ecstasy sublimated from
despair at the thought "So this is it, contact with real life!" — "real
life oppressed me with its novelty that J could hardly breathe". The
titular hero, when arriving at the end of his long confession, cannot
help but make it clear what the main preoccupation of the so-called
"literary work" is: he repeats the words "real life" three times in a
single paragraph. That the "underground man" who was speaking
was, in fact, Dostoyevsky is something no-one doubted, lest they
were naive enough to be fooled by the prefatory note to the
"Notes". An entire biography of Dostoyevsky could perhaps be
extracted from "Notes from The House of The Dead" and "Notes
from Underground" and Shestov positively attempts to do so. He
begins, rightfully so, at the theme "life is not literature", and
follows Dostoyevsky through to the very end, to his confessions as
a type of criminal, a scoundrel, a man divorced from real life, a
man disillusioned, he follows him through all of his confessions of
his "cultivation" and "refinement" as mere "literature"— and then,

something incredible happens: he believes Dostoyevsky’s final lie.

62

Dostoyevsky could not leave merely as a scoundrel, as a criminal
— he, like Nietzsche, needed justification for his condition — and
so he turns towards a new form of preaching. Shestov condemned
the preaching of Nietzsche as this vain "justification", which as we
have shown in the second part of this work, is not the whole
story— and yet Shestov believes Dostoyevsky when he tries to
exalt his "underground man" into heights of genius: what is more,
Shestov expressly makes him his formula, placing "Jet the world
go to hell, but I should always have my tea" with Nietzsche’s
"pereat mundus, fiam!". But what world is being destroyed? What
"I" is being affirmed? Whereas Nietzsche’s cry was one of the
necessity for distance, Dostoyevsky’s is that of the desire to
become a preacher— a disillusioned dreamer who wants to dream
on. Of course, Dostoyevsky does not turn back to the socialist
fantasies of his youth, at least not explicitly, but to Slavophilia, to
"Orthodox Christianity" (which paired with Slavophilia is not
much different from said socialism)— he becomes the popular

preacher.

In the essay "Dostoyevsky: The Gift of Prophecy" Shestov
positively diagnoses Dostoyevsky’s preaching as an attempt to

become something which he perhaps was not— a "prophet", as

Shestov says, is a Bismarck, a chancellor, not a Dostoyevsky.
Shestov considered this "preaching" to be a deviation from the true
message of Dostoyevsky’s works, which was found in the teaching
of the "underground man", the true saint, the true religious
message "at the heart" of Dostoyevsky’s work beyond "popular
preaching". But what was the message of the underground man? At
first, it seems that it is just this formula "life is not literature", that
much "literary refinement" is a faute de mieux, but then it seems
that it is something quite different, that the "underground man" has
actually divined something more... and thus he ends his notes, a
few sentences after a three-fold condemnation of our "divorce from
real life" with these words: "We are stillborn, and for generations
past have been begotten, not by living fathers, and that suits us
better and better. We are developing a taste for it. Soon we shall
contrive to be born somehow from an idea. But enough; I dont
want to write more from Underground." In general, this has been
interpreted, in part correctly, by the words which precede it — "we
try to contrive to be some sort of impossible generalized man" —
as a condemnation of the "generalized man" as opposed to the
"individual man" of flesh and blood embodied in the hero. But the
hero himself admits that we are "developing a taste for it" and that
without books we are "ashamed" of being men with real flesh and

blood... does this really sound any different from Dostoyevsky’s

64

underground man? Granted, in the very same paragraph our hero
takes pride in having gone further in his debauchery than
anyone... but then why the need for all these words, for all this
explanation? It is true that on the one hand Dostoyevsky elevates
pathology, as he continually does throughout his euvre, but on the
other he constantly feels a need to explain it, to justify it, to make
it seem sublime... In this sense, he tries to elevate his pathological
debauchery, his impossibility at adapting to real life, into an
ideal— an idea. Dostoyevsky himself is and wants to be born out
of an idea, not out of the idea of the "generalized men", but out of

an idea nonetheless. He, indeed, wants life to become literature.
IV

Mikhailovsky tells us in his "Dostoyevsky: a cruel talent" that the
main driving force behind Dostoyevsky’s work was not
compassion or love, but the pleasure of artistically stimulating the
organs, the senses of cruelty. This is, we may add, typical and
indeed characteristic of the entire Romantic movement —
Dostoyevsky was a Romantic — in that the mere play of all
emotion was the primary end goal of the work. Pity and cruelty, as
love and hate, are never far apart and often touch the same organs,
often raise or lower the temperature of the blood in the same way.

Both are barbarisms and when done "artistically" (artistically in the

65

lowest aesthetic sense, not on the sense of sight, sound or smell but
for physical impulse, constraint of the stomach etc.) play the
reader’s organs of emotions as an instrument— it becomes a
harmony composed of affects. The mixing of moral, religious
"preaching" acquires a charm in its paradoxical mix with cruelty,
but it is a charm nonetheless. Thus, Dostoyevsky, whom Turgenev
called "The Russian Marquis de Sade", managed to deliver a
message of cruelty under the guise of moral language. None of this
is new, however, and merely makes it clear for us that
Dostoyevsky, above all else, is not to be trusted in his words and
only be taken by his affects. His literature has precisely an appeal
insofar as it plays on the lowest and often most hurtful pathologies
of life into a coordinated, harmonious (in the sense of a harmony
of chaos), literary way— he makes life literature. This same
principle should be applied to Dostoyevsky’s "underground man",
who, precisely because he is portrayed as someone who "says the
truth" should perhaps be trusted the /east, as a vehicle to another
wish-fulfillment, to a new lie. This lie is a "new" preaching, which,
as we have previously established, is the "individual man" born out
of an idea, out of literature, out of his own whim. It rhymes
perfectly with what is stated in the first part of the "Notes" in
which the author declares open war upon the laws of nature and

arithmetic. These "laws of nature" are naught but a metaphor for

66

the hero’s own dreary moods and bad conscience, but the barbarian
needs a more noble enemy to admit that he suffers— hence, "the
laws of nature", hence also "satan". Dostoyevsky, to admit that he
suffers, creates the literary device of the "laws of nature" to make
his shameful and pathetic suffering seem heroic. If life is
disappointing and embarrassing, if it is not literature, then it should

become literature.
Vv

So, the preaching of the "underground man" is that of the
justification of his suffering at the hands of the "laws of nature",
making life into literature, into an idea— the popular preaching of
Dostoyevsky 1s that of justification of the fate of the Russian
people as messianic, as a literary phenomenon. How are these two
any different? Evidently, they aren’t, they are both preachings, they
are both "born out of an idea". "The laws of nature", just as the
"Messianic mission of the Russian people", is based first and
foremost on the aesthetic principle, as Dostoyevsky himself
pointed out and knew while trying to dress it up as "merely what
the philosophers call God." What a marvel of dialectic! What
rhetorical power! Dostoyevsky, the man of the decadent age, the
spiritual criminal, the dreamer, found his fulfillment in the role of

the prophet, of the Russian messiah. But what made him choose

67

one form of preaching over another? What made him abandon the
fight against "the laws of nature" for that of Slavophilia? It might
not be much of a difficult question at all when taking into
consideration how much better the role of the national prophet
befitted him; his greatest novels, after all, are those in which
national, personal forces and those of eternity seem to touch and
melt into one another, where the personal sufferings of the heroes,
the most downtrodden, the most base and filthy, find their
redemption in the picturesque spectacle of moral subjugation. One
would be hesitant to call Raskolnikov’s "fall before the cross" as
submission to the psychology of salvation in an absolute sense, as
what we see there is not expressly a cry before God but an active,
internal purging of the personality in favor of a new life in the
order of the world, in its "moral order". This is why Dostoyevsky’s
novels have often been praised for their portrayal of "the
personality" in relation to historical forces. The entire concept of
redemption in Dostoyevsky is centered around the world and in the
world; his novels, therefore, cannot be seen outside the political
and historical context in which the author found the necessary
aesthetic and pictorial material to form its most vivid, colorful
portrayal of redemption. Hence, this bizarre concept of "The
Russian" and his suffering in service of others. Dostoyevsky found

his rest and satisfaction 1n the service to "the world", and thus he

68

found his justification in returning to mankind, though only as an
aesthetic means. For Dostoyevsky this was quite enough,
however— despite his novels being primarily "psychological"
there is very little pictorial material in the dark labyrinth of man’s
mind, this is why "Notes from Underground" is so poor in

But what if'a world could be constructed out of the psychology of

enough, and so, Shestov, finding himself more and more deserted

This shift in rhetoric is very important: Whereas earlier the

images seemingly out of nothing when entering the spaces of
man’s psychology. And what means does he find there? Evidently,
he does not find the flowers nor the moon, he barely finds anything
palpable except one thing: pathologies. Despite his insistence upon
"groundlessness" every idea, every sensation, every image grows
from the ground as a tree laying its roots in the soil grows
upwards. If there be any attempt at affective seduction the center of

gravity must shift towards new ground, and Shestov finds its most

fertile ground, perhaps the only fertile ground in man’s isolated
soul, in appealing to the pathologies, the sick passions. Thus,
Shestov concentrates the entire barrage of his rhetorical gravity
upon one thing: cries. Nietzsche’s "pereat mundus, fiam" is turned
into a cry of despair not incomparable to that of Pascal’s "Je

n’approuve que de ceux qui cherchent en gemissant".
VII

In the doctrine of the affects, as well is in that of classical rhetoric,
repetition is very important. The repetition of a line, or a phrase,
given its appropriate context constantly strengthens the contours
and lines of the material rendering its affect far stronger than
before. For this reason, one should always be on the lookout for
when an author repeats something, and then guard one’s self
against it—what is repeated shows his strongest rhetorical weapon,
the affect he wishes to inflict upon you. Pascal’s quote is repeated
by Shestov numerous times throughout his work and gradually
paired with similar lines from classical and religious literature.
Pascal was a wonderful writer, and a most precious model for any
student of the style of the French language— any line lifted from
his writings is bound to appeal on an aesthetic level. What makes
his lines so particularly affecting is their seeming lack of

"philosophical" language, often touching on the borders of poetry

71

or psalm. The aesthetic quality draws a large extent of its force
from the syntactic craft, the parallels often employed in the
language, its laconicism. The precision with which Pascal utters
his words awakens a quite sublime admiration in the reader, and
intertwined with the content of the sentences often seem to melt
into it, dilute, eclipse the generic meaning. This warm-blooded
sensation of love which is awakened in the admiration of a certain
sublimity thus takes over the object of the original meaning of the
line, rendering ceux qui cherchent not as seekers out of despair, but
strivers towards a lovable object. Those who seek in lamentation,
however, always seek within despair, in which there 1s nothing
sublime. All of literature for, at least, the past two centuries has
managed to confuse sublimity with despair through the means
described above. The sublimity a suffering author manages to
present in his works is never the sublimity of the suffering itself,
but merely of its antidote. This antidote need not necessarily be a
real "cure", it may often be mere pity which nonetheless serves as
a positive, though ultimately hurtful, affect rendering the suffering
"lovable"— but never the suffering itself, only the positive object

or affect which "justifies" its striving, its "sublime horizon"...

XI

Shestov’s suffering was nothing remarkable, it was an "inner

humiliation, bad conscience, shame, a realization of one’s

weakness predisposes him to a tendency for pity, for self-pity, out
of which any cry great or small must be multiplied, made more and
more "worthy" of itself by its length. Shestov ultimately prizes this

as any suffering, must have its object, its great affective cause —
the search for causes begins with suffering — and this he finds in
the "laws of nature", and more in the "laws of nature" in its proxy
to man, in "reason". According to Shestov reason is this yoke that
binds man to the "laws of nature", known by its more philosophical
term as "necessity". Reason, then, and not merely "the laws of

nature" or "necessity" is what keeps man "bound" to his suffering

by demanding that he keep himself in accordance with "necessity".

socialists. In a state of suffering and impotence one sees everything
as equally impossible and impotent, when in reality it is not—
when this is long drawn-out by pity, it has enough time to grow

man and, consequently, the solution is not placed within man’s

absolute, that he could see beyond to tell that the "laws of nature"

statement that "reason" cannot know everything; the laws of nature

cannot know everything" there is always the possibility of unreal

phenomena which reason obstructs us from— reason must

But why, why would the "solution" somehow lie beyond "reason"?
It could very well be that if there is something reason does not
succeed in knowing it would be something terrible (which would
be more accurate), perhaps merely a scientific curiosity— why is it
assumed that "reason" obstructs something good? Reason, in the
mind of Shestov, is man’s yoke to reality, it is then reality which
obstructs the good, reality itself is bad. The linking of a mere
philosophical observation to a soteriology is thus conditioned by
the assumption that reality is bad and non-reality is good, it is
conceived out of hatred for reality, out of pity. Indeed, as several
experiences not only throughout man’s history but even throughout
your own life have proven that throwing yourself on the ground

you think it impossible. — But lo! Reason only knows what it has

reason is not to be trusted because it cannot know everything, that

This is done subtly, however, a change of definitions which, once
again, happens gradually (now there can be no doubt we are
dealing with a Jew), and not even moral action but action in
general, actions in this world are condemned with them. For
