FOREWARD.

In "Christosophie" |) I explained a method of thought,
which can be summarized in two laws:

1. Listen entirely without prejudice to the inborn
meaning of the life-words, until the Life that created those
words speaks clearly to you.

2. Open your imagination unconditionally to the
forms of nature, until you can experience the form of na-
ture as one with its content.

This method of thought does not neglect empiricism,
but presupposes it as lived through and overcome. The
following reflections are a trial of the practice of this
method of thought.

I call these reflections “mystical,” because they recog-
nize the fundamental Mystery of all life and do not at-
tempt to “solve” it, but wish by logical thinking to dis-
cover it as a contemplable Mystery.

4. Personality am I.

This every truly dead human being may say from out
of his deepest essential being. From out of, not about.

about something is to speak about an object, and person-
ality is subject. But personality must speak from out of it-
self, and only itself does it speak out. All things are utter-
ance of personality, utterance in the full sense: creation.
The self-consciousness of personality is the recognition of
her creation as her creation in objectivity. This self-con-
sciousness also, which is itself, she speaks out, and indeed
as re-creation of her things. And thus this self-conscious-
ness resounds and draws its lines in all things: “Creation
is my creation: I love it. Creation is my creation: all things

I bid to die, so that they may live, ever again. Creation is
my creation: all things I bid to suffer, so that they may be

continued life of that thing. For the continuation of life of
an object means for the thoughtful human being: to con-
tinue dying in and through the living Subject. But he not
only denies the continued life of that thing, that individual;
strictly speaking, he denies all life in it. For all life, after
all, comes from

8. The Cross as Conceptual Representation of the Cosmos.

By “Cosmos” we understand the totality of the hu-
manly perceptible, objective reality. We call that reality
“Cosmos” (order), because man either already perceives
it as ordered, or else by rational natural impulse wants to
perceive it as ordered. The concept “Cosmos” is therefore
not the same as the concept “Universe” or all that exists.
For it is conceivable that, beyond the things we perceive
as ordered or want to perceive as ordered, there still exists
something objective that can in no way appear within the
circle of human perceptibility. But since that something, if
it exists, does not concern us, at least for the present, it is
forgivable that the words “Cosmos” and “Universe” are
often interchanged.

The natural scientist, who as such is an empiricist, an-
alyzes the Cosmos, and, if he remains within his disci-
pline—if, for example, he makes no excursions into the
field of natural philosophy—is solely intent on perceiving
the analyzed details with the utmost precision. The spec-
ulative human being, however, who has lived through
and overcome empiricism (not neglected, but lived through
and overcome!), is aware of

16

his right to do precisely the opposite of what the empiri-
cist does: he contemplates the fundamental cosmic move-
ment, to which all movement can be reduced, and sees it
both in the whole Cosmos and in every smallest particle
of it. While the empiricist describes external forms and ex-
ternal action, the speculative human being creates a con-
ceptual representation of the cosmic gesture. That concep-
tual representation speaks from innermost humanity and
is therefore pure reality for the whole human being, pure
reality for the rational emotion but also for the imagina-
tive power of poetic dreaming; it is wisdom not only, but
also poetry of cosmic content.

The “Cross,” with all its wisdom and all its poetry, is
a conceptual representation of the cosmic movement. The
Cross, that is: the horizontal line of history, the gesture of
facts in cause and effect; the vertical line of evolution, the
gesture of objects from the primitive to the ever more
complex; and the point of intersection of both lines, from
which the complete movement proceeds, the “radiative”
movement, to which the most complicated "plastic" form
can be reduced.

11. The horizontal, cosmic movement is in essence: time.

If we wish to name the horizontal, cosmic movement
purely with a single word that says it all, we must first of
all be mindful to name objectivity as movement, for “I
know an object in truth only then, when I perceive it as
movement.” (9) And then we must indeed take care to
name the movement as cosmic movement and not as a
movement that is realized only in a separate reality. For:

27. What is a natural phenomenon?

Now that we have explained the fundamental natural
forms, it is the phenomena that are next in order. Natural
forms, which we may also call natural phenomena, are in
essence surface-movements, created by their latent con-
tent. A phenomenon, however, is horizontal line-move-
ment, or vertical ray-movement, or radiative point-move-
ment (quantity), but not surface-movement — at least, not
surface-movement as such. I say: as such. For it can indeed
occur, as we Shall later see in the case of electricity, that
surface-movement degenerates into another kind of
movement. In that particular case the phenomenon is in-
deed a surface-movement, but not a surface-movement as
such. It is then a surface-movement in so far as it becomes
another movement — one that is no longer surface-move-
ment.

In these preliminary indications there lies still a diffi-
culty concerning radiative movement. We said in propo-
sition 13 that radiative movement, in contrast to the hori-
zontal and the vertical, is embodied. Now the surface be-
longs to the essence of corporeality, and therefore also to
the essence of radiative movement. How then can radia-
tive movement be a phenomenon, if the

78

surface, and thereby form, belongs to its essence? Answer:
In so far as radiative movement is embodied, it is an ap-
pearance or form; but in so far as radiative movement cre-
ates a body or a form, it can be a phenomenon.

With all this, it is by no means said that a cosmic
movement may already be called a “phenomenon”
merely because it is not surface-movement as such. No, if
we are truly to call a cosmic movement a “phenomenon,”
it must fulfil two conditions: one negative and one posi-
tive. The negative condition requires that it shall not be
surface-movement as such. The positive condition re-
quires that it shall nevertheless be sensibly perceptible.
The vertical movement, for example, is in its essence:
space. But space is not yet a phenomenon. Only when
space becomes sensibly perceptible is it a phenomenon —
namely, as we shall soon see, light.

After these indications, we can, while remaining
within cosmic vision, give pure and verifiable definitions
of the fundamental natural phenomena: light, sound, vol-
ume, and electricity.

something wholly different from the horizontal. Even
though the vertical movement also issues from a centre,

everywhere retains a character of origin, for nothing de-

the pure concept of vertical movement, and all this is fun-

sound-vibrations, when left entirely free, propagate them-

30. Volume is the fundamental phenomenon of radiative
movement.

In so far as radiative movement is not yet body but
body-creating, it is volume or physically perceptible
quantity. Volume cannot be separated from corporeality,
yet it can very really be distinguished from it. While the
body as such must be form, the volume as such is form-
less. That the distinction between volume and body is no
fiction but pure reality appears at once from this, that sev-
eral forms can contain one and the same volume.

Volume is nothing other than the radiation-point, in
so far as it, tensioned by vertical and horizontal move-
ment, desires to become body. The radiation-point repeats
itself while it creates its body; it “steps outside itself” and
becomes quantity, taut and supple quantity, in one word:
volume. Why taut and supple quantity? Since the tension
of every radiation-point presupposes horizontal and ver-
tical movement, the essence of horizontal and vertical
movement must be perceptible in the phenomenal mani-
festation of the radiation-point — the volume. And this is
indeed the case. The volume shows horizontal, that is, es-
sentially pliable tension (27), usually as heat; and it shows
vertical, that is, essentially taut tension (28), usually as
gravity. The yieldingness of heat everyone knows: the
quantity of heat is so

supplely changeable that we measure it by the volume of
yielding mercury. Gravity, on the other hand, manifests
itself essentially in the fall of bodies: that fall is “rigid”; it
has in all objects one and the same speed and always pro-
ceeds in a straight, taut path.

31. Electricity is the fundamental phenomenon of surface
movement.

Surface-movement as such is, in its essence, material
reality itself. For surface-movement as such is “form,” and
in nature form and content are one. But we may not ex-
clude the possibility that a surface-movement can acquire
a tension that is no longer in harmony with the content;
then the surface-movement must transform itself into ray-
movement or line-movement or point-movement. Once
we have admitted that possibility, it is experimentally
confirmed by the phenomenon “electricity,” and electric-
ity appears to be the surface-movement which, through
abnormal tension — that is, tension alienated from the
content — becomes phenomenal movement.

Every physicist can show us through simple experi-
ments that electricity is surface-reality. Electricity, he says,
“collects itself” upon the surface of bodies. The form of
the surface even influences the greater or lesser density of
the electricity. It “gathers” itself, namely, most strongly at
those parts which taper to the sharpest point. Physicists
have advanced many

87

hypotheses to explain electricity. It is now quite generally
assumed that electricity is nothing other than a rarefaction
or condensation of the ether at the surface of bodies. But
whoever has learned to know the surface itself as life-
movement no longer needs any ether “at” the surface in or-
der to define electricity. He will simply define it as abnor-
mally heightened or lowered life-movement “of,” and not
“at,” the surface of bodies; abnormal in the sense that the
unity of form and content has been broken, whereby the
appearance (form) must become phenomenon, or, which
is the same, whereby surface-movement must become
ray-, line-, or point-movement.

Since surface-movement presupposes all phenomenal
movement, abnormal surface-movement must be able to
exhibit all phenomenal movement. This is indeed ob-
served in electricity. All the phenomena of electricity are
explained by these considerations as phenomenal move-
ments born of an abnormal surface-movement. When the
abnormal surface-movement becomes vertical ray-move-
ment, electric light arises. When the abnormal surface-
movement becomes horizontal line-movement, the electric
current arises. When the abnormal surface-movement be-
comes radiative point-movement, the electric spark arises.
The electric

the abnormal surface-tension, and it itself is now a new

point on the way to embodiment: it is “volume.” The elec-

taut and the yielding movement which it presupposes as

art. Those forms can also be inwardly sensually percepti-

of the word. For the poet likewise creates forms, he like-
wise creates images, but he does not set them before us,
visible for outward sensory perception. The poet speaks
from imagination to imagination, and the reader or lis-
tener must co-create in order to be able to see the poet’s
images in inward-sensual contemplation. That pictorial
power can also be very active outside what we in the nar-

man imagination can shape new forms — not "more"
forms, but genuinely "new" ones: essential renewals of the
forms that Life itself offers us outside all art. Whoever

live for him. For him the rain is not rain alone, but a
heaven that weeps for earthly sorrow. For him the flowers
are not flowers alone, but human beings who laugh and
grieve and share in all the joys and sufferings of mankind.
That pictorial power is no deception but truth — truth
that has not yet reached reflection. For the most reflective
man there is only one Life in the Cosmos, and the dream-
ing imagination knows that too, even though it does not
yet know it in strictly controlled vision.

But man’s imagination also possesses a constructive
power, and that holds even more truth than the pictorial.
In its constructive activity, imagination creates not forms
or renewals of form, but phenomena. Surfaces as such it
does not create or recreate — no curvature, no depths, no
perspective, and no plasticity — it accepts only the surface
as a foundation for its constructions. They are lines, geo-
metrical figures, supple bendings, angular turns. When
primitive, those constructions become naive decorations;
in higher perfection, they become artistic ornamentation.
