DYNAMIC AND CONFRONTATIONAL
ONTOLOGY:

From the Static to a Dynamic-
Confrontational Vision of Being

Abstract: This philosophical analysis explores the fundamental dichotomy
within ontology: the classic defense of Static Being (permanence,
substance, immutability, rooted in Parmenides and Aristotle) versus the
modern assertion of Dynamic Being (flux, process, becoming,
championed by Heraclitus and Process Philosophy). Classical static
ontology prioritizes fixed essence and stability, treating change as mere
accident, while dynamic ontology defines reality as continuous, active
Process and Action, inherently linked to time and history. The core
argument introduces the Confrontational Paradigm, which posits that
tension and struggle (Po/emos) are the essential engines of ontological
growth, meaning reality is not a finished product but an open-ended Task
requiring active participation. This paradigm rejects determinism and
places negativity and contradiction at the heart of creation, where
authenticity is achieved through continuous resistance against forces
that seek to fix or reduce Being to a passive state. The implications are far-
reaching: ethically, it imposes a historical responsibility to challenge
structures that enforce stagnation; epistemologically, it transforms
knowledge from passive discovery into active, participatory construction.
Ultimately, the philosophy mandates the courage to embrace uncertainty,
viewing the constant mediation between the need for security (static)
and the drive for freedom (dynamic) as the only way to achieve genuine,
complex, and living reality.

Key Keywords: Ontology, Static Ontology, Dynamic Being,
Confrontational Paradigm, Becoming, Process Philosophy, Polemos, Fixed
Essence, Action/Task, Ontological Tension, Historical Responsibility,
Dynamic Ontology

Introduction

Ontology, the fundamental discipline of metaphysics, addresses the
perennial and profound question of the essential nature of Being (Ontos)
and existence. Throughout the history of thought, the answer to this core
question has essentially polarized between a classical perspective, which
valued stability and permanence, and a more recent approach, which
celebrates movement, process, and change. The Confrontational
Paradigm is not merely a variant of this dynamic approach, but
represents a radical, large-scale challenge to the entire traditional
philosophical edifice. It proposes a fundamental understanding of reality
as a non-stop, active, perpetually unfolding process defined by tension.

This ontological confrontation signals a paradigm shift: a move from the
passive contemplation of a given Being to the active engagement in

Becoming, viewed as the authentic mode of existence. Consequently, the
stakes are not merely descriptive (what is Being?) but also normative
(how must Being manifest itself to be truly real?). The Confrontational
Ontology places action at the very center of metaphysics, treating it not
as an effect of Being, but as its very generative condition. Thus, the
difference between the two visions reduces to their relationship with
time: static ontology seeks to negate time in favor of eternity, whereas
dynamic ontology incorporates time, history, and the ephemeral as
constitutive elements of essence.

The dispute between the static concept of Being and the dynamic one
does not represent a simple terminological controversy; it touches upon
the very root of how we understand stability, meaning, and value in the
cosmos. While the static approach offers intellectual comfort by
guaranteeing eternal truths and unchangeable essences, the dynamic
vision accepts the inherent risk of existence, asserting that reality is,
fundamentally, history and becoming. Through this acceptance of the
ephemeral as a constitutive element, the confrontational paradigm opens
the path toward an ontology of permanent creation, where every
moment represents an opportunity and a responsibility to generate
ontological novelty. The tension between the static and the dynamic is not
a simple methodological difference but a fundamental conflict concerning
how Being relates to law, freedom, and irreversible time.

Philosophers who advocate for dynamism argue that equating Being with
immutability is, in fact, postulating a form of non-life or ontological
death, because everything that is alive manifests flux and continuous
transformation. True Being, therefore, cannot be a finite, passive object,
contemplated from the outside, but must be an active force, a field of
forces, or an emergent process. Understanding this internal dynamic of
Being, fueled by contradiction (Polemos), is essential for grasping not
only metaphysics but also the social and ethical movements that advocate
for change and self-transcendence.

This ontological dichotomy is also reflected in how the categories of
existence are defined. For static ontology, categories (such as substance,
quantity, quality) are fixed, timeless concepts, intended to capture the
unchanging essence of things. Conversely, for the dynamic approach, the
categories themselves are seen as provisional results of the process of
becoming. They are not starting foundations but unstable points of
equilibrium, constantly renegotiated under the pressure of change.
Therefore, a crucial question arises: can classical logic, based on the
principle of non-contradiction, be unconditionally applied to a reality
that is, by definition, self-contradictory and constantly transcending
itself? This is a central point in the critique posed by Hegelian and post-
Hegelian philosophy. This openness to ambiguity and contradiction is
what makes Confrontational Ontology, in essence, a metaphysics of
freedom.

The modern historical context has further accentuated the validity of the
dynamic vision. Discoveries in quantum physics, evolutionary biology, and
complex systems have confirmed that at the most fundamental level,
reality is defined by uncertainty, probability, and emergence. Thus,

the vision of a universe as a gigantic, perfectly determined clockwork
mechanism (promoted by classical materialism, derived from static
ontology) has become unsustainable. Consequently, Dynamic Ontology
is no longer just a philosophical option but appears to be the most
appropriate attempt to construct a metaphysics compatible with
contemporary natural sciences. It provides a coherent framework for
understanding phenomena such as self-organization, systemic creativity,
and, most importantly, how existence can generate order from chaos
without resorting to an external and immutable plan.

Ontology - The Fundamental Branch of
Philosophy

Ontology represents the fundamental philosophical discipline, widely
considered by many theorists to be the very heart of metaphysics. It is
dedicated to the study of Being as Being—investigating what makes a
thing real and examining the most general categories and relations of
existence. Essentially, ontology seeks to answer questions such as: What
are the fundamental modes of being? What does existence truly signify?
And what are the essential properties shared by all things that exist,
irrespective of their particular form?

Throughout the centuries, ontology has striven to construct a conceptual
map of reality, identifying universal concepts like substance, accident,
potentiality, actuality, unity, and multiplicity. It functions as the
foundation of any philosophical system, because the way we define Being
directly influences epistemology (the theory of knowledge), ethics, and
logic. A well-defined ontological system provides the necessary structure
for understanding the coherence of the world, serving as the groundwork
upon which all knowledge is constructed.

In this sense, any theory about the world, be it scientific, religious, or
artistic, operates on implicit ontological assumptions. The formal purpose
of ontology is to make these assumptions explicit and to subject them to
rigorous critical examination. Therefore, the debate between the Static
and the Dynamic is not a simple matter of preference, but an essential
confrontation over what ultimately constitutes fundamental reality.
Depending on the answer provided, philosophy orients itself either
toward contemplation and classification (Static Ontology) or toward
action and transformation (Dynamic Ontology). Furthermore, the scope of
ontology is currently being revisited, particularly in the analytical
tradition, where attempts are made to construct formal ontologies that
model specific domains of reality, yet even these must confront the
inherent tension between defining fixed categories and allowing for their
fluid application in a changing world.

The Classical Paradigm: Static Ontology

Ontologia clasica, puternic influentata de marii ganditori ai antichitatii,
precum Parmenide - care a negat schimbarea, afirmand ca Fiinta este
Una, eterna si imobila - si Aristotel, care a dezvoltat teoria Substantei
(termenul grecesc ousia), a definit Fiinta in primul rand prin prisma
acestei substante, vazand-o ca pe o realitate de baza, permanenta, care
persista neschimbata sub valul si sub aparenta schimbarii. In acest cadru
de gandire, adevarul si posibilitatea cunoasterii sigure sunt garantate
exclusiv de ceea ce este permanent si universal, de esenta
neschimbatoare care defineste lucrurile. Schimbarea sau miscarea
(dynamis) nu este considerata decat un accident, o manifestare
secundara si, in ultima instanta, imperfecta a unei naturi care, in
profunzime, este fundamental imobila. Entitatile sunt definite de Legea
Identitatii, iar stabilitatea si permanenta sunt vazute ca fiind singurele
garantii ale realitatii. Scopul filosofiei devine, astfel, acela de a clasifica si
de a cataloga aceasta esenta fixa, tratand intreaga existenta ca pe o stare
de fapt stabila, in care forma cea mai inalta de realitate este perfect
imutabila.

Aceasta concentrare pe imuabilitate deriva din necesitatea de a oferi un
fundament solid si rational cunoasterii, o traditie preluata si dezvoltata de
Platon prin teoria Formelor (Ideilor) Eterne, situate intr-un taram
transcendent, separat de lumea sensibila. Daca Fiinta ar fi fluida si
schimbatoare, cunoasterea ar fi imposibila sau, in cel mai bun caz,
subiectiva si tranzitorie. Prin urmare, ontologia clasica dezvolta o etica a
contemplarii, unde intelepciunea suprema consta in a desprinde mintea
de lumea sensibila, iluzorie a schimbarii, pentru a se atasa de formele
inteligibile, eterne. Totusi, rigiditatea acestui model static a intampinat
dificultati majore in explicarea libertatii si a creatiei. Daca totul este
predeterminat de o esenta fixa, spatiul pentru alegere si pentru aparitia
noutatii autentice este sever limitat. Aceasta paradigma tinde sa sacrifice
spontaneitatea si unicitatea in favoarea universalului si a necesarului,
lasand fara raspuns adecvat experienta umana a devenirii personale si
a responsabilitatii istorice.

Mai mult, ontologia substantei tinde sa favorizeze o metafizica a izolarii,
unde fiecare entitate este autonoma si completa in sine. Relatiile sunt
vazute ca fiind exterioare esentei, o problema care persista, de exemplu,
in metafizica lui René Descartes, unde substantele (ganditoare si
extinse) sunt fundamental independente. Prin urmare, dificultatea de a
intelege interconexiunea profunda si comuniunea ajunge sa fie o
problema centrala. Persistenta identitatii devine prioritatea absoluta, iar
orice influenta externa este perceputa ca o amenintare la adresa
integritatii ontologice a entitatii. Acest model, desi ofera confort
intelectual si o aparenta de ordine universala, in cele din urma, nu
reuseste sa cuprinda complexitatea vie a lumii. El reduce timpul la o
simpla succesiune mecanica de momente, golind istoria de capacitatea sa
reala de a genera noutate ontologica, tratand universul ca pe un
ceasornic gigant al carui mecanism este deja fixat si complet determinat.

O consecinta directa a acestei paradigme statice este subordonarea
calitatii in fata cantitatii sia masurabilitatii. Deoarece entitatile fixe
sunt mai usor de clasificat si de cuantificat, ontologia clasica a creat
premisele pentru dezvoltarea unei viziuni mecaniciste a lumii, unde
realitatea ultima este redusa la particule si forte supuse unor legi
imuabile. Aceasta reducere, desi extraordinar de eficienta pentru fizica
newtoniana, intampina dificultati in explicarea fenomenelor ireductibile
precum constiinta, intentionalitatea sau valorile estetice si morale, care
sunt prin natura lor calitative si emergente. Prin fixarea unei structuri
ontologice rigide, se blocheaza recunoasterea dimensiunii de mister si a
excesului de sens care depaseste orice categorizare definitiva. Filosofia
clasica risca astfel sa confunde harta (sistemul sau de categorii) cu
teritoriul (Fiinta insasi).

Critica moderna, lansata in forta de filosofi precum Nietzsche si preluata
de fenomenologie si existentialism, acuza ontologia statica de nihilism
implicit. Desi pare sa celebreze Fiinta eterna, prin negarea Devenirii si a
timpului istoric, ea neaga, de fapt, viata autentica in manifestarile sale
concrete. Exaltarea unui ideal abstract si neschimbator (precum "Binele"
platonic) duce la devalorizarea lumii prezente, a efortului, a patimilor si a
creatiei umane efemere. In acest sens, critica subliniaza ca ontologia
substantei nu este neutra, ci este adesea legata de sisteme de putere si
de ideologii conservatoare care cauta sa inghete structura sociala si
ierarhiile, argumentand ca ordinea existenta reflecta o logica naturala,
imuabila, si ca orice schimbare radicala este o deviatie periculoasa de la
esenta. Astfel, lupta ontologica dintre static si dinamic capata si o
dimensiune politica si etica profunda.

The New Paradigm: Dynamic and Confrontational
Ontology

The new paradigm of Dynamic and Confrontational Ontology vehemently
rejects the supremacy of static substance. Instead, it asserts that Being
is, by its very intrinsic nature, event, process, and relation. This vision
is defined by its two fundamental, inseparable principles that completely
redefine how we understand reality.

1. The Dynamic Principle (Ontos as Flux)

Paradigma Dinamica respinge vehement suprematia substantei statice,
afirmand ca Fiinta este, prin insasi natura ei intrinseca, eveniment,
proces si relatie. Principiul Dinamic sustine ca fluxul si schimbarea
constituie esenta Fiintei, facand ecoul faimoasei idei a lui Heraclit:
"Totul curge". In acest sens, actul (actiunea) este considerat primar in
raport cu starea. Aceasta perspectiva a fost dezvoltata in secolul XX de
Filosofia Procesului, in special de Alfred North Whitehead - care a
vazut universul ca pe o serie de evenimente interconectate - si de Henri
Bergson, care a pus accentul pe Durata (durée) ca esenta a realitatii vii,
ireductibila la masuratorile spatiale. Identitatea unei entitati nu este data
de o esenta interna si fixa, ci este constituita de reteaua sa activa de

relatii cu celelalte entitati si de istoria sa care este mereu in curs de
constituire. Fiinta este privita ca un sistem fundamental deschis, o
potentialitate inepuizabila care se actualizeaza si se exprima constant
in forme noi si neasteptate. Prin urmare, stagnarea si imobilitatea sunt
interpretate ca o forma de non-fiinta sau ca o regresie ontologica.

Acest accent pe dinamism introduce conceptul de temporalitate
radicala, unde timpul este un factor constituant al Fiintei, 0 idee
centrala si in filosofia lui Martin Heidegger. Realitatea este istorie, iar
Fiinta nu poate fi inteleasa decat prin traiectoria ei, prin seria ei de
evenimente creative care nu pot fi reduse la cauze precedente.
Adevarul este, asadar, un adevar al procesului, mereu in miscare.
Dezvoltarea acestei viziuni implica si o ontologie a creatiei continue,
opusa celei a conservarii. Lumea nu este doar mentinuta intr-o stare fixa,
ci este in mod constant refacuta, iar fiecare moment aduce cu sine
posibilitatea aparitiei unei noutati absolute, care nu era cuprinsa in
suma partilor anterioare.

Dinamismul subliniaza ireductibilitatea evenimentului. Nu putem
prezice pe deplin un eveniment din cunoasterea completa a
antecedentelor sale, deoarece insasi structura Fiintei include un element
de libertate si de spontaneitate care transcende determinismul, o tema
profund explorata de Existentialism. Aceasta abordare confera un statut
ontologic sporit creativitatii, pe care o vede ca pe o forta cosmica
fundamentala, nu doar ca pe o capacitate umana particulara. In plus,
aceasta perspectiva obliga la o intelegere a identitatii nu ca pe o
proprietate, ci ca pe o sarcina de indeplinit. Identitatea trebuie
reafirmata si renegociata constant prin interactiune, ea fiind un echilibru
mereu instabil, un rezultat provizoriu al miscarii neincetate a Fiintei.

In acest context, conceptul de relatie primeste o pondere ontologica
maxima. Daca pentru paradigma statica relatiile erau exterioare
substantei (accidentale), pentru principiul dinamic, entitatea este reteaua
sa de relatii. Aceasta inseamna ca esenta nu mai este un nucleu intern
izolat, ci o structura deschisa, determinata de interactiunile sale cu
Alteritatea - un punct esential care deschide usa catre dimensiunea
confruntationala. Prin urmare, autonomia nu inseamna izolare, ci
capacitatea de a influenta si de a fi influentat intr-un mod activ si creativ.
Ontologia dinamica ofera, asadar, un fundament mai solid pentru
fenomenele de comuniune autentica si interdependenta cosmica,
refuzand viziunea mecanicista a unui univers compus din parti complet
separate si inerte.

O alta implicatie cruciala a ontologiei fluxului este reevaluarea completa
a erorii si a negativitatii. Spre deosebire de viziunea statica unde
eroarea era o simpla aberatie sau o lipsa fata de Forma, in dinamism,
negativitatea (lipsa, contradictia, imperfectiunea) devine un factor
productiv. Esecul si imperfectiunea sunt intrinsece procesului de
devenire, fiind necesare pentru a declansa auto-corectia si pentru a
stimula auto-transcendenta. O Fiinta care nu se confrunta cu limitele si
contradictiile sale este o Fiinta care stagneaza. Acest aspect subliniaza
caracterul dramatic si adesea dureros al existentei, unde suferinta si
efortul nu sunt simple pedepse, ci mecanisme esentiale prin care Fiinta

isi depaseste propria conditie si genereaza structuri ontologice mai
complexe si mai evoluate. Principiul Dinamic este, in esenta, un principiu
al vitalitatii neingradite.

2. The Confrontational Principle (Polemos as the Creative
Engine)

Principiul Confruntational nu este o aditie optionala sau o complicatie
ulterioara la dinamism, ci este forta motrice activa care explica de ce
fluxul nu se prabuseste intr-un haos amorf si de ce miscarea continua a
Fiintei genereaza ordine, structura si noutate. El afirma ca adevarul,
sensul si existenta autentica se nasc din tensiune, din opozitie si
din lupta (Polemos). Filozofia confruntationala readuce in centrul
metafizicii intelegerea arhaica, dar profunda, a lui Heraclit: "Razboiul
(Polemos) este parintele tuturor lucrurilor, regele tuturor." Aceasta lupta
fundamentala nu trebuie interpretata simplist ca un conflict fizic sau
distructiv, ci ca acea contradictie necesara si structurala care
genereaza diferentiere, distinctie si noutate ireductibila in realitate.
Adevarata Fiinta este forjata in ciocnirea perpetua dintre fortele opuse,
precum necesitatea (ceea ce este dat si finit) si libertatea (ceea ce poate
fi si transcende).

Confruntarea devine, astfel, motorul esential al cresterii ontologice,
cerand si obligand entitatile sa-si depaseasca statutul actual, rigid sau
determinat. Fara 0 opozitie activa, dinamicul ar tinde fie spre o simpla
miscare mecanica si repetitiva, fie spre o dispersie inerta lipsita de
directie. Acest accent pe lupta plaseaza negativitatea (sau Alteritatea
radicala) in centrul ontologiei, refuzand categoric o metafizica a armoniei
prestabilite si confortabile. Existenta autentica este obtinuta prin
rezistenta activa la orice forma de determinare absoluta si prin actul de
a spune nu oricarei structuri (interioare sau exterioare) care incearca sa
o fixeze, sa o defineasca in termeni finali.

La nivelul existentei umane, confruntarea se traduce in alegere, risc si
responsabilitate. Adevarul si autenticitatea nu sunt atinse prin
resemnare sau acceptare pasiva a lumii asa cum este, ci prin actiunea
constanta de a confrunta si a schimba realitatea pentru a o aduce intr-o
stare de manifestare superioara. Unitatea, fie ca este sociala sau
personala, nu este realizata prin omogenizare, ci este intotdeauna un
rezultat precar al tensiunii mentinute intre entitati libere si distincte.
Prin urmare, confruntarea nu este o greseala morala sau o deviatie, ci
este insasi calea catre o Fiinta mai complexa, mai vie si mai implinita. Ea
confera suferintei si efortului un sens profund ontologic, transformand
obstacolul si lipsa in materie prima vitala pentru creatie. Din acest punct
de vedere, orice forma de pace absoluta, de echilibru perfect si
neschimbator, ar echivala cu moartea ontologica.

O implicatie cruciala a acestui principiu este intelegerea faptului ca
structurile emergente nu sunt simple sinteze, ci rezultatul unei lupte
care transcende partile initiale. Sinteza confruntationala (o tema centrala
la Hegel) nu este o medie, ci o noua realitate care inglobeaza si

depaseste contradictia initiala. Astfel, noutatea este garantata. Aceasta
viziune ontologica este extrem de relevanta pentru dinamica sociala,
explicand de ce progresul social si politic nu poate fi atins exclusiv prin
consens, Ci necesita adesea conflictul dintre teze si antiteze, dintre
ordinea existenta si fortele de eliberare. In esenta, principiul
confruntational sustine ca Fiinta este o lupta pentru libertate sub
masca luptei pentru existenta.

Persistenta Tensiunii ca Stare de Gratie Ontologica. in cele din
urma, paradigma confruntationala reconfigureaza ideea de perfectiune.
Perfectiunea nu mai este un punct terminus static (o stare de a fi
definitiva), ci devine mentinerea activa, cu succes, a tensiunii intre poli.
Aceasta este o "stare de gratie" in sens filosofic, un echilibru dinamic si
mereu precar, care necesita efort si constientizare constanta. Stabilitatea
nu este eliminata, ci este transformata dintr-o proprietate fixa intr-un
efect de durata al confruntarii. Astfel, principiul afirma nu doar ca
tensiunea creeaza Fiinta, ci ca tensiunea este Fiinta insasi in
manifestarea sa cea mai inalta si mai plina de sens. Orice tentativa de a
rezolva definitiv contradictia ar reprezenta o retragere din realitatea
complexa, vie.

Conclusions

The major philosophical significance of the Confrontational Paradigm
resides not just in its premises, but in its dramatic, essential contrast with
Classical Ontology. If the static paradigm fundamentally seeks the
permanence, security, and universal essence of things, this new dynamic
approach defines reality through uninterrupted motion, through inner
and outer struggle (the Polemos), and through unrelenting,
responsible actualization. This fundamental difference has profound
consequences, transforming ontology from a purely contemplative and
passive discipline, focused merely on cataloguing a stable universe, into
an ethical and activist framework that demands engagement. In this
new dynamic framework, the very condition of existence is defined by the
courage to enter into tension and fight for a continuous self-realization,
thereby transforming existence from a mere passive state into an
active, self-created adventure. The dynamism requires us to
acknowledge that the only certainty is the inevitability of change.

Thus, the Confrontational Paradigm is not merely an intellectual
description of the world's mechanisms, but functions as a potent call to
action. It invites, or rather obligates, us to recognize that authentic
Being is never a passive gift to be received, but an active, demanding
task to be fulfilled moment by moment. The supreme moral and
ontological responsibility for the individual is to participate consciously in
the existential Polemos—the creative struggle—to continuously generate
novelty and actively prevent any external structure or internal inertia
(any form of determinism) from fully defining or fixing us. This concept
fundamentally redefines human freedom as an ongoing, never-ending
achievement.

Essentially, the philosophical difference between these two great
ontological visions can be definitively summarized by examining their
respective attitudes toward finality or teleology. Static Ontology
characteristically postulates an already achieved, perfect, and fixed
finality (the Form or Essence) toward which the material world merely
strives or falls short, while Dynamic and Confrontational Ontology
radically asserts that finality is always open, never complete, and
always under dynamic, creative construction. Accepting this
constructive endlessness—this fundamental lack of closure—is the
ultimate and supreme act of philosophical maturity and courage. The
ongoing confrontation between the Static impulse (the drive for security)
and the Dynamic impulse (the impulse for growth) reflects an eternal,
fundamental tension within human experience itself: that between the
conservative need for security and comfort (offered by the permanence
of essence) and the transformative, ethical impulse toward freedom and
radical change (generated by the potential for change).

The Confrontational Paradigm affirms with force that this tension
between permanence and change must be maintained, embraced, and
creatively exploited for Fiinta (Being) to be fully alive and authentically
real. Accepting uncertainty and risk therefore becomes an ontological
virtue, reversing the values of classical thought. It recognizes that static
perfection, while offering a psychological comfort zone, is ultimately
inferior in value and complexity to the sheer richness of a Being always
open to the future and always capable of self-transcendence. This
Ontology of Becoming requires a mature philosophical vision that does
not fear internal or external contradiction, but instead views conflict and
tension as the inexhaustible, primary source of meaning and genuine
growth. Consequently, true, authentic Being is that which constantly
mediates and navigates the space between stability and turbulent
change, effectively using the necessary energy of conflict to propel itself
from what it currently is to what it must become. The continuous
becoming is the ultimate measure of reality.

The lasting legacy of this dynamic philosophy is its profound implication
for epistemology. If Being is static, knowledge is the passive discovery
of timeless truths. If Being is dynamic and confrontational, then
knowledge must be understood as an active, participatory
construction—a process of making sense through engagement, critique,
and continuous revision. The act of knowing becomes inseparable from
the act of intervening in and changing reality, reflecting the core insight
that reality is fundamentally a verb, not a noun. This shift demands
intellectual humility, recognizing that any given truth is inherently
provisional and bound by the current state of the process, always subject
to being overturned by the emergent novelty of the future. The certainty
prized by the static worldview is replaced by the robustness of a
knowledge system designed to accommodate radical uncertainty.

Finally, the Confrontational Paradigm necessitates a revaluation of value
and critique in both philosophy and society. Value, in the static sense, is
often derived from proximity to the immutable ideal. In the dynamic
framework, value is located in the intensity of life, the degree of self-

transcendence, and the capacity for creative rupture. The purpose
of philosophical critique, therefore, is not merely to detect errors in logic,
but to actively identify and dismantle those structures—whether
