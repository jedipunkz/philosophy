Early Years

LATO, the Athenian, the son of Ariston and
Perictione or Potone, was born in 428 B.c., the
fourth year of what Athens’ great historian, Thucy-
dides, writing as contemporary of the events, ex-
pressly denotes as the Great War. His birth followed
close upon the death of Pericles, Athens’ greatest
statesman, who had perfected her democracy as well
as her empire, which was being contested in this war
by Sparta and her confederates, and who had enjoyed
under that democracy a personal ascendancy com-
parable with that of the present American president.
Plato’s family, which on the father’s side traced its
legendary descent back to King Codrus and even to
the god Poseidon, could on the mother’s side show
historical connection with Solon, the revered founder

The Death of Socrates

The restoration was marked on the whole by
moderation and absence of vindictiveness, a special
act of amnesty being passed which forbade all political
recrimination. Consequently Plato’s thoughts began
once more to turn to politics, though this time more
slowly and cautiously. But once more Socrates was
destined to act as the touchstone of a government’s
morality. In 399 he was brought to trial on the
charge of “not believing in the gods in whom the
city believes, and of introducing novel divinities,
also of corrupting the youth.” The “ gods,” which
he, the most deeply religious man of classical anti-
quity, was really suspected of not believing in were
the dogmas of democracy, while the “ novel divini-
ties ’’ his accusers had in mind were the novelties of
fascism, and that, too, notwithstanding the fact that
under fascism he had risked death for “ corrupting ”

After reading Plato’s Apology, the Crito, and, above

a crisis in their thought and a confirmation of what

thinkers at all, but uncomprehending though devoted
folk, whom their beloved leader’s death seems to
have filled at first with nothing but fear, perplexity,
and despair. The immediate effect of Socrates’ death
on Plato was to make him look with a critical eye
not merely on the political life of his own city, to
which, in the absence of any party he could join,
he saw no way of contributing, but also on the
existing laws, customs, and constitution of all cities.

These were all diseased, he concluded. There was,

Second Visit to Sicily : An Essay in Miracle

In that year Dionysius died, and was succeeded by
his son Dionysius II. Dion, now a man of forty, had
remained throughout faithful to the hope and vision
with which he had been inspired at his first meeting
with Plato. In the court of Dionysius I., thanks
to his sheer ability, necessary to the despot, he had
enjoyed a unique position, being allowed even the
privilege of free speech. Now, helped in addition
by his princely wealth, he towered easily as a giant
amongst the boon companions and slavish toadies
who surrounded the young and inexperienced ruler,
and, in spite of the unpopularity earned him by his
mode of life so different from theirs, in spite also of
their constant attempts to undermine his influence,
he had the affairs of the empire practically in his
hands. These affairs he now saw going to pieces
through the loose living of the young tyrant which
was also loosening the iron bonds of authority
forged by the old one. A disinterested idealist with
no desire and no possibility of seizing power for
himself, what was he to do? An inspiration struck
him. The chief fault of Dionysius was his lack of
education. His father, afraid even of his own heir
if given the training and entourage befitting a future
ruler, had kept him confined to the palace in a
seclusion devoted to the unambitious and unexciting

pursuit of carpentry. Now, at twenty-eight, he was
