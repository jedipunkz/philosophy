Foreword

About 12,000 years ago, changes took place in the wild world of planet Earth that altered its
appearance forever. Intelligent but savage creatures suddenly changed their nomadic lifestyle to a
sedentary one. Lone survival, cannibalism, and territorial rivalry were replaced by collective labor
and brotherhood; hunting and gathering by the manual cultivation of crops and animals. The Stone
Age Homo sapiens' rejection of the cruel wild lifestyle occurred in a relatively short historical period
among populations separated by oceans and great distances.

Primitive people have been adapting to the conditions of existence and survival in the wild for
tens of thousands of years. Therefore, such a rapid and drastic change in the lifestyle of the global
population of Homo sapiens is difficult to explain in terms of a voluntary and independent transition.
Such changes are possible either in organized societies or in small groups that clearly benefit from
them. However, here we run into a dilemma: Neolithic Homo sapiens survived on the limited gifts of
surrounding nature and migratory animals. A sedentary lifestyle in such an environment would mean
starvation or even death for him.

On the other hand, the history of the modern world shows that even in an organized society,
plans to change the usual foundations, even when there are prospects for improving life, tear society
apart into contradictions that cause active resistance to these changes. In this context, the question
arises: how did the disparate, unrestrained, and rivalrous savages of the Neolithic period manage to
agree, organize, and force themselves and others to obey common laws, learn to work together, and
share the harvest among all members of the settlement? Moreover, the process of transformation from
nomadic hunter-gatherer to sedentary farmer occurred on almost every continent on earth. This leads
us to the idea that someone with the knowledge of the principles of a civilized society might stay
behind these changes. This statement completely contradicts the modern scientific view of how
agriculture arose and how human civilization began. Moreover, it is closer to the religious idea of a
single creator. However, we should make it clear that this article has nothing to do with religion or
religious ideas.

The changes in the life of the forest savage, which occurred 12,000 years ago, led him to the
path of civilized life: team productive work, observance of general laws, organization. This stopped
rivalry, cannibalism, violence, constant migration from place to place, struggle for survival and
eradicated other wild atavisms. The process of changing the system took place at a time when the
savages had no written language. Consequently, the process of knowledge and information transfer
took place orally. It is not known how well associative thinking was developed among Stone Age
people. However, in the mythological works that have come down to us from ancient times, allegorical
and metaphorical images are used quite actively. Moreover, these images are very deeply elaborated,
which is unusual for primitive life.

If we wanted to describe briefly the fundamental changes that took place in the lives of Stone
Age savages, while preserving their essence, what object would we use? Of course, it should be a
metaphor or an allegory, using a simple image that is always in front of our eyes. For example, the
image of night is associated with fear, danger, and uncertainty, and the image of day is associated with
the sun, warmth, and safety. That is, the transition of Homo sapiens from the half-starved and savage
state to a civilized way of life would be described as a transition from the darkness of savagery to the
light of civilized life. However, this allegory does not convey the fullness of the qualitative changes:

Plato confirms that the heaven and the cosmos are only allegories:

“Now in every subject it is of utmost importance to begin at the natural beginning, and so, on
the subject of an image and its model, we must make the following specification: the accounts we give
of things have the same character as the subjects they set forth.” [Plato. Timaeus. 29b]?

The word "cosmos" comes from the Greek word "kKéop0¢" (kosmos), which means "order,"
"arrangement," "beauty." In ancient Greece, the cosmos was associated with harmony and beauty
based on a certain ordered system. With this concept, Plato was trying to convey the transition from
an unordered existence to an organized life arranged according to certain laws, i.e., the acculturation
of savages to life according to universal laws.

Continuing the theme of metaphorical and allegorical images actively used in mythological
literature, it is worth noting that in addition to the above images, many others such as fire (battle,
watriors), water (population, society), earth (farmers, agriculture), stone (work team), mountain
(hierarchy, organization), the word (law, principle), the soul (a set of laws governing the behavior of
society as an integral organism) are also used. Many of these allegories are reflected in the meanings
of cuneiform signs.

The historical roots of the word "God" point us to the Indo-Iranian word "bhaga/baga,"
meaning "sharing the good," which is directly related to the cuneiform signs BA and GAz:

The semantic meaning of the coupled signs BA (to divide into shares, to share, to halve; to
allot) and GA2 (house; temple; (temple) household; house-lot; estate) describes a temple (society) in
which people share benefits among themselves.

The Slavic concept of "god (bor)" [pronounced: bo:g] likely came from the first three
characters (bag), and the English "god" [pronounced: ga:d] - from the last two characters (ga). Since

a temple or a dwelling. To confirm the semantic meaning of the term "God" as "sharing the good," we
can turn to the Latin language. The Latin words "divi" (gods), "div" (division), and the English words
"divine" and "divide," which have a common root "div," undoubtedly confirm the idea that God is a
community that distributes the food and goods it produces equally among its members.

Thus, the cuneiform sign AN reflects the same object — a civilized society based on productive
labor, public ownership and equal distribution of benefits. The concept of "Heaven" describes the ascent
of Homo sapiens to a new stage of development of social relations, while the concept of "God" conveys
the adoption of a new worldview and way of life, expressed through the sacrifice of their labor for the
benefit of the community, society. We find confirmation that the allegories of heaven and God are one

“For this our Cosmos has received the living creatures both mortal and immortal and been

The Stone Age savages led a scattered lifestyle, following only the law of survival, regardless
of anyone or anything. Satisfying their hunger was the main goal of their lives, and each of them
independently sought ways to achieve it. However, a team of civilized emissaries showed them
another way to achieve their goal - ending rivalry and working together to produce food. This meant
the creation of a society independent of Nature and on its own producing enough food for itself, i.e. a
self-sufficient and self-contained society. To achieve this, it was necessary to transform nomadic
hunters and gatherers into sedentary farmers:

“for it was so designed as to supply its own wastage as food for itself, and to experience by
its own agency and within itself all actions and passions, since He that had constructed it deemed
that it would be better if it were self-sufficing rather than in need of other things.” [Plato. Timaeus.
33c-d]

Similar ideas are expressed in lines #2.3 - #2.6 of the artifact we are discussing.

Settlement and agriculture imply a communal lifestyle, and this, in turn, requires maintaining
order and organization. The principle of collectivism comes to the rescue here: the minority obey the
majority. Furthermore, the majority should make its decisions based on the principle of equal and fair
distribution of crops and resources. This is stated in lines #2.1 - #2.2 and #2.7 - #2.9.

Lines #2.10 - #2.12 mention five regions where the high official and his regional
representatives brought the light of civilization and ended savagery. Paleontological studies show that
agriculture emerged almost simultaneously in five regions: North America (Mississippi Valley and
central Mexico), South America (northeastern region), Africa (Fertile Crescent), Asia (Fertile
Crescent, Yangtze and Yellow River Valleys), and the highlands of New Guinea. This marks a
significant shift from the foraging economy that humans had practiced for over a million years to a
producing economy. Interestingly, this synchronous rejection of savagery took place on different
continents when humans had not yet mastered the art of sea travel.

Existence in conditions of limited resources and competition forced each savage to find his
own way of survival, depending on his mindset and physical abilities. Some obtained food by hunting,
some by force, some by cunning and deception, some by cannibalism, some by forcing others to obtain
food for them. At the same time, it was unlikely that communities could be organized, since each
individual lived alone and could leave one group and join another at the first sign of conflict.
Therefore, in order to organize communities and accept nomadic hunter-gatherers as members, the

latter had to give up their wild habits. Thus, seven requirements, called the Seven, were presented:

Hermes put the "activity/energy of increasing and decreasing" first, which should be disposed
of when someone "sets in motion the way above," in other words, with the transformation of a savage
into a civilized person. What does this mean? Agriculture requires a sedentary lifestyle and discipline.
If the members of the community continue to behave as before, leaving the community at the first
contradiction and joining other groups, no community will last long under such conditions, and the
harvest will be lost. With the increase of contradictions, the conflict can escalate into physical
confrontation. Under such conditions, the whole point of agriculture is lost. Therefore, the first
atavism of savagery, independence from the community, had to be eradicated during the formation of
a civilized existence. The community should not have changed its composition because of
contradictions. The members of the society were obliged to recognize the importance of collective
work to meet the needs of each member, and therefore remained united with the team, as stated in
lines #2.7-#2.9.

Line #2.13 talks about fostering a civilized mentality among savages with the help of seven
rules. The so-called “regional instructors” were involved in the education, but since the nomadic
hunters and gatherers had no written language, they learned these rules orally. Therefore, the set of
seven rules and the way they are presented in mythological literature are referred to by the term
“word”:

“In the beginning was the Word, and the Word was with God, and the Word was God.
(John.1:1. NKJV)”

Given the previously revealed meaning of the word “God,” a phrase from the Gospel suggests
that the principle of collectivism existed before it began to guide the lives of savages. Logically, this
principle was the basis for some pre-existing society or state, rather than the invention of hunter-
gatherers.

In the mythological literature, there is another concept that describes the functionality of these
rules — “soul.” The soul is a set of instructions that govern the whole body, and a body is understood
as a labor team, a community. Plato defines this concept as follows

“God did not likewise plan it to be younger than the body; for, when uniting them, He would
not have permitted the elder to be ruled by the younger; but as for us men, even as we ourselves
partake largely of the accidental and casual, so also do our words. God, however, constructed Soul
to be older than Body and prior in birth and excellence, since she was to be the mistress and ruler
and it the ruled;” [Plato. Timaeus. 34c]!
