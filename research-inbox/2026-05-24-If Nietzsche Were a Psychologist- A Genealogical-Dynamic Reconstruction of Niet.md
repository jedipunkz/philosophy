## Abstract

Friedrich Nietzsche consistently identified himself as a psychologist, yet the discipline he envisioned bore little resemblance to the clinical and experimental traditions that would dominate the twentieth century. This treatise reconstructs what a formalized Nietzschean psychology would look like if Nietzsche had systematized his psychological insights rather than embedding them within philological and philosophical works. Drawing on Nietzsche’s published corpus—particularly Beyond Good and Evil (1886), On the Genealogy of Morality (1887), and Twilight of the Idols (1889)—and engaging critically with Freud, Jung, Adler, and contemporary frameworks including predictive processing and existential psychology, this paper identifies the core structural elements of Nietzschean psychology: a drive-pluralist ontology of the psyche, will to power as a motivational principle, genealogical method as diagnostic tool, an affect-centered rather than cognition-centered approach, a typological model of personality, and a developmental trajectory oriented toward self-overcoming rather than adaptation. The analysis argues that Nietzsche’s psychological framework, while never formalized during his productive life, constitutes a coherent and anticipatory system that challenges reductive, homeostatic, and moralistic tendencies in modern psychology. The treatise concludes by situating Nietzschean psychology as a necessary corrective within contemporary discourse, particularly for its insistence that psychology’s central question is not what ails the psyche but what kind of life a psyche makes possible.
