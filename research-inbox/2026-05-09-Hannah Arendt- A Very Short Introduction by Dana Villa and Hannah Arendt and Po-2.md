Hannah Arendt: A Very Short Introduction
offers a concise exposition of
Arendt’s most important ideas. Each chapter presents a group of related
concepts from Arendt (for example, Chapter 3 focuses on action, freedom,
and politics) and examines them by tracing them across Arendt’s works.
This offers the reader insight into foundational frameworks in Arendt’s
thought and allows one to begin to recognize common threads as well as
contrasts between her works.
As the title

suggests,
Hannah Arendt and Politics
introduces Arendt specifically as a political thinker. The authors take a unique approach to presenting
the reader with Arendt’s thinking about politics: exegesis of Arendt’s ideas
is combined with an application of her frameworks to current sociopolitical
events, such as anti-racist social movements and gender equality. The goal
of the book is, thus, twofold: the authors set out to help the reader understand Arendt’s political thought and then to guide the reader toward the
ability to use her ideas to think about the present political moment.

yet equal participant in plurality (Arendt 1958, 22–23). The present review

and

thinking about

In
Hannah Arendt and Politics
, Robaszkiewicz and Weinman understand
Arendt as “a thinker who engages in both word and deed with the practices
of the common world, and [invites] us to do the same” (Robaszkiewicz and
Weinman 2023, 3). The specific concept that facilitates this invitation is the
idea of “exercises in political thinking.” As Robaszkiewicz and Weinman explain, exercises in political thinking are thought experiments through which
one calibrates one’s political cognition. The exercises are a critical reflection
on the world
as it is
rather than as it should be, emphasizing the contingent
and changing nature of politics (Robaszkiewicz and Weinman 2023, 64). Politics is always changing, and, consequently, so is the focus of exercises in
political thinking. This is another reason why the term “exercise” is fitting:
the kind of political cognition that these exercises produce is situated in the
present moment and thus temporary, so
repetition
becomes their inherent
feature, with each iteration of an exercise turning its attention back to the
current state of the world.
While the concept of exercises in political thinking is introduced in
Robaszkiewicz and Weinman’s
Hannah Arendt and Politics

Hannah
Arendt: A Very Short Introduction
helps us understand Arendt’s reasons
for endorsing this mode of thinking about the world. Arendt rejects what
Villa calls the “contemplative ideal” of Western philosophy and religion,
which aimed to free one from engaging with the fleeting particulars of the
world for the sake of existence in undisrupted meditation (Villa 2023, 115).
In contrast to this perspective on thinking as being grounded in non-time
and non-world, Robaszkiewicz and Weinman argue that for Arendt, the
dialectical or Socratic tradition of thinking presents a sense of initial plurality that exists in every person (Robaszkiewicz and Weinman 2023, 45).
As Villa phrases it, this kind of “inner dialogue” of thinking is accessible
to everyone, according to Arendt, not just philosophers (Villa 2023, 104).
Unlike engaging with metaphysical universals, the subject of dialectical, inner dialogue-driven thinking is the world that surrounds us and of which
we are a part. Villa illustrates this kind of thinking using an example from

Arendt’s own life. Witnessing Eichmann’s trial, Arendt “found herself unable to place him under the ready-to-hand” universal concepts and instead
had to think through the particulars of the trial and testimonies in order
to form a judgment about Eichmann, leading her to the conception of the
now-famous idea of the “banality of evil” (Villa 2023, 100).
In the example above, the act of forming a judgment is crucial in regard
to exercises in political thinking, as judgment is
the
product of such exercises. Judgment, as Robaszkiewicz and Weinman explain, is speculative yet

While judgment itself is not a form of action, we can think of exercises in

the world rather than acting into the world preserves a degree of separation from it, but because it is thinking
about

various political actors and political realities in it, our willingness to make
a judgment might translate into our willingness to act. Exercises in political
thinking require a critical observation of the world and the cultivation of
an enlarged understanding of it, both of which allow us to appreciate the
potential of political action in the world.

Villa opens the book

with a brief retelling of Arendt’s life as one that
constantly made her practice thinking and judgment about politics, as she
experienced growing antisemitism in her home country of Germany, was
driven to years of statelessness by the war, became an immigrant in the
United States, and finally rose to prominence as an academic and public
thinker in her new home country. Arendt’s own life thus captures the idea
of exercises in political thinking as a mode of preparation for action in the
world that I discussed earlier, even though in Arendt’s case, action was not
always taken on voluntarily but necessitated by the turbulent and dangerous times through which she lived. Indeed, Robaszkiewicz and Weinman
cite the example of Arendt deciding to leave Germany in the early 1930s as
a judgment about politics that was immediately followed by radical action:

It is precisely this truth about the world—that we often do not have
the luxury of keeping our judgment merely a judgment; we have to transform it into a decision to act—that underscores the need for us to engage in
exercises in political thinking today. While most of us might not share in
Arendt’s experience with persecution, statelessness, and immigration, what
we do arguably share with her is living through different crises of our political reality, where crisis can be understood as a moment that
especially

reality, we might also be living in what Arendt called “dark times,” where
“the public realm has been obscured” and “the world [has] become so dubious that people have ceased to ask any more of politics than that it show due
consideration for their vital interests and personal liberty” (Arendt 1968,
11). The circumstances of living in dark times, where the space for political
action is at times intentionally obscured, make exercises in political thinking
an especially salient enterprise. Accordingly, in

Each exercise takes Arendt’s thinking as its starting point, which conceptually grounds the subsequent analysis of the contemporary variation of the
issue at stake. This is another complementary point between the two books:
thinking about current politics with the help of Arendt requires good insight

It needs to be emphasized that starting our thinking about politics with
Arendt does not always mean we end it alongside her, according to Robaszkiewicz and Weinman. This attitude of “thinking with Arendt against
Arendt” is borrowed by the authors from Seyla Benhabib and expanded
into a conceptual tool in
Hannah Arendt and Politics
. The need to think with
Arendt against Arendt becomes an especially beneficial framework in their
chapters on Arendt and feminist agency, racism and anti-racism, and even
the climate crisis, where the authors acknowledge some of Arendt’s limitations as a thinker and as a person. For instance, Arendt’s unwillingness to
see children as potential political actors needs to be considered when using
her work to think about the Fridays for Future movement. As another example, we need to be aware of Arendt’s racial prejudice when reflecting on the
Black Lives Matter movement with her help.
The approach of thinking with Arendt against Arendt does not amount
to undermining the value of engaging with Arendt when thinking about
current politics. Instead, it reminds us of the basic truth about Arendt’s understanding of politics: the spontaneity of action in the world constantly
generates new political realities, which means that our thinking about them
needs to evolve accordingly. In this sense, exercises in political thinking “offer a critical examination of past traditions” while at the same time, “in their
work as experiments, they provide a perspective on future possibilities,
without trying to define or delimit these possibilities” (Robaszkiewicz and
Weinman 2023, 4). Villa’s book also helps us understand Arendt’s perspective on the role of the past in thinking about future political possibilities. As
Villa explains, according to Arendt, totalitarian ideologies needed mythological, cohesive explanations of the past in order to “confidently [predict],
with pseudo-scientific accuracy, the shape of the future” (Villa 2023, 39–40).

In line with this Arendtian understanding of politics, Robaszkiewicz
and Weinman’s
Hannah Arendt and Politics
successfully accomplishes its goal
of using Arendt as a model for developing one’s ability to exercise political thinking while establishing that this mode of thinking can be applied
to different domains of our current political reality. Beyond the exercises
in thinking about current political issues that the authors themselves construct in their book, one can imagine thinking with (and possibly against
and beyond) Arendt about other topics where her work can serve as a starting point for careful consideration of the present political moment, such as
political polarization or the effect of emerging technologies on the human
condition.
Villa’s
Hannah Arendt: A Very Short Introduction
likewise delivers on its
promise to serve as a succinct introduction to Arendt’s most important ideas
and works. The book presents these in a manner that is easy to follow and
grasp, which, as I have argued, gives the reader a solid conceptual vocabulary to begin building a deeper future engagement with Arendt, potentially
in the form of exercises in political thinking.
Ultimately,
Hannah Arendt: A Very Short Introduction
and
Hannah Arendt and Politics
are both books about thinking with Arendt.
Hannah Arendt:
A Very Short Introduction
introduces us to how Arendt thought about the
world, and
Hannah Arendt and Politics
invites us to think about our current world with Arendt. As such, both books remind us that “life without
thinking cannot be said to be fully alive” (Villa 2023, 113). While, as I have
discussed earlier, thinking about politics is not a form of action, we know
that for Arendt, being “fully alive” amounts to a life in plurality, which is a
life of spontaneous action among fellow people (Arendt 1958, 7). By thinking about politics carefully, we are therefore preparing ourselves for a life of
acting into the world.
