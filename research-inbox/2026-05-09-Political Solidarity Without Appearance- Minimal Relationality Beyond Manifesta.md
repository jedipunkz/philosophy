## Abstract

This paper proposes a reconfiguration of political solidarity by removing manifestation—visibility, recognition, and addressability—as necessary conditions for political relation. Drawing on critiques of phenomenality in the works of Hannah Arendt and Judith Butler, it argues for a model of minimal solidarity grounded not in appearance but in structural endurance. By subtracting manifestation, political relation becomes more resilient, extending solidarity to those rendered invisible, unintelligible, or inaudible by existing structures of power. Minimal solidarity resists the commodification of suffering and the logics of spectacle, offering a foundation for political life that persists irrespective of visibility or recognition. It also broadens political ontology beyond the human, encompassing non-human and future beings. This approach has profound implications for contemporary political practice, particularly in contexts of systemic invisibility, and points toward a more inclusive and enduring conception of political life.

1. Introduction: The Politics of Visibility

Political solidarity, in both modern and contemporary theory, has been consistently
tethered to structures of visibility, recognition, and addressability. The notion that
solidarity arises from the public manifestation of individuals and groups has become
a
foundational assumption. From the classical polis to contemporary struggles for rights
and justice, appearing has been framed as the precondition for political existence and
collective action. The demand to be seen, to be recognized, to be counted satura tes not
only the rhetoric of social movements but also the conceptual scaffolding of political
theory itself.

This reliance on visibility is neither incidental nor superficial. It is structurally embedded
in the conceptual frameworks that have historically governed political thought. Arendt
famously situates the emergence of political life in the
space of appearance
, where
individuals disclose themselves to one another through speech and action

politically, on this view, is to appear before others in a shared world, to reveal one’s
distinctiveness and agency through public deeds. Butler extends this logic into the
domain of subject formation and vulnerability, positing that political and

ethical life are
constituted through processes of recognizability within normative frames
2
. Recognition
becomes the mechanism through which subjects gain social legibility and political voice.
